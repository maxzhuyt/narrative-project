A  Fawn  Asleep  The  long-awaited  revival  of  Her-  bert  H.  Parker's  &quot;The  Slumbering  Fawn&quot;  at  the  [REDACTED]  of  the  Americas  Southwest  Playhouse  last  Wednesday  is  certainly  one  of  the  most  altitudinous  events  of  the  helter-skelter  week  just  past.  It  proved  on  the  opening  night  to  be  a  classicist's  delight  in  spite  of  the  widely  reported  and  extravagantly  pho-  tographed  rift  in  backstage  circles  before  the  curtain.  This  rift  is  said  to  have  been  occasioned  by  the  celebrated  choreogra-  pher's  insistence  on  repeating  the  al-  leged  dissonant  pas  de  onze  precisely  as  it  was  rendered,  amid  general  amazement  on  the  part  of  both  reviewers  and  audi-  ence,  at  the  original  performance  at  the  Midtown  Opera  House  in  1924,  when  this  reviewer,  naturally,  was  unable  to  attend.  However,  if  such  a  rift  did  oc-  cur  before  the  curtain  rose  last  Wednes-  day,  all  was  serene  in  the  ranks  when  the  ten  mixed  dancers  and  the  one  odd,  or  eleventh,  member  of  the  onze,  came  leaping  into  view  simultaneously  and  vied  with  one  another  through  a  series  of  sus-  tained  fowett\xc3\xa9s,  gargouillades,  tours  en  Pair,  and  a  jig  &amp;  PEire  that  &quot;brought  down  the  house.&quot;  As  the  dancers'  tempo  gradually  decreased  and  the  noisy  hulla-  baloo  of  the  onze  diminished,  though  not  very  perceptibly,  the  entire  corps  joined  the  onze  and  veil  after  veil  of  trans-  parent  white  nylon  was  then  lifted  from  the  form  ofthe  slumbering  fawn  as  if  by  magic  but  actually  by  means  of  quasi-  invisible  wires  and  hooks  that  were  low-  ered  and  raised  at  regular  intervals  in  time  to  the  music  from  scaffolding  that  had  been  erected  behind  the  proscenium.  At  that  juncture,  all  the  dancers  ap-  proached,  as  softly  as  possible,  the  re-  cumbent  and  by  now  exposed  form  of  the  somnolent  fawn,  the  part  of  the  fawn  being  played  by  the  highly  talent-  ed  and  charming,  though  still  somewhat  childish  and  insecure,  Miss  Elizabeth  Maddox.  One  wishes  for  greater  space  for  &quot;Slumbering,&quot;  but  one  has  been  re-  fused  it.
