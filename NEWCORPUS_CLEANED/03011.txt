THE  RECIPES  OF  CHAIRMAN  MAO  HE  recent  cautious  rapproche-  ment  between  the  United  [REDACTED]  and  the  People's  Republic  of  4368696e61  has  encouraged  some  tentative  moves  by  each  side  in  the  direction  of  a  cultural  exchange.  Among  these,  the  just  published  &quot;Recipes  of  Chair-  man  Mao&quot;  is  a  refreshing  relief  from  the  highly  polemical  matter  heretofore  available  from  the  government-con-  trolled  foreign-language  press  in  Pe-  king.  The  notebook  that  formed  the  basis  of  the  &quot;Recipes&quot;  was  kept  by  Lin  Pao-tsien,  Mao  Tse-tung's  brilliant  field  chef  (later  Minister  of  All  Sea-  food)  during  the  Long  March  (1934-  35).  To  those  unfamiliar  with  the  cuisine  of  the  New  4368696e61,  it  should  be  pointed  out  that  the  recipes  are  not  idealistic  combinations  of  ingredients,  disengaged  from  political  reality,  as  is  so  often  the  case  with  traditional  cook-  books.  Rather,  they  are,  as  Comrade  Lin  says,  &quot;products  of  the  proletariat's  tire-  less  quest  for  dignity,  knowledge,  and  more  interesting  meat  dishes.&quot;  Edited  and  with  historical  notes  by  Lin  Pao-  tsien  himself,  this  English  edition  of  the  classic  &quot;Recipes&quot;  reveals  to  the  historian-  gourmet  yet  another  facet  of  the  great  Chinese  Cultural  Revolution  and  of  the  magnetic  Mao  Tse-tung-soldier,  poet,  philosopher,  revolutionary,  and  dietitian.  Following  are  some  excerpts.  N  December  of  1934,  after  a  bitter  struggle,  thirty  thousand  troops  of  the  People's  Liberation  Army  under   Mao  Tse-tung  took  the  province  of  Kweichow.  It  was  our  first  victory.  &quot;All  praise  to  the  masses  and  to  Mao  Tse-tung,  who  subdues  the  enemy  with  an  effective  stratagem,&quot;  sang  the  troops,  and  they  then  followed  with  the  popular  ballad  &quot;Oppose  Deviation-  ist  Counter-Productivity.&quot;  At  a  meet-  ing  of  the  provisional  committee,  the  week's  menu  was  hotly  discussed.  Dep-  uty  Commander  Chou  En-lai  voted  for  Lobster  with  Black  Bean  Sauce.  Ex-  warlord  Peng  Teh-huai,  notoriously  partial  to  Seven  Taste  Chicken,  flew  into  a  rage,  and  Chen  Yi,  a  native  of  Szechwan,  threatened  defection  unless  a  Hot  and  Spicy  Carp  &quot;found  its  way  to  the  table,  and  quickly.&quot;  A  major  al-  tercation  was  averted  by  Mao's  elo-  quent  plea  for  Mu-shu  Pork  followed  by  a  clear  soup-a  compromise  ap-  plauded  by  all.  We  then  recited  Mao's  poem  Many  are  the  ways  Of  a  fish.  But  only  one  the  way  Of  a  pork.  The  committee  also  met  to  deter-  mine  the  fate  of  Comrades  Po-ching  Tao  and  Shao-chi,  who  had  been  ac-  cused  of  opportunism  in  selling  a  valu-  able  soft-noodle  recipe  to  the  Kuomin-  tang.  Confronted  with  the  evidence,  Po-ching  admitted  his  error  and  prac-  ticed  self-criticism,  referring  to  him-  self  as  &quot;a  quick-fried  idiot.&quot;  Shao-chi  confessed  to  revisionist  tendencies,     AUGUST  27,1973  such  as  hoarding  mustard  and  &quot;dream-  ing  what  it  would  be  like  to  eat  with  a  fork.&quot;  Self-criticism  was  then  en-  couraged  generally,  with  everyone  first  criticizing  himself  and  then  the  per-  son  on  his  right,  at  Mao's  direction.  In  the  ensuing  spirit  of  optimism,  Po-  ching  Tao  and  Shao-chi  were  forgiven  and  thrown  off  a  mountain.  &quot;Chair-  man  Mao  fashions  history  asa  sculptor  molds  clay,  and  so  death  to  the  two  re-  visionists!&quot;  sang  the  troops,  until  Mao  told  them  to  knock  off  and  get  some  sleep.  Inspired  by  the  day's  events,  the  Chairman  worked  through  the  night,  at  dawn  summoning  me  to  his  tent  for  what  was  to  become  the  Red  Army's  daily  breakfast:  Two  Trarrors  Pork  DumrLinc  (For  100,000  dumplings  ;  i.e.,  approx.  3  per  serving.)  To  25,000  cups  of  shredded  pork  add  a  little  more  porkand  some  clipped  ginger.  Enclose  in  thin  sheets  of  dung  min  (wheat  starch).  Steam  in  a  very  large  wok.  Salt  to  taste.  We  ate,  and  the  Chairman  asked,  &quot;How  can  food  taste  so  loathsome  and  still  be  called  food?&quot;  We  pondered  this,  to  the  sound  of  troops  retching  in  the  tall  grass.  IN  the  bitter  cold  of  February,  1935,  the  U.S.-supported  imperialist  air  force  mounted  a  massive  attack  on  the  Red  Army.  For  six  days,  enemy  planes  raked  our  position,  dropping  over  five  hundred  thousand  tons  of  ham  loaf,  veal  birds,  and  fruit  cobblers  on  the  troops,  in  an  attempt  to  break  their  spirit.  'The  constant  barrage  of  inedibles  made  it  impossible  to  prepare  a  decent  fish  stock,  and  I  had  to  rely  on  commercial  chicken-bouil-  lon  cubes  wrapped  in  foil.  It  was  our  bleakest  period.  The  men,  denied  their  custom-  ary  assorted  appetizers,  grew  weak,  and  morale  became  dangerously  low.  Mao  then  made  the  difficult  decision  to  attack  the  government  instal-  lation  at  Chengtu,  with  its  vast  storehouses  of  shrimp  toast  and  other  crisp  savories.  Chu  Teh  objected,  claiming  it  was  suicide  torisk  our  tat-  tered  force  for  what  was  es-  sentially  a  first  course.  Mao  asked,  &quot;What,  one  shrimp  afraid  of  another?&quot;-a  pointed  reference  to  the  di-  minutive  Chu.  Later,  the  Chairman  and  I  met  to  plan  the  meal  that  would  precede  the  attack.  &quot;It  must  be  warm-  " b"THE  NEW  YORKER  ing,  vitamin-packed,  and  aromatic,  with  a  pleasing  variety  of  textures,&quot;  he  said,  handing  me  an  elab-  orate  menu.  I  pointed  out  that  as  we  had  been  sub-  sisting  for  a  month  ex-  clusively  on  leftovers,  the  matter  of  texture  and  aroma  was  at  best  aca-  demic,  but  Mao  was  al-  ready  deep  in  thought  on  a  new  pamphlet,  &quot;Some  Questions  Concerning  Eight  Precious  Pudding.&quot;  With  the  responsibility  fully  on  my  shoulders,  I  scraped  together  what  I  could  and  prepared  a  dish  that  skillfully  combined  crab  lips  and  chicken  beaks  with  local  fronds  and  roots.  Although  most  of  the  troops  threw  away  the  food  and  ate  the  bowls,  Mao  praised  the  meal,  for  the  next  day  we  reaped  a  brilliant  vic-  tory  at  Chengtu.  In  a  festive  mood,  Mao  retired  to  his  hut  with  a  portion  of  rice  wine  and  emerged  some  time  later  with  the  following  recipe.  &quot;His  first  dessert!&quot;  sang  the  troops,  em-  bracing  each  other. Throughout  this  period,  as  is  well  known,  theSoviet  Union  maintained  a  curiously  noncommittal  posture  to-  ward  Mao  Tse-tung,  especially  with  respect  to  direct  aid.  Chin  Wei  jour-  neyed  to  [REDACTED],  but,  after  exhaustive  secret  negotiations,  learned  only  that  the  butter  in  Chicken  Kiev  may  be  mixed  with  tarragon  or  chopped  shal-  lots,  in  addition  to  the  usual  garlic.  In  exchange,  he  revealed  to  the  Kremlin  the  enigmatic  ingredient  (peanut  but-  ter)  that  gives  Cold  Hacked  Chicken  its  characteristic  smoky  flavor,  the  source  of  which  had  stumped  top  So-  viet  chemists  for  years.  The  Bolshe-  viks  then  voted  to  ship  a  carton  of  sour  cream  to  our  base  in  Hangchow,  thus  revealing  their  abysmal  ignorance  of  the  Chinese  character.  Without  help  from  abroad,  the  Red  Army  was  en-  tirely  sustained  by  Mao  T'se-tung's  me-  ticulous  meal-planning  and  emphasis  on  protein!  Meanwhile,  Chiang  Kai-  shek,  upon  being  told  that  our  strength  25           derived  from  a  low-fat  diet,  putout  an  imbecilic  warrant  forthe  &quot;Red  bandit  Lo  Fat,&quot;  whom  he  believed  to  bea  chef. N  September,  1935,  the  tide  of  the  Revolutionturned  decisively,  when  Chiang  Kai-shek's  own  lackey-chef  at-  tempted  to  perk  up  the  flavor  of  a  tired  beef  dish  (Beef  with  Pond  Wa-  ter)  by  adding  nine  barrels  of  U.S.-sup-  plied  monosodium  glutamate  (MSG),  which  caused  the  entire  5th  Division  of  the  Kuomintang  Army  to  suffer  head-  aches  and  a  queasy  feeling.  In  a  move  brilliantly  timed  to  coincide  with  des-  sert,  the  Red  Army  attacked,  liberating  over  seventy  truckloads  of  almond cookies  and  pistachio  ice  cream,  which  were  returned  to  the  local  peasants  from  whom  they  had  been  stolen.  Lat-  er,  Mao  created  the  following  recipe,  remarking  to  me,  &quot;From  defeat,  the  seeds  of  victory;  contradiction  in  all  things,  and  easy  on  the  pepper.&quot;  Mock  Mock-FisH  Take  a  fish  and  prepare  it  by  the  mei-  tung  method  so  it  resembles  pork.  Treat-  ing  the  result  asreal  pork,  prepare  mock-fish.  The  final  result  is  served  as  a  fish-shaped  course  which  appears  to  be  fish  but  might  also  bepork.  Thus  the  duality  of  foodstuffs  is  demonstrated.  When  the  meal  was  served,  the  troops  rioted,  until  Mao  spoke  to  them  calmly  and  explained  the  dialectical  basis  of  the  recipe.  In  October,  1935,  Mao  Tse-tung  and  the  Revolutionary  Army  captured  the  ancient  walled  city  of  Lanchow.  The  Long  March  was  over!  It  had  covered  eleven  provinces,  crossed  twen-  ty-four  rivers,  and  produced  some  six  hundred  Marxist-Leninist  recipes  &quot;twice-nourishing,&quot;  as  Chairman  Mao  termed  them,  for  their  &quot;instructional  as  well  as  culinary  qualities.&quot;  In  April,  the  Central  Committee  of  the  Com-  munist  Party  of  4368696e61  convened  at  a  mammoth  banquet  to  proclaim  the  Peo-  ple's  Republic  of  4368696e61.  The  audience  wept  and  sang  through  a  thirty-course  dinner,  including  Great  Struggle  Duck,  Classless  Prawns,  and  Ideologically  Correct  Lotus  Balls,  as  Chairman  Mao  recalled  the  sacrifices  of  the  Long  March.
