 b"THREE  years  ago,  when  I  reached  the  mid-century  mark  and  began  to  receive  letters  commencing  &quot;Dear  Senior  Citizen,&quot;  it  dawned  on  me  that  I  had  arrived  at-if  not,  indeed,  jogged  beyond-an  age  at  which  one  is  widely  advised  by  medical  authorities  and  the  manufacturers  of  wood-carving  sets  to  take  up  a  hobby  in  order  to  avoid  boredom  and  an  almost  concomitant  demise.  Since  I  wished  to  cavort  about  for  another  few  decades  in  spirits  as  high  as  those  characterizing  my  first  five,  I  cast  around  for  a  hobby.'To  be  precise,  I  cast  around  fora  hobby.  I  am,  as  it  happens,  a  premature  hobbyist,  having  already  gone  in  for  British  and  British-  colonial  stamps,  first  editions,  insects,  the  relations  of  insects,  and  relations  (my  own),  in  defiance  of  medical  au-  thority.  As  it  turns  out,  I  have  done  all  this  to  no  therapeutic  avail,  since  my  afterhours  activities  with  albums,  water-  mark  detectors,  gummed  hinges,  the  works  of  Max  Beerbohm,  cocoons,  Riker  mounts,  spreading  boards,  cya-  nide  jars,  and  Hypo-Kill  Syrettes,  along  with  the  nocturnal  painting  of  tree  trunks  with  molasses  and  beer,  the  feed-  ing  of  flies  to  spiders,  the  buttonholing  of  third  cousins  at  cocktail  parties,  and  the  study  of  family  trees,  have  in  no  way  kept  me  from  becoming,  and  feeling,  middle-aged,  if  not  downright  elderly.  In  fact,  I  think  my  hobbies  aged  me.  What  is  calculated  to  underline  more  sharply  the  passage  of  time  than  rows  of  Indian  stamps  bearing  portraits  of  Victoria,  Edward  VII,  and  Georges  V  and  VI? Arachnids  are  the  devil  to  preserve.  Book  cata-  logues  are  often  printed  in  fine  type,  hard  on  the  eyes.  And  my  interest  in  my  defunct  relatives  once  almost  got  me  arrested-surely  no  way  to  fend  off  wrinkles.  I  had  embarked  on  a  research  expedition  to  a  family  mausoleum  in  [REDACTED].  Arriving  late  in  the  afternoon  at  the  mortuary  grounds,  I  found  the  gatehouse  locked.  I  climbed  over  a  fence  and,  seeking  my  kinsmen  in  the  gathering  dusk,  was  challenged  by  a  burly  cemetery  custodian,  who  reproved  me  for  trespassing  and  threatened  to  turn  me  in.  A  five-dollar  tip  got  me  off  the  hook  and  won  me  fifteen  minutes  in  the  mausoleum,  but  the  experience  was  not  rejuvenating.  So,  space  being  at  a  premium,  rents  high,  shelves  crowded,  and  cabinets  bulging,  I  decided  that  my  sunset  hobby  should  be  one  that  required  little  room.  I  resolved  to  collect  misspellings.  With  no  equipment  other  than  apocket  note-  book,  a  pair  of  shears,  and  a  pair  of  bifocals,  I  have  acquired  a  sheaf  of  treasures  no  thicker  (if  we  except  one  example,  which  I  have  preserved  in  bulk,  in  its  original  surroundings)  than  Volume  I  (or  II)  of  &quot;Scott's  Standard  Postage  Stamp  Catalogue.&quot;  In  the  event  of  a  coup  d'  \xc3\xa9tat,  it  would  beeasy  for  me  to  slip  it  across  the  border.
