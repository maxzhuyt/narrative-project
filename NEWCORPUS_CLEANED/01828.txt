 SUGAR  Y  mother  had  a  respect  for  M  truth,  but  she  was  not  a  truthful  woman.  She  once  said  to  me,  her  lip  trembling,  and  her  eyes  sharp  to  detect  my  opinion,  &quot;Your  father  says  I  am  a  terrible  liar.  But  I'm  not  a  liar,  am  I?  I'm  not?&quot;  Of  course  she  was  not,  I  agreed,  colluding  as  we  all  always  did  for  the  sake  of  peace  and  of  something  else:  a  half  desire  to  help  her,for  things  to  be  as  she  said  they  were.  But  she  was.  She  lied  in  small  matters,  to  tidy  up  embarrassments,  and  in  larger  matters,  to  avoid  un-  palatable  truths.  She  lied  floridly  and  beautifully,  in  her  rare  moments  of  relaxation,  to  make  a  story  better.  She  was  a  breathless  and  breathtaking  ra-  conteur-not  often,  and  sometimes  overinsistently,  but  at  her  best  reduc-  ing  her  audience  to  tears  of  helpless  laughter.  She  also  told  other  kinds  of  stories,  all  the  time,  in  her  later  days,  whenever  we  were  in  her  company:  monotonous,  malevolent,  unstructured  plaints,  full  of  increasingly  fabricated  evidence  of  wickedness.  But  that  is  another  matter.  I  did  not  set  out  to  write  about  that.  I  set  out  to  write  about  my  grandfather.  About  my  pa-  ternal  grandfather,  whom  I  hardly  knew,  and  about  whom  I  know  very  little.  When  my  father  was  dying,  I  once  came  into  his  hospital  room,  and  he  sat  up  against  his  pillows  and  looked  at  me  out  of  his  own  father's  face.  I  had  never  thought  of  them  as  being  alike.  My  father  was  a  handsome  man,  in  a  very  English  way:  blue-eyed,  fair-  skinned,  with  fine  red-gold  hairthat  very  slowly  lost  its  fire  and  turned  rusty  and  then  white.  He  had  quite  a  lot  of  it  still  left  when  he  died:  very  lively  silvery  hair,  floating.  He  had  a  wide,  straight,  decisive  mouth.  None  of  these  words  recall  him.  His  father,  my  grandfather,  never  had  any  hair  that  I  can  remember,  and  had  heavy  cheeks  and  a  fuller,  more  petulant  mouth.  Seeing  his  facein  my  father's  that  day  in  the  hospital,  it  occurred  to  me  for  the  first  time  to  wonder  if  my  grandfather's  hair  had  been  red.  He  had  had  six  children,  of  whom  my  father  was  the  youngest,  and  all  of  whom  had  that  fiery  hair.  My  grand-  mother's,  I  was  fairly  sure,  was  dark  brown.  I  did  not  tell  my  father  that  I  had  seen  this  resemblance,  partly  be-  cause  it  vanished  when  he  spoke,  partly  because  I  thought  of  it  as  unflat-  tering,  since  as  a  small  child  I  had  seen  my  grandfather  as  someone  off-  putting,  stout,  and  old.  The  old  were  old  in  my  early  childhood,  in  the  war  years:  there  was  an  absolute  barrier.  Though  he  was  seventy-seven  when  he  died,  my  father  never  came  to  seem  old  as  my  grandfather  did.  At  the  time  when  I  saw  my  grand-  father  in  him,  he  must  have  had  about  three  months  left  to  live.  He  was,  by  chance,  in  hospital  in  [REDACTED].  It  was  a  spotless  and  civilized  hospital,  full  of  seriously  gentle  doctors  and  nurses,  all  of  whom  spoke  an  English  more  nearly  perfect  than  might  have  been  found  in  any  hospital  at  home.  My  father  disliked  his  dependence,  and  they  made  it  decorous  for  him.  While  he  was  there,  which  was  sev-  eral  weeks,  we  all,  my  sisters  and  brother  and  I,  visited  him.  The  visit-  ing  hours  were  long-most  of  the  af-  ternoon  and  evening-and  for  most  of  this  time,  on  most  of  these  days,  he  talked  to  us.  All  my  life,  I  had  held  it  against  him  that  he  never  talked  to  us.  A  barrister,  later  a  judge,  he  worked  with  steady  concentration,  long,  long  hours,  and  was  often  away  on  circuit.  During  my  early  childhood,  when  his  parents  were  alive,  he  had  been  away  altogether,  in  the  R.A.F.,  in  the  Med-  iterranean,  in  the  war.  He  was  called  back  from  the  Nuremberg  trials  to  his  father's  deathbed,  or  so  my  mother  had  always  said.  What  he  had  been  doing  there,  if  indeed  he  was  there,  I  have  never  heard  and  cannot  imagine.  In  those  weeks  in  [REDACTED],  he  talked  a  great  deal-about  his  father,  about  his  mother,  about  his  childhood-as  he  had  never  done.  I  don't  know  if  he  realized  how  very  little  he  had  ever  said.  He  was  a  silent  man  but  by  no  means  a  cold  or  distant  man-not  as  you  might  think  of  a  distant  man,     JANUARY  12,  1987  if  you  read  that  word,  immediately.  He  was  a  judge.  When  I  say  that  of  him,  I  do  not  think  of  him  as  sitting  in  judgment.  I  think  of  him  as  a  man  with  an  unwavering  instilled  respect  for  evidence,  for  truth,  for  justice.  When  he  delivered  moral  opinions,  you  could  see  that  he  was  of  his  gener-  ation,  time,  and  place:  a  good  man,  a  Yorkshireman,  ambitious  to  better  himself;  aware,  largely  from  outside,  of  social  discriminations  and  niceties  of  class;  a  late-convinced  Quaker,  a  socialist  turned  social  democrat. (And  how  to  be  sure,  with  all  the  years  of  fading  between  then  and  that  last  cold  day?)  I  sat  up,  scrambled  to  my  feet,  and  leaped  an  enormous  leap,  over  my  bed,  over  the  gap,  over  the  bed  with  my  small  sleeping  sister.  I  don't  remember  the  trajectory  of  this  leap.  I  remember  its  beginning  but  not  its  end,  not  my  safe  arrival.  I  do  remem-  ber-this  is  surely  memory,  and  no  accretion-a  terror  of  happiness.  I  was  afraid  to  feel.  This  event  was  a  storied  event,  already  lived  over  and  over,  in  imagination  and  hope,  in  the  invented  future.  The  real  thing,  the  true  mo-  ment,  is  as  inaccessible  as  any  point  along  that  frantic  leap.
