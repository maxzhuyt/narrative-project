WHAT  HAPPENED  TO  CHARLES  FARM  horse  named  Charles  was  led  to  town  one  day  by  his  owner,  to  be  shod.  He  would  have  beenshod  and  brought  back  home  without  inci-  dent  if  it  hadn't  been  for  Eva,  a  duck,  who  was  always  hanging  about  the  kitchen  door  of  the  farmhouse,  eaves,  dropping,  and  never  got  anything  quite  right.  Her  farm-mates  said  of  her  that     she  had  two  mouths  but  only  one  ear.  On  the  day  that  Charles  was  led  away  to  the  smithy,  Eva  went  quack-  ing  about  the  farm,  excitedly  telling  the  other  animals  that  Charles  had  been  taken  to  town  to  be  shot.  ''They're  executing  an  innocent  horse!&quot;  cried  Eva.  &quot;He's  a  hero!  He's  a  martyr!  He  died  to  make  us  free!&quot;  &quot;He  was  the  greatest  horse  in  the  world,&quot;  sobbed  a  sentimental  hen.  &quot;He  just  seemed  like  old  Charley  to  me,&quot;  said  a  realistic  cow.  &quot;Let's  not  get  into  a  moony  mood.&quot;  &quot;He  was  wonderful!&quot;  cried  a  gulli-  ble  goose.  &quot;What  did  he  ever  do?&quot;  asked  a  goat.  Eva,  who  was  as  inventive  as  she  was  inaccurate,  turned  on  her  lively  imag-  ination.  &quot;It  was  butchers  who  led  him  off  to  be  shot!&quot;  she  shrieked.  &quot;They  would  have  cut  our  throats  while  we  slept  ifit  hadn't  been  for  Charles!&quot;  &quot;I  didn't  see  any  butchers,  and  I  can  see  a  burnt-out  firefly  on  a  moonless  night,&quot;  said  a  barn  owl.  &quot;I  didn't  hear  any  butchers,  and  I  can  hear  a  mouse  walk  across  moss.&quot;  &quot;We  must  build  a  memorial  to  Charles  the  Great,  who  saved  our  lives,&quot;  " b"quacked  Eva.  And  all  the  birds  and  beasts  in  the  barnyard  except  the  wise  owl,  the  skeptical  goat,  and  the  realistic  cow  set  about  building  a  memorial.  Just  then  the  farmer  appeared  in  the  lane,  leading  Charles,  whose  new  shoes  glinted  in  the  sunlight.  It  was  lucky  that  Charles  was  not  alone,  for  the  memorial-builders  might  have  set  upon  him  with  clubs  and  stones  for  replacing  their  hero  with  just  plain  old  Charley. 'The  others  set  upon  her  and  tarred  and  un-  feathered  her,  for  none  is  more  unpop-  ular  than  the  bearer  of  sad  tidings  that  turn  out  to  be  false.
