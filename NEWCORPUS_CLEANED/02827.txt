Our  conversations  and  pastimes  were  of  the  most  crude  and  limited  sort:  beer-drinking  con-  tests  and  bullsessions  where  sexual  and  scatological  jokes  were  exchanged.  The  walls  were  plastered  with  pinups,  and  throughout  the  day  a  loudspeaker  drooled  [REDACTED]  German  equivalent  of  Muzak.  My  job  consisted  in  attaching  steel  plates  to  the  claws  of  a  crane  and  taking  them  off  again.  Two  &quot;An-  schlager&quot;-literally  &quot;on-hitters,&quot;  which  doesn't  make  sense  in  either  lan-  guage-were  assigned  to  each  crane,  and  there  were  six  Anschliger  and  three  crane  operators  in  each  Anschlig-  erbrigade.  There  must  have  been  dozens  of  such  brigades  in  the  shipyard,  and  each  bore  the  name  of  some  hero:  &quot;Anschligerbrigade  Walter  Ulbricht,&quot;  &quot;Anschligerbrigade  Rosa  Luxemburg.&quot;  Ours  was  named  after  Stakhanov,  a  Soviet  coal  miner  who  had  distin-  guished  himself  as  a  maniacal  pace-  maker  during  Stalin's  race  for  in-  creased  productivity  before  the  war.  No  brigade  could  havebeen  less  in-  clined  to  exert  itself  unnecessarily.  We  did  what  had  to  be  done  and  no  more.  Since  the  volume  of  work  varied  from  day  to  day,  no  norms  could  be  imposed  by  the  management,  and  our  foreman,  a  mild,  pipe-smoking  man,  moved  at  a  leisurely  pace.  The  work  itself,  in  the  moderate  dosage  we  allowed,  could  be  enjoyed  as  a  sport;  at  least  I  and  my  partner,  who  was  just  a  few  years  older  than  I,  regarded  it  that  way.  Accuracy,  man-  ual  dexterity,  quick  reflexes,  a  cer-  tain  amount  of  physical  prowess,  and,  above  all,  precise  teamwork  between  the  two  Anschliger  and  the  crane  oper-  ator  (who  was  usually  a  woman)  were  needed.  Sometimes,  especially  under  the  eyes  of  visiting  schoolchildren  or  foreign  labor  delegations,  this  esprit  de  corps  inspired  us  to  efforts  that  even  Stakhanov  might  have  found  excessive,  had  they  not  been  so  brief.  During  the  night  shift,  we  took  turns  sleeping,  and,  day  or  night,  a  good  deal  of  time  was  spent  listening  to  someone  talk,  or  quietly  sipping  a  cup  of  coffee.  I  was  pleased  to  find  thatI  could  be  a  worker  among  workers  &quot;An-  schlager,&quot;  I  discovered  one  day  while  perusing  the  small  print  of  my  working  papers,  were  classified  among  that  vir-  ile-sounding  species  of  worker  called  &quot;Schwerarbeiter&quot;-&quot;hard  laborers.&quot;  I  stomped  the  wooden  planks  of  my  barracks  with  secret  pride.  No  one  here  had  ever  heard  of  Bodo  Uhse.  My  foreign-sounding  first  name,  too,  was  obliterated  by  the  nickname  Langer  (the  Long  One).  Eventually,  though,  they  must  have  guessed  at  my  Brah-  minical  origins.  When  I  arrived  I  had  been  noticeably  unskilled  with  even  the  simplest  tools.  Later  on,  I  innocently  revealed  my  literacy  and  knowledge  by  helping  people  write  letters  and  solve  crossword  puzzles.  And  when,  one  day,  I  made  some  pencil  sketches  of  my  colleagues,  they  regarded  me  with  a  little  more  respect  than  was  com-  fortable.  But  at  no  time  did  any  sense  of  class  distinction  separate  me  from  them.  We  spoke  the  same  language,  lived  in  the  same  world  of  work,  wages,  coffee  breaks,  and  weekends.  Still,  I  was  lonely.  I  missed  my  friends,  and  I  missed  home. It  occurred  to  me  that  I  ought  to  visit  him  in  the  hospital,  but  instead  I  acted  upon  the  general  advice  that  I  stay  away  from  him:  &quot;He'll  be  all  right-Hans  is  with  him.&quot;  Next  morning,  a  call  came  from  Frau  Meissner,  She  urged  Alma  not  to  leave,  and  begged  to  have  a  meeting  with  her.  She  promised  to  have  the  abortion,  and  declared  herself  willing  to  consider  any  other  conditions  Alma  might  make.  She  was  convinced  that  Bodo  would  not  survive  our  leaving.  But  it  was  too  late.  Not  that  our  steamship  tickets  couldn't  be  reclaimed  or  the  luggage  shipped  back  from  Bremen  or  Stefan  returned  from  the  [REDACTED],  but  a  stronger  deterrent  was  needed  to  break  the  momentum  of  Alma's  decision,  sprung  as  it  was  from  the  tension  of  months  of  painful  wran-  gling.  We  left  by  train  from  Bahnhof  Friedrichstrasse.  Some  twenty  people  had  come  to  see  us  off.  One  of  them  was  Helmut;  the  rest  were  either  friends  of  Alma  or  prominent  members  of  the  Intelligenz  who  had  come  to  register  the  Party's  position  on  Bodo's  behavior.  Most  of  them  made  light  of  the  dreary  occasion.  Being  highly  edu-  cated,  they  knew  how  to  blunt  the  edge  of  sorrow  with  a  little  irony,  a  little  philosophical  humor.  They  skipped  and  flapped  their  arms,  ostensibly  to  keep  themselves  warm.  Helmut  stood  a  lit-  tle  apart,  with  his  hands  buried  in  the  pockets  of  a  black  trenchcoat  he  had  recently  stolen.  He  looked  sad.  After  the  whistle  blew  and  the  group  of  wav-  ing  figures  had  vanished  from  sight,  Alma  said  how  sorry  she  was  that  she  hadn't  said  goodbye  to  Helmut.
