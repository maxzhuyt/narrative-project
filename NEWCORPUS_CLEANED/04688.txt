She  swatted  her  brothers'  hands  away  like  flies.  &quot;Get  off!&quot;  she  cried.  And  then  she  read  the  card  herself,  in  peace.  The  train  was  going  quite  fast  now;  they  were  well  out  of  the  station.  &quot;Have  you  ever  seen  sucha  big  box,  Mum-  my?&quot;  she  said,  aware  that  the  present  was  outside  the  range  of  her  experience.  &quot;It  must  have  cost  two  or  three  pounds,&quot;  her  father  said  to  her  mother,  with  a  note  of  reproach.  &quot;Well,  you  know  what  he  is,&quot;  said  the  mother,  half  admiringly,  half  con-  ceding  the  reproach.Smiling,  she  said  to  Denise,  &quot;Little  Willie  must  have  saved  for  months  to  buy  you  that.&quot;  The  child  slid  the  golden  tassel  through  her  fingers.  The  casket  lay  on  her  lap,  a  chorus  girl's  trophy.  She  was  looking  out  of  the  window,  without  at-  tention,  and  her  cheeks  were  sleepily  flushed.  She  thought  of  a  group  of  rag-  ged  children-of  a  bony-headed  boy,  &gt;  looking  on  at  her  pleasures  and  tri-  umphs.  A  dirty  boy  without  shoes.  She  was  ashamed  of  him.  She  would  never  speak  to  him  orlook  at  him,  and  he  knew  this.  But  the  present  in  her  lap  was  not  to  be  resisted.  She  had  been  brought  up  under  the  system  that  re-  warded  good  behavior  and  punished  selfishness,  and  had  been  taught  that  this  was  a  code  impossible  to  circumvent.  Yet  no  one  whom  she  had  loved,  been  kind  to,  or  tried  to  please  had  ever  re-  warded  her  with  something  as  fine  as  this.  It  was  magnificent,  it  was  like  nothing  she  had  ever  been  given  before,  and  she  had  earned  it  by  scorn  and  dis-  dain.  She  held  the  box  recklessly  tight,  and  when  she  peeled  the  gold  paper  off  the  first  chocolate  and  put  it  in  her  mouth,  the  cherry  inside  was  the  fruit  of  knowledge  on  her  tongue.
