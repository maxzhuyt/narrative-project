Here!,&quot;  hop-  ing  that  he  was  right  and  that  a  grouse  wouldn't  call  &quot;Go-back!  Go-back!  Go-  back!&quot;  the  way  they  do.  He  kept  calling.  He  didn't  feel  he  had  enough  energy  to  do  anything  else-just  keep  calling  while  his  tired  and  wet  and  muddy  body  sank  the  coat  lower  in  the  bog.  Then  he  felt  a  quaking  around  him,  and  he  saw  the  figure,  and  the  stick  being  held  out  to  him.  He  caught  it.  He  would  never  let  it  go.  He  pulled  himself  after  it,  and  then  Joe  (it  would  have  to  be  Joe,  the  other  one  would  never  come  back,  he  thought)  was  steadying  him.  &quot;Follow  me,&quot;  Joe  said.  &quot;Where  I  put  a  foot,  you  put  a  foot!&quot;  Tim  followed   him,  and  in  five  minutes  Joe  said,  &quot;It's  all  right  now.  We're  on  good  ground.&quot;  &quot;My  coat  is  back  there,&quot;  Tim  said.  &quot;Better  your  coat  than  you,&quot;  Joe  said.  &quot;Come  up  to  the  house.  Dry  off.&quot;  &quot;No,&quot;  Tim  said.  &quot;He's  an  old  man.  Even  so,  I  would  have  to  hit  him.  I  haven't  ever  hit  an  old  man.&quot;  &quot;Forgive  him,&quot;  Joe  said.  &quot;It's  the  travel.  All  that  travel.  It  upsets  them.&quot;  &quot;That's  right,&quot;  Tim  said.  &quot;Travel  is  dangerous.&quot;  &quot;Forget  about  the  pensions,&quot;  Joe  said.  &quot;Very  sorry,  man,  very  sorry.&quot;  &quot;I'm  the  sorry  one,&quot;  Tim  said.  &quot;'That's  the  terrible  part  of  it.  You'll  get  the  pension.  I'm  not  bad  enough-  see,  Joe?  That's  my  trouble.  Maybe  I  haven't  travelled  enough.&quot;  'You're  a  good  man,&quot;  Joe  said.  Tim  laughed.  It  was  a  still  night.  He  heard  his  laugh  hitting  off  the  hills  and  coming  back  to  him. "
