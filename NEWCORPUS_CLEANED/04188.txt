At  first,  I  couldn't  find  anything  my  size;  this  is  a  big  country,  and  it  isn't  all  that  easy  to  go  out  and  start  doing  things  for  it.  Lucki-  ly,  the  President  himself  soon  came  to  my  rescue.  In  his  Balance  of  Payments  message  to  Congress,  on  February  6th,  he  discussed  the  dollar-gap-and-gold-  shortage  problem,  noting  that  &quot;foreign  travel  to  the  United  [REDACTED]  constitutes  a  large  potential  market  hitherto  virtually  untapped.  ...  Economic  conditions  in  many  foreign  countries  have  improved  to  the  point  where  a  strong  promotion  effort  by  this  country  can  be  expected  to  yield  significant  results.&quot;  Some  time  later,  I  saw  figures  that  made  me  realize  how  serious  the  problem  is.  In  1960,  there  was  a  difference  of  over  a  billion  dollars  between  what  some  million  and  a  half  American  tourists  spent  abroad  and  what  some  six  hundred  thousand  foreign  tourists  spent  here,  and  this  sum  was  the  biggest  single  item  in  our  deficit  in  international  payments.  That  made  me  stop  and  think.  I've  gone  abroad  every  summer  for  several  years  now,  and  I  admit  I've  been  tossing  dollars  around  over  there  at  a  pretty  fast  clip.  Obviously,  my  country  needed  some-  thing  done  for  my  not  going  abroad  (Pan  American,  American  Ex-  press,  and  other  pillars  of  our  economy  wouldn't  like  that  very  well)  but  my  help  in  luring  foreign  tourists  to  it.  I  pitched  right  in,  and  as  soon  as  I  did,  I  found  that  a  number  of  other  peo-  ple  were  already  at  work  on  the  prob-  lem.  A  United  [REDACTED]  Travel  Service  has  been  set  up  inside  the  Department  of  Commerce,  to  promote  foreign  tourism,  and  Congress  has  voted  it  three  million  dollars  for  a  starter. But  the  more  I  looked  into  the  matter,  the  more  I  realized  that  we  hadn't  even  begun  to  do  our  spadework.  W  hat  do  American  tourists  go  abroad  to  see?  The  Louvre.  The  Alps.  &quot;The  Last  Supper.&quot;  St.  Peter's.  The  Spanish  Steps.  Now,  all  these  are  attractions  that  have  been  written  about  over  and  over  for  centuries.  They  are  obligatory  for  tour-  ists.  But  what  are  our  wonders?  What  have  we  in  this  country  that  tourists  mustn't  fail  tosee?  The  Empire  State  Building,  Niagara  Falls,  the  Grand  Canyon  ...  And  then?  Shocked  by  how  few  likely  attractions  I  could  name,  I  decided  that  what  I  would  do  for  my  country  was  draw  up  a  list  of  superla-  tive  Sights  of  one  sort  or  another.  So  I  wrote  to  the  capitals  of  a  majority  of  our  states,  received  in  return  their  publicity  handouts,  and  have  now  prepared-  and  append  herewith-a  handy  list  of  fifty  of  our  largest,  highest,  shortest,  longest,  oldest,  crookedest,  deepest,  etc.,  wonders,  as  proclaimed  in  this  litera-  ture.  They  are  not,  I  admit,  quite  what  I  had  in  mind,  but  they  are  the  best  that  came  to  hand.  At  least  they  will  serve  to  get  the  ball  rolling.
