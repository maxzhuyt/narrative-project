It  happened  with  such  shattering  sudden-  ness  that  afterward  Merrill  couldn't  be  sure  what  he'd  seen  and  what  he'd  imagined.  This  is  what  he  remembered:  He  had  just  passed  through  a  little  village  (no  more  than  a  store  and  an  inn  and  a  cluster  of  houses  at  a  crossroads,  really  )  and  was  rounding  a  curve  on  the  inside  lane,  with  a  scattering  of  trees  on  either  side  that  made  the  light  even  more  deceptive,  when  he  heard  a  thud  and  felt  a  shock  to  the  car  and-from  nowhere,  seemingly-what  looked  like  a  great  grayish  bird  flailing  ghostly  wings  lurched  above  his  right  front  fender  and  resolved  itself  instantly  into  a  human  body,  the  body  of  a  man,  and  one,  too,  that  he  now  remembered  see-  ing,  without  quite  seeing,  as  a  shape  at  the  edge  of  the  road,  just  outside  the  range  of  his  headlights,  as  he'd  swung  into  the  curve.  It  rose,  pitching  awkwardly  forward  and  sidewise;  even  as  Merrill  braked  and  swerved  the  car  in  a  wrenching  frenzy  of  belated  action,  it  still  seemed  to  hang  there,  and  he,  his  perceptions  quickened  by  the  awful  urgency  of  the  crisis,  saw  it  almost  as  if  in  slow  motion:  a  man,  his  coat  flipped  up  over  his  arms,  and  his  arms  flapping,  his  legs  sprawling,  his  face  seen  only  partly  as  his  head  was  jerked  sidewise  by  the  impact-a  man  in  gray  or  light-brown  pants,  the  light  shining  mostly  on  the  stretched  seat  of  them  grotesquely  as  he  spun,  hung,  and,  finally,  fell.  HE  man  was  out  of  sight  and  the  car,  still  moving,  was  just  past  him  when  he  hit  the  ground,  but  Merrill  heard  the  thump  distinctly.  Then  the  car  was  stopped,  and  so,  too,  for  a  while,  was  everything  else-the  trees,  the  countryside  in  the  deepening  darkness,  the  winding,  lonely  road. 'There  were  times  when  Merrill  seemed  to  himself  to  travel  in  a  world  of  sorrow.  It  may  have  been  that  the  very  weight  of  his  emotion  played  a  part  in  the  change  that  came  over  him.  At  any  rate,  sometime  along  the  way,  logic  gave  way  to  illogic  in  his  mind,  sanity  toat  least  partial  insanity.  At  the  start  of  the  process,  even  Merrill  himself  could  feel  it  happening.  HE  was  losing  control,  control  even  of  himself.  It  began,  perhaps,  when  he  sent  the  telegram  to  [REDACTED]. It  was  not  until  much  later,  when  he  was  on  the  road  again,  that  he  realized  how  puzzling  that  might  be  to  both  of  them.  And  in  the  office,  of  course,  he'd  be  remembered  as  the  man  who  had  changed  his  address.  .  ..  Or  his  watch-maybe  that  was  an-  other  symptom.  Earlier  in  the  day,  even  before  he  got  on  the  turnpike,  he  had  looked  at  his  watch  and  it  had  said  ten-  thirty.  And  yet,  later  on,  after  he  was  off  the  turnpike,  miles  off  it,  he'd  looked  at  the  watch  and  it  had  still  said  ten-  thirty.  It  was  stopped.  He  had  forgotten  to  wind  it  the  night  before,  and  it  had  stopped;  he  was  in  a  fever,  almost,  till  he  passed  through  a  town  that  had  a  clock  tower  and  he  got  the  right  time-  twelve-fourteen.  It  was  onlythen  that,  reassured,  he  had  stopped  and  slept  for  a  while;  he  couldn't  have  done  it  other-  wise.  ... 'That  came  gradually,  in  a  piling  up  of  little  landmarks  and  other  indications-a  house  here  that  looked  familiar,  and  then  a  certain  curve  of  the  hills;  a  town;  another  town;  and  then  the  marker,  [REDACTED].  Like  the  wanderer  in  the  Arctic,  like  the  lost  hunter  in  the  fields,  he  had  come  full  circle.  He  was  heading  right  back  where  he  had  started  from.  But  by  that  time  it  didn't  matter.  By  that  time,  in  the  state  he  was  in,  it  even  figured;  and  certainly  the  plan  he  evolved  from  the  circumstance  seemed  to  him  to  be  rationality  itself.  He  would  go  on  to  the  town  and  through,  to  the  curve  and  the  side  road  beyond  it.  He  would  drive  up  to  the  farm,  quite  casual-  ly,  and-  well,  when  he  got  there  he'd  think  of  something.  After  all,  Fate  had  taken  him  there  in  the  first  place,  and  now  that  Fate  seemed  bent  on  taking  him  there  again  he  had  only  to  follow  Fate's  direction.  He  might  laugh  and  say,  &quot;My  God!  Did  Itake  the  same  wrong  turning  again? He  was  going  around  a  curve,  not  worrying  (like  a  man  in  the  eye  of  a  hurricane,  he  was  now  in  an  area  of  calmness,  and  he  was  really  not  worry-  ing  at  all;  it  seemed  to  him  that  at  last  he  was  reaching  a  solution),  when  a  police  car  whipped  out  and  around  and  past  him.  He  had  no  idea  why  it  did  so,  and  he  never  did  know.  Actually,  he'd  been  driving  a  little  carelessly,  first  fast  and  then  slow  and  then  fast  again,  and  they'd  been  following  him  for  awhile,  to  check  on  him.  And  then,  concluding.  that  he  was  probably  all  right,  just  an  erratic  driver,  they'd  decided  to  leave  him.  But  it  startled  him,  just  the  same.
