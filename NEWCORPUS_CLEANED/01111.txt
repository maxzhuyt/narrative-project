The  poster  had  been  up  for  so  long  that  it  was  difficult  to  make  out  the  list  of  events  that  constituted  the  day-  long  show,  but  apparently  it  would  be  acelebration  in  drama,  recitation,  and  Native  American  song  of  the  life  and  times  of  the  great  Lakota  war  chief.  The  father  tasted  bile  in  the  back  of  his  throat  as  soon  as  Davey  started  talk-  ing  about  it.  When  he  told  the  boy  that  they  couldn't  go,  Davey  kept  repeating,  &quot;Dad,  I'd  understand  if  there  was  a  rea-  son.&quot;  The  father  was  infuriated  both  by  the  insufferable  expression  and  because,  although  there  was  a  reason,  it  was  one  of  pure,  punishing  inconvenience,  and  that  was  a  concept  which  no  eight-  year-old  could  be  made  to  understand.  Going  to  the  pageant  would  mean  get-  ting  up  at  six  o'clock  on  their  last  day,  to  strike  the  tent  and  pack  the  camping  gear,  and  driving  an  hour  and  a  half  in  the  wrong  direction,  away  from  home.  Then,  if  they  did,  in  fact,  stay  all  day,  the  three  of  them  would  have  to  spend  another  night  sleeping  in  a  crummy  motel  on  the  way  back.  He  remembered  the  chlorine-scented  motel  room  they'd  stayed  in  on  the  way  to  the  Black  Hills,  where  he'd  sat  rigidly  in  a  chair  damped  by  one  of  the  boys'  swimsuits  trying  to  read  a  book  but  managing  only  to  stare  endlessly  at  the  same  page  while  listening  to  the  clunk  and  hum  of  a  distant  ice  machine.        BY  ETHAN  COEN  He  had  been  thinking  about  the  series  of  irritating  tasks  the  camping  trip  held  in  store:  he  would  have  to  blow  up  air  mattresses,  clear  stones  from  beneath  one  child's  mattress,  pretend  to  clear  stones  from  beneath  the  aggrieved  other  child's  mattress,  build  a  fire,  make  the  fire  bigger,  make  the  fire  smaller,  make  the  fire  a  different  shape,  accompany  each  child  to  the  latrine  in  the  middle  of  the  night,  hyperextend  his  shoulder  groping  beneath  the  car  seat  for  im-  portant  toys  and  fetishes,  swab  soda  pop  and  sick-up  off  the  upholstery,  and  oth-  erwise  cater  to  the  children's  needs,  real  and  imagined,  while  listening  to  Davey's  non-stop  suggestions  and  complaints.  He  had  just  managed  to  work  himself  into  a  vibrating  rage  of  self-recrimination  for  having  agreed  to  take  the  boys  to  the  Black  Hills  when  a  man  in  the  room  next  door  had  a  trumpeting  bowel  movement.  Davey  had  screamed  with  laughter.  Later,  the  three  of  them  had  had  dinner  in  a  café  attached  to  the  motel,  Bart  eating  rice,  Davey  slurping  down  a  mucilaginous  beef  Stroganoff,  and  the  father  picking  at  a  club  sand-  wich.  Later  still,  with  the  children  asleep  next  to  him,  their  arms  outflung  and  mouths  agape,  the  father  had  sat  in  bed  watching  a  fuzzed-over  TV  talk  show  with  the  volume  turned  low.  The  guests-the  usual  show-business  whores  and  crybabies-did  not  interest  him,  but  the  inane  program  did  somehow  turn  his  leaping  fury  into  smaller  waves  of  anger  and  depression  that  rippled  across  his  body  like  water  over  a  smooth  flat  stone.  Bart,  who  had  finally  learned  to  talk  but  chose  to  do  so  only  at  odd  mo-  ments,  was  three  years  younger  than  Davey  and  had  developed  an  obsessive  interest  in  &quot;Sesame  Street.&quot;  He  enjoyed  all  the  books  and  videos,  but  for  some  months  now  his  chiefest  interest  had  been  a  catalogue  of  Sesame  Street  products.  He  preferred  the  catalogue  to  any  of  the  products  themselves  and  would  sit  hunched  over  it  for  hours  at  a  time,  carefully  examining  each  page.  When  he  reached  the  last  page,  he  would  gaze  at  it,  turn  it,  study  the  back  cover  at  length,  then  flip  the  catalogue  over  and  start  again.  He  insisted  that  his  parents  mend  each  small  tear  with  Scotch  tape,  and,  by  virtue  of  this  and  the  many  dog-ears  and  wrinkles  that  had  accumulated  over  the  months,  the  catalogue  was  now  about  twice  its  original  thickness.  When  Bart  learned  that  there  was  a  Sesame  Street  theme  park,  called  Ses-  ame  Place,  in  [REDACTED],  he  had  agi-  tated  to  visit  it  and,  when  he  was  told  that  it  was  too  far  to  visit,  had  had  a  disturbing  tantrum.  He  went  rigid  and  the  cords  in  his  neck  stood  out,  and  first  his  face  and  then  his  entire  body  turned  red.  Temper  tantrums  like  this,  worse  than  any  he  had  ever  had  before,  became  chronic.  They  began  to  over-  whelm  his  body  to  such  an  extent  that  his  parents  worried  that  they  were  seiz-  ures  of  some  sort.  They  took  Bart  to  the  pediatrician  and  triggered  one  by  prying  the  Sesame  Street  catalogue  out  of  his  hands.  The  doctor  observed  the  fit  and  told  them  that  it  was  not  dangerous  but  suggested  that  the  child  was  perhaps  immature.  Everyone  except  Bart  had  felt  foolish.  But  the  lobbying  to  visit  Sesame  Place  had  consisted  of  more  than  tantrums.  When  Bart  did  speak,  it  was  of  nothing  else.  Family  life  became  a  hellishly  end-  less  argument  about  Sesame  Place.  Bart  was  a  rock-small,  stolid,  immovable,  diverting  the  stream  of  every  conver-  sation  toward  one  Nirvana-like  end.
