 b"SEYMOUR JOHNSTONE  had  a  small  antique  shop,  which  might  have  sustained  him  in  modest  comfort,  but  he  spent  more  time  in  dreaming  of  the  future  than  in  studying  the  past.  In  consequence,  his  finances  were  as  rickety  as  his  chairs.  He  was  reduced  to  taking  his  lunch  in  cafeterias,  where  he  had  the  mortification  of  encountering  more  genuine  antiques  among  the  sand-  wiches  and  pastries  than  ever  he  man-  aged  to  pick  up  on  his  weekly  trips  around  the  country.  Seymour's  preoccupation  with  the  days  ahead  was  not  due  to  any  starry-  eyed  dream  of  social  progress  or  space  travel;  it  was  simple  and  practical  to  a  degree.  &quot;On  one  of  those  days,&quot;  said  he  to  himself  at  frequent  intervals,  &quot;my  dear  Aunt  Muriel  must  inevitably  die.&quot;  The  only  question  was:  Which  one?  Seymour  was  on  the  whole  inclined  to  give  her  five  years.  He  had  Biblical  authority  for  this  estimate,  for  his  aunt  was  in  the  midmost  of  her  sixties,  but  there  was  also  a  certain  amount  of  wish-  ful  thinking  behind  it.  Seymour  was  a  little  inclined  to  close  his  eyes  to  things  that  were  unpleasant  to  him,  and  he  managed  to  ignore  the  dire  implications  of  her  slimness,  her  activity,  and  her  per-  fect  set  of  teeth.  Her  mental  faculties  were  equally  un-  impaired;  she  had  no  great  illusions  con-  cerning  her  nephew.  He  was,  however,  the  only  relation  she  had  in  the  world.  &quot;I  shall  leave  you  my  money,&quot;  she  told  him,  &quot;for  after  all  blood  is  thicker  than  water.  It  may  be  true  that  I  have  more  than  I  need,  but  that  is  how  Ilike  it.  Seymour,  you  will  never  get  anywhere  pestering  me  for  a  loan.&quot;  She  added  some  banalities  concerning  work  and  character,  such  as  can  be  highly  offen-  sive  to  a  young  man  of  artistic  leanings,  and  Seymour  returned  to  New  York,  and  to  his  cafeteria,  more  preoccupied  than  ever  with  the  number  threescore  and  ten.  &quot;Five  years  is  a  long  time  to  wait,&quot;  thought  he  as  he  placed  his  tray  on  his  favorite  table.  &quot;Fortunately,  I  have  the  sort  of  face  that  is  likely  to  wear  ex-  tremely  well.&quot;  The  table  was  his  favorite  because  there  happened  to  be  a  large  mirror  rather  to  the  right  of  it,  in  which  he  could  examine  the  face  in  question  from  the  right-three-quarter  aspect,  which  was  also  his  favorite.  In  fact,  Seymour  loved  this  side  of  his  face  so  well  that  he  riveted  his  eyes  on  it  even  while  shaving  the  other,  a  feat  usually  achieved  only  by  film  stars.  He  eyed  it  now,  and  was  by     no  means  displeased.  One-third  poet,  he  -  decided,  and  two-thirds  diplomat-the  poet,  of  course,  being  of  the  proper  sort.  Not  a  bad  combination!  With  my  face  and  her  dollars  I  could  do  quite  a  lot.  With  that,  he  turned  his  attention  to  his  lunch,  which  was  already  growing  cold.  Someone  had  left  a  small  magazine  wedged  between  the  sugar  jar  and  the  box  of  paper  napkins.  Seymour  took  it  up  and  turned  its  pages  idly  as  he  ate.  It  was  one  of  those  cheerful  little  pub-  lications  made  up  of  articles  such  as  &quot;I  Learned  to  Live  with  My  Elephantia-  sis&quot;  and  &quot;Sex  After  Seventy?&quot;  Seymour  winced  away  from  the  rather  technical  reminiscences  ofa  lady  who  had  come  by  a  new  and  attractive  face,  made  en-  tirely  out  of  spare  parts  of  her  own  anat-  omy.  Operations  of  this  description  were  singularly  distasteful  to  Seymour.  He  was  so  extremely  fond  of  his  own  face  and  his  own  skin  that  a  mere  glimpse  of  terms  like  grafting  and  scar  tissue  caused  his  mind  to  contract  into  a  hard,  un-  thinking  knot,  which  only  gradually  re-  laxed,  permitting  him  to  steal  a  reassur-  ing  glance  or  two  at  the  mirror.  These  soon  restored  his  equanimity,  and,  for-  getting  whatever  it  was  he  had  not  thought  about,  which  after  all  is  not  very  difficult,  he  turned  again  to  the  magazine.  Fortunately,  the  nextcon-  tribution  was  simply  and  straightfor-  wardly  entitled  &quot;How  Long  Will  You  Live?&quot;  This  being  very  much  his  subject,  he  began  to  read  with  attention.  It  was  that  most  en-  couraging  article  which,  under  various  titles,  brings  us  every  month  or  so  the  glad  news  that,  given  the  head  start  of  life  expectancy,  we  are  no  more  likely  to  be  overtaken  by  death  than  was  the  tortoise  by  the  fleet  Achilles.  In  this  particular  handicap,  old  ladies  are  the  hottest  favorites  of  all,  and  Seymour's  cheek  paled  as  he  read  the  form  sheet.  It  demonstrated  that  his  aunt's  expected  demise,  which  he  had  been  absolutely  relying  upon,  was  noth-  ing  but  a  mirage,  endlessly  receding  before  both  her  and  him.  A*  birth,  he  discovered,  she  had  had  a  prospect  of  living  to  be  fifty-seven  and  a  fraction,  a  figure  to  which  he  had  no  objection  at  all.  At  fifty-seven,  she  might  have  ex-  pected  to  attain  seventy,  a  delay  that  he  had  disciplined  his  spirit  to  endure-  provided,  of  course,  that  there  was  no  more  hanky-panky. His  trembling  hands  let  the  maga-  zine  fall  into  his  neglected  Monday  Special.  He  looked  instinctively  for  the  right-three-quarter  view  of  his  face  in  the  mirror,  a  sight  that  had  rarely  failed  to  restore  his  spirits.  On  this  occasion,  it  looked  back  so  wan,  so  piteous,  and  so  drawn  that  he  averted  his  eyes  and  stared  into  space  instead.  Or,  rather,  he  stared  into  time,  gazing  hopelessly  down  the  endless  race  track  of  the  years,  along  which  his  immortal  aunt  sprinted  forever,  her  lead  over  her  grim  pursuer  diminishing  all  the  time,  yet  never  being  completely  wiped  out.  No  one  can  please  everybody;  the  writer  of  the  article  doubtless  enjoyed  the  widest  popularity  among  aunts,  but  he  had  succeeded  only  in  utterly  depressing  and  disgusting  this  unfortunate  nephew.  What's  more,  that  wicked  little  mag-  azine  had  not  yet  finished  with  Seymour. WITH  that,  Seymour  rose  and  left  the  cafeteria,  to  devote  himself  with  vastly  more  zeal  than  formerly  to  the  selling  of  his  cracked  bedroom  china  and  his  rickety  chairs.  In  a  surprisingly  short  time,  he  had  amassed  some  fifteen  hundred  dollars,  a  sum  he  judged  suf-  ficient  for  the  launching  of  his  enter-  prise.  A  sallow,  thickset,  dirty-handed  individual  appeared  in  the  village  of  Bowview,  New  Jersey,  where  he  rented  an  outlying  shack.  He  purchased  a  five-  year-old  Ford  from  a  used-car  dealer  in  the  neighboring  town  of  Plainfield,  and  this  car  was  registered  under  the  name  that  appeared  on  his  driver's  li-  cense,  with  the  addition  of  his  new  ad-  dress.
