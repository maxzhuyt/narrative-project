It  was  a  doubtful  enterprise,  a  voyage  in  an  untested  craft,  across  a  hos-  tile  ocean,  with  a  map  that  was  a  forgery  and  with  no  particular  destination  but  the  grave.  &quot;I  had  lunch  with  your  father  the  other  day,&quot;  he  began.  &quot;Shh!&quot;  Christy  said.  He  lay  beside  her,  listening.  From  downstairs  they  couldhear  the  sound  of  raised  voices.  A  man  and  a  woman  were  shouting  at  each  other.  The  man  was  Mr.  Hogue.  &quot;I'm  going  to  call  the  police,  Bob,&quot;  the  woman  said.  Daniel  and  Christy  looked  at  each  other.  They  stood  up  and  scrambled  to  reassemble  their  clothes.  Daniel  slipped  the  vial  of  lubricant  into  his  pocket.  Then  they  went  downstairs.  When  they  came  into  the  kitchen,  Mr.  Hogue  was  lying  on  the  floor,  amid  hundreds  and  hundreds  of  spilt  three-  penny  nails,  cupping  his  chin  in  his  hands.  Blood  leaked  out  between  his  fin-  gers  and  drizzled  down  his  neck  into  the  plaid  of  his  madras  jacket.  The  reclining  brass  pasha,  the  Ping-Pong  paddle,  Space  Needle,  and  all  the  other  things  he  had  stolen  lay  scattered  on  the  floor  around  him.  A  handsome  woman  with  red  spectacles,  whom  Daniel  recognized  as  Mrs.  Hogue,  was  kneeling  beside  him,  tears  on  her  face,  wiping  at  the  cut  on  his  chin  with  a  paper  towel.  &quot;Christy,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Hello,  Daniel.&quot;  She  smiled  ruefully  and  looked  down  at  Mr.  Hogue,  moaning  and  whispering  curses  on  the  terra-cotta  floor.  &quot;Is  he  O.K.?&quot;  Daniel  said,  pointing  to  the  realtor,  who  was,  he  saw  now,  no  stranger  to  these  troubled  rooms.  &quot;I  certainly  hope  not.
