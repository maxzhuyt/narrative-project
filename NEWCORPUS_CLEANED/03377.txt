During  those  cold,  soggy  days,  duck-  ing  from  bar  to  bar,  one  met  the  usual  town  eccentrics.  There  was  Ricardo's  brother,  who  always  carried  a  pebble  in  his  mouth  because  it  prolonged  the  taste  of  the  brandy.  And  Gregorio,  who  had  trained  a  sparrow  to  sip  other  men's  drinks  and  then  carry  them  back  to  him  by  the  beakful.  When  the  bird  died,  said  Gregorio,  he  would  weep,  weep,  weep.  Every  man  in  the  bar  agreed.  There  was  also  Pau,  a  young  fisherman  who  was  teaching  himself  to  write  by  using  the  tavern  wall  as  a  copybook,  but  sometimes  exploded  with  frustration  and  beat  the  wall  with  his  fist  till  his  knuckles  ran  with  blood.  Occasionally  a  day  turned  unhealthy,  when  idleness  and  ennui  led  to  an  out-  burst  of  mirthless  riot.  Then  the  vil-  lage  idiot  would  be  seized  and  strapped  to  a  chair  and  tormented  until  he  screamed.  Wine  would  be  poured  on  his  head,  or  a  man  would  hold  him  by  the  ears  while  another  spread  his  face  with  mustard.  After  a  session  of  this,  everyone  looked  flushed  and  relaxed.  Even  the  Civil  Guard  would  come  in  to  watch.  Ricardo  the  waiter  protected  me  when  I  was  drunk  and  humored  me  like  a  grandfather.  One  night,  I  re-  member,  during  a  particularly  flashy  thunderstorm,  the  sky  spitting  with  electric  sparks,  he  telephoned  the  light-  house  keeper  and  asked  him  to  stop  fooling  about,  saying  the  Englishman  didn't  like  it.  Ricardo  was  about  thirty  years  old,  handsome  as  a  playboy,  but  moody  and  idealistic.  He  was  the  leader  of  a  group  of  fishermen  and  laborers  into  which  I  was  gradually  admitted.  We  met  in  a  pink-washed  room  at  the  back  of  the  bar  and  talked  about  the  world  to  come-a  world  without  church  or  government  or  army,  where  each  man  alone  would  be  his  private  government.
