 b"SISTER  IMELDA did not take classes on  her  first  day  back  in  the  con-  vent,  but  we  spied  her  in  the  grounds  after  the  evening  Rosary.  All  we  could  be  certain  of  was  that  she  was  tall  and  limber,  and  that  she  prayed  while  she  walked.  No  looking  at  nature  for  her,  no  curiosity  about  the  seventy  boarders  in  gabardine  coats  and  blackshoes  and  stockings.  We  had  returned  from  our  long  summer  holiday  and  we  were  wretch,  ed.  The  convent,  with  its  high  stone  wall  and  green  iron  gates,  was  more  of  a  prison  than  ever  after  our  spell  in  the  outside  world.  My  friend  Baba  and  I  were  dreaming  of  our  final  escape,  which  would  be  in  a  year.  And  so  on  that  damp  autumn  evening  when  I  saw  the  chrysanthemums  and  saw  the  new  nun  intent  on  prayer  I  pitied  her    and  thought  how  alone  she  must  be,  cut  off  from  all  friends  and  conversa-  tion,  with  only  God  as  her  spouse.  The  next  day  she  came  into  our  classroom  to  teach  geometry.  Her  pale  long  face  I  saw  as  formidable,  but  her  eyes  were  blue-black  and  full  of  verve.  Her  lips  were  very  purple,  as  if  she  had  put  puce  pencil  on  them.  They  were  the  lips  ofa  woman  who  might  sing  in  a  cabaret,  and  she  had  formed  the  habit  of  turning  them  inward  as  if  she,  too,  was  aware  of  their  provocative-  ness.  She  had  spent  the  last  four  years  -the  same  span  that  Baba  and  I  had  spent  in  the  convent-at  the  university  in  [REDACTED],  where  she  studied  lan-  guages.  We  couldn't  understand  how  she  had  resisted  the  temptations  of  the  hectic  world  and  willingly  come  back  to  this.  She  was  different  from  the other  nuns:  there  was  more  bounce  in  her  walk,  more  excitement  in  the  way  she  tackled  teaching.  She  began  the  day's  class  by  reading  from  Cardinal  Newman,  who  was  a  favorite  of  hers.  She  read  howGod  dwelt  in  light  unapproachable,  and  how  with  him  there  was  neither  change  nor  altera-  tion.  Yet  it  was  amazing  how  her  looks  changed.  When  her  eyes  were  flashing,  she  lookedalmost  profane.  She  might  have  been  a  girl  going  to  a  dance  except  for  her  habit.  &quot;Hasn't  she  wonderful  eyes?&quot;  I  said  to  Baba.  That  particular  day,  they  were  like  blackberries,  large  and  soft  and  shiny.  &quot;Something  wrong  in  her  upstairs  department,&quot;  Baba  said,  and  added  that  with  makeup  Imelda  would  be  a  cinch.  &quot;Still,  she  has  a  vocation!&quot;  I  said,  and  I  aired  the  idiotic  view  that  I  might  have  one.  At  certain  moments  it  did  seem  en-  ticing  to  become  a  nun,  to  lead  a  life  unspotted  by  sin,  never  to  have  to  have  babies,  to  wear  a  ring  that  singled  one  out  as  the  bride  of  Christ.  But  there  was  the  other  side  to  it:  the  silence,  the  gravity,  having  to  genuflect  and  pray,  having  to  get  up  two  or  three  times  a  night,  and,  above  all,  having  no  op-  portunity  of  leaving  the  confines  of  the  convent  except  for  a  funeral.  For  boarders  it  was  torture,  but  for  the  nuns  it  was  nothing  short  of  doom.  We  could  complain  to  each  other,  and  we  did-food  being  the  source  of  the  greatest  grumbles.  Lunch  was  bacon-  and-cabbage  or  a  stringy  meat  fol-  lowed  by  tapioca  pudding;  tea  con-  sisted  of  bread  dolloped  with  lard  or  occasionally,  as  a  treat,  green  rhubarb  jam  that  did  not  have  enough  sug-  ar.  Through  the  convent's  long,  cur-  tainless  windows  we  saw  the  conifer  trees  and  a  sky  that  was  scarcely  ever  without  the  promise  of  rain.  Imelda  was  a  right  lunatic,  Baba  said,  having  gone  to  university  for  four  years  and  willingly  come  back  to  poverty,  chastity,  and  obedience.  We  concocted  a  scene  of  agony  in  some  [REDACTED]  hostel,  while  a  young  man  stood  beneath  her  bedroom  win-  dow  throwing  up  chunks  of  clay  as  a  supplication.  In  our  version  he  was  slightly  older  than  Imelda,  and  possi-  blya  medical  student.  His  advances  were  inflamed  and  rose  intermittently  to  overwhelm  her,  and  would  rack  her  with  memories  that  she  would  never  conquer.  It  was  rumored  that  she  pos-  sessed  a  fierce  temper-that  while  a  " b"postulant  she  had  hit  a  girl  so  badly  with  a  leather  strap  that  the  girl  had  to  be  put  to  bed.  Her  brother  Ambrose,  it  was  said,  had  been  sued  by  a  nurse  for  breach  of  promise.  The  first  morning  Sister  Imelda  came  into  our  classroom,  she  modestly  introduced  herself.  She  talked  in  a  low  voice,  as  if  she  did  not  want  her  words  to  go  beyond  the  bounds  of  the  walls,  and  constantly  she  stressed  the  value  of  work  both  to  enlarge  the  mind  and  to  discipline  thought.  One  of  her  eyelids  was  red  and  swollen,  as  if  she  was  getting  a  sty.  I  guessed  that  she  overmortified  herself  by  not  eating.  I  saw  in  her  some  premonition  of  terri-  ble  sacrifice  that  I  would  have  to  emu-  late.  But  when  she  absently  held  the  stick  of  chalk  between  her  first  and  second  finger,  the  very  same  as  ifit  were  a  cigarette,  Baba  whispered  to  me  that  in  [REDACTED]  she  must  have  been  a  chain  smoker.  Sister  Imelda  looked  sharply  at  me  and  asked  what  was  the  secret  and  would  I  like  to  share  it  since  it  seemed  so  comical.  I  said,  &quot;Nothing,  Sister,  nothing,&quot;  and  I  prayed  she  would  never  have  occasion  to  punish  me. NOVEMBER  came,  and  the  tiled  walls  of  the  recreation  hall  oozed  moisture  and  gloom.  Girls  who  had  sore  throats  were  told  to  mortify  themselves  in  order  to  lend  a  glorious  hand  in  that  communion  of  spirit  that  linked  the  living  with  the  dead.  It  was  the  month  of  the  Suffering  Souls  in  Purgatory.  Some  girls  gave  up  sweets,  and  some  gave  up  talking  and  at  recre-  ation  period  were  like  dummies,  mak-  ing  signs  with  thumb  and  finger  to  merely  say  &quot;How  are  you?&quot;&quot;  Baba  said  that  saner  people  were  locked  in  the  lunatic  asylum  a  mile  away.  Yet  in  the  thick  of  all  this  sacrifice  I  found  myself  becoming  dreadfully  happy.  I  had  met  Sister  Imelda  outside  of  class  a  few  times.  Once,  it  was  on  the  lawn,  and  she  did  a  reckless  thing.  She  broke  off  a  chrysanthemum  and  offered  it  to  me  to  smell.  The  chrysan-  themum  had  no  smell,  or  at  the  most  only  something  faint  that  suggested  autumn.  Another  time  we  met  on  the  chapel  porch,  and  as  she  drew  her  shawl  more  tightly  around  her  body  I  felt  how  human  she  was,  prey  to  the  cold,  In  the  classroom  things  were  not  so  congenial  between  us.  Geometry  was  my  worst  subject.  One  day  she  called  me  up  to  the  blackboard  to  draw  a  triangle  and  work  out  a  theorem.  I  could  scarcely  hold  the  chalk,  so  awkward  did  I  feel,  and,  asfor  working  out  the  theorem,  that  was  an  impossi-  bility.  &quot;Think,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Think.&quot;  I  hung  my  head  at  her  words,  and  all  of  a  sudden,  in  her  rage,  she  picked  up  the  blackboard  eraser  and  hurled  it  at  me,  showering  me  with  chalk  dust.  Girls  gasped  at  the  savagery  of  her  temper.  I  felt  a  fool,  but  I  also  felt  in  imminent  danger  of  sneezing  as  I  inhaled  the  chalk.  Suddenly  she  fled  the  room,  leaving  us  ten  minutes  free  until  the  next  class.  One  girl  said  it  was  a  dis-  grace,  said  I  should  write  home  and  say  I  had  been  assaulted.  &quot;We  could  get  her  defrocked,&quot;  Baba  said,  and  urged  me  to  in  God's  name  sit  down.  But  all  I  wanted  was  to  run  after  Sister  Imelda  and  to  say  that  I  was  sorry,  and  I  think  I  must  have  known  that  her  anger  had  as  much  to  do  with  liking  as  it  did  with  dislike.  That  evening,  at  Benediction,  the  choir  nuns  were  in  full  soaring  form  and  the  rows  of  candles  were  like  so  many  little  ladders  to  the  golden  chal-  ice,  which  glittered  all  the  more  be-  cause  of  the  beams  of  fitful  flames.  A  new  holy  picture  had  been  put  in  my  prayer  book,  and  before  I  dared  look  at  the  back  to  see  who  had  given  it  to  me  I  felt  and  guessed  that  this  was  no  ordinary  picture  from  an  ordinary  girl  friend,  that  this  was  a  talisman  and  a  peace  offering  from  Sister  Imelda.  It  was  a  pale-blue  picture,  so  pale  that  it  was  almost  gray,  like  the  down  of  a  pigeon,  and  it  showed  a  Mother  be-  holding  an  Infant  Child.  On  the  back,  in  her  beautiful  ornate  handwriting,  she  had  written  a  verse:  Trust  Him  when  dark  doubts  assail  thee,  Trust  Him  when  thy  faith  is  small.  Trust  Him  when  to  simply  trust  Him  Seems  the  hardest  thing  of  all.  This  was  her  atonement.  She  had  lo-  cated  the  compartment  in  the  chapel  where  I  kept  my  prayer  book-and  to  think  that  she  had  gone  so  far  as  to  write  in  it  and  give  me  a  chance  to  " b"boast  about  it  and  to  show  it  to  other  girls!  When  I  thanked  her  next  day,  she  bowed  but  she  did  not  speak.  The  nuns  wereon  silence  and  only  permit-  ted  to  talk  during  class.  In  no  time  I  had  received  another  present-a  miniature  prayer  book  with  a  leather  cover  and  gold  edging.  The  prayers  were  in  French  and  the  let-  ters  so  minute  it  was  as  if  a  tiny  insect  had  fashioned  them.  Soon  I  was  pub-  licly  known  as  Sister  Imelda's  pet.  I  opened  doors  for  her,  raised  the  black-  board  two  pegs  higher  (she  was  taller  than  other  nuns),  and  handed  out  the  exercise  books  that  she  had  correct-  ed,.  In  the  margins  of  my  geometry  proofs  I  would  find  &quot;Good,&quot;  or  &quot;Excel-  lent,&quot;  when  in  the  past  she  used  to  splash  &quot;Disgraceful.&quot;  Baba  said  it  was  treacherous  to  be  a  nun's  pet  and  that  any  girl  who  sucked  up  to  a  nun  could  not  be  trusted.  N  December,  Sister  Imelda  asked  me  to  carry  her  books  up  four  flights  of  stairs  to  the  school'skitchen.  She  taught  cookery  to  a  junior  class.  As  she  walked  ahead  of  me,  I  thought  how  supple  she  was,  and  when  she  paused  on  the  landing  to  look  out  through  the  long  curtainless  windows  I,  too,  paused.  Down  below,  in  the  street,  two  women  in  suede  boots  were  chatting  and  smoking.  Nearby  a  lay  nun  was  on  her  knees  scrubbing  the  granite  steps,  and  the  cold  air  was  full  of  the  smell  of  raw  Jeyes  Fluid.  There  was  a  potted  plant  on  the  landing,  and  Sister  Imelda  put  her  fingers  in  the  earth  and  shook  her  head,  saying  it  needed  water.  I  said  I  would  water  it  later.  I  was  happy  in  my  prison  then,  happy  to  be  near  her,  happy  to  walk  behind  her  as  she  twirled  her  beads  and  bowed  to  the  lay  nun.  I  no  longer  counted  the  days,  on  a  pocket  calendar,  until  the  Christmas  holidays.  &quot;Come  back  at  five,&quot;  Sister  Imelda  said  as  she  stood  on  the  threshold  of  the  kitchen.  Girls  in  white  coveralls  stood  around  the  long  wooden  table  waiting  for  her.  It  was  as  if  every  girl  was  in  love  with  her.  As  she  entered,  their  faces  broke  into  smiles.  She  must  have  liked  cookery  class;  she  called  to  someone-anyone-to  get  up  a  blaz-  ing  fire.  Then  she  went  across  to  the  wrought-iron  stove  and  spat  on  it  to  test  its  temperature.  It  was  hot,  and  her  spit  rose  up  and  sizzled.  When  I  returned  later,  she  was  sit-  ting  on  the  edge  of  the  table,  swinging  her  legs.  There  was  something  reckless  about  her  pose,  something  defiant.  It  seemed  as  if  any  minute  she  would  take  out  a  cigarette  case,  snap  it  open,  and  then  offer  me  a  cigarette.  The  wonderful  smell  of  baking  made  me  realize  how  hungry  I  was,  but  it  also  brought  memories  of  my  own  home,  my  mother  testing  orange  cakes  with  a  knitting  needle  and  letting  me  lick  the  half-baked  dough  down  the  length  of  the  needle.  &quot;I  bet  you  have  a  sweet  tooth,&quot;  Sister  Imelda  said,  and  she  jumped  down,  crossed  to  a  counter,  and  from                       under  a  wonderful  shining  silver  cloche  produced  two  jam  tarts  with  a  crisscross  design,  where  the  pastry  lat-  ticed  the  dark  jam.  The  tarts  were  still  warm.  &quot;What  will  I  do  with  them?&quot;  I  asked.  &quot;Eat  them,  you  goose,&quot;  she  said,  and  she  watched  as  if  she  herself  de-  rived  pleasure  from  my  eating.  I  was  embarrassed  by  the  pastry's  crumbling  and  the  bits  of  blackberry  jam  staining  my  lips.  Inherent  in  the  pleasure  was  the  terrible  sense  of  dan-  ger.  She  had  a  white  coverall  over  her  black  habit,  and  this  made  her  warmer,  freer,  and  caused  me  to  think  of  the  happiness  that  would  be  ours  if  she  and  I  were  away  from  the  con-  vent,  in  an  ordinary  kitchen.  But  we  weren't.  It  was  clear  to  me  then  that  my  pleasure  was  inextricable  from  pain  and  that  they  existed  side  by  side.  &quot;Had  you  a  friend  when  you  were  in  [REDACTED]  at  university?&quot;  I  asked  dar-  ingly.  &quot;I  shared  a  desk  with  a  sister  from  Howth  and  stayed  in  the  same  hostel,&quot;  she  said.  " b"     But  what  about  boys,  I  thought.  And  what  of  your  life  now,  and  do  you  long  to  go  out  into  the  world?  But  I  could  not  say  it.  I  knew  something  of  the  nuns'  routine:  the  itchy  wool  un-  derwear,  the  dry  bread  for  breakfast,  the  hours  of  strict  silence,  the  constant  vigil  on  their  thoughts.  They  slept  on  hard  beds,  with  no  sheets  and  with  horsehair  blankets.  At  four  o'clock  in  the  morning,  while  we  slept,  each  nun  got  out  of  bed  in  her  habit-which  would  oneday  be  her  death  habit-  and,  chanting,  they  flocked  down  the  wooden  stairs  to  fling  themselves  on  the  tiled  floor  of  the  cold,  unlit  chapel.  Each  nun-even  the  Mother  Superior  -lay  in  total  submission,  saying  her  prayers  in  Latin,  offering  up  her  thoughts  to  God.  Then  silently  she  returned  to  her  cell  for  one  more  hour  of  rest.  It  was  not  difficult  to  imagine  Sister  Imelda  face  downward,  arms  outstretched,  prostrate  on  the  tiled  floor.  I  had  heard  the  chanting  when  I  wakened  suddenly  from  a  nightmare,  because,  although  we  slept  in  a  differ-  ent  building,  the  dormitories  adjoined.  I  heard  that  monotonous  Latin  chant-  ing  long  before  the  birds  began,  long  before  our  own  bell  summoned  us  to  rise  at  six.  &quot;Do  you  eat  nice  food?&quot;  I  asked.  &quot;Of  course,&quot;  she  said,  and  smiled.  She  sometimes  broke  into  a  merry  smile,  which  she  tried  to  conceal.  &quot;Have  you  ever  thought  of  what  you  will  be?&quot;&quot;  she  asked.  I  shook  my  head.  My  hopes  changed  from  day  to  day.  She  looked  at  her  man's  silver  pock-  et  watch  and  closed  the  damper  of  the  range.  She  made  sure  that  all  the  wall  presses  were  locked  by  running  her  hand  over  them.  &quot;Sister,&quot;  I  called,  gathering  cour-  age  at  last.  &quot;What  color  hair  have  you?&quot;  &quot;'You  shouldn't  ask  such  a  thing,&quot;  she  said,  pink  in  the  face,  and  then  she  whispered,  &quot;I'll  tell  you  on  your  last  day  here,  provided  your  geometry  has  improved.&quot;  With  that  she  left  the  room.  She  had  scarcely  gone  when  Baba,  who  must  have  been  lurking  behind  some  pillar,  stuck  her  head  in  the  door  and  said,  &quot;Christ  sake,  save  me  a  bit.&quot;  She  finished  the  second  pastry,  then  went  around,  looking  in  the  drawers.  She  found  some  sugar  in  a  china  shaker.  She  ate  a  little  and  threw  the  remainder  into  the  dying  fire,  so  that  it  " b"flared  up  for  a  minute  with  a  yellow  spluttering  flame.  I  DID  not  speak  to  Sister  Imelda  again  in  private  until  the  evening  of  our  Christmas  theatricals.  She  came  to  help  us  put  on  makeup  and  get  into  our  costumes  and  fancy  headgear.  The  costumes  were  kept  in  a  trunk  from  one  year  to  the  next,  and,  though  sumptuous  and  strewn  with  braiding  and  gold,  they  smelled  of  camphor.  Yet  as  we  donned  them  we  felt  dif-  ferent,  and  as  we  sponged  pancake  makeup  onto  our  faces  we  became  saucy,  adding  dark  pencil  to  the  eyes,  and  bright  orange  lipstick.  There  was  only  one  tube  of  lipstick,  and  each  girl  clamored  for  it.  The  evening's  enter-  tainment  was  to  consist  of  scenes  from  Shakespeare  and  humorous  skits.  I  had  been  chosen  to  recite  Mark  Antony's  lament  over  Caesar's  body,  and  for  this  I  was  to  wear  a  purple  toga,  white  knee  socks,  and  patent-leather  shoes  with  buckles.  The  shoes  were  too  big,  and  I  moved  in  them  as  if  in  clogs.  Sister  Imelda  said  to  take  them  off,  to  go  barefoot.  In  my  efforts  to  memorize  my  speech  the  words  were  getting  all  askew,  flying  about  in  my  head  like  the  separate  pieces  of  a  jigsaw  puzzle.  Sis-  ter  Imelda  must  have  sensed  my  panic.  Very  slowly  she  put  her  hand  to  my  cheek  and  enjoined  me  to  look  at  her.  Her  eyes  seemed  fathomless  as  she  willed  me  to  be  calm.  The  lights  were  being  lowered  out  in  the  recreation  hall;  all  the  nuns  had  arrived,  had  settled  themselves,  and  were  eagerly  awaiting  this  annual  entertainment.  There  was  a  fearsome  hush  as  the  hall  went  dark  and  the  spotlights  turned  on.  Sister  Imelda  kissed  her  crucifix,  and  I  realized  that  she  was  praying  for  me.  Baba  said  afterward  that  I  bawled  like  a  bloody  bull,  but  Sister  Imelda,  who  stood  in  the  wings,  said  that  she  had  felt  the  streets  of  Rome  and  seen  the  corpse  of  Caesar  as  I  delivered  those  poignant,  distempered  lines.  When  I  came  offstage,  she  put  her  arms  around  me  and  I  was  encased  in  a  shower  of  silent  kisses.  Later,  when  we  had  taken  down  the  decorations  and  put  the  fancy  clothes  back  in  the  trunk,  I  gave  her  two  half-pound  boxes  of  chocolates-bought  for  me  illicitly  by  one  of  the  day  girls-and she  gave  me  a  casket  made  from  the  insides  of  match-  boxes  and  covered  over  with  gilt  paint  and  gold  dust.  It  was  like  holding  moths  and  finding  that  their  powder  adhered  to  your  fingers.  &quot;What  will  you  do  on  Christmas  Day,  Sister?&quot;  I  said.  &quot;I'll  pray  for  you,&quot;  she  said.  It  was  useless  to  say  \xc2\xabWill  you  have  turkey?&quot;  or  &quot;Will  you  have  plum  pudding?&quot;  or  &quot;Will  you  loll  in  bed?,&quot;  because  I  believed  that  her  Christmas  Day  would  be  as  bleak  and  deprived  as  any  other  day  in  her  life.  Yet  she  was  ra-  diant,  as  if  such  austerity  were  manna.  ON  a  cold  snowy  after-  noon  three  weeks  later,  when  we  returned  from  our  holidays,  Sister  Imelda  came  up  to  the  dor-  mitory  to  welcome  me  back.  All  the  other  girls  had  gone  down  to  the  rec-  reation  hall  to  dance  reels,  and  I  could  hear  someone  banging  on  the  piano. For  no  reason  at  all  I  thought  of  our  putting  on  makeup  before  the  school  play  and  how  innocent  it  was.  Now  our  skins  were  smothered  beneath  layers  of  pan-  cake,  which  we  didn't  even  wash  off  at  night.  Thinking  of  the  convent,  I  suddenly  thought  of  Sister  Imelda,  and  then,  as  if  prey  to  a  dream,  I  heard  the  rustle  of  serge,  smelled  the  Jeyes  Fluid  and  theboiled  cabbage,  and  envisaged  her  pale  shocked  face  in  the  months  after  her  brother  died.  Then  I  looked  around  the  bus  and  saw  her  in  earnest,  and  at  first  thought  that  I  was  imagining  things.  But  no,  she  had  got  on,  accompanied  by  an-  other  nun,  and  they  were  settling  themselves  in  the  back  seat  nearest  the  door,  She  looked  older,  but  she  had  the  same  aloof  quality  and  the  same  eyes,  and  my  heart  began  to  race  with  a     mixture  of  excitement  and  dread.  At  first  it  raced  with  a  prodigal  strength,  and  then  it  began  to  falter  and  I  thought  it  was  going  to  give  out.  My  fear  of  her  and  my  love  came  back  in  one  fell  realization.  I  would  have  gone  through  the  window  except  that  it  was  not  wide  enough.  The  thing  was:  how  to  escape?  When  I  told  her,  Baba  gurgled  with  delight,  stood  up,  and  in  the  most  flagrant  way  looked  around  to  make  sure  that  it  was  Imelda.  She  recog-  nized  the  other  nun  as  being  one  with  the  nickname  of  Johnny,  who  gave  piano  lessons.  Baba's  first  thought  was  revenge;  she  enumerated  the  punish-  ments  the  various  nuns  had  meted  out  to  us  and  said  how  nice  it  would  be  to  go  back  and  demolish  them.  She  could  not  understand  why  I  was  quaking  any  more  than  she  could  understand  why  I  began  to  wipe  off  the  lipstick.  Above  all,  I  knew  that  I  could  not  confront  them.  &quot;You're  going  to  have  to,&quot;  Baba  said.  &quot;I  can't,&quot;  I  said.  It  was  not  just  my  attire,  it  was  the fact  of  never  having  written  and  of  that  broken  promise.  Baba  kept  look-  ing  back,  and  she  said  that  they  weren't  saying  a  word  and  that  chil-  dren  were  gawking  at  them.  It  wasn't  often  that  nuns  travelled  in  buses,  and  we  speculated  about  where  they  might  be  going.  &quot;They  might  be  off  to  meet  two  fellows,&quot;  Baba  said,  and  visualized  them  in  the  golf  club  getting  blotto  and  hoisting  their  skirts.  For  me,  it  was  no  laughing  matter.  Then  Baba  came  up  with  a  stratagem:  as  we  ap-  proached  our  stop,  while  the  bus  was  still  moving,  we  were  to  jump  up  and  go  down  the  aisle  and  pass  them  without  even  looking.  Baba  said  most  likely  they  would  not  notice  us;  their  eyes  were  lowered  and  they  seemed  to  be  saying  their  office.  &quot;I  can't  run  down  the  bus,&quot;  I  said.  'You're  going  to,&quot;  Baba  said.  Insisting  that  I  couldn't,  I  had  al-  ready  begun  to  rehearse  an  apology.  I  was  blessing  myself  over  and  over  again,  and  Babawas  reminding  me  that  there  was  only  one  more  stop  before  ours.  When  the  dreadful  mo-  ment  came,  and  the  bus  was  approach-  ing  our  stop,  I  jumped  up  with  what  can  only  be  called  an  apology  of  a  smile.  I  followed  Baba  to  the  rear  of  the  bus.  But  already  they  had  gone.  Through  the  daubed  window  I  saw  the  backs  of  two  identical,  sable  figures,  their  veils  being  blown  wildly  about  in  the  wind.  They  looked  cold  and  lost  as  they  hurried  along  the  pavement,  and  I  wanted  to  jump  off  and  run  after  them.  In  some  way  it  was  worse  than  if  I  had  confronted  Sister  Imelda.  I  cannot  be  certain  what  I  would  have  said.  I  know  that  there  is  something  sad  and  faintly  distasteful  about  love's  ending,  particularly  love  that  has  never  been  fully  realized.  I  might  have  hinted  at  that,  but  I  doubt  it.  In  our  deepest  moments  we  say  the  most  inadequate  things. "
