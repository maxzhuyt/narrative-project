THE  FIGHT  GAME  ‘  N  J  HEN  a  junior  museum  in  my  town  asked  me  to  conduct  a  series  of  Saturday-afternoon  field  trips  for  children  who  wished  to  study  insects,  I  sprang  at  the  chance.  I  should  have  turned  it  down,  knowing  it  would  be  the  same  old  story,  but  hope  dies  hard  in  the  amateur  naturalist.  My  trouble  is  that  insects,  which  have  al-  ways  fascinated  me,  have  invariably  managed  to  baffle  my  desire  to  conduct,  under  field  conditions,  intelligent  ex-  periments  concerning  their  pugnacity.  My  interest  in  entomology  and  my  tendency  to  crawl  around  on  all  fours  with  my  nose  close  to  the  ground  orig-  inated  one  golden  afternoon  in  grade  school,  when  our  teacher  read  us  a  selection  about  a  battle  between  two  armies  of  ants.  It  was  many  years  be-  fore  I  realized  she  had  been  reading  from  &quot;Walden.&quot;  All  I  knew  at  the  time  was  that  those  few  pages  of  description  had  held  me  enthralled,  and  that  I  wanted  to  see  an  ant  battle  for  myself.  &quot;'The  legions  of  these  Myrmidons  cov-  ered  all  the  hills  and  vales  in  my  wood-  yard,&quot;  wrote  Thoreau,  &quot;and  the  ground  was  already  strewn  with  the  dead  and  the  dying,  both  red  and  black.&quot;  Here  was  Waterloo  in  miniature,  on  a  scale  you  could  really  get  a  look  at!  That  very  afternoon,  I  shot  out  into  the  vacant  lot  behind  our  house  to  look  for  ants.  Eagerly,  I  followed  various  columns  I  found  bustling  along  single  file  in  their  anxious,  dedicated  way.  Sooner  or  later,  I  told  myself,  two  of  those  columns  would  swing  close  to  each  other.  Somebody  would  put  a  foot,  or  two  or  three,  into  somebody  else's  terri-  tory.  Then  mandibles  would  clash  and  I  would  see  ant  warriors  who  were,  as  the  book  said,  &quot;prepared  tofight  till  the  sun  wentdown,  or  life  went  out,&quot;  or  I  went  home  to  supper.  I  followed  hundreds  of  ants  that  aft-  ernoon  without  observing  so  much  as  a  border  incident.  The  same  was  true  the  next  afternoon,  and  the  next.  No-  body  was  raiding  anybody  else's  nest.  So  far  as  I  could  determine,  not  one  solitary  ']  rumble  took  place  or  was  even  discussed.  Clearly,  if  I  wanted  war,  I  would  have  to  start  it.  I  set  about  capturing  an  ant.  After  a  few  tries,  I  managed  to  trans-  port  one  in  good  condition  from  his  own  |  territory  to  a  distant  anthill  and  drop  :A  him  there.  It  seemed  a  cruel  thing  to  |  do,  but  I  had  to  begin  somewhere.  Several  trips  proved  necessary.  My  first  gladiator  landed  on  the  side  of  the  (|  anthill  and  scurried  away  before  any  of  /|  the  residents  took  notice  of  him.  So  did  ;{  a  second  and  a  third,  My  fourth  I  managed  to  drop  squarely  into  the  anthill,  right  through  the  hole  in  the  middle.  I  squatted  there,  a  mean  little  ten-year-  old,  full  of  deliciously  guilty  thoughts.  How  horrible  to  know  that  even  now  the  stranger  was  being  torn  to  pieces  underground  by  a  merciless  horde!  Just  then,  however,  the  little  spoil-  sport  came  struggling  up  out  of  the  hole,  alone  and  unmangled.  No  enemy  guards  were  hanging  on  to  his  legs  or  shredding  his  thorax.  He  took  a  feel  around  and  brushed  antennas  with  a  local  ant  who  happened  up  at  that  moment.  Though  both  ants  were  black,  they  looked  differ-  ent,  even  to  me.  And  if  I  could  tell,  surely  they  could  tell.  Yet  no  semblance  of  a  challenge  passed  between  them.  They  were  deplorably  well-mannered.  My  ant,  almost  with  an  &quot;Oh,  sorry,&quot;  sidestepped  and  walked  away.  The  local  ant  waved  farewell  and  strolled  inside.  I  could  only  conclude  they  were  related.  In  the  story  at  school,  it  had  been  red  against  black,  and  no  doubt  that  was  the  secret.  It  took  a  while,  but  finally  I  found  some  red  ants.  Carrying  them  deep  into  black-ant  territory,  I  dropped  them  onto  a  hill  and  watched  breath-  lessly.  Both  kinds  ran.  Black  ants  and  red  ants  bumped  into  each  other,  scram-  bled  over  each  other,  and  completely  ignored  each  other  in  a  single-minded  effort  to  leave  the  vicinity.  Word  had  obviously  been  passed  that  a  ten-year-  old  idiot  was  going  around  grabbing  ants  and  that  the  smart  thing  to  do  was  to  take  cover.  Realizing  I  had  failed  to  witness  or  provoke  a  single  ant  battle,  ant  fight,  ant  scuffle,  or  even  ant  tiff,  I  sighed  wearily  and  gave  up.  HEN  I  accepted  the  junior-  museum  assignment,  more  than  thirty  years  had  passed,  and  I  dared  hope  my  luck  had  improved.  Not  that  I  ex-  pected  to  stage  an  ant  battle-ants  I  had  written  off  long  ago-but  there  were  other  and  even  more  exciting  matches  I  had  read  about  since  my  first  fight  promotion.  Having  retained  my  childhood  interest  in  miniature  carnage,  I  felt  sure  that  my  young  charges  would  not  be  entranced  by  watching  happy  little  insects  at  work  and  play.  They  would  want  action.  They  would  want  to  see  green  and  yellow  insect  blood  spilled  in  illustration  of  Nature's  grim-  mer  laws.  Something  like  spider  vs.  wasp-that  would  be  the  ticket.  A  wasp  caught  in  a  spider  web  is  said  to  be  a  dangerous  victim,  who  often  manages  to  sting  the  spider  before  she  can  get  in  her  fatal  nip.  Before  my  first  museum  group  meeting,  I  tried  several  times  to  arrange  such  a  duel.  At  con-  siderable  personal  risk,  I  managed  to  insert  several  wasps  into  webs  under  the  eaves  of  my  garage,  but  invariably  the  spider  took  one  look  and  scuttled  away,  hungry  but  not  thet  hungry.  After  thrashing  around  angrily  and  wrecking  the  web,  the  wasp  would  eventually  break  loose  and  take  off,  usually  after  me.  It  soon  became  plain  that  the  junior  museum  could  count  on  no  cooperation  from  spiders  and  wasps-at  least,  not  under  my  management.  Then  a  great  young  heavyweight  hope  turned  up  under  my  very  nose.  A  praying  mantis,  that  insatiable  tyran-  nosaur  of  the  grass-roots  jungle,  be-  neath  whose  feet  the  very  earth  of  the  insect  world  trembles,  appeared  one  Sunday  morning  on  a  rhododendron  leaf  in  my  yard.  Here  was  my  star  per-  former  for  next  Saturday  afternoon!  When  I  had  captured  her  and  installed  her  on  a  window  screen  for  study,  I  decided  to  start  things  off  easy.  No  fights-just  victims.  Flies.  It  was  a  sunny  June  day,  and  I  went  outside  confidently  to  gather  a  few  mor-  sels.  Ever  since  boyhood,  I  have  been  a  pretty  handy  flycatcher,  of  the  side-  swipe  school.  On  this  day,  however,  there  was  not  a  fly  in  town.  In  vain  I  circled  hedge,  tree,  and  bush.  I  began  to  wish  I  had  a  fish  to  clean  or  a  picnic  lunch  to  spread  out.  Or  some  syrup,  perhaps.  I  decided  on  a  spoonful  of  sugar  on  the  porch  rail,  dampened  with  a  few  drops  of  water.  All  I  had  to  show  for  this  clever  notion  was  a  sticky  porch  rail.  I  had  to  wait  until  lunchtime  for  my  flies,  when  two  of  them  turned  up  on  the  edge  of  my  plate.  With  a  fly  in  each  fist,  I  rejoined  my  mantis.  Turn-  ing  the  victims  loose  on  the  screen,  I  shut  the  window,  forming  a  rectangu-  |-  lar  jousting  arena,  and  settled  down  to  watch  my  champion  surge  into  action.  All  the  descriptions  of  mantes  I  had  read  agreed  that  one  lightning  strike  of  their  cruel,  powerful  forelegs  spelled  death  for  any  lesser  insect.  It  was  my  luck,  of  course,  to  fall  in  with  as  untalented  a  mantis  as  ever  missed  a  fat  fly  three  times  running.  At  first,  she  was  too  eager  and  made  a  clumsy  grab  before  the  fly  was  really  close  enough.  Next,  a  fly  marched  straight  under  her  nose-and  she  hesi-  tated.  Frowning  through  the  window  '  and  muttering  encouragement,  I  urged  her  on  with  all  the  frustration  of  a  Bos-  ton  fan  trying  to  get  some  action  out  of  the  Red  Sox.  Ileft  the  room  for  a  min-  ute  or  two  in  a  vain  search  for  more  flies.  When  I  returned,  she  was  munching  a  fly.  For  amateur  naturalists  like  me,  this  )f  is  typical  of  the  way  Nature  does  things.  Always  when  our  backs  are  turned.  After  that  first  afternoon,  my  mantis   began  to  show  more  promise.  I  still  think  she  must  have  been  the  despair  of  her  mother,  but  at  least  she  began  to  catch  the  slower  flies  with  some  regularity.  I  decided  a  sterner  test  was  in  order.  How  would  she  make  out  with  a  wasp?  What  a  drama  to  thrill  my  class  with  on  Saturday!  Of  course,  by  experimenting  now  I  was  taking  the  chance  that  my  mantis  might  be  K.O.'d  in  her  first  fight,  leaving  me  with  a  blank  on  my  Saturday  card,  but  then,  every  promoter  must  run  the  risk  of  losing  one  of  his  principals  in  a  pre-fight  injury  in  the  gym.  I  would  put  her  on  a  crash  diet  for  a  half  day,  and  meanwhile  I  would  look  around  for  something  black  and  bel-  ligerent  in  the  way  of  a  wasp.  All  went  according  to  plan.  Soon  a  large  wasp  was  walking  around  angrily  on  the  window  screen,  hunching  his  shoulders  and  shooting  fierce  glances  this  way  and  that.  And  as  he  went  stamping  about,  his  steps  took  him  straight  toward  the  terror  of  the  six-legged  set.  She  turned  her  head  for  a  look,  as  only  man-  tes  can.  Having  looked,  she  backed  up  hastily.  The  wasp  walked  on,  ignoring  her.  So  ended  Round  1-a  waltz.  I  lingered  impatiently  at  ringside,  hoping  for  action,  but  deep  in  my  heart  I  knew  that  my  tiger  was  a  coward.  I  wanted  to  tell  the  wasp  that  yonder  green  object  was  no  mere  leaf  waving  in  the  breeze  but  a  professional  assassin,  and  that  any  passing  wasp  would  be  wise  to  make  his  move  before  she  made  hers.  To  be  hon-  est,  I  actually  said  all  this  aloud,  but  I  only  succeeded  in  steaming  up  the  window.  The  wasp  kept  walking  around  and  around,  and  the  mantis  kept  shrink-  ing  into  corners.  This  went  on  for  hours.  On  Saturday,  the  kids  watched  my  mantis  grab  flies  and  chew  them  up  in  her  satisfactorily  disgusting  way,  but  when  they  asked,  &quot;What  else  can  she  do?,&quot;  I  was  forced  to  give  her  a  bitter  glance  and  say,  &quot;Nothing.&quot;  MIGHT  have  retired  from  the  insect-fight-promotion  racket  then  and  there  had  it  not  been  for  a  pam-  phlet  that  fell  into  my  hands  during  the  following  week.  It  was  called  &quot;Cricket  Fighting  in  China,&quot;  and  it  told  how  male  Chinese  crickets  were  bred  for  sin-  gle  combat,  just  like  roosters  in  43756261.  A  good  vicious  cricket  is  highly  valued,  it  said.  Prizefights  between  well-known  crickets  are  matters  of  great  local  in-  terest,  and  cricket  owners  (who  carry  their  pugilists  around  in  hand-carved  cages,  often  made  of  ivory)  and  spec-  tators  often  bet  fortunes  on  the  outcome.  Indeed,  these  fights  truly  resemble  prizefights,  because  the  contestants  usu-  ally  do  not  kill  each  other  but  only  jab    and  hook  and  uppercut  until  one  of  them  has  enough  and  crawls  away. What  I  have  be-  gun  to  wonder  is  whether  all  my  failures  in  the  fight  game  were  really  my  fault,  as  I  had  supposed,  or  whether  they  were  the  fault  of  the  insects  themselves. Have  our  ants,  mantes,  wasps,  and  crickets  lost  the  will  to  sacrifice,  to  do  battle  for  the  capitalist  way  of  insect  life?  I  turn  this  urgent  question  over  to  better-qualified  entomologists  than  I,  urging  them  to  consider  a  top-priority  program  of  study.  If  it  should  turnout  that  I  am  correct,  I  can  only  suggest  that  we  immediately  invite  the  Com-  munist  leaders,  Chinese  and  Russian  alike  (I  mean  human  leaders,  not  in-  sect  leaders),  to  a  summit  conference,  where  we  must  try  to  resolve,  once  and  for  all,  the  differences  and  prob-  lems  that  divide  us.  I  mean,  if  our  insects  have  gone  soft,  what  chance  have  we  got?  I  will  gladly  offer  myself  as  mediator  at  this  vital  gathering,  ajob  for  which  I  am  perfectly  suited.  I  seem  to  breed  pacifism  wherever  I  go.
