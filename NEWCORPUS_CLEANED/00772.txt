As  a  child,  I  used  to  think  that  Dos-  toyevsky's  second  wife,  Anna  Gri-  gorievna,  was  his  muse.  I  knew  her  story  before  I  knew  anything  else  about  Dos-  toyevsky,  before  I'd  even  read  any  of  his  books.  When  I  was  five,  my  father  left  my  mother  and  me  to  marry  a  tall,  square-  shouldered  woman  named  Maya.  Shortly  after  that,  he  died.  To  my  motherand  me,  it  was  a  kind  of  double  betrayal.  Around  that  time,  photographs  of  the  major  Russian  writers  began  to  line  the  wall  in  my  mother's  room,  gradually  re-  placing  the  pictures  of  my  father  pull-  ing  a  straining  fishing  rod  up  out  of  the  water  or  standing  by  the  shore  amid  fat,  glossy  sea  lions,  looking  through  binoculars.  I  never  liked  Tolstoy's  portrait.  He  looked  like  the  mean  Santa  Claus-the  one  who  never  brings  anything  good,  who  leaves  a  knitted  scarf  ora  pair  of  socks  under  the  tree.  I  believed  early  on  in  the  two  Santas:  they  were  broth-  ers.  The  good  one  left  pretty  boxes  filled  with  dolls,  dolls  furniture,  or  dolls'  dishes.  The  mean  one  left  nothingbut  clothes  that  my  mother  would  have  bought  for  me  anyway.  I  didn't  like  Chekhov's  portrait,  either.  There  was  something  suspicious  about  his  grin  and  his  pince-nez.  He  looked  as  if  he  knew  something  that  I  didn't  want  him  to  know.  Perhaps  he  knew  about  Maya's  favorite  vase,  which  I  had  shat,  tered,  carefully  disposed  of,  then  pre-  tended  not  to  know  anything  about.  I  definitely  didn't  like  Chekhov!  Pushkin  .  ..  Well,  Pushkin  was  O.K.  He  seemed  like  a  fine  guy,  but  he  didn't  look  serious  enough  to  be  a  writer.  Dostoyevsky  was  the  one  I  loved.  He  had  strong  hands  and  a  large  forehead,  so  large  that  it  appeared  almost  to  burst  through  his  skin.  He  had  serious  eyes,  and  he  looked  directly  at  me,  without  hiding,  without  the  fake  playful  expres-  sion  of  other  adults.  &quot;Dostoyevsky  had  different  eyes,&quot;  my  mother  said  when  she  spotted  me  staring  at  him.  &quot;One  brown  and  one  black.&quot;  &quot;Different  eyes!&quot;  I  repeated,  awe-  struck,  and  asked  if  he  had  a  wife.  &quot;He  is  dead&quot;  was  the  answer.  &quot;Buthe  did  have  a  wife,  when  he  was  alive.  Her  name  was  Anna  Grigorievna.&quot;  I  liked  her  name.  &quot;Was  she  nice?&quot;  &quot;Oh,  yes,  she  was  very  nice.  She  took  very  good  care  of  him.  When  he  was  cold,  she'd  serve  him  tea  and  cover  his  knees  with  a  plaid  blanket.&quot;  &quot;I  could  do  that,  too!&quot;  I  said.  I  imagined  Dostoyevsky  sharinga  dinner  table  with  my  dolls.  I  knew  how  to  prepare  kasha  for  the  dolls,  and  serve  them  tea.  I  would  spread  a  napkin  on  his  lap,  feed  him  my  kasha,  and  give  him  his  tea,  and  then  I  would  put  him  to  bed.  I'd  tuck  a  blanket  around  him  and  take  his  temperature  with  my  toy  thermometer,  just  in  case.  Dostoyevsky  was  fixed  in  my  imagi-  nation  as  a  dead  writer  who  had  different  eyes  and  a  nice  wife  until  I  turned  ten  and  my  grandmother  had  a  stroke  and  moved  in  with  us.  Then  I  learned  more  details  about  his  life.  &quot;Your  grandfather's  name  was  Fyodor  Mikhaylovich,  the  same  as  Dostoyev-  sky's.  Dostoyevsky  was  crazy,  and  your  grandfather  was  even  crazier!&quot;  My  grand-  mother  told  me  this  once  as  I  stood  cut-  ting  her  hair.  My  only  experience  cutting  hair  consisted  of  chopping  off  my  dolls  coarse  ringlets,  but  my  grandmother  had  been  complaining  that  long  hair  made  her  neck  itch,  and  my  mother  fi-  nally  decided  that  I  should  take  care  of  it.  &quot;You're  good  with  your  hands,&quot;  she  said.  I  rarely  disagreed  with  praise  that  was  directed  at  me,  so  one  day  I  took  the  scissors  from  the  drawer,  moved  a  chair  to  the  edgeof  the  bed,  and  guided  my  grandmother  into  it,  holding  her  under  the  arms.  She  felt  terribly  heavy,  but  she  looked  weight-  less.  After  months  in  bed,  she  seemed  to  have  lost  almost  all  material  sub-  stance.  I  imagined  that  she  was  com-  pletely  empty  inside,  her  bones  rattling  in  the  withered  sack  of  her  skin  like  my  wooden  building  blocks  in  their  canvas  bag.  I  draped  a  sheet  over  her  shoulders,  untiedher  blue  hair  band,  and  combed  her  hair,  trying  to  avoid  touching  her  scalp.  Her  skin,  though  warm,  had  a  corpselike  softness  about  it.  I  felt  that  ifI  pressed  against  it,  my  finger  would  break  through  into  some  darkness  that  reached  further  than  my  grandmother's  body.  After  the  stroke,  my  grandmoth-  er's  state  of  mind  kept  changing  from  crazy  to  sane  and  back,often  drifting  in  a  weird  transitional  condition  that  I  called  &quot;medium-crazy.&quot;  Her  mind  was  almost  clear  then:  she  didn't  confuse  our  names,  she  didn't  attempt  to  wan-  der  off  or  eat  soup  with  a  fork.  She  spent  most  of  her  time  with  an  open  bookon  her  chest-her  favorite  book,  the  diary  of  Anna  Grigorievna  Dos-  toyevskaya.  She  looked  almost  normal.  Still,  something  betrayed  the  sickness  of  my  grandmother's  mind  to  those  who  knew  her  well.  It  was  her  passion  for  storytelling,  an  unhealthy  vigor  that  wouldn't  let  her  rest,  that  kept  her  talking  in  a  manic  confessional  stream,  greedy  for  an  audience.  My  late  grandfather,  a  man  who  was  generally  avoided  in  family  conversation,  was  the  main  hero  of  her  stories-he,  and  the  other  Fyo-  dor  Mikhaylovich,  Dostoyevsky.  Both  men  emerged  as  bright  and  sinister  characters.  &quot;Dostoyevsky  was  a  terrible,  terrible  man,&quot;  my  grandmother  said  as  I  reached  for  the  scissors.  &quot;How  he  tortured  his  poor  wife!  She  was  his  muse,  you  know.  Without  her,  he  wouldn't  have  writ-  ten  shit.&quot;  &quot;I  know  her  name,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;Anna  Grigorievna.&quot;  &quot;Anna  Grigorievna.  That's  right.  Oh,  how  he  tortured  her!  He  would  take  all  their  money  and  gamble  it  away,  and  then  he  would  come  back  for  more.  But  of  course  there  wasn't  any,  so  he'd  pawn  every  good  thing  she  owned,  only  to  gamble  the  money  away  again.  He  gam-  bled  away  her  ring,  her  earrings,  her  shawl,  and,  once,  her  shoes  and  her  dress,  so  she  couldn't  even  go  shopping.&quot;  &quot;What  was  the  point  in  going  shop-  ping  if  he'd  already  gambled  away  all  the  money?&quot;  &quot;Well,  maybe  she  hoped  to  ask  a  nice  salesman  for  credit.  Or  maybe  she  just  wanted  to  window-shop,&quot;  my  grand-  mother  said.  &quot;And,  when  he  came  back,  instead  of  saying  he  was  sorry  he  yelled  at  her  for  not  fixing  his  supper  on  time!&quot;  &quot;Did  Anna  Grigorievna  yell  back  at  him?&quot;  &quot;Never!  She  apologized  for  not  hav-  ing  supper  ready.  You  see,  she  was  a  very  good  wife.&quot;  &quot;All  right,  now  shut  your  eyes.&quot;  I  let  her  long  bangs  fall  down  her  forehead  and  snapped  the  scissors  above  her  eyebrows,  careful  not  to  nick  the  skin.  It  was  easier  to  look  at  my  grand-  mother  when  her  eyes  were  closed.  She   seemed  smoother,  more  alive,  almost  normal.  &quot;You  know  what,  Ba?&quot;  I  said.  &quot;Dos-  toyevsky  was  a  genius.  Geniuses  are  a  crazy  lot.&quot;  One  sly,  glistening  eye  opened  under  my  cutting  hand.  They  are!  They  are!  They're  all  crazy  as  hell!&quot;  she  said.  &quot;You  never  know  how  toplease  them.  You  think  that  you  do.  You  keep  track  of  the  things  that  please  them.  But  they're  fickle-they  change  their  likings  on  a  whim.  You  serve  them  tea  with  sugar  and  cream-their  favor-  ite,  and  you've  gone  out  specially  to  buy  that  cream-and  then  they  yell  that  they  wanted  coffee  withlemon.  You  want  to  throw  that  tea  into  their  ugly  red  mug  and  then  smash  the  cup  on  their  head,  but  you  don't  do  it.  You  apol-  ogize.  And  then  when  they  come  home  drunk  and  in  soiled  pants-yes,  they  do  that,  they  soil  their  pants-you  don't  kick  them  out!  You  take  them  in,  you  undress  them,  you  scrape  the  shit  off  them,  and  you  put  them  to  bed.  And  after  that  you  go  to  the  communal  bath-  room  to  wash  their  pants.  It's  not  an  easy  thing,  I  tell  you,  to  wash  shitty  pants  in  a  communal  bathroom  with  all  the  neigh-  bors  watching  and  yelling  at  you  because  of  the  stench!  But  you  do  it  for  them  all  the  same.&quot;  She  couldn't  possibly  be  talking  about  Dostoyevsky,  could  she?  &quot;Do  it  for  whom,  Ba?&quot;  I  asked.  &quot;Who  soils  their  pants?&quot;  Both  eyes  opened  like  nimble  ani-  mals,  lurking  beneath  the  hair.  &quot;Them!  Them!  The  geniuses!&quot;  The  tirade  had  left  her  out  of  breath.  She  closed  her  eyes,  hung  her  head,  and  sat  quietly  without  moving--a  conve-  nient  position  for  chopping  the  hair  on  hercrown.  I  finished  the  job  in  silence,  careful  to  make  the  layers  even.  Yet,  at  some  point,  my  ten-year-old  overconfi-  dence  let  me  down.  By  the  end  of  the  session,  my  grandmother's  hair  was  cir-  cling  her  head  in  all  directions.  I  tried  to  even  it  out  but  wound  up  cutting  too  close  to  the  skin.  By  the  time  my  grandmother  finally  raised  her  head  and  opened  her  eyes,  there  was  nothing  but  yellow-  -white  down  fluttering  on  her  bare  scalp.  With  her  beaky  nose  and  her  neck  thinned  by  illness,  she  looked  like  a  baby  chick.  A  sick  baby  chick.  An  old  and  sick  baby  chick,  if  such  a  thing  is  possi-  ble.  My  grandmother  moved  her  head  from  side  to  side,  then  shook  it.  &quot;Nice  and  cool,&quot;  she  said.  y  grandmother  died  shortly  after  I  turned  fifteen,  leaving  behind  an  empty  bed  and  the  cluster  of  boxes  into  which  my  mother  put  her  belong-  ings  after  the  funeral-clothes  in  a  big  box,  documents  in  a  small  one,  and  ev-  erything  else  in  a  medium-sized  one.  A  heavy,  nauseating  sensation-the  close-  ness  of  death-pervaded  the  apartment  long  after  my  grandmother's  body  had  been  taken  away.  Sometimes  when  I  lay  in  bed  trying  to  sleep  I  could  almost  hear  her  moaning  on  the  other  side  of  the  wall,  complaining,  calling  for  help.  Then  I'd  dive  under  the  blanket  and  bury  my  head  under  my  pillow.  At  other  times,  I  would  hearvery  real  moans,  coming  from  the  direction  of  the  staircase  and  the  next-door  neighbor's  daughter.  The  neighbor's  girl  was  just  two  years  older  than  me,  but  she  led  a  very  different  life.  Independent.  Unsheltered.  Tough.  She  worked  as  an  apprentice  in  the  supermarket,  andbought  her  own  clothes  and  makeup  with  the  money  she  earned.  &quot;Get  lost,  you  moron,&quot;  she  yelled  at  her  father,  exactly  the  way  her  mother  did.  &quot;Shut  up,  you  bitch,&quot;  she  yelled  at  her  mother  when  they  argued.  They  argued  like  equals,  not  in  the  whiny  way  I  argued  with  my  mother  when  she  accused  me  of  something.  The  neighbor  girl  looked  nothing  like  me,  either.  She  looked  thirty,  as  my  mother  commented.  I  looked  twelve.  Probably  even  less  than  that.  Tanya  has  preschool  boobs,&quot;  the  girls  at  school  whis-  pered  behind  my  back.  For  a  year,  every  Wednesday  and  Fri-  day  at  about  ten  or  eleven  at  night,  the  neighbor  girl  came  home  with  a  boy.  As  soon  as  I  heard  the  bang  of  the  elevator  opening  on  our  floor,  I  hurried  into  the  hallway  and  pressed  my  face  to  the  peep-  hole  in  the  cold  wood  of  our  door.  There  she  was-sailing  out  of  the  elevator,  her  eyes  half  closed,  her  winter  coat  unbut-  toned,the  boy's  body  pressed  to  her  be-  hind.  They  moved  to  the  staircase  and  leaned  against  the  railing.  I  never  saw  the  boy's  face,  only  his  large  red  hands  fum-  bling  across  her  chest,  struggling  with  the  layers  of  fabric-the  fur  of  her  coat,  the  wool  of  her  cardigan,  the  cotton  of  her  blouse,  the  satin  of  her  bra.  I  saw  her  flushed  face  and  her  parted  lips,  which        she  licked  from  time  to  time.  I  heard  their  panting  and  their  coarse  voices.  His  chanting  &quot;Where  are  they?  Where  are  they?  Here  they  are!  Here  they  are!&quot;  and  her  moaning  &quot;Shut  up,  you  moron&quot;  in  a  tone  utterly  different  from  the  one  she  used  with  her  father.  When  I  went  back  to  bed,  I  slipped  my  hand  under  the  blanket.  I  didn't  fan-  tasize  about  the  neighbor  girl  and  her  boyfriend.  On  the  contrary,  as  soon  as  I  got  into  bed  I  found  the  image  of  them  repulsive.  I  fantasized,  instead,  about  the  dead  writers,  whose  solemn  faces  lined  the  greasy  pages  of  my  schoolbooks.  Gogol,  Chekhov,  Turgenev.  Dostoyev-  sky.  Graying  hair,  prominent  foreheads,  knowing  eyes.  At  times,  the  face  of  my  writer  suddenly  turned  into  the  face  of  oneof  the  men  I  knew,  and  I'd  put  an  end  to  my  fantasy  immediately.  It  was  too  weird,  too  shameful,  to  imagine  how  I  would  react  the  next  time  I  saw  that  man.  It  was  safer  to  stick  with  the  dead.  A  dead  writer  would  materialize  in  my  room  in  his  shabby-for  some  rea-  son,  I  always  imagined  them  shabby-  nineteenth-century  clothes  and  sit  down  on  the  edge  of  my  bed  with  an  expression  so  serious  and  so  kind  that  it  made  me  want  to  cry.  He  would  gently  sit  me  up,  supporting  my  back  with  his  hand,  and  press  my  hand  to  his  chest.  He  would  stroke  my  hair  and  my  back  and  then  unbutton  my  pajama  top,  slowly,  button  by  button,  whispering  gentle  words.  Soon  I  was  completely  naked,  with  a  fully  clothed  dead  writer  in  my  bed  (I  didn't  find  the  idea  of  a  naked  dead  writer  ap-  pealing),  waiting  for  something  wonder-  ful  to  happen.  After  it  was  all  over,  the  dead  writer  left  my  bed  and  my  room,  going  back  to  the  safe  confinement  of  my  textbooks.  This  kind  of  sex  certainly  wasn't  enough  to  make  life  worthwhile.  Yet  there  was  a  promise  in  it;  a  glimpse  of  something  bigger  lurking  in  the  shadow  of  puck-  ered  fingers  and  unbuttoned  blouses.  ‘  N  Je  started  studying  Dostoyevsky  in  the  tenth  grade.  The  teacher  announced  that  before  we  began  reading  his  works  she  wanted  us  to  prepare  re-  ports  on  various  aspects  of  his  biography.  I  picked  &quot;The  later  years:  the  marriage  to  Anna  Grigorievna.&quot;  When  I  went  home  that  afternoon,  I  raided  the  boxes  that  held  my  grandmother's  things.  In  the  medium-sized  one,  buried  under  faded  postcards,  pairs  of  broken  reading  glasses,  and  bottles  of  unused,  expired  medicine,  I  found  a  small,  dishevelled  book:  Anna  Grigorievna's  diary.  I  brushed  the  dust  off  the  cover,  and  slumped  on  the  couch  with  a  sigh  of  an-      ticipation.  After  all  these  years,  I  was  re-  turning  to  the  writer  with  different  eyes;  I  was  about  todiscover  what  it  was  like  to  be  his  muse.  But  as  soon  as  I  had  read  the  first  couple  of  pages  I  knew  something  was  wrong,  very  wrong,  either  with  me  or  with  the  book,  and  I  couldn't  understand  what  it  was.  Here  she  was:  Anna  Gri-  gorievna  Snitkina,  a  young  stenographer,  fresh  from  school,  by  lucky  chance  as-  signed  to  work  for  Fyodor  Dostoyevsky.  He  needed  a  stenographer  because  he  was  in  a  hurry  to  finish  a  novel  (some  con-  tractual  obligation-I  skipped  the  bor-  ing  parts).  They  arranged  that  he  would  dictate  and  she  would  write  shorthand  and  then  later  transcribe  what  she  had  written.  They  worked  like  that  for  some  time,  became  attached  to  each  other,  and  one  day  he  proposed  and  she  accepted.  I  stopped  reading  and  went  back  to  the  beginning  of  the  book.  When  Anna  Grigorievna  went  to  see  Dostoyevsky  for  the  first  time,  she  broughtwith  her  a  stenographer's  note-  book  and  a  supply  of  sharpened  pen-  cils  of  thebest  quality.  She  described  in  minute  detail  her  trip  to  the  stationery  store  to  buy  those  pencils-she  wanted  to  impress  him  with  her  pencils?  Liat!Liar!  She  didn't  even  mention  how  much  time  she'd  spent  fixing  her  hair  or  choosing  her  clothes!  I  smacked  the  book  against  the  couch  with  the  force  of  a  sixteen-  year-old's  disgust,  but  I  didn't  put  it  down.  I  read  on,  my  imaginationsup-  plying  the  details  that  Anna  Grigorievna  had  omitted.  She  sat  bending  over  her  work.  She  didn't  raise  her  eyes,  but  she  felt  his  pres-  ence.  He  was  pacing  back  and  forth,  back  and  forth.  The  folds  of  his  coat  brushed  against  her  back  from  time  to  time;  his  heavy  feet  made  the  floorboards  creak.  He  sighed.  He  groaned.  He  murmured  some  unintelligible  sounds.  He  looked  at  her-first  without  realizing  that  he  was  looking  at  her,  but  gradually  becoming  more  and  more  aware  of  her.  He  stared  at  her  delicate  girlish  neck,.  He  took  in  the  shape  of  her  back,  the  color  of  her  hair,  the  quick,  firm  movements  of  her  hand,  as  he  wove  and  spun  the  intricate  patterns  of  his  sentences.  I  found  it  hard  to  breathe.  Yes,  &quot;the  other  woman,&quot;  along  with  the  man,  had  been  dead  for  almost  a  hundred  years,  but  that  didn't  make  my  jealousy  any  less  real  or  less  painful.  I  hated  Anna  Gri-  gorievna.  I  hated  her  guts. As  ordinary  as  Anna  Gri-  gorievnawas,  she  had  the  man  of  my  dreams,  and  she  was  his  muse.  It  was  she  who  had  inspired  Dostoyevsky  to  write  what  my  textbook  called  &quot;the  pearls  of  world  literature.&quot;  Dostoyevsky  dictated  to  her.  But  she  was  the  one  who  actually  wrote  &quot;The  Gambler.&quot;  Her  hand  touched  the  paper,  the  movements  of  her  fingers  trans-  formed  the  short-lived  sounds  into  eter-  nal  words.  Her  eyes  were  the  first  to  see  the  words  he  had  uttered.  And  it  was  her  work  that  enabled  others  to  see  them.  Wasn't  she,  then,  even  more  than  a  muse?  It  was  unbearable.  I  shoved  Anna  Grigorievna's  diary  under  my  mother's  sofa,  where,  years  before,  I  had  hidden  my  uncle's  scary  &quot;Atlas  of  Dermatology.&quot;  I  had  to  read  &quot;The  Gambler&quot;  now,  the  novel  that  she  had  written.  I  had  to  read  it,  even  though  it  might  be  painful.  I  found  it  on  the  upper  bookshelf,  a  thin  book  with  a  faded  blue  cover.  I  started  reading  right  there,  standing  on  a  chair,  my  feet  sinking  into  the  plush  upholstery.  After  a  few  pages  I  stepped  down,  dragged  my  feet  across  the  room,  and  cautiously  lowered  myself  onto  thecouch,  never  tak-  ing  my  eyes  from  the  book.  I  soaked  up  every  word,  getting  progressively  drunker.  Something  about  the  book  made  me  feel  almost  euphoric,  but  I  couldn't  put  a  fin-  ger  onwhat  it  was.  It  was  bigger,  more  important,  more  exciting  than  the  char-  acters  or  the  plot-it  was  something  that  existed  beyond  the  book's  pages.  In  the  middle  of  Chapter  6,  it  hit  me.  There  was  another  woman!  A  real  woman,  who  had  given  life  to  the  charac-  ter  Polina,  and  to  the  novel  itself:  she  was  Dostoyevsky's  muse.  Dostoyevsky  had  had  a  relationship  with  her  before  he  met  his  zealous  stenographer,  Anna  Grigori-  evna.  And  it  was  that  other  woman  whom  he  had  really  loved.  The  magnitude  of  the  passion  in  the  novel  was  so  great  that  even  I,  who  was  unfamiliar  with  anything  remotely  close  to  love,  could  recognize  it.  I  read  one  passage  again  and  again:  What  there  is  so  attractive  about  her  I  can-  not  think.  Yet  there  is  something  attractive  about  her-something  passing  fair,  it  would  seem.  Others  besides  myself  she  has  driven  to  distraction.  She  is  tall  and  straight,  and  very  slim.  Her  body  looks  as  though  it  couldbe  tied  into  a  knot,  or  bent  double,  like  a  cord.  The  imprint  ofher  foot  is  long  and  narrow.  It  is  a  maddening  imprint-yes,  simply  a  maddening  one!  And  her  hair  has  a  reddish  tint  about  it,  and  her  eyes  are  like  cat's  eyes-though  able  also  to  glance  with  proud,  disdainful  mien.  The  maddening  imprint  of  her  foot-  that  expression  moved  me  so  much  that  I  dropped  the  book  and  fell  face  down  onto  the  couch,  laughing  happily,  as  if  I  my-  self  had  driven  a  man  like  Dostoyevsky  to  distraction.  I  didn't  resemble  Polina  at  all.  I  wasn't  tall,  I  wasn't  a  redhead,  I  didn't  have  cat's  eyes,  I  wasn't  proud,  or  cruel,  or  disdainful-there  wasn't  one  single  trait  in  Dostoyevsky's  description  that  I  could  call  mine.  But  Polina  was  different  from  the  detested  Anna  Gri-  gorievna,  and  that  enabled  me  to  identify  with  her.  I  allowed  Dostoyevsky  to  be  in  love  with  her.I  blessed  their  love.  I  imagined  Anna  Grigorievna  sitting  at  his  desk  with  her  perfect  pencils,  tran-  scribing  the  frenzied  world  of  his  novels,  where  there  was  no  place  for  her.  That  world  was  dominated  by  Polina,  who  wouldn't  serve  him  tea,  offer  him  a  plaid  blanket,  kiss  him  on  his  bald  crown,  or  take  shorthand,  but  who,  instead,  had  the  power  to  ignite  him  with  the  imprint  of  her  foot,  torture  him,  drive  him  mad.  When  I  am  a  muse,  I  will  be  Polina,  I  decided.  Never,  never  will  I  become  Anna  Grigorievna.
