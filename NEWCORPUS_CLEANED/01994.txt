Feeling  numb,  I  sat  down  in  the  front  pew.  Four  men  in  ragged  tweed  suits  came  walking  down  the  aisle.  For  a  mad  moment,  knowing  that  Haggie  had  been  able  to  commune  wherever  she  went,  I  thought  that  they  must  be  Sligo  acquaintances.  But  they  were  carrying  the  coffin.  They  placed  it  in  front  of  me  and  left  the  church.  The  rector  spoke  at  me,  but  I  was  not  hearing  a  word.  I  was  reflect-  ing  that,  albeit  in  another  field,  but  also  under  Ben  Bulben,  Yeats  had,  by  his  command,  the  words  cut  on  his  gravestone:  Cast  a  cold  eye  On  life,  on  death.  Horseman,  pass  by!  And  it  seemed  to  me  that  Haggie  de-  served  a  share  in  this.  I  walked  back  down  the  aisle  of  the  empty  church.  Whether  out  of  shy-  ness,  or  because  she  wouldn't  enter  a  Protestant  church,  there  was  a  pretty  girl  standing  just  inside  the  doorway.
