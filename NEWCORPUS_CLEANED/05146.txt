One  Friday  afternoon,  I  came  home  from  school  and  my  mother  asked  me  to  go  to  my  grandmother's  to  spend  the  night.  Grandpa  was  in  the  Catskills  on  business,  she  said,  and  wasn't  expected  home  until  Sunday,  so  it  would  be  a  blessing  if  I  went  and  kept  Grandma  company.  I  arrived  as  Grandma  was  finishing  her  cooking  for  the  Sabbath.  I  lit  the  gas  lights  in  the  kitchen  and  in  the  din-  ing  room,  although  the  rooms  were  still  glowing  with  the  late-afternoon  sun.  'To  strike  a  light  aftersunset,  when  the  Jewish  Sabbath  began,  would  be  a  desecration.  It  would  also  be  a  desecration  to  turn  the  gas  jets  off.  A  colored  man  who  did  odd  jobs  for  the  Jewish  families  in  the  neighbor-  hood,  and  who  was  called  Black  Men-  del,  to  distinguish  him  from  a  neighbor  named  Mendel,  came  through  the  street  between  eight-thirty  and  nine  o'clock  on  Friday  evenings  and,  stopping  at  build-  ing  after  building,  turned  the  lights  off. From  the  stoop,  I  could  see  up  and  down  the  block. He  must  be  sick.  He  hasn't  made  the  rounds.&quot;  I  knew  my  grandmother  would  never  be  satisfied  to  leave  the  gas  burn-  ing  all  night;  it  wasn't  safe.
