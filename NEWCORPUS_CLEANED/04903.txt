In  summer,  the  sun  beat  relentlessly  down  on  the  island,  and  the  pines  shed  their  needles  in  dry  little  coughs,  and  the  scant  crab  grass  turned  brown.  There  was  no  shade  on  the  island,  and  no  water,  nor  was  there  a  beach.  It  rose  sharply  from  the  waves,  and  more  rocks  lay  hidden  under  the  sea  around  it,  discouraging  young  explorers  in  sailboats,  who  had  been  warned  by  their  fathers  to  give  the  place  a  wide  berth.  It  was  not  even  much  of  a  curiosity  to  the  children  who  played  on  the  strip  of  sand  by  the  Beach  Club.  They  had  grown  used  to  seeing  it,  gray  and  brown,  lying  off  beyond  the  Club  float.  The  float,  with  its  diving  board  and  slide,  was  their  far  boundary  through-  out  the  long,  delirious  summer.  They  were  allowed  to  go  as  far  as  the  float,  paddling  on  rubber  rafts,  bobbing  up  and  down  in  inflated  inner  tubes,  or  swimming,  gasping  and  proud,  while  Marty  Cook,  the  lifeguard,  kept  a  sharp  eye  on  them  from  his  chair,  where  he  sat  on  the  sandy  bluff  near  the  Club  porch  like  a  king  on  a  throne.  Once  a  day,  after  the  youngest  children  had  had  their  naps  and  appeared,  screaming,  on  the  beach,  he  pushed  his  old  rowboat  into  the  water.  He  was  assisted  by  a  group  of  slightly  older  children,  who,  when  the  boat  was  afloat,  stood  aside  silently  and  waited  for  him  to  make  his  decision.  They  watched  him  with  the  intent,  apprehensive  eyes  of  terriers  as  he  called  out  the  names  of  the  lucky  ones-ten  or  so-who  could  ride  in  the  boat  with  him  that  day.
