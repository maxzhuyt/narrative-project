 b"IT  is  many  more  years  ago  now  than  it  should  be,  the  war-the  hot  and  noisy  tanks,  the  times  when  we  killed  and  were  killed,  passionless,  all  strangers  to  one  another,  whether  friend  or  foe,  all  caught  in  an  intricate  dance  of  death  and  suffering,  each  man  fulfilling  his  role,  bowing  to  present  the  shells  into  the  breech  or  crooking  his  finger  deli-  cately  over  the  machine-gun  trigger.  But  that  is  not  what  I  best  remember.  I  cannot  forget  the  carnage,  but,  truth-  fully,  itis  now,  as  it  was  then,  merely  a  hideous  vision,  baffling  and  affecting  but  not  personal  at  all.  We  were  all  possessed,  and  I  suppose  I  look  upon  our  battles  as  one  looks  upon  illness  or  rage  or  the  other  abdications  of  the  de-  liberate  self-I  exclude  them  from  the  pattern  of  my  life.  What  I  do  remem-  ber  well  out  of  those  years  are  the  points  of  sanity.  They  are  few  enough,  but  they  suffice,  and  I  even  call  upon  them  now  for  comfort.  I  remember  one  nightbeing  sent-  though  this  was  at  the  front-to  stand  guard  over  an  officer  who  was  awaiting  court-martial,  for  what  breach  of  con-  duct  I  never  learned.  I  remember  that  we  were  given  rifles  to  carry  and  that,  being  accustomed  to  the  lighter  carbine,  I  felt  the  chafe  of  the  sling  on  my  shoul-  der.  We  arrived  in  utter  darkness  at  the  place  where  the  prisoner  was,  a  long  building  on  low  stilts,  with  a  tin  roof,  and  were  shown  briefly  about  by  the  sentries  we  were  relieving.  We  hardly  got  the  measure  of  our  posts,  and  of  what  lay  beyond  on  any  side  we  knew  nothing.  We  might  have  been  no-  where-archangels  guarding  a  limbo  in  which  the  officer  was.  But  I  remember  thinking  about  the  psalm,  about  the  verse  that  I  had  seen  translated  &quot;My  soul  waits  for  the  Lord  more  than  sentinels  wait  for  the  dawn.&quot;  I  was  yearning  for  the  light  of  morn-  ing,  and  as  time  passed,  the  more  I  yearned  and  the  more  I  admired  the  psalmist's  image.  The  sling  cutting  my  shoulder,  my  perfect  loneliness  (guards  did  not  speak  to  one  another  at  night,  for  fear  of  attracting  snipers),  the  silence  of  that  night,  the  path  on  which  I  passed  and  repassed,  as  if  walking  a  high  wall-  for  everything  outside  the  path  was  strange-gave  me  so  strong  a  sense  of  the  exactness  of  the  soul-and-sentinel  simile,  which  had  not  even  come  to  mind  for  years,  that  I  felt  almost  exultant  at  the  revelation.  Of  course,  the  dawn  did  come,  but  not  so  much  with  light  as  with  song,  the  songs  of  the  birds-birds  of  this  other  land,  whose  appearances  I  did  not  know  but  whose  throats  I  knew,  singing  out  of  their  alien  little  bodies  the  universal  song.  First  one  bird,  startling  me,  so  that  I  shifted  the  rifle  and  wondered  whether  the  human  cords  of  my  enemy  could  vibrate  to  that  pitch  and  timbre.  There  was  no  light  that  I  could  make  out  then,  and  this  harbinger  sang  and  was  silent,  so  that  I  waited  upon  the  edge  of  his  silence  for  him  to  sing  again.  He  sang  again,  was  silent,  sang  again,  was  joined  by  another;  both  were  silent,  both  sang  again.  As  the  sky  lightened  in  what  I  knew  now  was  east,  the  songs  swelled  and  joined,  so  that  there  was  one  song,  and  I  cannot  say  how  moving  it  was  to  hear  these  creatures  reach  back  and  over,  announcing  their  sameness  with  the  blue  jays  and  cardinals  of  my  boyhood,  of  my  own  land-raising  an  incredible  roar  of  melody,  hidden  in  the  trees  and  bushes,  so  that  trees  and  bushes  seemed  to  be  singing,  the  world  was  metamorphosed  into  song.  My  companion  said,  now  that  we  could  be  seen  better  than  heard,  &quot;Did  you  ever  hear  so  many  birds?&quot;  I  re-  garded  this  as  a  remarkable  statement,  a  corroboration  of  my  own  feelings,  a  perceptive  interpretationof  the  event,  and  I  beamed  at  him.  As  long  as  we  were  there,  we  were  beaten  upon  by  song,  fairly  assailed.  We  pushed  our  way  through  song  as  we  walked,  and  the  notes  rifled  apart  like  confetti  before  us.  When  our  relief  came,  I  delighted  in  showing  my  post,  and  I  set  off  back  to  my  tank  with  a  cer-  tain  feeling  of  regret  to  magnify  my  joy  at  going,  as  gain  is  always  set  off,  point-  ed  up,  by  an  element  of  loss. What  I  best  remember  arethe  in-  frequent  occasions  when  I  was  recollect,  ed  for  sleep.  Sometimes,  when  our  com-  pany  was  in  reserve,  we  backed  and  wheeled  through  what  must  have  been  covering  movements  against  the  pos-  sibility  of  defeat.  From  our  view,  they  were  reprieves-stays  in  the  execution  of  the  general  sentence  ofpain  and  death  that  hung  over  all  of  us.  Incredulously,  we  turned  our  tanks  away  from  the  places  where  our  enemy  waited,  and  headed  in  wide  circles  through  quiet  lands  inhabited  by  peace.  When  night  came,  we  halted,  switched  off  our  en-  gines,  and,  if  we  were  far  enough  away  from  the  lines,  dismounted.  As  each  en-  gine  stopped,  the  level  of  the  clamor  about  us  fell  by  another  degree,  until,  sound  climbing  down  rung  by  rung  into  silence,  there  was  at  length  only  the  murmur  of  our  voices  and  movements  and  of  the  land  itself.  It  was  as  if  we  had  been  projected  out  of  a  cave  into  the  open  air,  or  from  a  storm  into  a  sudden  calm,  or  from  anger  into  delight.  The  world  fell  upon  our  senses  in  a  flash,  and  all  we  could  do  at  the  beginning  was  to  look  dumbly  at  it  and  at  each  other.  Conversation  faltered,  and  our  move-  ments  were  stilled.  We  admired  what  we  had  wholly  forgotten  there  was  to  admire.  On  one  of  these  nights,  my  squad  had  been  posted  on  a  plateau  where  the  air  was  not  so  rank  as  in  the  jungle  below  and  the  cover  not  so  heavy.  After  the  shock  of  silence  hadgone  and  our  talk  had  picked  up  again,  we  began  to  occupy  ourselves  with  eating  and  with  servicing  our  equipment.  We  found  ways  to  be  kind  to  one  another.  As  the  darkness  became  deeper,  we  laid  our  beds  beside  our  tanks,  arranging  them  gently  and  carefully.  We  settled  upon  them,  those  of  us  who  were  not  on  watch,  speaking  softly  to  our  neighbors,  watching  the  sky  above,  listening  to  our  pulses  beating  in  our  ears.  There  was  no  moon,  but  for  some  reason  the  sky  was  as  bright  as  if  the  sun  were  just  over  a  hill.  There  was  a  faint  breeze.  I  remember  particularly  my  wakefulness  just  before  I  went  to  sleep  that  night.  Quite  alert,  I  con-  templated  the  sleep  that  was  coming,  turning  it  over  in  my  mind  and  admir-  ing  the  possibilities  of  dreaming.  Down  one  ofthe  corridors  of  sleep  were  my  people-my  father  and  mother,  uncles,  aunts,  cousins,  my  girl  friend-gathered  in  a  solemn  semicircle,  standing,  or  sit-  ting  on  chairs  or  cross-legged  on  the  ground,  waiting  to  welcome  me,  Down  another  was  my  childhood,  the  games  and  ghosts  of  that  time  gone  by,  the  poor  confusions  and  sorrows,  arrayed   like  scenery  flats,  one  behind  the  other.  Down  another  were  the  years  ahead,  figures  of  peace  and  opulence,  power  turned  to  virtuous  ends,  courage  re-  warded  not  immoderately  but  in  meas-  ure,  with  due  regard  for  the  realities.  When  I  fell  asleep,  I  chose  to.  Eventually,  my  sergeant  shook  me  by  the  shoulder  to  rouse  me  for  my  watch.  I  got  up  gladly  this  time  and picked  up  my  carbine.  A  mist  had  risen,  lying  lightly  where  we  were  but  below  us  lacing  the  trees  thickly  in  heavy  streamers.  I  walked  among  the  tanks,  making  no  noise,  seeing  the  other  men  where  they  lay  securely  sleeping,  the  mist  passing  my  face  with  a  light  touch  of  moistness.  There  was  no  breeze  at  all  now,  but  the  cooler  night  air  felt  good  anyway.  I  walked  rhythmically,  lulling  myself  with  the  movement  of  my  feet,  singing  in  my  throat,  &quot;I  Saw  Three  Ships.&quot;  My  thoughts  soared  away  from  the  place  where  I  was. In  an  instant,  their  image  in  my  mind  had  shifted  so  that  I  could  not  help  believing   that  I  was  seeing  them  now  as  they  had  always  been,  so  that  I  recalled  them  from  my  earliest  youth  as  they  were  now  -the  same  marks  of  age  in  my  father's  flesh  when  he  taught  me  the  mystery  of  balancing  a  bike,  my  moth-  er's  hair  as  faded  when  she  held  me,  too  big  to  be  kept  there  longer,  high  in  her  arms  for  a  moment  to  see  a  Fourth  of  July  parade  over  the  heads  of  the  crowd.  Now  my  mother  raised  her  hand  shyly  in  the  same  gesture  of  greeting  that  had  followed  every  other  parting.  My  father  removed  his  hat.  Someone  jostled  me,  and  I  took  this  as  the  signal  to  move  toward  them,  but  I  could  not  seem  to  pick  my  bag  up.  I  got  it  halfway  up,  and  it  slipped,  and  I  had  to  try  again.  I  finally  started  to-  ward  them.  They  had  waited  for  me,  I  was  thinking.  With  all  the  plans  of  the  world's  millions  of  people,  these  two  had  intersected  with  me  once  again,  as  they  had  for  all  my  life.  What  call  did  I  have  upon  them  that  they  should  work   their  intricate  ways  so  as  to  be  here  now?  Why  weren't  they  doing  some-  thing  else-my  father  at  his  office,  my  mother  at  home?  And  what  in  myself  had  brought  me  here  at  this  time,  after  all  the  death  and  suffering?  With  my  eyes  on  them,  I  neverthe-  less  was  thinking  not  only  about  them  but  about  all  the  layers  of  belief  and  action,  all  the  complex  interactions,  all  the  justice  and  injustice,  the  cruelty  and  kindness  in  me  and  in  all  men,  at  peace  or  at  war.  And  I  thought  that  these  elements  were  bound  together,  so  that  every  motion  in  one  part  was  rippled  to  the  others,  and  I  was  sorry  for  me  and  for  my  parents  and,  equally,  proud  and  loving.  I  could  feel  the  whole  army  getting  off  that  train  with  me  and,  wait-  ing,  all  the  old  people  and  the  children  of  the  world,  the  Red  Cross  ladies  and  the  girl  friends,  and  the  dead  waiting,  too,  and  as  we  met  we  spoke  not  a  word  of  what  had  gone  before  but  made  vows  of  compassion."
