A  wind  also  had  risen  outside,  and  it  blew  through  the  key-  holes.  She  hugged  him.  &quot;Yes,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;'Take  me  away  from  here  in  the  morn-  ing.&quot;  &quot;We'll  leave  first  thing,&quot;  he  said  reassuringly.  Two  or  three  times  that  night  he  woke  up  and  struck  a  match  and  looked  at  her  to  make  sure  she  was  breathing.  IN  the  morning,  he  helped  her  down  the  stairs,  and  they  started  off.  Even  as  they  left  the  shore  and  went  inland,  a  sense  of  safety  came  over  him,  a  feel-  ing  that  she  would  recover.  They  reached  thetop  of  the  moun-  tain  in  time  for  the  nine-o'clock  bus.  He  looked  down  with  sadness  at  the  tower,  which  for  the  first  time  and  in  his  great-  est  need  had  been  inhospitable  to  him.  He  took  her  to  Florence.  A  good  thing,  too,  for  her  illness  was  diagnosed  as  typhoid.  In  a  hospital  there,  she  was  given  chloromycetin,  and  after  a  few  weeks  she  recovered.  When  she  came  out,  their  feeling  for  each  other  wasn't  the  same.  Regretful-  ly,  sadly,  they  both  acknowledged  it.  He  left  Florence  and  went  home  with  a  grudge  in  his  heart-a  grudge  not  against  her  or  her  husband  but  against  whoever  ordained  things;  a  moralist,  maybe."
