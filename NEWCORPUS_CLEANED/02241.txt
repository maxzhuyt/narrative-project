Those  were  the  very  words  the  priest  used,  because  he  de-  scribed  the  incident  in  detail  to  my  mother  the  day  she  had  Mass  said  in  our  house.  &quot;Marriage,  Father,  is  out  of  the  question,&quot;  said  Jack.  &quot;I  was  be-  trothed  for  a  long  number  of  years  to  a  certain  little  lady  in  this  parish,  who  jilted  me. When  told  of  this,  Jack  said  he  would  get  the  sergeant  to  deal  with  them,  but  whenever  the  tinkers  came  they  did  not  sleep  out  in  the  fields,  as  they  had  before,  but  used  the  shelter  of  the  house.  The  following  winter,  Jack  came  down  with  shin-  gles  and  would  open  his  shop  only  at  odd  hours,  when  it  suited  him.  As  I  got  older,  I  thought  of  Jack-of  how  embarrassed  he  must  have  been,  and  of  how  callous  I  must  have  seemed.  I  wanted  to  talk  to  him  and  somehow  to  make  amends.  One  Easter,  when  I  was  home  from  [REDACTED]  for  a  visit,  I  said  to  my  mother  that  I  ought  to  speak  to  Jack.  My  mother  warned  me  that  he  was  very  peculiar,  and  that  if  I  went  to  the  shop  he  would  not  let  me  past  the  door  but  would  drive  me  out,  as  he  had  done  her. There  he  was,  monopolizing  the  aisle  seat  of  a  pew  and  scowling  when  he  had  to  let  others  in.  He  was  bald  and  stooped,  and  heprayed  feverishly  on  his  black  horn  rosary  beads.  His  fingers  could  have  been  an  old  wom-  an's,  so  small  were  they,  so  gnarled.  The  chapel  itself  seemed  a  smaller  and  humbler  place,  and  I  thought  of  the  missioners  and  the  terrorof  their  ser-  mons.  The  parish  priest  was  very  slow,  and  at  times  hesitated  as  if  he  had  forgotten  the  words.  At  the  Last     Gospel,  Jack  jumped  upand  left  on  tiptoe.  Forewarned,  I,  too,  got  up,  genu-  flected,  and  left.  He  hurried  out  under  the  cypress  trees  andopened  the  church  gate  but  did  not  wait  to  close  it,  and  let  it  clang.  &quot;Jack,  Jack,&quot;  I  called  softly.  I  could  not  shout,  lest  it  be  disrespectful  to  the  proceedings  in  the  church.  I  closed  the  gate  and  walked  quickly,  all  the  while  calling,  but  he  pretended  not  to  hear.  He  took  great  strides,  and  it  was  clear  that  he  was  avoiding  me.  I  caught  up  with  him  as  he  reached  his  own  shop  door  and  was  pushing  it  in.  &quot;Jack,&quot;  Isaid.  &quot;It's  lovely  to  see  you  again.&quot;  He  must  have  heard,  but  he  did  not  turn.  He  stepped  inside  the  shop,  pushed  the  door  shut,  and  immediately  drew  a  bolt.  I  stood  at  the  door,  think-  ing  that  he  would  change  his  mind.  The  gray  gauze  blind  that  had  once  had  the  name  &quot;Vintner&quot;  printed  on  it  was  like  a  filament.  Any  minute  it  might  disintegrate.  I  tapped  and  tapped,  but  he  did  not  relent.  Indeed,  I  do  not  know  if  he  stood  there  vacil-  lating  or  if  he  had  gone  on  tiptoe  into  the  kitchen  and  was  brewing  a  cup  of  tea.  HEN  Jack  died,  he  left  his  premises  to  a  cousin  whom  we  had  never  seen  and  scarcely  knew  of.  His  gloom  had  cut  him  off  and  his  friends  would  not  call  on  him  until  they  came  as  mourners.  My  mother  was  eager  to  hear  what  he  did  with  his  possessions,  and,  realizing  that  he  had  not  remembered  us  at  all,  she  said  she  was  genuinely  surprised  that  he  hadn't  left  her  a  decanter  ora  biscuit  barrel.  She  brooded  on  it  and  recited  the  motto  aboutblood  being  thicker  than  water.
