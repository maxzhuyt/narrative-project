 CHILD,  I  KNOW  YOURE  GOING  TO  MISS  ME  S  a  matter  of  historical  fact  I  know  that  I  was  born  in  Douglas  Street,  Cork,  over  a  small  sweet-and-tobacco  shop  kept  by  a  mid-  dle-aged  lady  named  Wall,  but  my  memory  has  nothing  to  do  with  living  in  Douglas  Street.  My  memories  begin  in  Blarney  Street,  which  we  used  to  call  Blarney  Lane,  because  it  follows  the  track  of  an  old  lane  from  Cork  to  Blarney.  It  begins  at  the  footof  Shan-  don  Street,  near  the  riverbank,  in  sor-  didness,  and  ascends  the  hill  to  some-  thing  like  squalor.  No.  251,  where  we  lived,  is  one  of  the  cottages  near  the  top  on  the  right,  though  I  realize  now  that  it  would  be  more  properly  described  as  a  cabin,  for  it  contained  nothing  but  a  tiny  kitchen  and  a  tiny  bedroom  with  a  loft  above  it.  For  this  we  paid  two  and  sixpence-sixty  cents-a  week.  Up  here  we  were  just  on  the  edge  of  the  country,  and  behind  the  house  were  high,  windy  fields  that  are  now  all  built  over.  A  hundred  yards  farther  up,  the  country  properbegan,  and  there  asteep  lane  called  Strawberry  Hill  de-  scended  into  the  classy  quarter  of  Sun-  day's  Well.  The  Women's  Prison  was  at  the  footof  this  lane,  where  it  turned  into  Convent  Avenue,  and  beside  the  Women's  Prison  was  the  Good  Shep-  herd  Convent.  The  convent  had  a  peni-  tentiary  for  &quot;fallen&quot;  women  and  an  orphanage,  and  it  was  in  the  orphan-  age  that  Mother  had  been  brought  up.  At  the  foot  of  Convent  Avenue,  just  on  the  left,  was  a  house  where  Mother  had  been  a  maid  for  eight  years  with  a  family  called  Barry,  and  where  she  had  been  happier  than  at  any  other  time  in  her  life.  To  the  right  was  a  shop  the  owner  of  which  had  once  wanted  to  marry  her.  All  these  places  were  full  of  significance  for  me-the  convent  because  my  moth-  er  and  I  often  visited  it  to  see  Mother  Blessed  Margaret  and  Mother  of  Per-  petual  Succour,  who  were  my  mother's  friends  there,  the  Barrys'  house  because  of  the  elegance  of  the  life  that  Mother  described  having  experienced  in  it,  and  the  shop  because  of  a  slight  feeling  of  resentment  at  the  thought  that  if  Moth-  er  had  only  been  sensible  I  should  have  had  a  pretty  elegant  life  myself.  That  was  the  exalted  end  of  Blarney  Lane.  At  the  other  end  it  descended  to  the  river  and  across  the  bridge  to  the  North  Main  Street,  where  Mother  took  me  shopping,  and  beyond  the  North  Main  Street,  over  another  bridge,  to  Douglas  Street,  where  I  was  born,  and  where  my  mother's  brother,  Tim  O'Connor,  had  a  cobbler's  shop  across  the  wharf  from  Miss  Wall.  My  memo-  ries  of  the  cobbler's  shop  are  hazy;  I  can  remember  my  uncle  only  when  he  was  dying  in  the  [REDACTED]  Infirmary  of  dysen-  tery  he  had  contracted  in  the  Boer  War;  and  yet  I  seem  to  have  a  very  vivid  recollectionof  him-tall,  thin,  and  fair-haired,  unlike  Mother,  who  had  very  dark  hair-because  he  seemed  to  be  always  gay.  One  of  the  things  I  have  inherited  from  my  mother's  side  of  the  family  is  a  passion  for  gaiety.  I  do  not  have  it  myself-I  seem  to  take  more  after  my  father's  family,  which  was  brooding,  melancholy,  and  vio-  lent-but  I  love  gay  people  and  books  and  music,  Not  that  Tim  had  much  to  be  gay  about;  his  wife,  as  I  remember  her,  was  coarse  and  jealous,  and  disliked  Moth-  er's  politeness  and  gentleness,  while  Mother  never  ceased  to  resentthe  hys-  terical  scenes  Annie  O'Connor  made  over  Tim's  grave.  Mother  disliked  and  distrusted  any  form  of  demonstrative-  ness.  On  the  other  hand,  Tim  had  ob-  jected  to  Mother's  marrying  my  father,  Michael  O'Donovan.  The  two  men  had  been  in  the  Army  together.  They  were  stationed  at  Charles  [REDACTED],  near  Kin-  sale.  Tim's  girl,  Annie,  and  Mother  visited  Tim  together,  and  Mother  re-  turned  to  visit  Father.  Though  Tim  and  Father  were  friends  and  drinking  companions,  Tim  told  him  he  wasn't  good  enough  for  Mother-and  Father,  to  give  him  his  due,  did  not  hold  it  against  him.  Certainly  neither  Tim  nor  my  mother  had  much  to  boast  of  in  their  marriage.  Maybe  there  is  about  these  men  and  women  of  Mozartean  tem-  perament  a  certain  unworldliness  that  makes  them  get  the  worst  of  any  bar-  gain. The  Cork  bands  were  div.ded  into  sup-  porters  of  WilliamO'Brien  and  sup-  porters  of  John  Redmond,  two  rival  Irish  politicians  with  little  to  distinguish  them  except  their  personalitie-flam-  boyant  in  O'Brien  and  frigid  in  Red-  mond.  The  Blackpool  Band  was  an  O'Brienite  group,  and  our  policy  was  &quot;Conciliation  and  Consent.&quot;  The  Red-  mond  supporters  we  called  Molly  Ma-  guires,  and  I  have  forgotten  what  their  policy  was-if  they  had  one.  Our  na-  tional  anthem  was  &quot;God  Save  Ireland&quot;  and  theirs  &quot;A  Nation  Once  Again.&quot;  I  was  often  filled  with  pity  for  the  poor  degraded  children  of  the  Molly  Ma-  guires,  who  paraded  the  streets  with  tin  cans,  singing  (to  the  tune  of  &quot;John  Brown's  Body&quot;)  &quot;We'll  Hang  Wil-  liam  O'Brien  on  a  Sour  Apple  Tree.&quot;  Sometimes  passion  overcame  me  to  such  an  extent  that  I  got  a  tin  can  of  my  own  and  paraded  up  and  down  singing  &quot;We'll  Hang  Johnny  Redmond  on  a  Sour  Apple  Tree.&quot;  The  bandsmen  shared  our  attitudes.  There  were  frequent  fights,  and  during  election  times  Father  came  home  with  a  drumstick  up  his  sleeve-a  useful  weapon  if  he  was  attacked  by  Molly  Maguires.  There  were  even  more  seri-  ous  incidents.  Bandsmen  raided  a  rival  band  room  and  smashed  up  the  instru-  ments,  and  one  of  Father's  most  gloomy  sons  l'sted  some  of  the  men  who  had  Jone  this:  Creedy,  Reidy,  Dessy  and  Snell,  Not  juging  their  souls,  they're  already  in  Hell.  The  night  of  the  battle  we'll  show  them  some  fun;  We'll  hang  up  the  ruffian  who  stole  our  big  drum.  Almost  all  the  bandsmen  were  ex-  bandsmen  of  the  British  Army,  as  Fa-  ther  was,  and  I  think  it  may  havebeen  something  ofa  tragedy  to  them  that  when  once  they  had  returned  to  Cork,  music  became  less  important  than  the  political  faction  for  whom  one  made  it.  Father  loved  the  policy  and  personality  of  William  O'Brien,  who  had  married  the  daughter  of  one  of  the  great  Fran-  co-Jewish  bankers.  Once,  when  there  were  threats  of  a  Molly  attack,  Father,  an  enormously  powerful  man,  had  act-  ed  as  bodyguard  for  William  O'  and  William  O'  had  thanked  him  and  hand-  ed  him  a  pound  note;  but  for  several  years  Father  had  been  thebig  drummer  of  a  Molly  band.  It  was  a  superb  band,  and  Father  liked  music  so  well  that  he  preferred  it  to  politics.  For  the  sake  of  the  music  he  even  endured  the  indig-  nity  of  playing  for  John  Redmond.  Naturally,  whenever  he  attended  a  THE  NEW  YORKER  demonstration  at  which  Wil-  liam  O'  was  criticized,  he  withdrew,  like  a  good  Catho-  lic  from  a  heretical  service.  What  induced  him  to  leave  the  Molly  band  and  join  the  Blackpool  Band  I  do  not  know.  It  was  a  period  that  for  some  reason  he  never  liked  to  talk  about,  and  I  sus-  pect  that  someone  in  the  band  must  have  impugned  his  hon-  or  by  calling  him  a  turncoat.  That  is  the  sort  of  thing  that  would  have  broken  his  spirit,  for  he  was  a  proud  man  and  a  high-principled  one,  though  what  his  principles  were  based  on  was  more  than  I  ever  knew.  He  would  not  permit  a  slighting  reference  to  Wil-  liam  O',  and  reading  the  Echo,  the  only  evening  pa-  per  in  Cork,  and  a  Molly  one,  was  as  much  a  torment  as  a  pleasure  to  him.  &quot;Oh,  listen  to  George  Crosbie,  the  dirty  little  caffier!&quot;  he  would  cry  with  mortification.  In  days  when  no  one  else  seemed  to  worry  about  it,  he  was  a  firm  believer  in  buying  Irish  manufactures,  and  often  sent  me  back  to  the  shop  with  a  box  of  English  matches  that  had  been  passed  off  on  me.  He  was  a  passionate  supporter  of  Jim  Larkin,  the  Irish  la-  bor  leader,  and  for  months  when  he  was  out  on  strike  we  practically  didn't  eat.  According  to  Mother,  there  was  a  period  in  my  infancy  when  Father  didn't  drink  for  two  years.  He  had  drunk  himself  penniless,as  usual,  and  some  oldfriend  had  refused  him  a  loan.  The  slight  had  cut  him  so  deep  that  he  stopped  drinking  at  once.  A  pity  he  hadn't  a  few  more  friends  of  the  sort!  It  was  no  joke  to  go  with  Father  on  one  of  his  Sunday  outings  with  the  band,  and  I  often  kicked  up  hell  about  it,  but  Mother  liked  me  to  go,  because  she  had  some  strange  notion  that  I  could  restrain  Father  from  drinking  too  much.  Of  course,  I  was  fond  of  music,  and  proud  of  Father,  as,  with  the  drum  slung  very  high  about  his  neck,  he  glanced  left  and  right  of  it,  waiting  to  give  the  three  taps  that  brought  the  bandsmen  in.  He  was  a  drummer  ofthe  classical  type;  he  hated  to  see  a  man  carrying  his  drum  on  his  belly  instead  of  on  his  chest,  and  he  had  nothing  but  scorn  for  the  showy  drum-  mers  who  swung  or  crossed  their  sticks.  He  was  almost  disappointingly  unpre-  tentious.  When  Father  was  on  the  drink,  he  was  so  uncertain  that  I  always  had  the  feeling  that  one  day  he'd  lose  me  and  forget  I  had  beenwith  him.  Usually  the  band  would  end  their  piece  in  front  of  a  pub  at  the  corner  of  Coburg  Street.  The  pubs  were  always  closed  on  Sunday  until  after  the  last  Mass,  and  when  they  opened  it  was  only  for  an  hour  or  two.'The  last  notes  of  &quot;Brian  Boru's  March&quot;  would  hardly  have  been  played  when  Father  would  unsling  the  drum,  thrust  it  on  the  young  fellows  who  had  the  job  of  carrying  it,  and  dash  across  the  road  to  the  pub,  accompanied  by  John  P.,  his  great  buddy.  John  P.-  I  never  knew  what  his  surname  was-  was  a  long  string  of  misery,  with  an  air  of  unutterable  gravity.  He  was  one  of  the  people  vaguely  known  as  &quot;fol-  lowers  of  the  band&quot;-a  group  of  lone-  ly  souls  who  gave  some  significance  to  their  miserable  lives  by  attaching  them-  selves  to  the  band.  They  endlessly  dis-  cussed  its  policies  and  personalities,  looked  after  the  instruments,  and  knew  every  public  house  in  Cork  that  would  risk  entertaining  the  band  during  the  hours  when  they  were  closed.  John  P.,  looking  like  Einstein  after  be  first  en-  tertained  the  idea  of  relativity,  would  give  a  secret  knock  on  the  door  of  the  pub  and  utter  what  seemed  to  be  whis-  pered  endearments  through  the  key-  hole,  and  more  and  more  bandsmen  would  join  the  group  peppering  outside,  while  messengers  rushed  up  to  them  shouting  &quot;Come  on,  can't  ye,  come  on!  The  bloomin'  train  will  be  gone.&quot;  And  then  it  would  be  the  seaside  at  Aghada,  or  Crosshaven,  or  the  grounds  of  Blarney  Castle,  and  in  the  intervals  of  playing  the  band  would  sit  in  various  public  houses  with  the  doors  barred,  and  if  I  was  inside  I  couldn't  get  out,  and-  what  was  much  worse  for  a  shy  small  boy-if  I  was  out  I  couldn't  get  in.  It  was  all  very  boring  and  sometimes  alarming,  and  I  remember  once  at  Blarney,  in  my  discouragement,  staking  my  last  penny  on  a  dice  game  called  the  Harp,  Crown,  and  Feather  in  the  hope  of  retrieving  a  wasted  day.  Being  a  pa-  triotic  child,  with  something  of  Father's  high  principle,  I  put  my  money  on  the  national  emblem,  and  lost.  'This  was  prophetic,  because  since  then  I  have  lost  a  great  many  pennies  on  the  national  54  emblem,  but  it  cured  me  of  the  more  obvious  forms     of  gambling  for  the  rest  of     my  days.  On  another  oc-     casion,  after  a  Jong  day  in  Crosshaven,  I  found  my-  self  outside  a  locked  pub-  lic  house  in  Cork  by  the  North  Cathedral  late  at  night,  waiting  for  some  drunk  to  emerge,  so  that  I  could  stick  my  head  in  the  door  and  wail,  &quot;Daddy,  won't  you  come  now?&quot;  At  last,  in  despair,  I  decided  to  make  my  own  way  home  through  the  dark  streets,  though  I  had  never  been  out  alone  at  night  before  this.  I  crept  down  the  sinister  length  of  Shandon  Street  in  terror,  and  crossed  the  street  to  escapeseeing  what  I  might  see  by  the  old  graveyard,  and  then,  at  the  foot  of  Blarney  Lane,  I  saw  a  tiny  shop  still  open.  'There  were  steps  up  to  the  hall  door,  and  railings  round  the  area,  and  the  shopwindow  was  small  and  high  and  barely  lighted  by  the  one  oil  lamp  in-  side,  but  I  could  plainly  see  a  toy  dog  in  it.  I  prayed  that  it  would  not  be  beyond  my  means  and  climbed  the  steps.  Inside,  a  door  on  the  right  led  to  the  shop,  where  the  counter  was  higher  than  my  head. FATHER  had  been  brought  up  in  the  neighborhood  of  Cork  Barracks,  a  mile  or  two  away  on  the  other  side  of  the  town,  and  his  family  still  lived  there.  For  this  neighborhood  he  seemed  to  pine  as  an  Irish  immigrant  in  Brook-  lyn  is  supposed  to  pine  for  Galway  Bay,  though,  unlike  the  [REDACTED]  im-  migrant,  Father  meant  it.  He  used  to  take  me  to  my  grandparents'  house  in  Harrington's  Square-an  uneven  piece                 of  ground,  between  the  Old  Youghal  Road  and  the  Ballyhooley  Road,  that  seemed  to  have  been  abandoned  by  God  and  was  certainly  abandoned  by  the  Cork  Corporation.  In  [REDACTED]  such  areas  are  politely  known  as  &quot;non-  adopted,&quot;  a  word  that  well  suits  their  orphaned  air.  It  was  inhabited  largely  by  washerwomen  who  worked  mainly  for  the  British  officers  in  Cork  Bar-  racks,  and  there  were  three  sets  of  iron  poles  in  the  middle  of  the  square  to  support  their  lines.  One  set  belonged  to  my  grandmother,  a  stout,  coarse  peasant  woman,  who  flopped  about  the  floor  in  bare  feet  because  these  were  what  she  was  used  to  and  the  boots  still  con-  tinued  to  give  her  trouble.  She  had  a  pronounced  Mongolian  look,  and  the  protrusion  of  the  brows  and  the  high  cheekbones  gave  her  a  constant  look  of  peering  at  things.  With  it  went  a  curious  shrugging  of  the  shoulders,  which  I  never  noticed  again  till  I  saw  it  in  an  eminent  Irish  writer  and  traced  it  down  to  a  common  dislike  of  soap  and  water.  I  remember  little  of  my  grandfather,  a  quiet,  bearded  old  man.  My  father  had  a  sister  who  was  a  deaf-mute,  and  dur-  ing  the  early  part  of  my  childhood  I  met  her  only  once  or  twice,  when  she  was  home  on  holidays  from  Glasgow,  where  she  lived  with  her  husband-another  deaf-mute-a  tailor  named  Hanlon. One  day,  two  nuns  came  to  the  house  in  a  covered  car,  and  Mother  was  ordered  to  return  to  the  orphanage.  She  refused,  and  the  nuns  reminded  her  of  the  penalty  she  was  incurring.  Any  girl  who  without  permission  left  one  of  the  pleasant  homes  provided  by  the  nuns  was  not  allowed  to  return  to  the  orphanage,  which  was  the  only  home  most  of  the  girls  had.  In  the  sameway,  one  who  refused  to  leave  when  ordered  to  was  not  allowed  to  return.  Mother  still  refused  to  go  back  with  them,  and  when  they  left  in  anger,  she  knew  she  had  now  no  place  in  the  world  to  go  to.  When  I  tried  to  get  her  to  explain  this  extraordinary  conduct,  she  said,  almost  impatiently,  that  she  could  not  go  back  in  that  state  among  clean,  well-dressed  girls.  Possibly  there  was  an  element  of  almost  insane  vanity  behind  her  refusal  to  return  to  the  convent,  but  that  cannot  be  the  real  explanation.  My  own  guess  is  that  it  was  despair,  rather  than  vanity.  I  think  that  subconsciously  she  hated  the  nuns  for  what  they  had  made  of  her  innocent  life,  and,  without  being  aware  of  it,  had  already  decided  to  commit  suicide.  Her  parents  were  dead,  Mar-  garet  had  died  while  Mother  was  at  the  Bowens,  Tim  she  had  seen  only  once  for  a  few  hours  in  all  the  years,  and  she  had  nothing  left  to  livefor.  OR  eight  or  nine  months  longer,  it  dragged  on  like  that.  Even  Kath-  leen  took  pity  on  her,  and  helped  her  when  her  mother  wasn't  looking.  But  one  winter  day  Joyce  came  in  at  one  o'clock  for  his  dinner  and  it  wasn't  ready.  His  wife  ordered  Mother  out  of  the  house.  She  put  off  the  apron  she  had  been  wearing,  put  on  her  black  straw  hat,  threw  the  ragged  old  coatee  over  her  shoulders-the  hat  and  jacket  were  all  the  possessions  she  had  in  the  world-and  went  out  onto  Mulgrave  Road.  She  saw  people  stop  and  stare  at  her,  and  realized  the  extraordinary  fig-  ure  she  cut.  She  ran  up  a  laneway  by  the  North  Infirmary  and  left  the  ragged  jacket  there,  but  people  still  continued  to  look.  She  ran  for  shelter  to  the  nearest  church,  the  Dominican  Church  on  the  Sand  Quay,  and  prayed.  She  knew  now  that  only  one  hope  remained  to  her,  and  that  a  miracle.  None  of  the  nuns-not  even  her  favor-  ite,  Mother  Blessed  Margaret-could  overrule  the  Mistress  of  Studies,  and  if  she  went  to  the  orphanage  she  would  be  turned  away.  She  knew  too  many  to  whom  it  had  happened.  The  only  one  who  could  overrule  the  Mistress  of  Studies  was  Reverend  Mother.  It  was  she  who  had  arranged  my  grand-  mother's  funeral.  But  lay  sisters,  not  Reverend  Mothers,  answer  convent  doorbells,  and  from  one  o'clock  until  darkness  fell  she  waited,  most  of  the  time  on  her  knees  praying  for  a  miracle.  She  had  decided  that  if  the  miracle  did  not  happen,  she  would  return  to  the  river  and  drown  herself.  It  was  only  when  she  was  telling  me  about  this  period  of  her  life  that  I  ever  heard  her  use  such  an  expression  in  any  matter  that  concerned  herself,  for  not  only  did  she  believe  suicide  was  wrong,  she  thought  it  demonstrative,  and  she  was  almost  fiercely  undemonstrative  in  grief  or  pain.  Nor,  when  she  talked  of  that  afternoon,  as  an  old  woman,  did  she  make  it  sound  particularly  significant.  Father  and  I,  with  our  deep  streak  of  melancholia,  would  have  added  some-  thing  to  it  that,  by  making  it  seem  dra-  matic,  would  also  have  made  it  seem  less  terrible.  It  is  an  awful  moment  when  gaiety  dies  in  those  who  have  no  other  approach  to  life. She  never  made  much  of  her  own  misery.  Other  girls,  as  she  said,  had  had  a  worse  time.  But  she  never  ceased  to  speak  of  what  happened  as  a  miracle,  and,  in  the  way  of  those  to  whom  miracles  occur,  never  by  so  much  as  a  harsh  word  attempted  to  blame  the  Mis-  tress  of  Studies.  I  feel  sure  it  was  sig-  nificant  that  when,  a  week  later,  the  Mistress  of  Studies  found  her  another  nice  home,  in  a  public  house  off  Blarney  Lane  that  cattle  dealers  used  for  lodging,  Mother,  without  unpacking  her  bag,  returned  to  the  convent  and  told  the  Mistress  of  Studies  that  it  was  not  a  suitable  place  for  a  young  woman.  It  may  also  be  significant  that,  a  few  days  later,  the  Mistress  of  Studies  was  re-  placed  by  Mother's  great  friend,  Moth-  er  Blessed  Margaret,  whom  I  knew  and  loved  when  she  was  an  old  lady. It  was  in  a  house  whose  gardens  reached  to  the  convent  wall,  and  the  house  was  occupied  by  a  butter-  and-egg  merchant  named  Barry  and  his  unmarried  sister,  Alice. Ned  Barry  was  a  man  of  about  forty,  tall  and  fat,  with  a  hooknose  and  high  color.
