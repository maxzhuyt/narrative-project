He  was  so  aware  that  his  wife  wondered  what  had  kept  him  alive,  how  he  managed  to  talk  or  work,  and  what  had  brought  him  to  her.  He  was  aware  of  shifts  of  mood,  rhythms  of  luck,  undertones,  contradictions  (the  way  flattery  can  estrange  or  humilia-  tion  addict,  the  way  a  moment  of  per-  fect  trust  can  trigger  a  groundless  suspicion  of  years).  He  was  aware  of  more  than  he  needed  or  could  use,  more  than  was  true,  even.  Mostly,  he  was  aware  ofdisastrous  possibilities.  He  dreaded  good  luck  for  its  backlash,  bad  luck  for  itself.  He  maintained  an  anxi-  ety  nearly  commensurate  with  all  the  dangers  of  life.  He  considered  all  of  life's  dangers  on  a  par-crossing  a  street,  catching  a  germ,  losing  a  friend,  going  to  war.  He  kept  his  mind  focussed  and  scan-  ning  those  dangers  like  a  radarscope.  Since  most  of  his  daily  dramas  were  trivial,  his  early-warning  system  was  slightly  crazed.  He  was  wired  to  regis-  ter  subtexts  as  seismic.  Death,  risk,  tragedy  on  a  higher  scale  hardly  trou-  bled  him  at  all.  In  his  childhood,  the  scanner  had  veered  him  way  off  course  and  turned  into  doubt  of  himself.  Lat-  er,  as  he  noticed  his  sense  of  things  con-  firmed,  as  he  saw  that  his  doubts  were  brought  on  in  fact  by  his  habit  of  notic-  ing  things  (he  remembered  nearly  all  conversations  word  for  word;  he  re-  played  them  endlessly,  with  inflections,  in  his  head),  he  thought  of  becoming  a  writer,  and  later  still  a  psychiatrist.  But  writing  was  entirely  false  to  the  reality  he  knew,  the  reversibility  of  his  truths;  and  psychiatry  left  too  little  room  for  luck,  for  the  superstitious  rhythms.  In  the  end,  looking  for  some  mental  floor  of  certainty  and  fighting  all  the  procliv-  ities  of  his  mind,  he  became  a  physicist.  Now,  at  thirty-five,  he  had  two  marriages,  four  children,  a  warm  and  ample  house,  success,  a  certain  wit.  His  first  wife's  tender  mercies  had  blown  up  in  his  face.  She  had  been,  like  him,  a  worrier  with  an  implicit  faith  that  fear  wards  things  off,  that  worry  can  shut  specific  dangers  out.  With  time,  their  contract  of  anxiety  had  turned  into  com-  bat-as  mortal  in  its  tentative-  ness  and  mutual  concern  as  the  wars  of  couples  who  wished  each  other  dead.  Their  vigil  over  each  other's  happiness  turned  into  literal  sleeplessness,  pure  dread  of  not  seeing  and  being  seen,  even  by  strangers  on  the  street.  They  scarcely  slept.  They  paced  each  other's  mental  wards.  The  competition  to  be  more  gentle,  more  helpful,  more  aware,  more  secretive  in  that  gentle-  ness  and  helpfulness  began  to  wear  them  down.  They  were  in  love  and  scared.  They  knew  it  and  they  joked  a  lot-about  unwatched  dangers  and  where  God  would  zero  in.  It  was  as  though  they  had  cased  the  joint  of  fate  and  struck  a  bargain:  to  be  com-  pletely  worried,  vigilant,  and  sad,  and  deflect  the  worst.  Since  there  was  a  certain  satisfaction  and  sense  of  safety  for  them  in  that  bargain,  even  it  was  void.  One  summer  afternoon,  a  child  they  met  gave  them  a  small,  round  dried  fish,  with  a  friendly-looking  face.  &quot;To  keep,&quot;  he  said.  They  kept  it,  talismanic,  on  the  dashboard  of  their  car.  Months  later,  they  drove  to  the  hospital  to  pick  up  a  friend  who  was  recovering  from  hepatitis.  They  stopped  at  an  intersec-  tion.  The  dead  fish  fell  into  the  sick  friend's  lap.  He  screamed  for  many  seconds.  &quot;Perhaps  we  shouldn't  see  peo-  ple,&quot;  Matt  said.  She  was  less  patient  than  he  and  less  level.
