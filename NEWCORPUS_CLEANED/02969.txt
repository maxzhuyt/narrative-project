 b"SHE  was  the  third  child  and  in  all  sorts  of  ways  lucky.  For  one  thing,  she  was  born  on  the  Sab-  bath  day  and  consequently  was  reputed  to  be  fair  and  wise  and  good  and  gay.  They  were  able  to  tell  her  in  later  life  that  she  was  a  model  baby,  had  no  faults,  had  contracted  no  fevers  except  such  harmless  things  as  measles  and  mumps.  The  time  she  went  to  be  vac-  cinated,  the  doctor  gave  her  a  little  gift-an  egg,  under  green  wire  mesh,  which  when  squeezed  revealed  a  small  canary-colored  chick.  She  played  and  played  with  it.  She  kept  it  at  right  in  the  bottom  layer  of  a  box  of  chocolates,  in  a  nest  made  of  chaff.  When  she  had  to  take  turpentine  for  the  tape-  worm,  they  put  it  on  a  loaf  of  sugar  and  it  was  not  like  medicine  at  all  but  like  a  treat;  having  sucked  the  ugly  taste  away,  she  used  to  let  the  cube  dis-  solve  in  her  mouth  and  savor  it,  slowly,  slowly. But  at  the  cookery  class  she  shone,  would  be  given  charge  of  the  fire,  and  she  was  the  first  to  taste  a  little  of  the  boxty,  ora  little  of  the  bubbling  jam  from  the  tart,  or  some  of  the  steamed  pudding,  or  what-  ever. She  never  went  out  without  bringing  something  back-a  small  packet  of  biscuits,  two  or  three  mush-  rooms,  an  item  of  news.  Her  name  was  Tess,  but  her  pet  name  was  Whitey  because  of  her  blond  hair,  and  when-  ever  her  father  came  in  from  the  fields  and  would  still  be  taking  off  his  boots  he  would  say,  &quot;Where's  Whitey?&quot;  and  upon  spotting  her-because  she  often  hid  behind  an  old  trenchcoat  hanging  on  the  scullery  door  or  ducked  down  be-  hind  one  of  the  sacks  of  flour-he  would  say,  &quot;There's  my  girl!&quot;  and  oftener  than  not  tell  her  how  her  foal  was  doing. She  had  a  maid  to  help  her,  and  a  big  tall  boy,  a  bit  soft  in  the  head,  to  dig  in  the  garden.  They  had  loganberries,  raspberries,  several  varieties  of  currant  bushes;  they  made  jam  in  season  and  bottled  fruits  in  the  autumn.  Her  parents  came  on  a  visit  the  first  Sun-  day  of  every  month.  Her  father  used  to  say  it  was  well  worth  the  fiver  that  the  hired  car  cost.  When  asked,  Tess  could  honestly  say  &quot;Can't  complain&quot;  about  any  aspect  of  her  life.  At  Christmas,  she  and  her  husband  threw  card  parties  for  their  men  friends,  and  though  she  never  played,  Tess  went  from  table  to  table,  looking  at  the  players'  hands  and  making  ob-  scure  but  meaningful  remarks  such  as  &quot;Not  bad,  Nicko&quot;  or  &quot;That'll  shake  'em.&quot;  At  midnight,  she  served  sup-  per-cold  meats,  stuffing,  sausage  rolls,  along  with  pots  of  teato  liven  them  up.  Her  father  would  sing  the  song  he  had  composed  about  the  doctor,  old  and  blind,  who  so  lovedcards  that  he  put  up  his  own  Arab  pony  and  lost  and  had  to  drag  the  trap  home  by  the  shafts,  where  his  complaining  wife,  Dilly,  met  him  at  the  top  of  the  drive.  The  song  brought  to  mind  the  old  days  and  various  card  games-tense  situations  where  men  at  the  very  last  minute  gambled  their  all,  where  tables  were  turned  upside  down  because  of  people's  cheating,  and  where  the  Irish-  speaking  teacher  convinced  everyone  that  he  was  the  Holy  Ghost  because  of  the  fine  hand  he  always  got:  aces  and  kings  and  knaves.  After  the  tea  and  the  singing,  Tess  would  pass  tumblers  of  hot  punch  around,  and  in  no  time  they  would  be  clearing  the  dishes  from  the  arbutus  table  in  order  to  start  again;  while  dealing,  her  father  might  remark  that  the  old  doctor  always  carried  a  pack  in  his  inside  pocket,  or  that  it  was  a  pity  youngsters  didn't  pursue  the  same  kind  of  sport  as  their  forebears.  The  children  would  come  in  in  their  pajamas  to  say  good  night,  and  sometimes  they  would  sing  a  carol-usually  &quot;Silent  Night-  and  they  might  be  asked  by  one  of  the  men  if  Santa  Claus  was  up  the  chim-  ney.  Then  Tess  would  dismiss  the  chil-  dren,  stoke  up  the  fire,  and  sit,  happily  surrounded  by  all  the  squabbling  men.  One  ofthe  younger  players  might  say  to  her  husband  what  a  lucky  man  he  was,  and  Tess  would  smile,  smile,  and  appear  not  to  be  affected.  The  prizes  for  the  winners  were  white  turkeys  that  would  be  hanging  by  their  feet  in  the  pantry-white  and  fluffy  and  unblem-  ished  except  for  the  spot  of  blood  around  the  throat  where  their  necks  had  been  joyously  wrung  by  the  idiot.  One  year,  her  husband  won,  and  Tess  did  the  sporting  thing-she  raffled  all  the  names  but  his.  The  card  games,  the  Bishop's  visits,  and  the  times  their  mammy  went  away        " b"to  have  a  new  baby  constituted  the  epochs  of  the  children's  lives.  They  were  wild  as  hares-got  lost  for  hours  on  end,  fell  off  their  bicycles,  or  were  off  up  the  country  watching  a  cow  calve  ora  cat  having  kittens.  They  would  bring  home  the  kittens  in  card-  board  boxes  and  make  pets  of  them-  paint  them  and  put  bows  in  their  hair-  until  the  kittens  would  have  to  be  drowned  or  put  in  a  sack  and  left  in  the  middle  of  nowhere.  Tess  was  not  an  animal-lover.  All  the  dogs  they  had-and  they  were  all  mongrels-  were  called  Biddy  regardless  of  their  sex,  and  invariably  died  of  the  same  thing,  which  was  distemper.  When  the  family  sat  down  at  table,  there  were  never  fewer  than  six  or  seven-sometimes  a  visiting  child,  and  sometimes  one  of  theirs  missing  or  on  vacation.  Tess  had  thehabit  of  calling  them  by  the  wrong  names,  as  if  all  their  names  were  in  her  head  jumbled  to-  gether  and  it  did  not  matter  too  much  which  was  which.  She  always  took  an  afternoon  nap,  and  then  the  children  would  have  to  tiptoe  about  the  house,    or  go  down  the  garden,  or  play  out  in  the  street,  and  her  favorite  son  would  be  appointed  to  wake  her  at  four.,  He  wrote  poems  for  her  and  left  them  on  her  dressing  table,  and  she  would  show  them  to  her  visitors  and  say  weren't  they  &quot;arch.&quot;  She  preferred  her  two  sons.  She  was  rarely  alone  with  any  of  the  children,  because  at  night  they  read  or  did  their  homework  or  listened  to  the  gramophone  in  their  various  dens.  Naturally,  she  provided  birthday  par-  ties,  and  as  the  years  went  on  they  re-  ceived  Holy  Communion  and  were  confirmed  and  the  sitting  room  was  full  of  framed-sometimes  colored-photo-  graphs  to  commemorate  the  occasions.  Her  daughters  did  not  resemble  her;  they  were  not  nearly  so  pretty.  Her  sons  had  her  coloring,  so  it  was  said.  Not  one  of  them  was  studious.  Her  daughters,  especially  her  elder  daugh-  ter,  Maria,  got  moodier  each  year  and  refused  to  eat  anything  except  steak  and  bananas.  Tess  said  she  would  have  them  in  the  workhouse  with  her  ex-  pensive  tastes.  Whenever  they  went  to  the  city  to  shop,  they  grumbled  be-cause  Maria  chose  dark,  rather  forlorn  clothes  and  Tess  would  say  it  was  no  way  for  a  young  person  to  dress.  Maria  referred  to  her  mother  as  the  Duchess.  It  was  a  joke  but  not  a  welcome  joke.  It  seemed  no  length  of  time  at  all  till  theeldest  was  due  to  go  away  to  school,  and  Tess  was  studying  prospec-  tuses,  buying  uniforms,  sewing  on  name  tapes,  packing  trunks.  If  a  child  cried  or  snivelled,  she  would  say,  &quot;  'Tis  well  for  ye.&quot;  In  the  same  way,  when  a  tink  er  or  a  horde  of  tinkers  passed  by  the  door  she  would  say,  &quot;Sure,  they're  hap-  pier  than  you  or  I,&quot;  and  close  the  shut-  ters  in  the  front  room  to  discourage  them  from  rapping  on  the  window-a  thing  they  did  when  the  doorbell  was  not  immediately  answered.  HE  first  little  cloud  to  appear  in  Tess's  life  came  when  she  turned  forty  and  began  to  put  on  weight.  She  developed  a  roll  of  fat  around  her  waist,  She  was  soon  having  to  have  her  dresses  let  out  and  took  to  wear-  ing  smocks  about  the  house.  Her  hus-  band  said  he  had  no  objection.  But  had  he?  One  night  when  he  came  home,  she  got  a  distinct  smell  of  cheap  carnation  scent  from  his  lapel.  No,  he  said  when  she  spoke  of  it,  he  had  not  been  to  the  barber's,  he  had  not  been  anywhere;  he  couldn't  account  for  it,  and  neither  could  she.  On  an-  other  occasion  when  he  was  late,  he  couldn't  say  exactly  what  had  detained  him.  She  had  rung  the  office  to  be  told  by  the  new,  awkward  secretary  that  she  was  having  a  whale  of  a  time  and  that  the  boss  had  gone  ages  ago  and  was  probably  boozing.  Yet  an  hour  went  by  and  he  did  not  appear.  His  dinner  was  dried  up-Tess  had  taken  it  in  and  out  of  the  oven  a  number  of  times.  He  came  in  with  a  bit  of  grass  between  his  teeth,  chewing  it.  The  pupils  of  his  eyes  were  big  and  jet  black.  His  deceit  came  to  light  when,  the  following  Sunday,  one  of  the  two  sisters  from  another  area  who  had  come  to  town  to  open  a  boutique  ran  up  to  him  in  the  church  ground  and  offered  him  a  peppermint  sweet.  They  were  twins,  man-mad.  They  wore  red  a  lot,  were  conspicuous  miles  off,  and,  along  with  being  identical  in  appear-  ance,  had  the  same  ingratiating  voices.  One  of  them  had  the  gall  to  ring  Tess  up  and  invite  her  husband  to  the  Mass  they  were  having  offered  in  their  new  premises.  Tess  wrote  the  invitation  down  on  the  message  pad  and  didn't  refer  to  it  when  he  came  in.  He  attended  the  Mass  the  next  eve-  ning,  and,  as  he  admitted  to  Tess  dur-  ing  their  big  reconciliation  afterward,  when  he  almost  crushed  her  ribs,  his  " b"heart  felt  cut  in  two  as  he  stood  up  for  the  Gospel.  He  had  never  deceived  her  before  and  would  never  again.  After  the  Gospel,  he  had  made  a  show  of  him-  self  by  leaving;  he  came  home  with  his  head  down  and  just  sat  in  the  front  room  smoking  and  staring  at  her  and  now  and  then  asking  to  be  forgiven.  His  next  gift  was  the  biggest  gift  he  had  ever  made  her-a  mink  stole.  Tess  wore  it  to  dress  dances  and  used  to  say  to  the  ladies  who  admired  it  that  it  was  much  warmer  than  wool.  Then  she  would  treat  it  in  some  careless  way  and  eventually  toss  it  on  a  chair,  as  much  as  to  say  that  it  meant  nothing  to  her.  But  at  home  it  was  kept  in  a  plastic  bag  on  thetop  of  the  ward-  robe,  where  no  child  could  reach  it  when  playing  charades  or  improvising  fancy  dress.  When  her  husband  be-  gan  to  follow  the  hunt,  she  would  drive  him  to  the  meet,  get  out  of  the  car,  wrap  the  stole  around  her,  and  accept  a  sherry  or  a  hot  bouillon  and  chat  with  one  of  the  Lords  or  Ladies  who  never  missed  a  chase.  They  lost  friends  and  made  friends.  The  girls  from  the  boutique  tried  to  worm  their  way  into  her  life  by  asking  her  to  play  badminton,  but  Tess  de-  clined  and  instead  gave  them  a  small  do-  nation  toward  their  team's  fund.  She  would  say  jokingly  that  their  prices  were  diabolical  or  that  they  would  be  left  on  the  shelf  and  find  no  husband. No  such  luck,  People  went  to  the  houses  of  younger  couples,  as  they  had  once  flocked  to  hers;  and  her  husband,  though  up  to  no  monkey  business  with  the  twins,  was  no  longer  quite  the  same-did  not  surprise  her  with  half-pound  boxes  of  chocolates,  did  not  whistle  when  she  came  down  the  stairs  dressed  to  go  out.  He  was  busy  with  his  various  commitments,  had  mectings  two  or  three  evenings  a  week,  and  on  Sundays  followed  the  hunt.  She  had  time  on  her  hands-time  to  pluck  her  eyebrows  daily,  time  to  brood.  The  children  wrote  letters,  most-  ly  about  the  food  or  the  harsh  conditions  in  their  schools,  or  some  little  treat,  like  beinggiven  a  toffee  apple  for  a  feast.  She  found  her  elder  daugh-  ter's  old  diary  and  was  shocked  when  she  read  &quot;Mammy  is  as  usual  gallivant-  ing,  but  Daddy  does  not  see  through  her.&quot;  She  could  not  say  that  it  was  then,  any  more  than  it  was  the  moment  when  she  smelled  the  cheap  perfume  on  his  lapel  or  the  nausea  she  felt  when  she  read  a  disgusting  magazine  article  about  sex;  it  was  none  of  these  events,  and  yet  they  were  foreshadowings.  One  night,  her  husband  was  sound  asleep,  and  for  no  reason  she  remem-  bered  a  fit  her  favorite  child  had  once  taken  because  he  had  seen  a  man-a  devil-in  black,  inside  the  windowpane.  She  herself  had  not  seen  him,  but  she  remembered  her  son's  fibulations  and  how  she  had  not  been  able  to  comfort  him.  Motherliness  was  beyond  her.  Her  husband  was  beside  her-warm,  his  face  almost  beefy-and  she  had  no  idea  what  he  thought  anymore,  sleep-  ing  or  waking.  Her  children,  her  sis-  ters,  even  her  younger  sister,  who  had  grown  odd  and  become  attached  to  nature-all  these  people  seemed  to  vi-  brate  with  more  life,  more  urgency,  more  of  an  appetite  for  the  things  they  did.  She  felt  odd,  lonely;  she  felt  afraid.  Perhaps  the  manes  of  herdear  dead  aunt  was  at  last  coming  to  dis-  quiether.  Everything  was  in  order-  the  same  dinner  each  day  of  the  week,  the  same  night  set  aside  for  brief  and  overfamiliar  intercourse,  the  same  gro-  cery  order  on  Saturday,  the  same  hu-  moring  of  the  maid  on  Monday  morn-  ing  after  the  dance:  the  same,  the  same,  the  same-and  yet  it  was  as  if  she  had  just  stepped  onto  ice.  &quot;Oh,  Jesus,&quot;  she  said  aloud.  &quot;Is  this  how  it  is  when  one  begins  to  be  un-  happy?&quot;  And  she  closed  her  eyes  tight,  so  that  all  of  her  sensibilities  were  like  two  hot  points  crammed  in  her  head.  Her  husband  heard  her  laughing.  She  had  just  smashed  a  hand  mirror  and  was  gloating  over  the  prospect  of  the  seven  years'  bad  luck  in  store.  He  did  not  know  what  to  say.  He  had  seen  trouble  coming.  &quot;What  is  it?&quot;  he  asked  softly.  &quot;What  is  it!&quot;  she  said,  and  her  voice  was  like  slivers  as  she  went  on  laughing  at  what  she  had  done.
