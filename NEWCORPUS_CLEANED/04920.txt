 b"ALL  through  my  childhood,  I  lived  in  a  gray  stone  village  on  the  side  of  a  steep  hill  in  the  in-  dustrial  North  of  England.  Above  it  stretched  the  moor,  and  below,  in  the  valley,  was  the  big  woollen  mill.  There  was  a  church  and  a  chapel.  Oftenon  a  Sunday  afternoon,  just  about  the  time  my  brother  and  I  were  getting  ready  to  go  to  Sunday  school,  we  would  hear  a  brisk  knocking  on  our  door  and  know  that  Aunt  Emily  Jane  had  come  to  visit  us.  She  was  really  Mother's  aunt,  and  our  great-aunt,  and  though  twenty  years  older  than  Mother,  she  didn't  look  five  years  older.  She  had  a  delicate  pink-  and-white  complexion,  rarely  seen  in  our  region,  and  glossy  golden  curls.  Her  hair,  which  she  wore  piled  un-  fashionably  high  on  her  head,  was  so  long  that  she  could  sit  on  it.  When  she  was  twelve  years  old,  she  caught  some  frightful  disease,  which  left  her  with  a  curved  spine,  and  though  she  became  quite  healthy  and  vigorous  again,  the  slight  hump  had  cancelled  her  good  looks  and  gaiety,  and  ended  her  pros-  pects  as  far  as  proposals  of  marriage  were  concerned.  My  maternal  grand-  mother,  who  was  her  elder  sister  and  not  half  so  handsome,  had  married  twice,  and  while  both  husbands  had  proved  financially  unreliable,  they  had  given  her  six  healthy  children  to  care  for  her  in  her  old  age.  Aunt  Emily  Jane  hadn't  even  a  home  of  her  own.  Till  she  was  forty,  she  lived  with  her  brother  Tom  and  his  wife,  and  when  he  died  of  pneumonia,  she  continued  to  share  a  cottage  with  his  wid-  ow,  who  was  a  success-  ful  dressmaker.
