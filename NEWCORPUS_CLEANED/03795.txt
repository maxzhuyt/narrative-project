Before  he  could  speak,  he  had  to  reclaim  those  parched  areas  in  his  mouth  that  had  dried  out  during  the  night.  &quot;Come  down,  please.&quot;  &quot;Why?&quot;  &quot;Come  quickly.&quot;  In  a  dressing  gown,  he  joined  her  at  the  open  front  door.  &quot;It  must  be  a  mistake,&quot;  she  was  muttering.  &quot;I'm  certain  it's  a  mistake.&quot;  A  deliveryman  stood  in  the  drive,  concealed  by  one  of  the  largest  wreaths  Graham  had  ever  seen.  Into  a  vast  wheel  of  cypress,  interwoven  with  gold  and  silver  tinsel  and  maiden-  hair,  were  wired  a  dozen  cabbage  roses,  rubbery  and  half  deflated,  like  balloons  on  the  morning  after  a  party.  The  wheel  was  backed  with  palm  fronds,  and  across  its  diameter  was  strung  a  banner  with  &quot;In  Loving  Memory&quot;  on  it  in  gilt  Gothic  script.  ''There's  an  envelope  attached,  Mad-  am.&quot;  &quot;Very  well.&quot;  Priscilla,  signing  the  receipt,  appeared  to  be  furiously  insulted  about  some-  thing.  Since  there  was  no  perceptible  evidence  of  grief  in  this  household,  the  deliveryman  drove  off  whistling.  For  a  second  only,  Graham  experi-  enced  the  odd  feeling  that  he  himself  had  ordered,  during  his  garrulous  dreams,  this  most  extravagant  of  floral  tributes,  to  fill  a  gap  in  the  ceremonies  attendant  on  his  father's  death.  He  opened  the  envelope;  Priscilla  and  he  read  the  card  together.  &quot;Oh,  zo!&quot;  she  breathed.  &quot;How  dreadful!&quot;  Graham's  eyes  smarted  with  unshed  tears.  She  gave  him  a  fierce  look.  &quot;What-  ever  happens,  we  mustn't  let  Ma  know.&quot;  &quot;What  do  you  propose  to  do  with  it?&quot;&quot;  Graham  asked.  Propped  on  the  ground  against  his  hand,  the  wreath  looked  the  least  disposable  of  objects.  Stiff  and  creaking  on  its  wire  frame-  work,  it  glittered  in  the  early  light.  &quot;Get  rid  of  it.  Out  in  the  shed. It  must  have  cost  the  earth.  Do  you  think  it  was  Gabriella's  money?&quot;  &quot;No  fear.  She  sends  every  penny  back  home.  It's  their  birthday  money.  They'd  been  saving  for  an  aquarium.&quot;  Priscilla's  nails  disentangled  the  roses  from  their  wiring  and  pulled  thecy-  press  foliage  away  from  the  frame.  &quot;We  must  hurry.  Ma  is  bound  to  have  heard  the  doorbell.&quot;  He  watched  her  kneeling  there  working  away  furiously;  in  a  short  time,  she  was  surrounded  by  wreckage.  She  crushed  the  frame  to  her  until  it  was  no  longera  circle  but  a  half  moon.  At  length  she  stopped,  ex-  hausted,  and  her  head  sank  down.  He  put  his  arms  round  her.  She  was  trem-  bling.  Her  breath  sobbed  as  though  she  was  nearing  the  end  of  a  race.  &quot;I'll  kill  Gabriella!&quot;  she  cried.  &quot;How  dare  she?  How  dare  she?&quot;  He  held  her  more  tightly,  resting  his  chin  on  thetop  of  her  head.  From  the  door  of  the  shed,  he  could  see  almost  the  entire  garden.
