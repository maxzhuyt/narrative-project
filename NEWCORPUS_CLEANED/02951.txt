 THE  SEA  OF  LOST  TIME  OWARD  the  end  of  January,  the  sea  grew  harsh.  It  had  be-  gun  to  dump  its  heavy  garbage  on  the  town,  and  within  a  few  weeks  everything  was  contaminated  with  its  unbearable  mood.  From  then  on,  the  world  would  not  be  worth  living  in-  at  least  not  until  December-so  no  one  stayed  awake  after  eight  o'clock.  But  the  year  Mr.  Herbert  came  the  sea  didn't  change-not  even  in  February.  On  the  contrary,  it  became  smoother  and  more  phosphorescent,  and  during  the  first  nights  of  March  it  gave  off  a  fragrance  of  roses.  Tobias  smelled  it.  His  blood  attract-  ed  the  land  crabs,  and  he  spent  half  the  night  chasing  them  off  his  bedun-  til  the  breeze  rose  up  again  and  he  was  able  to  sleep.  During  his  long  periods  of  wakefulness,  he  learned  to  distin-  guish  the  changes  in  the  air.  When  he  detected  a  smell  of  roses,  he  didn't  have  to  open  the  door  to  know  that  it  was  asmell  from  the  sea.  He  got  up  late.  Clotilde  was  starting  a  fire  in  the  courtyard.  The  breeze  was  cool  and  all  the  stars  were  in  place,  but  it  was  hard  to  count  them  down  to  the  horizon,  because  of  the  lights  from  the  sea.  After  having  his  coffee,  Tobias  could  still  taste  a  trace  of  night  on  his  palate.  &quot;Something  very  strange  happened  last  night,&quot;  he  said,  remembering.  Clotilde,  of  course,  had  not  smelled  it.  She  slept  so  heavily  that  she  didn't  even  remember  her  dreams.  &quot;It  was  a  smell  of  roses,&quot;  Tobias  said,  &quot;and  I'm  sure  it  came  from  the  Sea.”  &quot;I  don't  know  what  roses  smell  like,&quot;  said  Clotilde.  She  could  have  been  right.  The  town  was  arid,  with  a  hard  soil,  furrowed  by  saltpeter,  and  only  occasionally  did  someone  bring  a  bouquet  of  flowers  from  outside  to  cast  into  the  sea,  where  the  dead  were  thrown.  &quot;It's  the  smell  that  drowned  man  from  Guacamayal  had,&quot;  Tobias  said.  &quot;Well,&quot;  Clotilde  said,  smiling,  'if  it  was  a  good  smell,  then  you  can  be  sure  it  didn't  come  from  this  sea.&quot;  It  really  was  an  unforgiving  sea.  At  certain  times,  when  the  nets  brought  in  nothing  but  floating  garbage,  the  streets  of  the  town  were  strewn  with  dead  fish  when  the  tide  went  out.  Dyna-  mite  only  brought  the  remains  of  old  shipwrecks  to  the  surface.  Like  Clotilde,  the  few  women  left  in  town  were  boiling  with  bitterness.  There  was  old  Jacob's  wife,  Petra,  who  got  up  earlier  than  usual  that  morning,  put  the  house  in  order,  and  sat  down  to  breakfast  with  a  look  of  adversity.  &quot;My  last  wish,&quot;  she  said  to  her  hus-  band,  &quot;is  to  be  buried  alive.&quot;  She  said  it  as  if  she  wereon  her  deathbed,  but  she  was  sitting  at  the  dining-room  table;  the  bright  March  light  came  pouring  in  the  dining-room  windows  and  spread  throughout  the  house.  Opposite  her,  calming  his  peace-  ful  hunger,  was  old  Jacob,  a  man  who  had  loved  her  so  much  and  for  so  long  that  he  could  no  longer  conceive  of  any  suffering  that  didn't  start  with  his  wife.  &quot;I  want  todie  with  the  assurance  that  I'll  be  laid  beneath  the  ground  like  proper  people,&quot;  she  went  on.  &quot;And  the  only  way  to  be  sureof  it  is  to  go  around  asking  people  to  do  me  the  blessed  charity  of  burying  me  alive.&quot;  &quot;You  don't  have  to  ask  anybody,&quot;  old  Jacob  said  with  the  greatest  of  calm.  &quot;I'll  put  you  there  myself.&quot;  &quot;Let's  go,  then,&quot;  she  said,  &quot;because  I'm  going  to  die  before  very  long.&quot;  Old  Jacob  looked  her  over  care-  fully.,  Her  eyes  were  the  only  thing  still  young. Clotilde  promised.  &quot;There's  a  village  at  the  bottom  of  the  sea,&quot;  Tobias  said,  &quot;with  little  white  houses  with  millions  of  flowers  on  the  terraces.&quot;  Clotilde  raised  her  hands  to  her  head.  &quot;Oh,  Tobias!&quot;  she  exclaimed.  &quot;Oh,  Tobias,  forthe  love  of  God,  don't  start  up  with  those  things  again.&quot;  Tobias  didn't  say  anything.  He  rolled  over  to  the  edge  of  the  bed  and  tried  to  go  to  sleep.  He  couldn't  sleep  until  dawn,  when  the  wind  changed  and  the  crabs  left  him  in  peace.
