A  cane,  held  in  an  inflexible  grip,  stands  upright  between  his  knees.  His  thin  lips  are  clamped  tight,  his  face  is  gray  and  unshaven,  and  only  his  eyes,  hooded  by  prominent  bone,  pale  blue,  as  gleaming  and  fierce  as  an  eagle's,  indicate  life.  They  flash  back  and  forth,  following  a  mental  picture  of  the  base-  ball  game  he  listens  to.  &quot;Alfred!  Do  you  hear?&quot;  He  doesn't.  And  it  is  not  so  much  a  question  of  his  hearing,  which  in  fact  has  failed  over  the  past  years,  as  it  is  him;  he  doesn't  wish  to  hear.  He  doesn't  want  her  to  intrude  into  this  baseball  game.  The  sport,  though  taken  vicariously  now,  is  one  of  hislast  pleasures.  It  reprieves  him  from  the  present,  where  he  has  gradually  grown  old,  eighty  years  old,  and  has  been  forced  to  turn  the  labor  of  the  farm-  this  clean  and  prosperous  farm  in  the  Red  River  [REDACTED]-over  to  his  wife  and  son.  His  body,  stiff  and  crippled  now,  will  no  longer  carry  out  the  duties  he  expects  it  to;  it  has  left  him  stranded.  His  broad,  short  fingers,  swollen  at  each  knuckle  from  dislocations  and  breaks,  are  a  reminder  of  the  young  man  who  once  caught  for  the  best  pitch-                 ers  in  professional  baseball,  in  the  days  when  there  was  only  one  major  league,  when  jobs  and  pay  were  unpredict-  able,  when  equipment  and  mitts  were  poorly  made,  and  the  fastball  was  the  most  frequent  pitch.  Now  when  there  is  a  crack  of  the  bat  and  a  possible  foul  tip,  these  fingers  flinch  with  remem-  bered  pain.  And  when  the  team  he  favors  (and  in  his  imagination  is  catch-  ing  for)  makes  an  error,  the  fingers  tighten  their  grip  around  the  cane.  She  goes  up  beside  him  and  jerks  a  week-old  copy  of  the  Fargo  Forum,  May  19,  1959,  from  between  his  back  and  the  back  of  the  chair,  where  he's  draped  it  to  absorb  his  sweat.  He  doesn't  even  lift  an  eyebrow  in  response.  She  shakes  him  by  the  shoulder  and  says,  &quot;Alfred,  for  God's  sake,  come  and  eat!&quot;  He  turns  his  fierce  eyes  on  her.  She  has  become  his  nurse  and  guardian,  his  barber  and  his  hired  man.  She  does  the  farm  chores  he  can  do  only  in  his  mind-which  is  the  way  he  oc-  cupies  himself  when  he  isn't  listening  to  baseball.  If  she  loved  him,  she  wouldn't  shame  him  by  doing  the  work  he  cares  for  and  is  no  longer  capable  of  doing;  she  would  let  their  son  do  it  all.  If  she  loved  him,  she  would  leave  him  to  himself.  She  would  let  him  die.  &quot;Do  I  have  to  lift  you  out  of  that  chair?&quot;  she  asks.  &quot;You  do-&quot;  The  tobacco  in  his  mouth,  which  he  had  forgotten  about,  and  now  tastes,  has  drawn  up  so  much  saliva  there  is  no  room  for  his  tongue  to  move,.  He  swallows  it  down,  and  then  says  in  his  penetrating,  throaty  voice,  &quot;You  do,  and  I'll  crack  you  with  this  goddamn  cane.&quot;  She  seems  less  frightened  than  hurt,  and  her  offended  eyes  retreat  from  him.  &quot;You're  a  mean  old  fool,&quot;  she  says.  &quot;And  you  get  meaner  every  day.&quot;  &quot;You're  damn  right.&quot;  &quot;Stop  it.  Come  and  eat  now.&quot;  &quot;Wait  till  this  inning.&quot;  &quot;'This  inning  nothing!  After  this  inning  it'll  be  the  next  inning,  and  after  that  the  next  game,  and  then  it'll  be  night.  Right  now!  The  food's  getting  cold.&quot;  &quot;Ill  turn  it  up  so  I  can  listen  in  there.&quot;  &quot;Up?  Up!  My  God,  it's  been  going  full  blast  all  day! He  picks  up  the  bar  of  rough,  honey-  colored  homemade  soap,  and  it  slips  through  his  grip  and  drops  to  the  floor.  She  hurries  over  and  picks  it  up  and  places  it  in  the  basin,  all  the  while  avoiding  his  fierce  eyes  and  blushing. He  hangs  the  cane  on  the  back  of  the  chair.  In  his  mind  the  baseball  game  is  nearing  its  finish,  and  this  fills  him  with  unrest  and  a  great  sadness.  He  looks  up  to  find  her  eyes  on  him.  They  stir  him  with  their  warmth,  their  interest  in  him,  and  their  lack  of  pity.  He  picks  up  his  fork,  fum-  bling,  and  begins  to  eat.  He  remembers  when  he  courted  her.  He  was  in  his  late  thirties,  had  recently  left  his  first  wife,  and  was  working  as  a  swipe  and  jockey  for  a  stable  in  Fargo.  He  had  come  from  [REDACTED].  He  wore  tailor-made  shirts  and  suits,  was  looked  upon  as  a  dandy,  and  had  a  repu-  tation  for  being  a  lady's  man,  which  he  was,  and  which  he  could  be  with  a  great  deal  of  ease  because  he  didn'tgive  a  damn  about  ladies.  All  he  cared  about  were  horses  and  baseball,  and  real  baseball-  paying  baseball-hadn't  yet  made  its  way  West.  Then  he  met  her,  a  coun-  try  girl,  her  hair  long  and  gold  and  piled  high  on  her  head,  her  skin  smooth  and  cool-looking  as  ivory,  her  profile-the  high  brow,  straight  nose,  and  full,  parted  lips-both  sensual  and  strong.  She  was  eighteen,  shy  and  untouched,  but  refined  in  a  way  that  Eastern  women  were  supposed  to  be  and  weren't;  they  were  just  jaded  or  cold,  the  ones  he  had  known,  while  she  was  as  sensitive  and  spirited  as  the  fine  animals  he  worked  with.  And  she  was  tall,  tall  and  graceful,  much  taller  than  he  was.  Immediately  he  felt  ill  at  ease,  which  had  never  happened  to  him  when  he  was  alone  with  a  woman,  no  matter  who  or  what  the  woman  was  or  how  she  was  built,  and  he  hated  himself  for  being  so  short.  He  hated  his  fancy  clothes;they  called  attention  to  his  size.  Those  first  hours  with  her  put  him  in  such  torment  he  never  wanted  to  see  her  again.  But  he  couldn't  stay  away.  He  came  to  her  house  and  sat  in  the  drawing  room,  in  silence  and  in  pain,  scarcely  able  to  speak,  and  watched  her  fingers  move  over  her  needlework  as  she  told  him  pleasant  and  intelligent  stories  about  school,  about  the  children   she  taught  there.  She  never  spoke  about  herself.  After  this  agony  had  gone  on  for  several  months,  he  finally  said,  in  a  rage,  &quot;By  God,  Katherine,  you  better  marry  me.&quot;  She  paled  at  the  tone  of  his  voice,  and  then,  with  her  eyes  lowered,  told  him  that  she  had  loved  him  for  a  long  time  and  wanted  to  be  with  him.  He  was  terrified.  He  felt  sure  she  was  try-  ing  to  do  him  in.  He  went  back  East,  hoping  to  rid  himself  of  her,  of  the  confusion  she  stirred  up  in  him,  but  he  couldn't  stand  being  away  from  her,  and  so,  hating  himself  all  the  more  for  having  gone  off  like  a  coward,  he  came  back  and  married  her.  For  a  long  time  it  was  terrible.  He  cursed  her  and  browbeat  her  and  berated  her.  He  wouldn't  let  her  do  what  she  liked-  go  visiting,  go  to  church,  wear  her  hair  down  in  public,  wear  flattering  clothes,  have  women  friends-and  he  went  on  drinking  bouts  that  lasted  for  weeks.  Then,  one  morning,  waking  in  the  web  of  her  hair,  he  realized  that  he  wasn't  in  torment  any  more,  that  he  hadn't  broken  her  spirit,  and  that  he  loved  her.  E  looks  at  her  eyes,  which  are  cast  down;  at  her  profile,  her  face  demure  and  vulnerable-looking  now  while  she  eats;  at  her  hands.  He  flushes  with  pride  and  arrogance.  She  still  has  her  beauty.  She  is  the  same.  It  would  take  some  doing,  he  thinks,  to  win  her  overonce  more,  to  make  her  respect  him  and  quail  at  the  sound  of  his  voice.  As  he  eats,  he  makes  plans  on  how  he  will  go  about  it;  him  storming  out  to  the  barn,  harnessing  up  the  team,  hitch-  ing  them  to  the  mower,  and  mow-  ing  the  whole  yard  while  she  watches;  her  coming  out  of  the  house  and  com-  manding  him  to  get  down  from  there,  and  him  rising  out  ofthe  seat,  bal-  ancing  on  the  axle,  and  crying,  &quot;Get  away  before  you  gethurt!  Go  back  in  the  house  where  you  belong!&quot;  And  then  coming  in  at  night,  covered  with  pollen  and  filth,  sweat-soaked,  smelly,  and  crawling  into  bed  like  that.  He  begins  to  break  out  with  low  cries  of  pleasure  and  delight.  Maybe  he  will  have  to  try  that.  Maybe  tomor-  row.
