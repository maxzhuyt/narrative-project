This  was  in  the  autumn  of  1933,  and  drinking  beer  after  the  years  of  prohibition  was  something  of  an  adventure.  The  place  we  went  to  was  a  drugstore  that  had  been  con-  verted  into  a  kind  of  tavern-  the  old  soda  fountain  served  as  a  bar,  and  the  tables  were  more  suit-  able  for  malted  milks  than  ale  and  lager.  Ken  said  the  beer  was  &quot;green,&quot;  and  he  put  salt  in  his  and  persuaded  me  to  try  it,  too.  Another  thing  I  remember  strongly  about  Ken  is  his  voice.  The  only  actor  I  have  ever  heard  whose  voice  was  as  deep  and  powerful  was  a  member  of  the  Habimah  Players,  from  [REDACTED],  who  played  in  New  York  about  ten  years  ago.  You  could  have  heard  him  whis-  per  as  far  back  as  the  last  row,  and  now,  just  because  of  that  voice,  when-  ever  I  see  a  reference  to  [REDACTED],  I  think     of  Ken.  Over  the  years,  such  silly  things  as  this  keep  cropping  up  to  remind  me  of  the  time  he  and  I  were  at  drama  school  together.  He  was  a  good  actor,  impressive  for  a  student.  Once,  he  played  the  part  of  a  bearded  Russian  peasant  in  aplay  about  Rasputin,  and  in  one  scene  he  thundered  so  loudly  at  Ras-  putin  that  you  had  a  feeling  he'd  blow  out  the  doors  to  the  fire  escapes.  The  entire  theatre  seemed  to  tremble  when  he  spoke,  and  if  you  were  sitting  out  in  the  auditorium,  your  body  felt  the  re-  verberations. I  do  know  it  was  the  day  after  the  final  cleanup  crew  had  finished  and  the  theatre  was  all  but  deserted  for  the  summer.
