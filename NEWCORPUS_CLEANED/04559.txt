  BARNST  ORMER  COUPLE  of  Sundays  ago,  I  A  took  my  three  children,  none  of  whom  had  ever  been  up  in  an  airplane,  on  an  exploratory  visit  to  the  airport  of  the  large  Southern  town  in  which  we  live.  There  was  considerable  activity  in  the  area  where  private  planes  are  housed  and  serviced,  and  while  we  were  watching  it,  a  uniformed  guard  approached  us  and  asked  whether  we'd  like  to  take  a  trip  over  the  town.  He  said  the  price  would  be  three  dollars  for  the  four  of  us,  which  struck  me  as  extremely  reasonable.  The  children  wanted  to  go.  The  plane,  standing  nearby,  was  a  trim,  reassuring  little  four-place  ship  with  a  tricycle  landing  gear,  and  in  a  few  min-  utes  we  were  climbing  aboard.  The  chil-  dren  sat  in  the  rear  of  the  well-designed  cabin,  and  I  in  front,  beside  the  pilot.  We  took  off,  under  the  direction  ofthe  control  tower,  first  rolling  smoothly  down  an  asphalt  runway.  And  as  we  left  the  ground,  I  glanced  back  at  the  three  children,  comfortably  and  happily  making  their  first  flight,  and  I  thought  of  my  home  town  in  [REDACTED],  and  its  airport,  and  the  Sunday  flights  of  a  quarter  century  ago.  Like  most  other  small  towns  in  the  late  twenties  and  early  thirties,  Hunts-  ville-as  I  will  call  it-  was  far  removed  from  the  travelled  paths  of  commer-  cial  aviation.  What  fly-  ing  needs  it  had  were  ca-  tered  to  in  the  main  by  a  man  known  as  Red  Carpenter,  a  superb  pilot  engaged  in  the  precarious  occu-  pation  of  barnstormer.  I  suppose  Red  could  be  considered  primarily  as  an  entrepreneur  whose  chief  income  was  derived  from  taking  people,  two  at  atime,  for  short  flights  at  the  standard  rate  of  five  dollars  per  per-  son,  but  in  looking  back  I  realize  that  he  was  much  more  than  that.  In  addi-  tion  to  being  a  hard-working  business-  man  and  a  skillful  and  courageous  pilot,  he  was  an  actor  playing  the  lead  in  his  own  production.  Richard  Barthelmess  himself  could  not  have  looked  more  au-  thentic.  Red  was  a  poised,  compactly  built  man  of  about  thirty,  exuding  con-  fidence  and  an  air  of  romance.  He  never  really  smiled,  but  he  was  occasionally  observed  to  screw  up  his  face  into  the  sort  of  frozen  grin  I've  always  associated  with  the  art  of  taxidermy.  His  uniform  consisted  of  dusty  riding  boots,  white  breeches,  a  black  sugde  jacket,  a  white  helmet,  and  a  set  of  goggles  pushedup  over  his  forehead.  In  this  outfit,  even  his  most  routine  movements  seemed     tremendously  significant;  entering  or  leaving  his  airplane,  for  instance,  was  an  elaborate  ceremony.  When  he  stood  motionless  beside  it,  a  cigarette  dangling  casually  under  hiscrisply  trimmed  mustache,  and  grimly  surveyed  the  awed  onlookers  gathered  at  the  edge  of  the  landing  field,  he  appeared  to  be  issuing  a  challenge  to  every  red-blooded  man  in  the  crowd  to  come  aloft  with  him.  Red's  acting  might  not  have  with-  stood  expert  scrutiny,  but  to  me  in  those  days  he  represented  the  spirit  of  flight.  Huntsville's  young  bloods  used  to  say  of  him,  &quot;He  don't  give  a  damn  for  noth-  ing,&quot;  by  which  they  meant  that  he  was  fearless  and  beyond  reproach.  These  words  of  adulation,  never  lightly  be-  stowed,  had  in  the  past  been  reserved  for  such  local  celebrities  as  a  boxer  who  had  once  been  the  ninth-ranking  middle-  weight  contender,  and  a  promising  foot-  ball  player  who  had  emigrated  to  Cali-  fornia  and  become  a  second-string  guard  for  U.S.C.  While  no  one  had  ever  dis-  puted  Red's  clear  title  to  the  phrase,  he  had  irrevocably  established  his  right  to  it  by  one  of  his  most  daring  exploits-the  winning  of  a  five-hundred-dollar  bet  that  he  could  fly  his  plane  under  a  nar-  row  bridge  spanning  an  unpretentious  stream  called  the  Catawald  River.  Not  only  did  he  clear  the  bridge,  with  just  inches  to  spare,  but  he  promptly  celebrated  his  achievement  with  a  daz-  zling  half-hour  display  of  aerial  acro-  batics  that  stopped  traffic  all  over  the  county.  RED  flew  a  large  and  powerful  green  biplane,  with  a  leather-cushioned  forward  cockpit  that  would  accommo-  date  two  passengers,  and  a  cockpit  aft  for  the  pilot.  The  plane  was  equipped  with  such  streamlined  novelties  as  a  hood  for  its  radial  engine  and  pants  for  its  balloon-tired  wheels.  All  in  all,  it  was  quite  the  most  elaborate  ship  Hunts-  ville  had  ever  seen.  During  the  good  weather,  Red  would  make  his  appear-  ance  at  intervals  of  about  eight  weeks,  announcing  his  arrival  by  screaming  his  plane  upside  down  over  the  town  at  an  altitude  of  some  four  hundred  feet.  After  a  couple  of  passes  in  this  position,  he  would  run  through  an  extensive  rep-  ertoire  of  stunts  before  bombarding  the  area  with  leaflets  saying  that  he  and  his  airplane  would  be  available  forhire  at  the  airport  the  following  Sunday.  The  airport  was  an  uneven,  rutted  field  with  a  very  short  north-south        runway,  on  which  an  extraordinarily  skillful  pilot  could  land  a  small,  slow  plane  when  both  the  direction  and  the  velocity  of  the  wind  were  favorable.  The  field  was  bounded  on  the  north  by  a  power  line  and  a  bank  of  billboards  with  a  highway  beyond  them,  on  the  south  by  a  large  barn,  on  the  east  by  a  fence,  and  on  the  west  by  a  dirt  road,  across  which  was  the  Penelope  Baptist  Church.  (Just  how  the  Penelope  Bap-  tist  Church  persevered  against  the  noise  and  dust  accompanying  Red's  visits  is  a  mystery  to  me  now,  but  I  remember  wishing  at  the  time  that  my  church  could  have  been  so  fortunately  situated.)  Red  always  landed  from  north  to  south-on  the  theory,  I  suppose,  that  if  he  overshot  the  runway,  it  would  be  better  to  hit  the  barn  than  smash  through  the  billboards  and  across  a  highway  full  of  heavy  traffic.  It  was  a  difficult  achievement  under  the  best  of  circumstances,  but  when  the  wind  blew  strongly  from  the  north,  the  prob-  lem  became  major.  Red  would  have  to  sideslip  his  airplane  in  over  the  runway,  pull  it  up  at  the  last  minute,  mush  it  into  a  landing,  and  then  have  it  make  a  sharp  turn-a  sort  of  ground  loop-  just  before  it  reached  the  barn. The  first  thing  I  thought  of  once  we  were  airborne,  was  that  I  had  not  been  given-had  not  even  been  of-  fered-a  helmet  and  goggles.  This  was  a  deep  affront  that  it  was  now  too  late  to  correct.  Swallowing  it,  I  tried  to  look  nonchalantly  about,  but  I  was  too  far  down  in  the  cockpit  to  see  anything.  There  was  a  greasy  rag  on  the  floor.  I  studied  it,  and  the  holes  in  the  in-  strument  panel  where  the  instruments  would  have  been  if  the  passengers'  cock-  pit  hadhad  any.  I  watched  the  rudder  pedals  moving  back  and  forth  as  Red  operated  them.  I  listened  to  my  brother  and  Herb  yelling  at  each  other  when-  ever  they  spotted  a  point  of  interest  be-  low.  At  last,  Livius-sensing,  perhaps,  that  I  was  not  getting  all  I  shouldout  of  the  experience-boosted  me  onto  his  lap  for  a  minute,  and  I  caught  a  glimpse  of  the  Catawald  River  before  I  slid  back  to  the  floor.  I  thought  thatthe  only  way  I'd  really  be  able  to  see  anything  would  be  for  Red  to  fly  upside  down,  but  the  stunting  he  had  promised  turned  out  to  be  pretty  tame  stuff-conceivably  be-  cause  if  it  had  beenanything  else,  I  would  have  fallen  out.  Just  the  same,  Livius  and  Herb  enjoyed  it  thoroughly.  It  consisted  mainly  of  some  shallow  dives  and  pullouts,  with  a  few  steep  turns  thrown  in,  and  before  I  knew  it  I  felt  the  plane  bump  onto  the  field  again.  I  don't  think  I  would  have  minded  terribly  not  seeing  anything  if  Red  hadn't  bypassed  me  with  the  helmets  and  goggles.  At  least  half  the  thrill  of  flying,  I  was  sure,  came  from  wearing  those  essential  pieces  of  equipment.  Many  nights,  I  had  ridden  my  bicycle  at  high  speed  down  the  steepest  hill  in  Huntsville,  my  Montgomery  Ward  helmet  and  goggles  firmly  in  place,  and  imagined  that  I  was  diving  with  Red  Carpenter  in  the  big  green  plane.  Down,  down,  down  we  went,  with  me  peering  over  the  side,  then  gazing  back  triumphantly  at  Red  as  I  spotted  what-  ever  it  was  we  were  looking  for  and  di-  rected  his  attention  to  it  with  an  elabo-  rate  motion  of  my  arm.  Now,  after  all  that  practice,  I  had  been  flying  in  a  real  airplane  and  hadn't  even  been  provided  with  the  equipment  of  a  real  flier.  Why,  without  his  helmet  and  goggles  Eddie  Rickenbacker  himself  would  have  been  laughed  out  of  the  sky!  Still,  I  was  grateful  to  Livius  (the  twelve  dollars  and  fifty  cents  he  had  spent  amounted  to  more  than  the  com-  mission  from  the  sale  of  six  hundred  Saturday  Evening  Posts),  and  I  re-  solved  that  he  would  never  know  how  crestfallen  I  was.  I  comforted  myself  with  the  thought  that  I  was,  after  all,  the  only  boy  in  my  school  who  had  been  up  at  all,  and  that  my  classmates  would  regard  me  with  considerable  awe.  Someday,  I  determined,  I  would  take  Herb  and  Livius  up.  Red  would  have  a  larger  plane  then,  and  we  would  all  be  able  to  see  over  the  side  as  we  recon-  noitred.
