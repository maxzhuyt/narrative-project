In  a  period  of  forced  inactivity  due  in  fact  to  the  presence  of  the  irksome  parasite,  and  thus  moved  by  a  purely  circumstantial  interest,  Bernard  W.  Losurdo,  professor  of  Assyriology  at  Michigan  State  University,  happened by  chance  tosee  Flory's  photographs.  Nevertheless,  several  singularities  that  no  one  had  noticed  until  then  caught  his  professional  cryptographic  eye:  the  lines  of  the  mosaic  are  made  up  of  a  number  of  spines  that  varies  within  a  rather  narrow  range  (approximately  twenty-five  to  sixty  );  there  exist  groups  of  spines  that  are  repeated  with  a  very  high  frequency,  almost  as  though  these  were  obligatory  associations;  finally  (and  this  was  the  key  to  the  enigma),  the  terminal  spines  of  each  line  are  sometimes  arranged  according  to  a  pattern  that  could  be  described  as  rhythmical.  It  was  certainly  a  piece  of  good  luck  that  the  first  photograph  Losurdo  ex-  amined  should  present  a  particularly  simple  pattern:  the  last  four  spines  of  the  first  line  were  identical  with  the  last  four  of  the  third;  the  last  three  of  the  second  line  matched  the  last  three  of  the  fourth  and  sixth-ABA,  BCB,  CDC,  and  so  on-in  a  rhyme  schemelike  Dante's  famous  terza  rima.  Yet  it  required  great  intellectual  courage  to  take  the  next  step:  to  formulate  the  hypothesis  that  the  entire  mosaic  rhymes,  and  not  only  metaphorically  speaking-that  it  actually  constitutes  a  poetic  composition,  and  conveys  a  meaning.  Losurdo  had  this  courage.  His  crypt-  analysis  was  long  and  patient,  and  it  confirmed  his  original  intuition.  The  conclusions  the  scholar  reached  can  be  briefly  summarized  as  follows:  Approximately  fifteen  per  cent  of  the  adultindividuals  of  Teenie  are  bearers  of  a  Flory  mosaic.  The  mosaic,  when  it  occurs,  is  repeated  identically  in  all  their  mature  proglottid  segments,  and  is  congenital:  itis  thereforea  specific  characteristic  of  a  single  individual,  comparable  (the  observation  is  made  by  Losurdo  himself)  to  a  man's  finger-  prints,  or  the  lines  on  his  palm.  It  is  formed  by  a  number  of  &quot;lines&quot;  which  can  vary  from  about  ten  to  two  hun-  dred-and,  what's  more,  is  sometimes  rhymed  and  at  othertimes  is  best  de-  scribed  as  rhythmic  prose.
