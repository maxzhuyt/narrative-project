"THE  PROGRESS  OF  LOVE  HEMIE's  father  phoned  her  at  Pwork.  This  was  not  long  after  she  got  her  divorce  and  had  started  working  in  the  real-estate  of-  fice.  Both  of  her  boys  were  in  school.  It  was  a  hot  day  in  September.  Her  father  was  so  polite,  even  in  the  family.  He  took  time  to  ask  Phe-  mie  how  she  was.  Country  manners,  Phemie  thought.  Even  if  somebody  phones  to  tell  you  your  house  is  burn-  ing  down,  theyask  first  how  you  are.  &quot;I'm  fine,&quot;  Phemie  said.  &quot;How  are  you?&quot;  &quot;Not  so  good,  I  guess,&quot;  said  her  father  in  his  familiar,  self-respecting  tone  of  apology.  &quot;I  think  your  moth-  er's  gone.&quot;  Phemie  knew  that  &quot;gone&quot;  meant  &quot;dead.&quot;  But  for  a  second  or  so  she  saw  her  mother  in  her  black  straw  hat  setting  off  down  the  lane.  The  word  &quot;gone&quot;  seemed  full  of  nothing  but  a  deep  relief,  and  even  an  excitement-  the  excitement  you  feel  when  a  door  closes  and  your  house  sinks  back  to  normaland  you  let  yourself  loose  into  all  the  free  space  around  you.  That  was  in  her  father's  voice,  too-behind  the  apology,  a  queer  little  sound,  al-  most  a  giggle.  But  Phemie's  mother  hadn't  been  a  burden-she  hadn't  been  sick  a  day-and,  far  from  feeling  re-  lieved  atthis  death,  her  father  was  to  take  it  hard.  He  never  got  used  to  livingalone,  so  he  said,  and  before  long  he  went  into  the  Home  for  the  Aged  quite  willingly.  He  told  Phemie  how  he  found  her  mother  on  the  couch  in  the  kitchen  when  he  came  in  at  noon.  She  had  picked  a  few  tomatoes  and  set  them  on  the  windowsill  to  ripen,  and  then  she  must  have  felt  weak  and  lain  down.  Now  his  voice  went  wobbly,  meander-  ing,  in  amazement.  Phemie  saw  in  her  mind  the  couch  and  theold  quilt  that  protected  it,  right  under  the  phone.  &quot;So  I  thought  I  better  call  you,  Phemie,&quot;  her  father  said,  and  waited  for  Phemie  to  say  what  he  should  do  now.  Phemie's  mother  prayed  on  her  knees  at  midday,  at  night,  and  first  thing  in  the  morning.  Every  day  opened  up  to  her  to  have  God's  will  done  in  it.  Every  night  she  totted  up  what  she'd  done  and  said  and  thought,  to  see  how  it  squared  with  Him.  Peo-  ple  might  think  that  kind  of  life  was  dreary,  but  Phemie  believed  they  were  missing  the  point.  For  one  thing,  such  a  life  can  never  be  boring.  And  noth-  ing  can  happen  to  you  that  you  can't  make  use  of.  Even  if  you're  racked  by  troubles,  and  sick  and  poor  and  ugly,  you've  got  your  soul  to  carry  through  life,  like  a  treasure  on  a  platter.
