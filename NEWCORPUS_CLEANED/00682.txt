We  have  little  detailed  knowledge  of  the  next  episode  in  Alegria's  story,  for  although  he  did  occasionally  consent  to  speak  of  what  had  happened  before  his  resurrection,  he  hardly  ever  said  a  word  about  how  he  made  his  way  from  Ar-  ganda  del  Rey  to  La  Acebeda-a  village  of  adobe  and  slate,  south  of  the  Somosierra  Pass,  which  hibernates  in  the  snow  all  winter  and  then,  in  the  first  mild  days  of  spring,  scatters  its  busy  inhabitants  like  red  lavender  across  the  fields. He  was  alive.  Alegria  later  spoke  of  that  moment  as  a  kind  of  birth. This  was  a  time  when  only  the  dead  did  not  inspire  fear.  Farmhands  finally  found  him  col-  lapsed  and  dying  in  the  fields  of  La  Acebeda,  and  at  first  believed  that  he  was  dead,  but  as  they  proceeded  to  re-  move  his  boots,  they  heard  a  plea  for  water  issue  from  his  bloody  head.  He  was  wearing  the  uniform  of  the  victori-  ous  army,  but  his  body  was  shuddering  in  the  throes  of  defeat.  We  now  know  that  various  options  were  considered,  from  burying  him  alive-since  he  might  have  been  shot  by  a  local-to  letting  him  die  among  the  rockroses  and  then  informing  the  authorities  that  a  body  had  been  found.  But  a  determined  old  woman  decided  to  give  him  the  water  he  was  begging  or  and  cleaned  his  face  with  her  skirt.  &quot;We  are  all  children  of  God,  even  his  kind,&quot;  she  said.  Hers  was  the  first  of  a  se-  ries  of  caring  acts  that  continued  for  three  days  and  succeeded  in  keeping  the  dead  man  alive.  Everything  was  conspiring  to  make  it  impossible  for  him  to  abandon  his  life  as  one  abandons  a  dream  upon  waking.  The  villagers  kept  him  there  among  the  rockroses,  partly  out  of  fear  and  partly  because  they  thought  that  hemight  die  if  moved.  They  dressed  his  wound  with  futile  ointments,  wrapped  him  in  a  blan-  ket,  gave  him  water  and  a  little  food.  We  now  know  that,  under  the  circumstances,  these  were  acts  of  prodigal  mercy,  and  Alegria  showed  his  gratitude  by  never  telling  anyone  their  names.  That  someone  should  approach  a  man  with  oozing  wounds,  a  man  en-  crusted  with  excrement  and  blood,  should  lift  his  head  and  gently  wet  his  lips,  patiently  feed  him  spoonfuls  of  a  soup  that  even  the  dead  could  digest,  and  speak  a  few  words  of  comfort-all  this,  he  thought,  was  proof  that  some-  thing  human  had  survived  the  ravages  of  war.  If  it  weren't  for  the  cracks  in  his  parched  lips,  Alegria  would  have  smiled.  That's  what  he  said  afterward.  He  also  told  the  nurses  who  cared  for  him  later  in  prison  that  while  he  had  lain  there,  tryingto  ignorethe  voice  of  the  earth  demanding  its  due,  what  had  tormented  him  most  was  not  the  fear  of  death  but  the  shame  of  being  seen  in  such  a  state  of  decay,  the  mortifying  thought  that  the  Good  Samaritans  of  the  village  might  smell  his  nauseating  breath  or  be  soiled  by  the  pus  seeping  from  his  wounds.  He  covered  himself  with  the  blanket  when  they  brought  him  food  and  would  not  let  anyone  come  too  close.It  seems  to  us  now  that  this  was  also  a  way  to  avoid  having  to  give  explanations.  The  fourth  day  dawned  dimly  through  fog,  and  the  blanket  was  so  soaked  with  dew  that  he  woke  riddled  with  fever.  That  hostile  place  was  tearing  at  his  life;  he  wanted  to  die  in  Huérmeces.  He  gathered  all  his  strength,  making  use  even  of  his  fevered  spasms  to  get  himself  moving.  After  carefully  folding  the  blan-  ket  to  show  that  he  was  grateful,  he  put  the  water  and  the  boiled  potatoes  back  into  the  bag  in  which  the  villagers  had  brought  them  and  set  off  for  his  town  beyond  the  mountains,  which  hid  their  ferocity  among  the  clouds.  He  started  climbing  toward  Somosierra. Dazed  by  fever,  Alegria  struggled  to  find  his  way,  climbing  the  slope  at  a  dis-  tance  from  the  road  so  as  not  to  be  seen  by  those  travelling  along  it-mostly  military  convoys,  transporting  supplies,  soldiers,  arms,  and  all  that  is  required  to  keep  an  occupied  territory  under  con-  trol.  Like  other  wars,  this  one  had  come  to  an  official  end  but  would  never  truly  be  resolved.  Civilian  vehicles  were  fewand  far  between;  the  ones  that  did  appear  had  likely  been  requisitioned.  Alegria  knew  that  anyone  who  could  move  freely  was  a  potential  enemy.  Not  that  those  who  re-  mained  in  one  place  could  be  counted  on  as  allies.  What  side  can  a  soldier  choose  when  he  has  simultaneously  won  and  lost  the  war?  And  yet,  although  he  wanted  to  hide,  he  dared  not  stray  too  far  from  the  road,  because  he  feared  that  his  strength  might  fail  him,  in  which  case  he  planned  to  lie  down  on  the  verge  in  the  hope  that  his  body  would  be  found  and  given  a  Christian  burial,  or  at  least  not  left  to  feedthe  wolves  and  wild  dogs  that  he  had  seen  on  the  prowl,  patiently  await,  ing  the  end  of  his  pilgrimage.  The  res-  urrection  of  the  flesh,  he  thought,  re-  quired  a  certain  tidiness  on  the  part  of  the  deceased,  but  all  he  had  left  was  re-  volting,  humiliating  decay.  His  stench  was  so  strong  that  it  could  not  be  ig-  nored,  despite  the  heather,  the  rock-  roses,  the  primroses,  and  the  thyme.  These  precautions  slowed  his  prog-  ress.  The  journey  lasted  three  days,  on  the  first  of  which  he  ate  the  potatoes  and  drank  the  water.  After  that,  in  the  cold  of  the  mountains,  he  had  only  the  bag  to  wrap  around  himself  at  night  and  to  protect  the  raw  wound  on  his  head  from  the  fierce  midday  sun.  Finally,  he  reached  Somosierra,  a  village  of  granite  and  slate  that  would  have  no  charm  if  it  weren't  for  the  sur-  rounding  landscape.  He  arrived  at  eve-  ning,  with  the  sun  setting  behind  him,  and  that  dense,  slanted  light  afforded  him  a  kind  of  cover,  allowing  him  to  approach  the  tollhouse,  which  had  been  converted  into  a  checkpoint.  There  they  were,  the  soldiers  of  the  army  that  had  won  the  final  battle,  with  the  uni-  forms,  boots,  jackets,  and  arms  that  he  had  distributed  for  so  many  years.  He  felt  neither  nostalgia  nor  remorse,  only  sadness.  For  hours,  he  watched  those  figures,  blurred  by  his  myopia.  When  darkness  fell,  the  soldiers  built  fires  to  light  the  road  and  keep  them  warm.  He  observed  a  perfunctory  changing  of  the  guard,  ex-  ecuted  with  a  lack  of  enthusiasm  that  bespoke  boredom  rather  than  victory. No,  all  they  want  is  to  go  home,  where  instead  of  getting  a  hero's  welcome  they'll  be  strangers  to  life,  cutoff  from  what  was  once  theirs,  and  over  time  they  will  sink  into  defeat.  They  will  become  one  with  those  who  lost  the  war,  separated  only  by  the  stigma  of  mutual  bitterness.  And,  like  the  los-  ers,  they  will  come  to  fear  the  real  victor,  who  defeated  both  the  enemy  army  and  his  own.  Only  a  few  dead  men  will  be  regarded  as  the  heroes  of  this  war.&quot;  Fever,  hunger,  and  disgust  at  his  own  state  must  have  quelled  these  thoughts,  and  even  his  memory,  for,  gathering  what  little  strength  he  had  left,  dragging  himself  along-at  this  point  he  could  not  even  wall-he  slowly  approached  the  checkpoint,  oblivious  of  the  soldiers'  shock  and  repulsion  at  the  sight  of  that  crawling  bundle  of  rags.
