 MOVING  DAY  their  sixteen  years  in  the  orchard  valley  they  couldn't  remember  a  better  one.  There  had  been  day  after  day  of  cloudless  blue  sky  with  just  a  hint  of  a  breeze  from  the  lake  to  take  the  edge  off  the  heat.  The  garden  had  excelled  itself,  beginning  with  a  spec-  tacular  display  of  daffodils,  iris,  and,  of  course,  the  tall  red  tulips,  which  caused  passing  motorists  to  stop  in  admiration.  Then  came  the  roses,  begonias,  dahl-  ias,  zinnias,  snapdragons,  daisies,  lilies,  and  now,  finally,  the  mums.  &quot;They're  blooming  their  hearts  out  for  us,&quot;  Mrs.  Webster  had  said.  &quot;They  seem  to  know  it's  our  last  summer.&quot;  And  now  the  last  day  of  this  last  summer  had  arrived.  They  would  spend  that  night  in  a  motel  in  town,  fly  to  Victoria  the  next  day,  spend  two  nights  in  a  hotel  there,  waiting  for  their  furniture  to  catch  up  with  them,  and  then  they  would  move  into  their  apart-  ment.  Fortunately  they  had  been  able  to  sell  the  furniture  they  wouldn't  have  room  for  to  the  people  who  had  bought  the  house,  though  they  hadn't  got  any-  thing  like  &quot;the  real  worth  of  it,&quot;  as  Mrs.  Webster  told  her  husband.  They  hadn't  seen  the  apartment  they  were  to  move  into.  It  had  been  found  for  them  by  their  son,  Stephen,  who  was  a  law-  yer  in  Victoria,  and  his  wife,  Bernice.  Their  son  and  daughter-in-law  had  suggested  that  Mrs.  Webster  fly  out  to  have  a  look,  but  she  said  to  her  husband,  &quot;After  living  in  this  house  forso  long  I  know  I'm  going  to  hate  it  anyway,  soI  might  just  as  well  hate  it  unseen  until  we  have  to  move  in.&quot;  Bernice  had  sent  them  a  floor  plan  and  the  colors  of  carpet  and  walls,  and  Mrs.  Webster  had  taken  an  hour  one  evening  to  sketch  in  the  furniture  they  could  use  and  then  put  the  whole  business  out  of  her  mind.  Mrs.  Webster,  at  eighty-two,  was  a  year  older  than  her  husband,  and  until  her  heart  attack  the  previous  autumn  had  believed  that,  come  what  might,  she  could  continue  to  copewith  house,  garden,  and  her  husband's  well-being  until  one  or  the  other  of  them  died.  Her  heart  attack  had  not  at  first  dis-  abused  her  of  this  notion,  but  after  her  return  from  the  hospital  she  realized,  with  bewilderment  turning  gradually  to  anger,  that  she  could  no  longer  look  after  things  in  the  way  that  things  should  be  looked  after.  The  house  got  dirty,  but  she  was  too  tired  to  do  more  IT  had  been  a  glorious  summer. Even  so,  for  a  time  she  tried  to  convince  herself  that  they  could  manage.  Then  two  events,  happening  within  a  week  of  each  other,  forced  on  her  the  bitter  realization  that  they  would  have  to  move.  The  first  of  these  was  the  death  of  Happy.  There  was  something  almost  symbolic  about  it.  Happy  was  a  West  Highland  terrier  that  they  had  bought  the  day  before  they  moved  into  the  house.  &quot;A  pup  is  one  thing  we  don't  need  until  we're  settled,&quot;  she  had  told  her  husband  at  the  time,  but  he  had  seen  it  in  the  window  of  a  pet  shop  and  had  not  only  bought  it  but  insisted  that  they  take  it  with  them  then  and  there.  &quot;It  will  bea  good  omen,&quot;  he  told  her.  She  knew  that,  feeling  about  animals  as  he  did,  he  couldn't  stand  to  see  the  tiny  creature  on  display  in  its  little  cage. He  had  helped  them  unpack  by  carrying  slippers  down  to  the  basement,  pulling  one  of  Mrs.  Webster's  dresses  from  a  hanger  and  then  making  a  mess  on  it,  tearing  the  hem  out  of  the  floor-length  living-room  curtains,  and  making  soggy  confetti  out  of  the  packing  paper.  He  had  grown  up  to  become  far  more  than  just  a  household  pet,  and  one  of  their  major  concerns  when  they  talked  of  moving  to  an  apartment-  never  really  thinking  they  ever  would-was  what  would  become  of  Happy.  They  would  consult  him  about  it  as  they  sat  sipping  the  remnants  of  their  home-  made  plum  wine  and  watch-  ing  the  dying  fire  just  before  they  went  to  bed.  The  various  medicines,  their  final  nightcap,  were  taken  in  the  kitch-  en.  Happy  made  deep  throaty  noises  and  half  opened  his  eyes  when  they  asked  his  advice.  Then,  one  morning  when  she  came  down  to  get  breakfast,  while  her  hus-  band  shaved,  she  found  Happy  lying  dead  in  his  box  beside  the  stove. She  didn't  call  out  to  her  husband,  and  when  he  came  down  her  tears  were  dry.  &quot;Happy  is  dead,&quot;  she  told  him.
