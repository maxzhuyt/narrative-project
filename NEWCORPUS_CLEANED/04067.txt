We  stared  at  the  closed  door.  The  closed  door  in  the  sitting  room  at  Head-  ing  came  into  my  mind.  I  don't  know  what  was  in  Noisy's  mind,  but  we  both  went  after  her.  And  at  once  we  heard  a  shout  from  her  across  the  yard.  &quot;Bloody  funny!&quot;  she  shouted  and  came  marching  in  a  fury  across  the  yard,  opened  the  door  of  Noisy's  car,  and  got  in  and  slammed  it.  Noisy  sauntered  up  to  her.  &quot;You  damn  well  drive  me!&quot;  she  shouted  at  him.  Noisy  turned  to  me,  shrugged,  and  beckoned  to  me,  but  I  was  staring  at  her  car.  Someone  had  let  the  air  out  of  the  two  back  tires.  Suddenly  I  heard  Noisy's  car  roar.  He  had  taken  off.  MRS.  BrAackETT's  car  stood  in  our  garage  at  home  for  three  weeks.  It  took  me  twenty  minutes  to  pump  up  its  tires,  and  where  she  and  Noisy  went  that  night  I  do  not  know.  I  chased  after  them.  They  weren't  at  Heading.  They  weren't  at  his  cottage.  Nor  in  the  days,  even  the  weeks,  that  followed.  The  damp  got  into  the  hostess  of  the  Argen-  tine  Air  Lines.  She  peeled  and  she  buckled  and  fell  over.  I  told  one  of  the  men  to  pitch  her  in  the  dustbin.  He  brought  the  key  hanging  on  the  back  to  me,  and  I  told  him  to  throw  that  away,  too.  On  second  thought,  I  broke  up  the  dummy  girl  myself.  Mother  said  nothing,  but  once  or  twice  she  goes  on  about  the  future-  the  usual  thing.  &quot;If  you  knew  what  it  was  going  to  be,  you'd  act  differently,&quot;  she  says.  &quot;People  ought  to  tell  you,  then  you'd  know,&quot;  she  says.  And  then  she  gets  on  to  Teddy  Longfellow's,  saying  there  isn't  any  future,  and  I  tell  her  I  agree  with  him.  A  few  weeks  ago,  Heading  came  up  for  sale.  Mother  says  the  class  of  trade  is  changing  in  our  town."
