Naturally,  when  we  moved  into  the  old  house,  we  were  the  source  of  un-  limited  curiosity,  but  people  were  too  shy  and  too  polite  to  ask  us  what  we  were  doing  there.  Jim  and  I  had  long  considered  spending  some  time  in  Ire-  land-we  are  both  of  Irish  descent-  and  we  wanted  to  do  it  before  we  were  too  old  to  withstand  what  we  knew  to  be  the  rigors  of  Irish  living.  So  in  the  spring  of  1950,  when  Jim  suddenly  found  himself  between  jobs,  we  hur-  riedly  rented  our  house  in  [REDACTED]  and  left.  A  real-estate  agent  in  Killar-  ney,  where  we  stopped  for  many  weeks  after  our  arrival,  directed  us  to  Dirreen.  The  part  of  the  valley  the  house  was  in  was  also  called  Dirreenfinhilid.  (The  name  means  &quot;little  oak  wood  of  fair  hue,&quot;  but  there  were  no  oak  trees  any  more-only  pines  and  apple  trees  around  our  house.)  It  was  a  kind  of  township,  though  there  seemed  to  be  no  political  or  economic  reason  for  its  existence,  and  no  geographical  boundary  that  we  could  see  between  it  and  the  adjoining  township,  Dirreendi-  roch.  The  valley  was  rather  barren.  A  few  farms  were  scattered  through  it,  and  some  perched  on  the  sides  of  the  sur-  rounding  mountains.  There  was  a  store  with  a  small  dance  hall  attached  to  it,  and  a  one-room  schoolhouse.  The  nearest  church  was  tenmiles  away,  in  Kenmare,  and  on  Sunday  the  valley  people  would  travel  there  in  pony  traps.  After  we  had  spent  about  three  weeks  in  Dirreen,  it  became  clear  to  our  neigh-  bors  that  we  were  not  trying  to  inter-  fere  with  their  farms  or  their  flocks  of  mountain  sheep,  and  the  community  went  to  work  fitting  us  in.  Jack  O'Don-  ovan,  our  nearest  neighbor  (he  lived  half  a  mile  away  and  grazed  his  cows  in  Dirreen's  fields),  was  the  first  to  visit  us.  Then  he  brought  other  neighbors  to  call.  During  the  long  sessions  passed  drinking  stout  before  the  marble  fire-  place  in  our  living  room,  it  would  come  out  that  one  of  our  visitors  would  be  glad  to  sell  us  chickens  or  eggs,  which  we'd  been  buying  at  the  store.  Then     that  visitor  would  return  several  eve-  nings  later  with  someone  else  in  tow,  who,  it  would  develop,  could  supply  us  with  some  other  commodity-butter,  perhaps,  or  potatoes,  or  turf.  In  this  way,  our  trade  was  parcelled  out  to  ev-  eryone's  satisfaction.  No  one  was  left  out  of  the  arrange-  ment  except  a  few  individuals  whom  the  community  had  been  happily  carrying  on  its  back  long  before  our  arrival.  Among  these  was  a  woman  named  Kathleen.  She  had  a  need  for  help  and  sympathy  that  was  as  boundless  as  the  community's  supply  was  endless.  Kath-  leen,  who  lived  a  few  miles  from  us,  was  brought  to  see  us  rather  late  in  the  round  of  visits.  She  was  only  in  her  mid-thir-  ties,  but  even  for  Ireland,  where  the  marks  of  work  and  privation  are  a  com-  mon  sight,  the  discrepancy  between  her  years  and  her  appearance  was  remark-  able.  She  was  hollow-chested  and  stoop-  shouldered;  above  her  gaunt  face,  her  thin  brown  hair  was  streaked  with  gray.  We  learned  from  Mrs.  Monaghan,  the  neighbor  who  first  brought  her  around,  that  some  years  earlier  Kath-  leen's  intended  had  been  killed  in  an  ac-  cident,  and  was  buried  on  the  very  day  they  were  to  have  been  married.  Ever  since,  Kathleen  had  sought  to  keep  her  life  at  that  pitch  of  high  tragedy.  She  would  almost  make  up  her  mind  to  &quot;go  for  a  nun.&quot;  Then  she  would  speak  wistfully  of  devoting  her  life  to  children-to  make  up  for  the  ones  she  would  never  have-  and  she  continually  refused  the  entreaties  of  a  suitor,  a  local  farmer,  who  had  loved  her  long  before  her  engagement  to  the  other  man.  'There  were  intervals  when  she  consid-  ered  saying  yes  to  him;  but  in  the  end  she  would  always  refuse.  The  agony  she  endured  when,  to  solace  himself,  he  periodically  took  to  the  drink  brought  her  renewed  compassion  from  all  the  countryside.  Nothing  that  happened  to  Kathleen  was  allowed  to  pass  as  an  ordinary  event.  When  death  struck  a  member  of  her  family,  even  though  the  deceased  was  a  great-aunt  in  her  nineties  or  a  fifth  cousin  who  had  spent  the  last  half  cen-  tury  in  [REDACTED],  Kathleen's  mourning  not  only  was  expected  to  exceed  all  normal  bounds  but  invariably  did.  And,  whatever  the  occasion,  the  entire  com-  munity  tended  and  nourished  her  sor-  row.  HEN  it  became  known  that  Jim  and  I  were  planning  a  trip  to  Glendalough,  the  famous  shrine  to  St.  Kevin,  one  of  Ireland's  favorite  saints,  the  neighbors  (not  one  of  whom  had  ever  been  able  to  visit  the  place  himself,  because  it  was  far  beyond  the  range  of  a  pony  cart)  instantly  began  inquiring  whether  there  would  be  room  for  Kath-  leen  in  our  car,  a  Vauxhall  we  had  bought  in  Killarney.  The  trip  would  do  her  so  much  good,  they  said.  Jim  and  I  were  quick  to  agree,  and,  through  Mrs.  Monaghan,  extended  our  invitation.  The  next  day,  Mrs.  Monaghan  re-  ported  that  Kathleen,  feeling  that  such  a  treat  was  not  her  lot,  had  declined.  We  would  have  let  it  go  at  that,  but  Mrs.  Monaghan  told  us  not  to  be  so  easily  discouraged.  During  the  next  few  days,  she  kept  us  minutely  informed  of  every  indication  that  Kathleen  might  yetbe  induced  to  accept.  After  a  week  of  solicitation,  Kathleen  gave  her  consent,  stipulating  only  that  her  friend  Mary  be  allowed  to  accompany  her.
