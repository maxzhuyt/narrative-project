Wherever  she  was,  the  shagreen  cigarette  case  and  the  gold  lighter  were  nearby.  Ashtrays  never  were.  He  got  up  and  fetched  one  before  Etta  could  do  so.  The  girls'  school  was  being  dis-  cussed-one  of  the  few  topics  the  two  mothers  had  in  common.  Mrs.  Lipp-  mann  had  never  taken  it  seriously.  She  laughed  at  the  uniform  and  despised  the  staff-an  attitude  she  might  at  least  have  hidden  from  her  daughter,  Mrs.  Salkeld  felt.  The  tea  trolley  was  being  wheeled  away  and  her  eyes  followed  the  remains  of  the  poppy-seed  cake.  She  had  planned  a  special  supper  for  Etta  to  return  to,  but  she  felt  now  that  it  was  no  use.  The  things  of  the  mind  had  left  room  for  an  echo.  It  sounded  with  every  footstep  or  spoken  word  in  that  house  where  not  enough  was  going  on.  She  began  to  wonder  if  there  were  things  of  the  heart  and  not  the  mind  that  Etta  fastened  upon  so  desperately  when  she  was  reading. People  had  thought  her  wonderful  to  have  made  her  own  life  and  brought  up  her  child  alone. It  may  speed  her  home-  ward,  thought  Mrs.  Lippmann,  playing  idly  with  her  charm  bracelet,  having  run  out  of  conversation.  When  Mrs.  Salkeld  rose  to  go,she  looked  round  the  room  as  if  to  fix  it  in  her  memory-the  setting  where  she  would  imagine  her  daughter  on  future  occasions. Mrs.  Salkeld,  driving  homeward  in  the  lowering  sun,  knew  that  Etta  had  tears  in  her  eyes.  &quot;I'm  glad  you  enjoyed  yourself,&quot;  she  said. On  the  dressing  table  was  a  jar  of  marigolds.  She  was  touched  by  this-just  when  she  did  not  want  to  be  touched.  She  turned  her  back  on  them  and  opened  her  case.  On  thetop  was  the  book  she  had  left  on  the  terrace.  Roger  had  brought  it  to  her  at  the  last  moment.  Taking  it  now,  she  found  a  letter  inside.  Simply  &quot;Etta&quot;  was  written  on  the  envelope.  Roger  had  felt  that  he  had  done  all  he  was  capable  of,  and  that  was  to  write  in  the  letter  those  things  he  could  not  have  brought  himself  to  say  even  if  he  hadhadan  opportunity.  No  love  letter  could  have  been  less  anticipated  and  Etta  read  it  twice  before  she  could  realize  that  it  was  neither  a  joke  nor  a  mistake.  It  was  the  most  extraordinary  happening  of  her  life,  the  most  in-  credible.  Her  breathing  grew  slower  and  deeper  as  she  sat  staring  before  her,  pondering  her  mounting  sense  of  pow-  er.
