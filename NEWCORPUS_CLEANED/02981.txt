Mother  took  a  perfumed  white  handkerchief  from  her  purse  and  of-  fered  it  to  Pablo,  but  he  did  not  take  it.  With  his  own  soiled  handkerchief  he  blew  his  nose.  After  a  long  silence,  he  turned  to  me  and  said,  &quot;And  Carlos,  how  is  he?&quot;  &quot;I  don't  know,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;He  phoned  a  couple  of  times.&quot;  Then,  in  unison,  Mother  and  I  said,  &quot;It's  all  over  with  Carlos!&quot;  A  cold  breeze  swept  the  yard.  As  so  often  happens  in  Bogoti,  the  sun  had  suddenly  retreated  toward  the  savanna,  leaving  a  gray,  metallic  sky.  The  yard  had  turned  silent.  A  few  of  the  prison-  ers  were  still  eating  slowly,  their  eyes  gazing  into  space.  Pablo  mumbled  something,  almost  to  himself.  I  could  make  out  &quot;I've  in-  flicted  ...&quot;  and  then  the  words  &quot;hu-  miliation&quot;  and  &quot;shame.&quot;  I  looked  at  his  eyes  behind  the  dark  glasses.  They  were  the  eyesof  Pablo,  the  little  boy  frightened  by  shadows  and  by  the  beasts  of  my  uncle's  hacien-  da.  The  eyes  shifted  back  and  forth  between  my  mother's  face  and  mine,  pleading.  I  looked  away.  After  a  prolonged  silence,  he  asked  me  for  a  cigarette  and  I  gave  him  an  unfinished  pack  I  was  carrying  in  my  purse.  I  watched  his  hands  as  he  lit  the  cigarette-so  pale  and  thin  they  were,  and  like  porcelain.  Suddenly  I  wanted  to  hold  my  brother's  hands,  to  say  ...  What  could  I  have  said?  What?  I  looked  at  his  drawn  face,  but  that  after-  noon  my  brother  was  a  stranger,  in-  spiring  only  silence.  A  loud  whistle  blew.  &quot;You  must  leave  now,  before  the  second  whistle,&quot;  Pablo  said.  He  took  my  mother's  arm  lightly  and  began  to  guide  us  toward  the  cor-  ridor.  From  his  pocket  he  took  a  piece  of  paper  folded  in  the  shape  of  an  envelope  and  gave  it  to  me.  &quot;Give  this  to  Amelia,  please.  Inside  are  two  let-  ters  for  the  little  girls.&quot;  Mother  was  fumbling  in  her  purse.  She  took  out  a  tiny  package  wrapped  in  white  silk  paper.  &quot;Here,  Pablo.  It's  a  medal  of  St.  Jude.  Wear  it,  dear.He'll  look  after  you.&quot;  Pablo  said,  &quot;Gracias,&quot;  and  dropped  the  package  in  his  pocket.  &quot;Go  now,  please-quickly,  before  the  others  begin  to  leave.&quot;  Mother  reached  up  and  kissed  his  cheek.  He  started  to  say  something,  but  it  was  interrupted  by  a  fit  of  coughing.  This  was  the  last  time  any  ofus  saw  Pablo.
