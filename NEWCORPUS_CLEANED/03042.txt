 CONGRESS  IN  CRISIS:  THE  PROXIMITY  BILL  HE  Ninety-third  Congress  con-  vened  in  January  in  very  poor  light,  overshadowed  as  it  was  by  the  President's  initiatives  abroad  and  beset  by  dark  omens  of  declining  pow-  er,  if  not  actual  impotence.  Among  members  of  both  parties,  there  was  general  agreement  that  Congress  must  act  soon  to  reassert  itself  as  a  sepa-  rate  and  equal  branch  of  the  national  government,  especially  since  Congress-  men  found,  upon  opening  their  Jan-  uary  pay  envelopes,  that  an  addition-  al  $25.75  had  beendeducted  under  &quot;Misc.&quot;  Calls  to  the  Treasury  Depart-  ment  failed  to  clear  up  the  matter,  largely  because  telephone  service  is  so  poor  on  the  Hill.  Most  members  of  Congress  have  been  put  on  four-party  lines  by  a  recent  Presidential  directive  aimed  at  curbing  inflation,  and  tele-  phone  static  is  very  heavy  on  cold  days  and  whenever  it  rains.  Nowhere  is  the  disparity  of  pow-  er  between  executive  and  legislative  branches  more  evident,  however,  than  in  the  area  of  personal  inviolability.  While  the  Presidency  is  whisked  away  to  Camp  David  without  an  unfriendly  eye  laid  on  it,  it  isn't  uncommon  to  see  the  legislative  branch  late  at  night  pac-  ing  the  [REDACTED]  sidewalk  and  calling  in-  to  the  darkness  for  acab.  Unlike  the  Presidency,  which  hasn't  been  touched  without  its  prior  knowledge  and  con-  sent  since  Sammy  Davis,  Jr.,  hugged  it  in  [REDACTED],  the  office  of  Congressman  is  a  lonely  one;  it  walks  crowded  hallways  and  waits  for  elevators  like  the  rest  of  us.  And  every  time  it  is  jostled  or  its  hand  shook  or  its  shoulder  grabbed,  a  little  bit  of  power  is  drained  away.
