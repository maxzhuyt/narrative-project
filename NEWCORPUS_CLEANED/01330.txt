Russell  lived  in  the  area  known  as  Mixerton,  which  took  its  name  from  the  Mixers,  a  utopian  society  that  lived  around  the  turn  of  the  century  and  broke  up  under  the  strain  of  constant  squabbling.  There  is  nothing  much  in  Mixerton  now  except  a  medical  clinic  and  some  houses.  Rain  fell  steadily.  Dan  sat  in  his  car  looking  at  Russell's  house.  The  wipers  went  back  and  forth  as  he  unscrewed  the  metal  lid  of  his  thermos.  Steam  rose  from  the  coffee.  Hunting  is  considered  recre-  ational,  but  when  you  get  up  in  the  dark,  the  act  acquires  unusual  gravity. Wildlife  Court  met  on  the  second  Tuesday  of  every  month,  pre-  sided  over  by  Ken  Hemphill,  a  retired  judge  and  permanently  tanned  outdoors,  man  who  ran  his  courtroom  in  the  affable  style  of  Curt  Gowdy  in  &quot;The  American  Sportsman.&quot;  In  the  summer  months  the  court  mainly  concerned  itself  with  fishing  violations,  but  dur-  ing  the  winter  most  of  the  defendants  were  young  or  middle-aged  menwho  had  been  keeping  illegal  traplines.  One  innovative  step  taken  by  Ken  Hemphill  had  been  to  order  the  sur-  render  of  any  trapline  deployed  with-  out  atrap  stamp,  and  defendants  were  accordingly  required  to  bring  their  traps  to  court  incase  the  verdict  should  go  against  them.  Thus  it  was  that  in  the  cold  months  Wildlife  Court  became  a  sort  of  purgatory  of  downcast  men  wear-  ing  Red  Wing  boots  and  orange  coats  and  clinking  their  chains  up  and  down  the  aisles  of  the  courthouse.  Dan  ordi-  narily  enjoyed  this  spectacle,  but  today  he  was  testifying.  Russell's  lawyer  was  Ned  Kuhlers,  a  mousy  man  who  represented  so  many  people  in  Grouse  County  that  he  more  or  less  ran  the  courtdocket,  and  when  he  went  on  vacation  the  system  slowed  to  a  crawl.  Ned's  strategy  was  simple.  He  tried  to  show  that  the  wit-  nesses  for  the  prosecution  could  not  know  what  they  claimed  to  know.  First  he  established  that  Bev  had  not  been  at  the  slough  and  so  could  not  say  firsthand  who  shot  what.  Her  response  was  that  of  the  flustered  citizen  trying  to  defend  her  common-sense  conclu-  sions  only  to  be  told  that  common  sense  has  no  place  in  the  judicial  sys-  tem.  &quot;But  he  told  me,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;We  had  just  finished  our  waffles,  and  he  said  that  he  had  shot  this  bird  that  he  could  not  identify.  I  mean,  I  suppose  that's  hearsay,  but,  for  heaven's  sakes,  it's  hearsay  from  the  guy  who  did  the  shooting.&quot;  &quot;I'm  afraid  I  object  to  that,&quot;  Ned  said.  Judge  Hemphill  chuckled  softly.  &quot;Overruled,&quot;  he  said.  Dan  took  the  stand  next.  On  a  signal  from  Ned,  the  bailiff  went  out,  return-  ing  several  minutes  later  with  a  large  stuffed  bird.  There  was  a  moment  of  sur-  prise  as  Ned  held  the  bird  up  before  the  members  of  the  jury.  The  jury  foreman,  who  had  seemed  uncertain  of  his  role  throughout  the  trial,  said,  &quot;Excellent  taxidermy.&quot;  &quot;Sheriff,&quot;  said  Ned.  &quot;You  have  testi-  fied  that  Russell  shot  a  waterfowl.  I  would  ask  you  now  to  look  very  carefully  at  this  example,  which  comes  to  us  courtesy  of  the  folks  at  the  Stone  City  Museum  of  Natural  History.Please  take  your  time,  because  this  is  impor-  tant.  Is  this  the  species  of  bird  that  Russell  shot?&quot;  Dan  looked  at  the  bird.  It  was  gray  and  spindly,  with  a  glazed  expression,  and  a  red  mark  over  the  eye.  The  courtroom  was  hushed.  &quot;I  don't  know,&quot;  said  Dan.  &quot;In  other  words,&quot;  said  Ned,  &quot;it  might  be,  or  it  might  not  be?  Don't  look  at  Bev.  We  want  your  opinion,  Sheriff.Isn't  it  true  that  you  don't  know  what  kind  of  bird  Russell  shot?&quot;  &quot;I'm  not  an  ornithologist,&quot;  Dan  said.  &quot;I  think  the  two  birds  are  similar.  But  whether  it  was  this  exact  one,  no,  I  don't  know.&quot;  &quot;Sheriff,&quot;  said  Ned  Kuhlers,  &quot;what  if  I  were  to  tell  you  that  this  is  a  sandhill  crane;  that  he  spends  his  winters  in  [REDACTED]  and  [REDACTED];  that  there  is  virtually  no  way  a  sandhill  crane  could  have  been  in  Lapoint  Slough  on  that  Novem-  ber  day?&quot;  &quot;What  does  that  prove?&quot;  said  Dan.  &quot;What  indeed,&quot;  said  Ned.  &quot;The  de-  fense  rests.&quot;  The  jury  deliberated  all  morning  and  all  afternoon.  At  twelve-thirty,  they  were  givena  lunch  of  deli  sandwiches,  coleslaw,  and  chips.  At  three,  they  re-  quested  a  snack  and  received  two  bags  of  pretzels  and  some  Rolos.  At  four-  thirty,  a  message  came  from  the  fore-  man  informing  Judge  Hemphill  that  one  of  the  jurors  was  on  a  salt-free  diet  and  that  this  should  be  kept  in  mind  when  ordering  the  evening  meal.  At  this  point,  Russell  entered  a  plea  of  nolo,  or  no  contest,  and  Ken  Hemphill  called  the  jury  in  and  told  them  they  were  free  to  go  home  and  make  their  own  suppers.  \xc2\xae  "
