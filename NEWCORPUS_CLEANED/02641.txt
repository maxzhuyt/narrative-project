Our  house  stood  on  a  cascade  of  cement  and  grass  thrown  up  like  the  wake  of  a  ship.  I  oc-  cupied  the  bridge,  perched  above  a  col-  umned  porch  at  the  frontof  the  house,  and  I  steered  the  ship  through  the  neighborhood  among  tugs  and  native  boys. One  night  as  the  crew  slept,  my  brother  Jim  scaled  the  porch  and  en-  tered  by  a  window  like  a  pirate,  while  I  lay  terrified  under  the  covers.  I  sold  that  house  a  few  years  ago.  My  parents  had  died;  I  had  outgrown  it,  and  it  had  become  an  empty  capsule.  The  echo  of  my  brother's  voice  had  outflown  that  shell;  his  footfalls  had  been  absorbed  by  the  ground;  the  bits  of  paint  he  scattered  here  and  there  had  been  cov-  ered;  the  neighbors  re-  member  him  and  his  es-  capades  only  faintly.  Jim  was  born  in  1921,  within  a  year  of  my  parents'  marriage;  I  was  born  eight  years  later,  less  than  a  year  before  the  Black  Thursday  of  the  stock  market,  and  I  remember  having  the  uneasy  feeling  that  I  had  slipped  in  under  the  wire.  I  saw  very  little  of  my  brother.  His  friends  tolerated  me,  but  with  effort.  I  had  the  virtue  of  being  quiet  and  I  was  not  fat,  but  these  were  hardly  enough  to  compensate  for  the  eight-  year  gap  and  the  fact  that  I  sweated  copiously.  Jim's  room  was  arranged  so  that  he  stood  sideways  to  the  door  while  he  combed  his  hair  before  awall  mirror. He  was  a  hero  in  our  neighbor-  hood  for  having  saved  my  life  when  I  was  seven. I  swung  for  a  moment  twenty  feet  in  the  air,  with  the  bricks  in  my  face  and  the  whole  world  in  my  wrists,  un-  til  he  pulled  me  back  in.  I  havenever  been  able  to  convince  anyone  of  the  truth:  He  pushed  me  out.                  MOTHER  and  I  spent  three  weeks  each  summer  at  Rismon's  Resort  on  a  sandy  shore  in  birch  and  pine  of  northernWisconsin,  Dad  and  Jim  usually  joined  us  after  the  first  week.  Life  was  pinched  down  to  the  seed  at  Rismon's:  plumbing  was  accomplished  with  heavy  pitchers  and  porcelain  bowls  and  slop  jars;  fish  were  scaled  on  a  board  in  the  woods  and  then  stored  under  dark  sawdust  in  the  ice-  house;  weekends  were  marked  only  by  afternoon  he   the  arrival  of  new  guests  or  by  a  soft-  ball  game.  Before  Jim  arrived  on  the  scene  at  Rismon's  my  days  were  spent  fishing  in  a  small  rowboat  or  reading  on  a  vacant  island  across  the  bay,  but  when  he  arrived  I  traded  these  for  snake-  skinning,  mapping  the  lake  bottom,  rid-  ing  horses,  and  exploring  abandoned  railroad  beds.  Dad  introduced  us  to  trout  fishing.  He  had  acquired  the  knowledge  as  a  child  in  [REDACTED],  and  although  he  remembered  the  procedure  he  had  lost  the  art.  We  learned  the  procedure  but  could  not  teach  each  other  the  art,  be-  cause  we  were  at  the  three  corners  of  a  triangle:  Dad  went  to  be  at  the  stream;  I  went  to  entice  the  fish;  Jim  went  as  an  apprentice  hunter,  delighted  to  be  with  us  and  eager  for  the  kill.  We  pursued  trout  water  in  our  Lincoln  Zephyr,  which  I  remember  as  a  tan  mouse  running  with  its  nose  to  the  ground.  We  pushed  that  old  Lincoln  along  rudimentary  roads,  racking  its  belly  on  rocks,  plunging  the  wheels  into  the  sand  and  over  log  bridges  that  promised  disaster  but  always  held,  peer-  ing  through  the  bushes  for  a  glimpse  of  water.  Mother's  role  in  these  expedi-  tions  was  to  be  nervous.  She  never  failed,  and  her  tension  seemed  to  be  the  drive  that  sent  us  forward.  Jim  responded  with  unalloyed  pleasure,  and  she  spentyears  seeking  ways  to  make  him  nervous  in  retaliation. Shortly  after  the  war,  Jim  joined  our  father's  engineering  partnership,  and  the  symbiosis  of  father  and  son  was  wonderful  to  watch.  The  two  men  talked  endlessly  about  current  and  future  jobs,  schedules,  techniques,  pour-  ings,  abutments,  freezing  weather, contractors,  and  overtime.  Their  con-  versations  had  one  virtue  in  Mother's  eyes:  Jim  was  a  good  sounding  board,  and  his  arguments  allayed  her  fear  that  her  husband  was  going  off  the  deep  end  of  a  dream.  I  listened.  Jim  and  I  seldom  talked  of  my  studies  in  astronomy.  He  was  interested  in  my  career,  but  he  did  not  enjoy  abstractions-neither  principles  of  en-  gineering  design  nor  the  structures  of  stars.  His  world  was  the  here-now  and  the  here-tomorrow.  He  never  looked  at  the  past,  or  sensed  a  world  beyond  his  own,  because  he  could  not  live  it  and  could  not  change  it-nor  could  it  change  him.  Jim  knew  how  to  speak  eloquently  with  small  gestures,  to  express  love  in  subtle  ways,  to  laugh,  to  drink  quarts  of  beer,  to  work  until  exhausted  and  then  sleep  on  and  on  until  revived.  His  favorite  aphorisms  were:  anything  worth  doing  is  worth  doing  to  excess;  no  train  is  worth  catching  unless  you  have  to  run  for  it.  I  never  saw  him  walk  to  a  train.  One  morning  in  Greenwich  Village  near  his  apartment,  we  sat  at  breakfast  in  a  diner,  Jim  nodded  toward  the  pretty  girls  walking  by  the  window  and  said  that  if  he  couldn't  persuade  me  to  move  to  New  York  perhaps  the  girls  could.  I  was  grateful  for  that  remark;  it  was  a  statement  of  friendship  and  a  promise.  He  was  reaching  toward  me.  JIM’S  death  took  us  all  by  surprise,  and  we  felt  it  must  have  taken  him  by  surprise  as  well.  I  hope  it  did,  be-  cause  to  know  that  the  next  minute  is  your  last  and  that  the  world  will  be  taken  away  from  you-this  thought  brings  to  me  a  sadness  I  can  almost  smell.  While  Jim  was  dying  in  the  ocean,  Mother  was  preparing  to  celebrate  her  birthday.  I  was  driving  with  my  wife  across  New  York  State  to  visit  her  parents.  They  told  me  at  breakfast;  and  as  Isat  in  their  enamelled  white  kitchen  chair  my  skin  went  numb,  and  the  numbness  crept  slowly  inward,  and  the  room  froze  Jim  had  gone  scuba  diving  outside  the  coral  reefs  surrounding  Nassau,  leaving  sunglasses  and  a  pile  of  clothes  on  the  sand  next  to  his  wife  and  son.  He  never  reémerged  from  the  water.  I  joined  Mother  in  [REDACTED],  and  when  my  father  returned  from  Nassau  with  my  sister-in-law  and  her  older  child  it  was  a  gray,  Sunday-seeming  Tuesday  afternoon.  He  entered  the  living  room  where  we  sat  with  a  few       friends  and  came  to  anchor  like  an  immense  ship.  He  was  exhausted  and  had  little  to  tell,  so  we  rode  quietly  at  anchor  all  afternoon,  making  plans  for  ways  to  kill  time  during  the  next  few  days.  Jimwas  unburied,  uncremated,  simply  washed  away-back  into  the  sea.  The  police  had  told  Dad  that  Jim  had  probably  been  attacked  by  a  shark-&quot;attacked&quot;  was  the  word  they  used,  to  avoid  the  more  explicit  truth:  eaten.  We  each  had  our  own  vision  of  Jim's  death.  ...  .  I  see  my  father,  sitting  in  a  small  sunlit  airplane,  peering  through  a  scratched  plastic  window  down  onto  the  bright  sand  and  brilliant  water-  looking  for  his  son,  half  hoping  to  find  nothing,  fearing  to  find  only  remnants  of  loved  flesh  rolled  gently  by  the  windblown  waves.  That  was  sixteen  years  ago,  and  I  am  already  eight  years  older  than  Jim  was  the  day  he  died.
