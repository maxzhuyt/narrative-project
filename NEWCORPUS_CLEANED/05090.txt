Not  all  doctors  wear  beards  nowadays,  and  few  farmers  chew  straws,  but  there  are  marks  to  identify  both  these  and  other  professions,  to  the  point  where  one  might  almost  think  that  each  occupa-  tion  imposes  its  own  habits  and  man-  nerisms.  (When  I  lived  in  [REDACTED],  for  example,  the  most  farmerish  farmer  I  knew-even  down  to  the  seamed,  weather-beaten  face,  the  raw,  bony  wrists,  and  the  nasal  intonation-was  a  former  insurance  broker  from  Brook-  lyn;  changing  jobs,  he  had  somehow  changed  attitudes,  too,  that  was  all.)  This  extends-as  Fred  Barclay,  for  one,  discovered-to  the  criminal  trades  as  well,  and  here  it  seems  as  if  some  really  baneful  influence  was  at  work.  For  these  are  fields  where  conformity  to  a  common  pattern  is  a  handicap,  and  dangerous-and  yet  who  hasn't  been  struck  by  how  often,  in  life  asin  the  movies,  the  gangster  betrays  himself  by  his  tight-belted  topcoat  and  snap-brim  hat,  the  pickpocket  by  his  furtive  alertness,  and  the  robber  by  his  burly  as-  sertiveness?  Is  it  simply  the  instinct  for  conformity  that's  at  work  here,  so  strongly  that  it  overrides  everything  else?  Or  does  the  way  of  life  carry  its  own  identification,  so  that  the  thievingness  of  the  thief,  say,  must  reveal  itself,  no  matter  how  hard  the  thief  himself  tries  to  conceal  it?  Fred  Barclay,  at  any  rate,  tried  to  get  outside  the  norm.
