However,  by  the  time  we  reached  Abu  Tor  (we  were  heading  for  the  Old  City)  Rose  had  al-  ready  put  me  on  the  spot.  &quot;I  can't  believe  it,&quot;  she  began.  &quot;You've  been  going  around  town  asking  everybody  if  you  should  marry  me.  Do  you  realize  how  humiliating  that  is?&quot;  I  was  nonplussed.  &quot;How  do  you  know  that?&quot;  I  asked,  which  was  a  mis-  take-I  should  have  gone  for  flat  denial.  &quot;First,  your  shrink  has  a  mouth  the  size  of  the  Grand  [REDACTED]. I  had  known  for  a  longtime  that  where  love  is  concerned,  feelings  can't  be  fought  with  reason,  so  I  decided  to  begand  plead.  By  this  time  we  had  reached  Jaffa  Gate.  I  was  saying  some-  thing  that  had  no  chanceof  being  persuasive-&quot;Your  father  thinks  we  should  live  together  for  a  year&quot;-when  I  caught  sight  of  Ahmed  out  of  thecorner  of  my  eye.  He  was  standing  watching  two  men  play  backgammon.  I  called  his  name  across  the  square.  When  he  looked  up  I  gestured  toward  Rosie  as  if  to  say,  &quot;See,  she  did  come  back.&quot;  Ahmed  looked  right  through  me  (Morris  must  have  skinned  him  terribly  in  the  bargain-  ing),  then  he  turned  toward  the  wall,  where  he  had  laid  down  his  equipment,  and  began  to  unwind  his  ropes.  A  sharp  shaft  of  sunlight  pierced  Jaffa  Gate,  like  a  diamond-cutter's  knife,  and  cut  me  off  from  Rose."
