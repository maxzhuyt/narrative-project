Who,  forinstance,  would  have  thought  that  the  head  of  a  successful  business  had  such  a  passion  for  sugar?  Yet  during  the  war,  when  sugar  was  rationed  in  Ireland,  Celia,  who  was  a  bit  of  a  Jansenist,  had  felt  herself  bound  to  give  up  sugar  and  divide  her  ration  between  Ned  and  Jimmy,  then  quite  a  small  boy.  And,  even  at  that,  Ned  con-  tinued  to  suffer.  He  did  admire  her  self-  denial,  but  he  couldn't  help  feeling  that  so  grandiose  a  gesture  deserved  a  better  object  than  Jimmy.  It  was  a  matter  of  scientific  fact  that  sugar  was  bad  for  Jimmy's  teeth,  and  anything  that  went  wrong  with  Jimmy's  teeth  was  going  to  cost  his  father  money.  Ned  felt  it  un-  fair  that  in  the  middle  of  a  war,  with  his  salary  frozen,  Celia  should  inflict  additional  burdens  on  him.  Most  of  the  time  he  managed  to  keep  his  dignity,  though  he  could  rarely  sit  down  to  a  meal  without  an  angry  glance  at  Jimmy's  sugarbowl. As  a  philosopher,  Ned  won-  dered  that  he  should  resent  this  so  deeply,  but  resent  it  he  did.
