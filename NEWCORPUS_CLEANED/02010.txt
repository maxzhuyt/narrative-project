Kids  were  loung-  ing  on  the  steps  of  the  libraries  to  catch  the  last  of  the  waning  sun,  and  Frisbee  players  shouted  and  leaped  on  the  grass  around  the  freshman  houses.  In  a  single  sunny  afternoon  the  mira-  cle  had  occurred  that  always  galva-  nizescollege  campuses:  summer,  the  shining  irresponsible  season  that  lies  beyond  the  barrier  of  term  papers  and  exams,  had  come  into  view,  I  shifted  my  knapsack  on  my  shoulders  and  re-  flected  that  summer  meant  commence,  ment  for  Curry,  who  would  then  take  off  on  his  peculiar  pilgrimage  to  eat  feyjoada  among  the  Bahians,  or  what-  ever  he  intended  to  do.  For  me,  the  summer  meant  the  beginning  of  a  time  without  my  friend.  This  was  some-  thing  I  thought  of  unhappily,  but  without  tremendous  perturbation,  be-  cause  I  had  the  feeling  that  no  matter  what  happened  I  would  always  be  run-  ning  into  Curry-that  our  particular  bond  of  fascination  and  repulsion  would  bounce  us  together  to  resume,  again  and  again,  a  dialogue  that  would  always  seem  familiar  but  never  dull.  We  walked  through  the  Yard  and  paused  at  one  of  the  gateways,  where  a  friend  of  mine,  a  tall  Jamaican  kid  named  Hunter,  was  passing  out  leaflets  praising  revolutionary  activity  in  the  Third  World.  Across  the  street  nine  Hari  Krishnas,  looking  uncharacteris-  tically  buoyant  and  chipper  in  the  bright  spring  afternoon,  were  hopping  and  chanting  in  front  of  the  Harvard  Coop.  Curry  had  to  go:  Philippa  was  taking  him  to  see  &quot;Yojimbo,&quot;  and  I  had  to  camp  out  in  the  library  tofinish  an  overdue  paper  on  Yeats.  When  I  had  said  goodbye  and  started  walking  up  toward  the  Rad-  cliffe  dorms,  I  heard  footsteps  behind  me,  and  turned  around  tofind  Curry,  out  of  breath  from  the  run.  &quot;Hey,  please  don't  throw  away  those  pic-  tures!&quot;  he  said,  taking  off  his  glasses,  which  had  gotten  fogged  with  perspiration,  and  raking  his  fingers  through  his  dark  curls,  which  were  standing  up  even  more  wildly  than  usual.  With  his  glasses  off,  his  little  tear-shaped  birthmark  was  clearly  visible,  and  the  sight  of  it  was  enough  todissolve  any  trace  of  the  irritation  and  grief  I  had  felt  in  the  darkroom.  &quot;At  least  hang  on  to  the  nega-  tives,&quot;'  he  went  on.  \xc2\xab'Maybe  I  can  try  to  shoot  you  again.  I'd  like  to  have  something  to  work  with.&quot;  I  promised  him  I  would  keep  everything  -but  in  fact  at  the  first  trash  can  I  came  to,  on  Garden  Street,  I  had  a  change  of  heart,  and  tossed  in  the  manila  envelope,negatives  and  all.  When  we  were  hanging  outtogether  in  the  next  few  weeks,  I  was  afraid,  once  or  twice,  that  he  might  try  to  get  the  pictures  back  from  me,  but  he  never  mentioned  them  again.  That  summer,  after  Curry  gradu-  ated  and  I  had  come  home  to  Philadel-  phia  for  two  months,  my  mother,  with  the  indiscreet  eagerness  that  sometimes  overwhelms  even  tactful  parents,  asked  if  there  was  &quot;anything  romantic&quot;  be-  tween  Curry  and  me.  &quot;&quot;Nota  chance,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;'There  are  chances  for  all  kinds  of  things,&quot;  she  saidcoyly.  &quot;Not  for  that.&quot;  At  that  point,  I  didn't  feel  sure  of  much  in  my  life,  but  for  once  I  felt  I  had  said  something  that  was  absolutely  true."
