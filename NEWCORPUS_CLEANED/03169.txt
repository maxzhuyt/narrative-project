  ONE  OF  OURS  F  all  my  early  boyhood  friends,  only  Jacko  Keating  still  bothers  to  keep  in  touch.  Twice  or  three  times  a  year,  generally  of  a  win-  ter  Saturday,  he  invites  me  to  bring  my  gun  and  walk  his  fields  and  the  parcel  of  mixed  woodland  he  owns  beyond  the  river  boundary  of  his  farm.  We  shoot  a  few  of  whatever  wood  pigeons  might  be  flying,  and  some-  times  in  the  final  daylight  we  get  a  rabbit  or  two  that  he  skins  and  cleans  for  me  to  take  home.  The  birds  we  leave  wherever  they  fall,  once  we  are  sure  they  are  dead.  They  are  a  serious  pest  to  his  crops,  and  he  has  to  keep  them  down;  but  though  he  grumbles  about  them,  as  a  good  farmer  must,  I  know  he  always  likes  to  see  a  reason-  able  number  of  them  about  his  land.  Half  a  dozen  pairs  or  so  are  worth  their  keep,  he  reckons,  for  the  pleasure  their  strong,  direct  flight  gives  him  after  the  summer  birds  have  gone;  he  wouldn't  be  without  that  soft-throated  cooing  among  the  spring  branches,  or  those  sudden  arisings  out  of  the  dark  crowns  of  elms,  like  the  clatter  of  hail  on  a  shingled  roof.  It's  Jacko's  own  trees  that  harbor  his  birds.  They  glide  down  to  gorge  themselves  on  his  peas  and  grain  and  the  tender  watercress  that  grows  in  the  bluish  shallows  under  his  banks;  and  then,  their  chests  ballooning,  they  browse,  half  hidden  in  pasture  or  the  clover  leys.  That  is  in  summer,  when  they  do  most  harm  and  when  he  leaves  them  alone.  Just  before  Christmas,  or  in  the  first  weeks  of  the  new  year,  we  thin  them  out  as  they  fly  up  from  the  bright-brown  shives.  &quot;You'd  never  be  able  to  get  them  all,&quot;  he  says.  But  I  know  he  could.  Jacko's  one  of  the  finest  shots  in  Sussex-the  only  man  I  know  who's  ever  taken  a  right  and  left  of  woodcock-and,  if  he  wanted  to,  he  could  wipe  out  every  last  wood  pigeon  on  his  farm.  He's  content,  though,  if  we  pick  off  a  pair  between  us  out  of  each  dense  gray  cloud  we  flush.  The  rest  make  for  cover;  we  see  where  they  settle,  and  then  we  close  on  them  again. His  gun  arm  swings  in  a  graceful,  economical  arc,  the  safety  catch  double-clicks,  his  left  heel  jabs  hard  into  the  earth  before  the  bird  has  time  to  jink  or  dip  or  climb,  and  he  fires,  and  I  see  the  wings  stopped  in  mid-beat  and  the  bundle  drop  fast  as  a  brick.  Jacko  nearly  always  kills  clean-  ly;  when  his  pigeons  fall,  they  look  as  though  they  could  never  havebeen  alive,  so  far  off  do  they  seem  in  their  stillness.  They  appear  for  all  the  world  like  those  realistic  decoy  pigeons  that  sit  on  cartridge  belts  in  gunsmiths'  win-  dows.  But  mine  are  no  fakes.  Some-  times  they  limp  wounded  into  some  bush,  where  they  hang  twitching,  pathetic,  unbearable  to  look  at,  like  bits  of  a  sodden  pillow  that  somebody  has  left  to  rot  and  collapse  in  wind  and  rain;  underfeathers  fall,  snagging  on  thorns.  We  run  clumsily  along  the  fur-  rows  to  put  an  end  to  them.  ND  it's  then,  lumbering  out  of  breath  behind  Jacko,  that  I  re-  call  most  vividly  those  wartime  Satur-  days  we  seldom  talk  about.  Nearly  thirty  years  fall  away  as  I  see  him  jump  down  from  a  stile,  both  arms  outstretched  for  sec-  onds  after  he's  steady  again  on  his  feet.  It's  a  manner-  ism  he's  never  lost.  For  the  rest  of  the  day,  as  we  trudge  together  through  his  hollow  lanes  of  black  leaves  and  up  the  slippery  paths  of  exposed  chalk,  I  shall  be  thinking  of  Little  Jacko  (as  he  was  while  his  father  was  alive)  and  myself,  aged  eight,  nine,  ten,  and  of  another  time  that  would  come  years  later.  Jacko,  I  am  sure,  is  remembering,  too.  'There  are  signs  I  notice,  things  he  says,  that  make  me  certain.  These  are  the  same  folds  in  the  South  Downs  that  we  scoured  after  the  Battle  of  [REDACTED]  for  scattered  bits  of  crashed  planes. I  wonder  whether  to  make  him  grin  some  more  by  taking  cover  under  a  holly  or  by  lobbing  a  flint  as  though  it  were  a  gre-  nade;  but  before  I  have  a  chance  to,  he's  lighting  his  pipe  or  reloading  or  moaning  about  his  subsidies.  Certainly,  though,  he's  still  remembering  as  we  pad  along  close  to  his  hedges  toward  the  next  party  of  birds,  and  after  we've  put  them  up  and  shot  into  them.  A  few  years  back,  when  cartridges  started  to  be  packed  in  plastic  cases,  it  became  my  habit  to  pick  them  up  after  they'd  been  ejected,  smoking,  from  the  breech;  they  wouldn't  disintegrate  in  the  wet  like  the  old  cardboard  ones,  and  I  didn't  like  to  leave  the  bright-pink,  in-  destructible  cylinders  littering  up  his  trim  headlands.  But,  &quot;Let  them  lie,&quot;  he  saidto  me  one  day.  &quot;The  kids  like  to  find  them  and  take  them  home  to  play  with-you  should  know.&quot;  Sometime  I  must  ask  him  if  he  still  has  any  of  those  pieces  of  Spitfire  and  Messerschmitt  we  used  to  hoard  away-misshapen  lumps  that  Jacko  and  I  and  dozens  of  small  boys  like  us  rummaged  out  of  long  grass  some-  times  several  fields  away  from  where  the  crippled  planes  had  spun  down  into  their  flames.  Have  &amp;/l  our  treasures  been  thrown  out,I  wonder;  or  do  some  of  them  still  lie  in  rusting  tin  boxes  in  cupboards  that  are  never  opened,  or  in  the  unswept  corners  of  disused  yards?  In  my  memory,  they  shine  as  bright  and  mint  as  the  mornings  we  scrabbled  them  out  of  brambles  or  a  fox's  earth  and  polished  them  against  our  jerseys-curlicues  of  pitted  alumi-  num  that  wore  holes  in  our  pockets,  a  rusk  of  charry  rubber  to  take  out  and  smell  during  the  mental-arithmetic  test,  a  severed  bolt  with  a  Whitworth  thread  that  rattled  in  a  pencil  box.  Were  we  made  to  throw  them  all  away,  every  one  of  them?  Mrs.  Ashe,our  teacher,  told  us  it  was  wrong  to  scavenge  where  men  had  died,  and  taught  us  a  new  word,  &quot;ghoul,&quot;  which  she  wrote  on  the  blackboard  and  made  us  learn  to  spell.  We  couldn't  expect  her  to  understand  how,  in  our  imaginations,  those  ugly  tokens  were  lucky  talismans  for  us.  Strangely  comforting,  they  grew  warm  in  our  palms  and  their  rough  edges  were  smoothed  with  long  rubbing.  We  fashioned  crucifixes  out  of  windscreen  Perspex  with  our  penknives,  and  when  we'd  polished  the  sharpness  away  (there  was  a  special  wall  of  an  air-raid  shelter  we  used  for  this)  we  bored  a  hole  with  a  red-hot  needle  for  a  neck-  lace  of  fine  wire.  Are  there  any  moth-   ers  still  alive  who  have  one  such  in  a  trinket  box?  And  how  many  hearths  are  there  left  where  poker  and  fire  tongs  are  placed  in  gleaming  brass  shell  cases  that  the  likes  of  Jacko  and  me  brought  down  from  the  hills?  There  have  never  been  such  unfussy,  practical  receptacles.  And  what  perfect  head  scarves  we  made  from  the  little  silk  parachutes  that  floated  down  some  nights,  lost,  gently  depositing  flares  onto  the  sheep  runs!  Y  early  afternoon,  we've  usually  reached  the  topof  Jacko's  woods.  He's  got  a  small  wooden  hut  up  there,  where  he  keeps  a  few  tools  and  fence  posts.  Inside  it  we  find  enough  pieces  of  timber  to  improvise  a  table  and  a  couple  of  seats,  and  we  spend  an  hour  over  our  " b"38  lunch,  exchanging  news.  The  hut  serves  as  a  useful  hide.  While  we  eat  and  talk,  we  keep  an  eye  open  for  birds  that  fly  down,  and  our  guns  lean,  load-  ed,  against  the  door.  Not  that  we've  ever  disturbed  ourselves  to  do  more  than  put  down  our  bread  and  cheese  and  sit  alert  and  silent  for  a  moment,  staring  out.  &quot;Let  him  be  for  another  few  minutes,&quot;  Jacko  says.  &quot;Look  at  the  fat  thing,  begging  to  be  shot  at.  Well,  he'll  just  have  to  wait  till  I'm  good  and  ready.&quot;  It  soon  becomes  warm  and  snug,  and  we  delay  going  out  into  the  cold  again.  We  count  how  many  shells  we  have  left  between  us  and  divide  them  equally.  Jacko  falls  asleep.  I  watch  for  any  flocks  of  other  people's  wood  pigeons  that  might  be  moving  from  field  to  field  up  the  hill  toward  Jacko's  land,  so  that  we  can  go  after  them  when  he's  awake. Whenev-  er  I  dig  in  my  garden,  I  recall  old  Diddler  Collins  telling  me  of  how  his  great-grandfather  worked  for  an  angry  Scotsman  who  kept  a  kennel  of  grey-  hounds  in  the  corner  where  my  bud-  dleia  grows. I  hope  that  Jacko  will  tell  the  newcomers  about  the  Christ-  mas  we  were  eighteen.  They  ought  to  know.  We'd  got  deep  into  the  wood,  as  far  as  where  the  ground  begins  to  slope  up.  Old  Jacko  held  the  shafts,  and  Little  Jacko  and  I  shouldered  a  wheel  each.  Inside  the  cart,  our  axes  were  rattling  with  the  knives  and  shears.  It  wasn't  very  late,  but  the  day  was  dark  and  lower-  ing,  and  we  could  scarcely  see  fifty  yards  into  the  trees  right  and  left.  That  year  there  wasn't  much  berry,  All  we  had  was  a  small  bunch  of  sparse  stuff  we  couldn't  offer  as  a  gift. They  ought  to  have  a  tractor,  he  said,  and  take  a  saw  to  some  of  the  diseased  stands  of  hardwood.  When  the  old  man  could  take  no  more  of  it,  he  dropped  the  shafts  and  plunged  into  the  tangled  darkness.  He  carried  a  billhook  and  a  ball  of  string.  Jacko  and  I  beat  a  path  through  the  ride  until  we  came  to  an  eight-foot  spruce  that  would  do  for  the  church. I  trimmed  the  lower  branches  and  started  to  cut  into  it  while  Jacko  went  back  to  the  cart  to  get  some  cord  to  tie  it.  It  was  only  a  mat-  ter  ofa  few  strokes.  I  sat  down  to  wait.  My  hand  was  bleeding  from  a  long,  shallow  cut;  I'd  tried  to  pull  a  rose  briar  out  of  the  way,  and  it  had  torn  open  the  flesh  between  my  thumb  and  forefinger.  I  sucked  it  clean  and  watched  the  slow  blood  oozing.  When  it  stopped,  and  Jacko  had  still  not  re-  turned,  I  got  up  and  ambled  back  to  look  for  him.  It  was  a  wild  place.  As  the  cart  came  into  view  and  I  saw  that  Jacko  wasn't  there,  I  guessed  he'd  gone  to  help  his  father  reach  up  for  mistletoe.  But  then  I  heard  him  calling  my  name,  his  voice  pealing  weirdly  among  the  dank  trunks.  It's  Old  Jacko,  I  thought.  He's  fallen  down  and  broken  his  leg.  As  fast  as  I  could,  I  followed  the  trail  of  flattened  underbrush  to-  ward  Jacko's  shouting.  &quot;Come  on,  come  on,  for  Christ's  sake  come  on!&quot;  he  yelled.  And  when  I  reached  them  they  were  looking  up  into  a  massive  yew.  Old  Jacko  was  all  right.  I  thought  they  were  playing  some  trick,  to  bring  me  rushing  there  when  nothing  was  amiss. Lying  along  the  broad  crook  of  the  tree,  where  five  vast  branches  di-  vided  to  make  him  a  platform,  a  skele-  ton  lay  in  the  last  shreds  of  his  clothes. HEN  Jacko  wakes  up,  we  pad-  lock  the  hut  and  then  work  round  the  skirt  of  his  wood  and  down  to  the  river.  Usually  we  lay  off  the  pigeons  until  he's  bagged  my  brace  of  rabbits,  and  then  we  see  the  lights  being  turned  on  in  his  house.  By  this  end  of  the  afternoon,  I've  decided  against  asking  how  his  neighbors  treat  him.  They  are  irrelevant,  forgotten,  out  of  sight  behind  the  hill  where  his  own  trees  still  grow.  He  is  my  host,  and  I  don't  want  to  pry  into  his  affairs;  he'll  tell  me  what  he  wants  me  to  know  in  his  own  good  time,  the  way  countrymen  do.
