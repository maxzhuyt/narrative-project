After  a  long  hour  of  whispered  banalities  she  began  a  slow  exploration  with  her  fingers  from  his  chest  to  his  lower  belly.  She  used  her  feet  to  explore  his  legs  and  discovered  that  hisentire  body  was  covered  with  soft  curly  hair  that  reminded  her  of  grass  in  April.  Then  she  began  to  arouse  him  with  kisses  on  his  ears  and  neck,  and  for  the  first  time  they  kissed  on  the  mouth.  This  was  when  he  showed  himself  to  be  an  exquisite  lover  who  carried  her,  without  haste,  to  the  heights  of  excite-  ment.  She  was  surprised  that  such  inex-  pressive  hands  were  capable  of  so  much  tenderness.  But  when  he  tried  to  shift  her  into  the  conventional  missionary  position  she  resisted,  fearful  that  the  miracle  of  the  first  time  would  be  ru-  ined.  But  he  was  firm  and  imposed  his  will,  he  moved  her  as  he  liked  and  in  his  own  way,  and  he  made  her  happy.  It  was  after  two  when  she  was  awak-  ened  by  a  clap  of  thunder  that  shook  the  foundations  of  the  building,  and  the  wind  forced  open  the  latch  on  the  window.  She  hurried  to  close  it,  and  in  the  instantaneous  daylight  of  an-  other  flash  of  lightning  she  saw  the  choppy  water  in  the  lagoon,  and  the  blue  herons  feebly  beating  their  wings  in  the  squall.  On  her  way  back  to  bed  her  feet  be-  came  tangled  in  their  clothes.  She  left  hers  on  the  floor  to  pick  up  later,  and  hung  his  jacket  on  the  chair;  over  that  she  hung  his  shirt  and  tie,  she  folded  his  trousers  with  care  so  as  not  to  wrin-  kle  the  crease,  and  on  topsheplaced  the  keys,  pocketknife,  and  money  that  had  fallen  out  of  his  pockets.  The  air  in  the  room  was  cooling  in  the  storm,  and  she  put  on  her  pink  nightgown  made  of  a  silk  so  pure  it  gave  her  gooseflesh.  The  man,  sleeping  on  his  side  with  his  legs  drawn  up,  looked  to  her  like  an  enor-  mous  orphan,  and  she  could  not  resist  a  rush  of  compassion.  She  lay  down  be-  hind  him  and  put  her  arms  around  his  waist,  and  the  ammoniac  smell  of  his  body  soaked  in  perspiration  touched  her  soul.  He  took  a  sharp  breath  and  began  to  snore.  She  dozed  off  and  woke  in  the  void  of  the  electric  fan  when  the  power        had  gone  and  the  room  was  bathed  in  the  green  phosphorescence  of  the  la-  goon.  He  was  snoring  now  with  a  con-  tinual  whistle.  She  began  to  tap  her  fin-  gers  on  his  back  out  of  sheer  mischief.  He  gave  a  start  and  stopped  snoring,  and  his  exhausted  member  began  to  revive.  She  left  him  alone  for  a  mo-  ment  to  pull  off  her  nightgown.  But  when  she  turned  back  to  him  her  wiles  were  useless,  and  she  realized  he  was  pretending  to  be  asleep  to  avoid  the  prospect  of  a  third  time.  She  moved  to  the  other  side  of  the  bed,  put  on  her  nightgown  again,  and  fell  into  a  deep  sleep  with  her  back  to  the  world.  Her  internal  clock  woke  her  at  day-  break.  For  a  moment  she  lay  drifting  with  her  eyes  closed,  not  daring  to  admit  the  throb  of  pain  in  hertemples  or  the  taste  of  copper  in  her  mouth  because  of  her  uneasy  feeling  that  something  un-  known  was  waiting  for  her  in  real  life.  From  the  sound  of  thefan  she  knew  the  power  had  returned  and  the  room  was  visible  in  the  dawn  over  the  lagoon.  Without  warning,  like  the  lightning  bolt  of  death,  she  was  struck  by  the  bru-  tal  awareness  that  for  the  first  time  in  her  life  she  had  fornicated  and  slept  with  a  man  who  wasn't  hers.  Shaken,  she  turned  to  look  at  him  over  her  shoul-  der,  and  he  wasn't  there.  And  he  wasn't  in  the  bathroom.  She  turned  on  the  lights  andsaw  that  his  clothes  weren't  there  but  hers,  which  she  had  left  on  the  floor,  were  folded  and  placed,  almost  with  love,  on  the  chair.  Until  then  she  hadn't  realized  that  she  knew  noth-  ing  about  him,  not  even  his  name,  and  all  that  remained  of  her  night  of  mad-  ness  was  a  faint  lavender  scent  in  the  air  that  had  been  purified  by  thestorm.  Only  when  she  picked  up  the  book  on  the  night  table  to  put  it  in  her  bag  did  she  realize  that  in  its  horror-filled  pages  he  had  left  a  twenty-dollar  bill.
