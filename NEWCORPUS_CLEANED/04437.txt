In  the  months  since  he  had  come  to  live  with  his  grand-  parents,  James  had  spent  many  hours  there,  until  the  attic  had  become  for  him  a  dark  cavern  with  many  grottoes,  each  of  them  peopled  with  its  own  set  of  ghosts.  By  the  stairwell  was  a  crib,           now  folded  up  and  put  away,  in  which  he  had  slept  the  one  time  his  mother  had  brought  him  to  visit  there.  It  was  al-  most  the  earliest  thing  he  could  remem-  ber.  In  one  of  the  alcoves  made  by  the  dormer  windows  was  a  leather  trunk  he  had  been  told  not  to  open  again.  He  had  gone  through  it  once,  and  he  knew  that  everything  in  it  was  much  older  than  he  was-faded  brown  snapshots,  keys  powdering  into  rust,  sea  shells,  tas-  selled  dance  programs,  plaited  rings  of  hair.  Behind  him,  in  another  alcove,  were  things  from  home-books  and  dishes  and  silver,  packed  up  in  New  York  early  that  spring.  The  raw-wood  smell  of  the  boxes  and  barrels  was  still  fresh.  James  switched  off  the  bare  overhead  light  and  crawled  into  the  cot  that  had  been  made  up  for  him  near  the  windows  looking  west  toward  the  lake.  It  was  still  muggy.  The  rain  had  not  yet  cooled  the  attic  off  at  all,  but  he  was  cold  under  the  mildewed  covers.  His  ankles  were  sore  where  they  had  pressed  against  the  legs  of  the  chair  during  the  fireworks.  So  was  his  right  arm;  it  seemed  as  though  he  had  not  stopped  shaking  hands.  &quot;Goodbye,  James.  Good-  bye  until  next  year,&quot;  the  voices  kept  say-  ing.  &quot;'Take  good  care  of  Gran.&quot;  He  felt  more  tired  than  he  had  the  first  night  he  came  to  the  house  to  stay,  when  the  joggle  and  clack  of  the  train  had  gone  on  and  on  in  his  head.  Now  it  was  the  fireworks  he  saw,  blossoming  slow-  ly,  with  their  explosions  muffled  and  remote.  He  lay  upon  his  back  with  his  eyes  closed  and  watched  them,  listening  to  the  steady  rain  and  waiting  for  sleep.            He  was  also  waiting,  as  he  waited  every  night,  for  the  crash  of  the  plane,  but  tonight  it  did  not  come.  The  breeze,  freshening,  brought  with  it  the  smell  of  the  pines  in  the  yard,  and  soon  he  was  aware  of  nothing  outside  himself  but  the  pines  and  the  rain.  Gradually,  the  colored  fires  in  his  head  grew  smaller  and  fainter,  until  they  seemed  like  the  lights  and  ornaments  upon  a  Christmas  tree.  And  now  he  could  see  and  smell  the  tree-the  one  they  had  had  last  Christmas  at  home,  when  there  had  been  so  many  presents  that  he  and  his  mother  and  father  had  taken  most  of  the  morning  to  unwrap  them.  43616e616461ly  James  saw  his  mother  and  father  clear-  ly,  tousled  and  laughing,  just  as  they  looked  that  morning.  He  was  astonished  at  the  recollection.  For  the  first  time,  he  was  able  to  remember  his  parents  without  pain.  He  held  the  picture  in  his  mind  as  long  as  he  could,  savoring  it.  He  lay  in  his  cot,  wide  awake  again,  and  listened  to  the  rain  falling  steadily  upon  the  roof  above  his  head.  Strength  was  flowing  back  into  him  from  some  source  he  did  not  understand,  and  the  aches  in  his  muscles  were  no  longer  there.  It's  settling  down  to  an  all-night  rain,  he  thought,  and  tomorrow  the  perch  should  be  biting.  In  the  morning,  if  it  clears  up  a  little,  I'll  get  up  early,  while  Gran  and  Grandpa  are  still  asleep,  and  go  down  to  the  pier  to  catch  a  string  of  perch  for  our  breakfast.  They  will  be  tired  tomorrow,  he  said  to  him-  self.  They'll  sleep  late.  I'll  be  back  before  they  know  I'm  gone."
