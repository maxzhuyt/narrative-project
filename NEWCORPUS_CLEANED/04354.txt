Not  far  from  our  house  was  Goulding's  Glen,  in  those  days  a  valley  with  a  stream  that  led  from  the  clean  open  country  by  Ballyvolane  to  a  ma-  nure  factory  in  Blackpool  where  Father  worked  for  a  time. Mother  and  I  were  equally  mortified  at  my  lack  of  observa-  tion,  and  when  the  Countess  died,  hav-  ing  failed  to  provide  for  my  education  as  a  priest,  I  was  left  with  the  guilty  feeling  that  I  had  forever  forfeited  my  chances  by  failing  to  notice  that  cows  were  not  green.  Now  that  the  Countess  was  dead  and  her  house  was  a  hostel  for  refugees,  Kate  always  called  &quot;Entrez!&quot;  when  we  knocked  on  thedoor.  This  made  a  tremendous  impression  on  me-to  hear  someone  I  knew  speak  a  foreign  lan-  guage.  She  found  the  refugees  disgust,  ing,  but  I  loved  them  because  they,  too,  spoke  a  foreign  language.  In  the  Chris-  tian  Brothers  school,  which  was  not  run  by  the  state,  we  said  our  prayers  in  Irish.  The  Belgians  said  theirs  in  Flemish,  and  I  wasn't  happy  till  I  could  do  the  same.  For  a  year  after  that,  I  never  used  any  other  language  for  my  night  and  morn-  ing  prayers.  The  monks  were  wrong  in  thinking  me  a  complete  fool;  I  was  real-  " b"ly  a  serious  student,  but  it  was  always  of  something  that  could  be  of  no  use  to  me,  like  the  pageant  songs  of  a  couple  of  years  before;  and  they  finally  called  Mother  to  the  school  and  explained  to  her  that  I  would  never  be  able  to  pass  the  Intermediate  examinations,  and  clapped  me  into  the  Trades  School,  where  I  could  pick  up  something  more  suitable  to  my  station  in  life  than  Flem-  ish.  In  the  Trades  School  I  didn't  pick  up  anything  at  all,  useful  or  otherwise.  A  tall,  gloomy,  sardonic  monk  named  Kiely  taught  us  in  an  atmosphere  of  complete  hopelessness,  as  though  he  felt  he  was  there  because  he  couldn't  teach  and  we  were  there  because  we  couldn't  be  taught.  I  still  read  English  school  stories,  and  modelled  myself  entirely  on  the  characters  in  them,  and  this  con-  tinued  to  get  me  intotrouble,  because  neither  Kiely  nor  my  classmates  had  read  boy's  weeklies,  or,  indeed,  any-  thing  else  that  I  could  ever  discover,  and  so  they  had  no  clue  to  my  strange  behavior.  It  culminated  in  a  scene  when  myself  and  another  quiet  boy  were  at-  tacked  on  our  way  home  from  school  by  a  real  little  fighter.  Cowardice  was  no  name  for  my  normal  attitude  to  vio-  lence;  I  slunk  home  through  the  streets  like  one  of  those  little  mongrels  I  sym-  pathized  with  that  flitted  from  side  to  side  in  an  effort  to  avoid  tormentors;  but  when  I  was  driven  desperate  I  had  my  father's  dangerous  temper,  and  somehow  on  that  occasion  I  managed  to  bloody  the  mouth  of  the  boy  that  at-  tacked  us  and  leave  him  crying.  Next  morning,  he  complained  to  Kiely--  a  thing  nobody  in  an  English  school  story  would  do-and  when  Kiely  ques-  tioned  me  I  told  the  truth,  which  was  an  offense  in  itself.  Irish  teachers  tend  to  regard  truthfulness  as  Irish  farmers  regard  the  old-fashioned  Quaker  re-  fusal  to  haggle.  Kiely,  with  his  dys-  peptic  humor,  thought  it  a  neat  idea  to  hand  my  opponent  the  strap  and  order  him  to  punish  us  himself.  The  class,  which  was  almost  as  mature  as  Kiely,  thought  it  an  excellent  joke,  but  when  it  came  to  my  turn  I  told  Kiely  that  if  the  boy  came  near  me  I'd  give  him  the  same  thing  I  had  given  him  before.  Kiely,  who  wasn't  unkind,  only  de-  pressed  and  cranky,  became  a  bright  brick  red  and  turned  nasty,  but  I  was  fighting  mad  and  held  my  ground.  I  paid  dear  for  the  pleasure  of  spoiling  his  idea  of  a  joke,  but  it  was  one  of  the  few  occasions  when  I  had  no  regrets  for  having  made  a  fool  of  myself.  Actually,  I  learned  more  from  the  penny  dreadfuls  than  I  did  from  anyone " b"except  Corkery,  and  long  years  after,  when  George  Orwell  wrote  a  priggish  article  analyzing  them,  I  was  enchanted  that  &quot;Frank  Richards,&quot;  the  author  of  the  &quot;Magnet&quot;  stories,  appeared  as  from  the  dead  and  urbanely  made  smithereens  of  Orwell.  These  weekly  stories  fre-  quently  featured  Germans  who,  even  in  the  years  before  the  war,  were  spies  and  said  &quot;Donner  und  Blitzen!&quot;  to  their  agents,  and  French  students  who,  though  ridiculous  as  all  Froggies  were,  were  on  the  right  side  and  said  &quot;Ma  foi!&quot;  On  the  subject  of  foreign  lan-  guages,  in  spite  of  the  efforts  of  the  Christian  Brothers,  I  was  half  dotty.  They  still  sometimes  affect  me  in  a  pe-  culiar  way,  and  a  single  phrase,  how-  ever  trivial,  will  flood  a  whole  eve-  ning  with  emotion.  N  1916,  a  handful  of  men  took  over  the  city  of  Dublin  and  were  finally  surrounded  and  overwhelmed  by  British  troops.  Photographs  in  the  daily  papers  showed  Dublin  as  the  English  pa-  pers  showed  Belgian  cities-smoking  ruins  inhabited  by  men  with  rifles  and  machine  guns.  At  first  my  only  reac-  tion  was  horror  at  the  thought  that  Irishmen  could  deliberately  commit  such  a  crime  against  [REDACTED],  the  land  of  the  boy's  weeklies.  I  was  sure  that  phase  had  ended  with  the  Boer  War,  in  which  Father  had  fought,  because  one  of  his  favorite  songs  said  as  much.  The  brave  Irish  troops  whose  names  were  on  &quot;Glencoe's  death  roll&quot;  were  repre-  sented  as  singing:  You  used  to  call  us  traitors  because  of  agitators,  if  But  you  can't  call  us  traitors  now.  Now  the  English  were  calling  us  trai-  tors  again,  and  so  far  as  I  could  see,  they  were  perfectly  right.  It  was  a  tragic  situation  for  a  boy  of  twelve,  with  no  spiritual  homeland  but  that  of  the  Eng-  lish  public  schools,  and  no  friends  but  the  imaginary  friends  he  knew  there.  I  had  defended  their  code  of  honor  with  nothing  to  support  me  but  faith,  and  now,  even  if  a  miracle  happened  and  Big  Tim  Fahy  returned  from  the  States  with  bags  of  money  and  sent  me  to  school  in  [REDACTED],  the  boys  would  be  bound  to  look  on  me  with  suspicion-  almost,  God  help  me,  as  if  I  were  a  Ger-  man  and  said  &quot;Downer  und  Blitzen.&quot;  The  English  shot  the  first  batch  of  prisoners,  and  this  was  a  worse  blow  to  me,  for  the  newspapers  said-the  pro-  British  ones  with  a  sneer-that  several  of  them  had  been  poets,  and  poets  were  a  recognized  part  of  education.  One  of  them,  Patrick  Pearse,  on  the  night  " b"before  his  execution  wrote  some  poems  that  were  printed  in  all  the  papers,  and  even  I  could  recognize  that  they  were  genuine  poetry-  Sometimfss  my  heart  hath  shaken  with  great  joy,  To  see  a  leaping  squirrel  in  a  tree,.  Or  a  red  ladybird  upon  a  stalk,  Or  little  rabbits  in  a  field  at  evening,  Lit  by  a  slanting  sun.  A  revolution  had  begun  in  Ireland.  The  same  revolution  had  begun  in  me.  It  is  only  in  the  imagination  that  the  great  tragedies  take  place,  and  I  had  only  my  imagination  to  live  in.  I  en-  joyed  English  school  stories  more  than  ever,  and  felt  even  closer  to  the  Invisi-  ble  Presences,  but  now  I  was  develop-  ing  a  bad  conscience  about  them.  The  presences  reminded  me  that  they  had  taken  me  in  and  made  me  one  of  them-  selves,  and  I  agreed,  but  I  had  to  point  out  to  them  that  I  was  different,  and  it  was  because  of  what  they  and  theirs  had  done  to  me.  For  months  I  concentrated  on  Irish  history,  and  wrote  my  first  es-  say.  It  listed  the  crimes  and  atrocities  the  English  had  committed  on  the  Irish  for  several  hundred  years,  but  it  had  no  more  effect  than  the  deceived  husband's  listing  of  his  wife's  crimes  and  frailties,  so  often  forgiven,  has  on  his  need  of  her.  My  heart  still  cried  out  for  them.  In  the  early  mornings,  Mother  and  I  went  through  town  to  some  Franciscan  or  Augustinian  church  where  Mass  was  said  for  the  dead  rebels-their  position  was  still  too  ambiguous  to  permit  of  any  hope  of  their  salvation  in  the  parish  churches.  On  the  way  back  we  bought  picture  postcards  of  the  victims.  On  Saturday  afternoons  I  went  to  the  Irish  classesat  the  Gaelic  League  hall  in  Queen  Street.  The  Irish  we  spoke  there  was  of  less  importance  to  me  than  the  folk  songs  we  learned,  and  those  than  the  kilt  worn  by  one  of  the  boys,  and  I  persecuted  my  poor  mother  till  she  made  me  a  kilt  as  well.  Yet  when  Asquith,  the  English  Prime  Minister,  visited  Cork  on  some  peace  mission,  I  cheered  with  the  rest.  Later,  I  was  ashamed  of  it,  but  I  couldn't  help  it.  I  needed  peace  more  than  Ireland  did-peace  in  my  own  mind  between  the  Invisible  Presences  and  the  new  ones  that  were  already  knocking  at  the  door.  Somewhere  I  had  picked  up  Eleanor  Hull's  &quot;Cuchulain,&quot;  a  retelling  of  the  sagas  for  children,  and  I  became  pas-  sionately  involved  with  the  boy  hero  of  Ulster.  Having  exhausted  most  of  the  school  stories  in  the  children's  room  of  the  Carnegie  Library,  I  had  discovered  the  adult  main  library,  and  by  using  the  card  I  had  for  Mother,  I  could  get  a  " b"school  story  from  upstairs  and  a  book  on  Irish  history  from  downstairs.  One  of  the  books  I  borrowed  at  this  time  was  O'Curry's  great  &quot;Manuscript  Mate-  rials,&quot;  which  contained  a  lot  about  Cu  Chulainn.  No  more  than  O'Curry  him-  self  was  I  disturbed  by  the  fact  that  this  was  written  in  a  form  of  Irish  I  didn't  know,  ranging  from  the  eighth  century  to  the  twelfth;  and,  casting  my-  self  in  the  part  of  a  medieval  scribe,  I  copied  it  all  out,  with  colored  initials  imitated  from  the  &quot;Book  of  Kells.&quot;  But,  though  I  knew  as  little  about  the  hero  of  a  modern  English  public-school  story  as  I  did  about  the  hero  of  aprimi-  tive  saga,  modelling  myself  on  the  one  was  child's  play  compared  with  the  other,  and  I  nearly  ruptured  myself  try-  ing  to  perform  the  least  of  the  feats  that  Cu  Chulainn  had  performed  when  he  was  only  seven.  I  had  wasted  my  time  practicing  with  a  bow  and  arrow;  Cu  Chulainn  preferred  a  slingshot.  And  though  I  now  practiced  hard  with  this,  I  never  came  near  to  being  able  to  kill  an  enemy  in  a  crowd  a  half  a  mile  away.  Trouble  enough  it  gave  me  to  hit  agate-  post  at  twenty  yards.  Most  of  my  endeavors  were  wasted  on  a  single  episode  of  Cu  Chulainn's  life.  He  left  home  alone  when  he  was  little  more  than  an  infant,  hurling  his  toy  spear  before  him,  pucking  his  hurling  ball  after  that,  throwing  his  hurling  stick  after  the  ball,  and  catching  all  three  before  they  alighted.  Nobody  who  has  not  tried  this  can  even  imagine  how  difficult  it  is.  There  was  more  sense  to  the  story  of  how  he  killed  the  great  watchdog  by  throwing  the  hurling  ball  |.  down  his  throat  and  then  beating  him  over  the  head  with  the  hurley,  and  I  practiced  that,  too,  beginning  with  very  small  dogs;  but,  knowing  my  character  much  better  than  I  ever  did,  they  de-  cided  that  I  only  wanted  to  play  with  them,  and  ran  away  with  my  ball,  and   when  they  finally  let  me  catch  up  on   them,  and  grinned  at  me  with  the  ball  in  their  teeth,  I  could  no  more  hit  them  than  I  could  do  anything  else  that  Cu  Chulainn  did.  I  clutched  some  queer  treasures  to  my  chest,  coming  over  Parnell  Bridge  in  the  dusk  on  my  wayhome  from  the  public  library.  Poor  Mother  must  have  been  astonished  at  becoming  a  borrower  of  art  books  containing  pictures  of  naked  gods  and  goddesses,  and  I  had  great  dif-  ficulty  in  persuading  the  stupid  girls  in  the  Carnegie  Library,  who  tried  to  stop  me  from  taking  them  out,  that  they  were  Mother's  favorite  reading.  But  I  knew  I  should  never  become  a  great  painter  unless  I  could  copy  Bronzino  and   " b"toretto  and  knew  all  about  the  rules  of  perspective,  and  there  is  a  sort  of  irre-  sistible  force  behind  a  small  spectacled  boy  with  an  aim  in  life.  One  evening,  passing  by  a  bookshop  window  in  Great  George's  Street,  I  saw  a  book  called  &quot;A  Munster  Twilight,&quot;  the  author  of  which  was  someone  called.  DanielCorkery.  Even  today  to  see  a  book  under  a  friend's  name  gives  me  a  start,  and  in  those  days  I  knew  no  au-  thors.  It  seemed  a  coincidence,  but  I  had  to  reassure  myself,  so  I  waylaid  Corkery  to  inquire,  and  he  admitted  that  he  was  the  author.  This  was  more  than  ashock,  it  was  a  revelation.  I  was  impressed  not  only  at  Corkery's  ability  but  at  my  own  shrewdness  in  having  discovered  him  before  anyone  else  had  done  so.  I  felt  about  that  book  as  though  I  had  written  it  myself.  I  managed  to  get  it  from  the  public  library,  and  one  day  when  Mother  was  at  work  I  sat  on  a  warm  rock  at  the  footof  the  square  and  read  steadily  through  it  without  un-  derstanding  a  word.  But  that  didn't  worry  me;  I  was  accustomed  to  reading  things  I  didn't  understand.  It  was  not  as  good  as  &quot;The  Gem&quot;  or  &quot;The  Mag-  net,&quot;  but  Corkery  certainly  had  talent,  and  after  that  the  boy's  weeklies  ceased  to  mean  the  same  to  me.  I  did  not  know  their  authors  as  I  knew  Corkery,  and  their  creations  somehow  were  no  longer  as  real  to  me  as  those  of  Corkery,  little  as  I  might  understand  them.  Y  the  time  I  was  fourteen  or  fifteen,  it  had  become  altogether  clear  that  education  was  something  I  could  not  afford.  Not  that  even  then  I  had  any  intention  of  giving  it  up.  I  was  just  looking  for  ajob  that  would  enable  me  to  buy  the  books  from  which  I  could  pick  up  the  education  myself.  So  with  the  rest  of  the  unemployed  I  went  to  the  newsroom  of  the  Carnegie  Library,  where  on  wet  days  the  central  heating  warmed  the  perished  bodies  in  the  broken  boots  and  made  the  dirty  rags  steam  and  smell.  I  read  carefully  through  the  ad-  vertisements  and  applied  for  every  job  that  demanded  &quot;a  smart  boy.&quot;  Some-  times,  as  a  sort  of  bonus  from  Provi-  dence,  I  found  a  new  number  of  The  Times  Literary  Supplement,  The  Spec-  tator,  The  New  Statesman,  or  The  Studio  free,  and  I  sat  there  and  read  reviews  of  books  I  would  never  be  able  to  read  and  discussions  of  paintings  I  would  never  see,  but  usually  some  hungry  old  man  would  have  toppled  asleep  over  it  and  I  was  cheated.  The  real  out-of-works  always  favored  the  high-class  magazines,  at  which  they  were  not  likely  to  be  disturbed.  After  " b"a  while,  I  got  up  and  went  out  and  wandered  aimlessly  round  town  till  hunger  or  darkness  or  rain  sent  me  home.  When  it  became  clear  that  I  would  never  bea  priest,  Mother's  only  ambi-  tion  was  for  me  to  become  a  clerk:  someone  who  would  wear  a  white  collar  and  be  called  &quot;Mister.&quot;  Knowing  no  better  but  always  willing,  I  went  to  the  Technical  School  and  the  School  of  Commerce  at  night  to  learn  arithmetic,  bookkeeping,  and  shorthand-typing.  Of  bookkeeping  all  I  remember  is  a  saying  quoted  approvingly  on  the  first  page  of  our  textbook:  &quot;In  business  there  is  no  such  thing  as  an  out-and-out  free  gift.&quot;  And  the  very  first  thing  I  was  asked  to  type  in  the  School  of  Commerce  threw  me  into  a  fresh  fever,  for  it  was  Tennyson's  &quot;Blow,  Bugle,  Blow,&quot;  pre-  sented  as  an  example  of  advanced  punc-  tuation. From  my  reading  I  had  deduced  that  German  was  the  real  language  of  culture,  and  that  the  greatest  of  cultured  persons  was  Goethe,  so  I  read  Goethe  right  through  in  English  and  studied  German  out  of  the  Self-Educator  to  read  him  in  the  original.  I  also  made  an  attempt  to  learn  Greek,  which  struck  me  as  a  very  im-  portant  cultural  medium,  but  as  I  had  never  learned  the  rudiments  of  gram-  mar  in  any  language,  I  didn't  get  far  with  Greek.  I  got  my  first  job  through  my  con-  fessor,  agentleold  priest  who  regarded  me  as  a  very  saintly  boy,  and  if  inno-  cence  and  sanctity  are  related,  he  was  probably  not  so  far  wrong.  The  job  was  in  a  pious  wholesale  drapery  busi-  ness,  where  every  single  member  ofthe  staff  seemed  to  have  been  recommend-  ed  by  his  confessor,  but  I  hated  my  im-  mediate  boss-a  small,  smug,  smooth,  greasy  man,  who  tried  hard  to  teach  me  that  whenever  he  called  my  name  I  was  instantly  to  drop  whatever  I  was  doing  and  reply  smartly  &quot;Yessir.&quot;  I  never  minded  dropping  whatever  I  was  doing,  which  was  usually  folding  shirts-the  two  arms  neatly  across  the  breast,  as  if  I  were  laying  out  a  corpse-  and  I  had  no  objection  to  calling  any-  body  &quot;[REDACTED];  but  it  was  several  seconds  before  my  armor  of  daydreaming  was  penetrated  by  any  voice  from  outside,  and  several  seconds  more  before  I  real-  ized  that  it  was  the  voice  to  which  I  should  reply  &quot;Yessir;&quot;  so  at  the  end  of  a  fortnight  I  stopped  folding  shirts  and  saying  yessir  and  went  home  to  do  some" b"more  work  at  Greek.  Then  I  tried  a  spell  in  a  chemists  shop  that  was  looking  for  a  smart  boy,  but  I  soon  discovered  that  I  was  only  wanted  to  deliver  mes-  sages.  I  have  a  vivid  recollection  of  the  end  of  this  job,  with  myself,  a  small  boy  looking  up  at  a  tall  counter,  and  a  still  taller  man,  refreshed  by  a  visit  to  the  pub  next  door,  looking  down  at  me  pity-  ingly  and  begging  me  in  a  thick  Dublin  accent  to  get  to  hell  out  of  this  for  my  own  good.  There  was  an  even  briefer  spell  at  ajob  printer's.  While  I  was  be-  ing  shown  the  ropes,  the  printer  asked  me  if  I  could  spell  and  I  replied  airily  &quot;Oh,  that's  my  forte!,&quot;  which  was  the  sort  of  language  we  used  on  the  heights  and  I  saw  nothing  wrong  with  it.  That  evening  I  met  the  manwho  had  rec-  ommended  me  to  the  printer,  and  he  repeated  my  reply  with  a  good  deal  of  laughter,  and  I  realized  that  as  usual  I  had  made  a  fool  of  myself.  I  was  so  mor-  tified  that  I  never  went  back.  I  was  sorry  about  that,  because  I  really  was  quite  good  atspelling,  and  I  still  think  I  might  have  made  an  excellent  type-  setter.  I  went  to  the  railway  as  a  messenger  boy,  because  I  despaired  of  ever  becom-  ing  anything  better,  and,  besides,  though  the  hours-eight  to  seven-  were  cruelly  long  for  a  growing  boy,  the  pay  was  good-a  pound  a  week-  and  with  money  like  that  coming  in  I  could  buy  a  lot  of  books  and  get  a  lot  of  education.  My  job  was  to  assist  the  in-  voice  and  claims  clerks,  bringing  in  dockets  from  the  warehouse,  and  going  to  the  warehouse  to  inquire  for  missing  goods.  All  transport  companies  have  colossal  claims  for  missing  goods,  most  of  which  are  not  really  missing  at  all  but  lying  around  forgotten.  Whiskey  and  tobacco  were  easy  to  trace,  as  they  had  to  be  loaded  into  sealed  wagons  in  the  presence  of  one  of  the  old  railway  police-  men,  who  recorded  them  in  his  book.  But  nobody  took  much  responsibility  for  other  articles,  and  it  dependedon  the  uncertain  memory  of  checkers  whether  or  not  you  could  find  out  what  had  hap-  pened  to  them.  A  friendly  checker  could  often  remember  a  particular  con-  signment,  and  if  he  were  in  really  good  humor,  find  it  where  it  had  lain  for  weeks  in  a  corner,  covered  by  a  moun-  tain  of  fresh  merchandise.  Usually,  no-  body  remembered  anything  at  all,  and  you  solemnly  marked  your  memoran-  dum  or  wire  with  some  code  word,  like  &quot;Bison,&quot;  which  signified  &quot;Certainly  forwarded  please  say  if  since  received.&quot;  Back  came  a  wire  saying  &quot;Moose,&quot;  meaning  that  it  hadn't  been  received,  and  you  had  to  go  to  the  file  room," b"where  the  dockets  were  stored,  and  search  through  scores  of  dusty  files  to  find  the  original  docket  and  the  name  of  the  porter  or  checker  who  had  signed  for  the  goods.  Sheehy  and  Cremin,  the  two  other  tracers,  were  the  sons  of  railwaymen  and  protected  by  their  fathers'  presence  from  anything  worse  than  good-na-  tured  ragging,  but,  apart  from  the  pa-  tience  and  kindness  of  two  orthree  checkers,  I  had  to  depend  on  my  wits,  which  were  all  but  nonexistent.  I  hated  the  file  room,  and  when  I  worked  there  with  Cremin  or  Sheehy  I  realized  that  they  found  six  dockets  in  the  time  it  took  me  to  find  one.  I  had  bad  sight,  and  often  failed  to  see  a  docket  properly,  particularly  when  it  was  written  in  the  semi-literate  scrawl  of  carters  or  porters,  and  even  when  I  should  have  seen  it  I  was  daydreaming,  and  when  I  wasn't  daydreaming  I  was  harassed  by  panic,  shyness,  and  ignorance.  Sheehy  sneered  all  the  time;  Cremin  sneered  only  some  of  the  time,  because  he  had  a  sort  of  im-  patient  pity  for  my  stupidity,  and  oc-  casionally,  smiling  as  I  bogged  myself  deeper  in  some  job  I  couldn't  do,  he  took  it  from  me  with  a  complacent  air  and  did  it  himself  in  a  moment.  Cremin  was  really  what  the  advertisements  meant  when  they  asked  fora  smart  boy.  I  envied  him,  storming  through  the  store  with  his  bundle  of  documents  in  his  fist,  exchanging  noisy  greetings  with  the  porters,  shouting  back  laughing  in-  sults  at  the  checkers-at  home  with  everybody  but  most  of  all  with  himself.  I  had  no  self  to  be  at  home  with,  the  only  self  I  knew  being  then  in  wait  for  me  till  seven  o'clock  outside  the  passen-  ger  station.  Years  later  during  the  Irish  Civil  War,  I  found  myself  in  the  same  hut  with  Cremin  internment  camp  where  we  were  both  prisoners,  and  though  our  positions  had  changed  somewhat  and  I  was  no  longer  any-  body's  butt,  he  was  still  the  same  young  fellow,  mixing  with  everybody  and  no-  body  in  particular,  briskly  hammering  rings  or  weaving  macram\xc3\xa9  bags-a  cheerful,  noisy  little  universe  of  self-  satisfaction.  Yet  the  moment  I  fell  ill,  henursed  me  with  the  same  amused  ex-  asperation,  cluck-clucking  with  an  in-  credulous  smile  over  my  inability  to  look  after  myself.  My  boss  was  obviously  a  man  who  had  once  been  a  smart  boy,  and  owed  his  promotion  to  it,  and  I  imitated  his  elegant  signature,  as  I  imitated  Cork-  ery's  articulation,  in  a  hopeless  attempt  at  becoming  a  smart  boy  myself.  He  swept  through  the  office  and  the  ware-  house  with  an  efficient  air.  I  fancy  he " b"was  really  clever,  and  probably  kind,  but,  annoyed  at  my  stupidity,  he  would  adjust  his  pince-nez  and  shout  abuse  at  me  till  he  had  the  whole  office  listening,  and  I  went  away,  stupider  than  ever,  muttering  aspirations  to  the  Sacred  Heart  of  Jesus  and  the  Blessed  Virgin,  in  the  hope  that  they  might  as-  sist  me  in  whatever  impossible  task  he  was  now  asking  me  to  perform.  In  mockery  of  my  passion  for  Irish  he  nick-  named  me  The  Native.  This  was  a  subject  that  most  of  the  clerks  didn't  even  bother  to  jeer  me  about,  though  one,  a  prissy  little  man,  did  once  sniff  at  me  and  ask  what  literature  we  had  in  Irish  that  could  compare  with  Shake-  speare.  Not  having  read  Shakespeare,  I  couldn't  say,  but  it  wouldn'thave  made  any  difference,  because  I  am  quite  sure  he  hadn't  read  Shakespeare,  either.  One  of  my  jobs  was  to  answer  the  telephone,  and  I  did  it  with  such  in-  tensity  that  I  could  never  hear  a  word  the  other  person  said,  and  usuallyI  was  too  ashamed  to  admit  that  I  hadn't  un-  derstood.  When  I  did  admit  it,  it  was  worse,  for  the  person  at  the  other  end  grew  furious-a  fatal  thing  to  do  with  me,  as  it  deprives  me  of  the  last  shred  of  my  wits.  The  trouble  was  that  I  couldn't  believe  in  the  telephone  or  the  messages  that  came  by  it.  I  couldn't  believe  that  the  missing  goods  I  was  sup-  posed  to  trace  had  ever  existed,  or  if  theyhad,  that  their  loss  meant  anything  to  anybody.  All  I  could  believe  in  was  words.  I  would  read  the  word  &quot;un-  sophisticated&quot;  and  at  once  I  would  want  to  know  what  the  Irish  for  it  was.  In  those  days  I  didn't  ask  to  be  a  writer.  All  I  wanted  was  to  translate,  to  feel  the  unfamiliar  become  familiar,  the  fa-  miliar  take  on  all  the  mystery  of  some  dark  foreign  face  one  glimpsed  in  the  street.  The  extraordinary  double  life  I  was  leading  was  so  divided  against  itself  that  it  comes  back  to  me  now  as  a  hal-  lucination  rather  than  a  real  memory.  Usually  there  is  some  connection  be-  tween  the  real  and  imaginary  worlds,  some  acquaintance  in  whom  the  two  temporarily  merge,  but  when  I  left  the  railway  for  good,  I  did  not  leave  a  friend  behind,  and  never  after  so  much  as  crossed  the  road  to  inquire  what  had  happened  to  any  of  the  hundreds  of  peo-  ple  I  knew  there.  One  life  I  led  in  Eng-  lish,  a  life  of  drudgery  and  humilia-  tion,  the  other  in  Irish  and  whatever  scraps  of  foreign  languages  I  had  man-  aged  to  pick  up,  and  it  was  almost  en-  tirely  daydreaming.  'That  was  the  real  significance  of  my  passion  for  lan-  guages-they  belonged  entirely  to  the" b"world  of  my  imagination-and  even  to-  day  when  some  figure  of  fantasy  enters  my  mind  in  sleep,  he  or  she  is  always  apt  to  burst  out  into  copious  and  in-  accurate  French  (the  fantasy  seems  to  have  no  use  for  grammar).  Irish  was  merely  the  most  convenient  of  the  roads  into  dream,  and  that  was  why,  on  Satur-  day  nights,  I  attended  lectures  in  the  Gaelic  League  hall  in  Queen  Street,  or  stood  admiringly  in  a  corner  while  my  seniors  discussed  in  Irish  such  profound  questions  as  &quot;Is  Shakespeare  a  drama-  tist?&quot;  and  &quot;Is  dancing  immoral?&quot;  or  perhaps  &quot;Is  dancing  national?&quot;  and  &quot;Is  Shakespeare  immoral?&quot;  I  still  had  no  education  except  such  as  fitted  me  for  the  byways  of  literature,  and  I  read  Goethe  a  few  lines  at  a  time,  with  the  aid  ofa  translation,  or  a  chapter  from  some  obscure  novel  in  Spanish,  or  poetry,  which  had  a  simpler  working  vocabu-  lary,  based  on  words  like  Herz  and  Schmerz,  amour  and  toujours,  ardor  and  rumor,  the  meaning  of  which  I  could  frequently  guess  when  a  transla-  tion  was  not  available.  I  had  taken  a  checker's  discarded  notebook  from  the  railway  storeroom  and,  having  patiently  rubbed  out  all  the  pencil  notes,  begun  a  poem  book  of  my  own.  And  though  I  was  stupid,  I  really  did  care  madly  about  poetry,  good  and  bad,  without  understanding  why  I  cared.  Chidiock  Tichbourne,  the  Eliza-  bethan  martyr,  on  the  night  before  his  execution  in  Tyburn  wrote,  &quot;My  prime  of  life  is  but  a  frost  of  cares,&quot;  and  Patrick  Pearse  before  his  execution  in  Kilmain-  ham  wrote,  &quot;The  beauty  of  this  world  hath  made  me  sad.&quot;  When  life  is  at  its  harshest-&quot;when  so  sad  thou  canst  not  sadder  be&quot;&quot;'-poetry  comes  into  its  own.  More  even  than  music  itis  the  universal  speech,  but  itis  spoken  fluently  only  by  those  whose  existence  is  already  all  aflame  with  emotion,  for  then  the  beau-  ty  and  order  of  language  are  the  only  beauty  and  order  possible.  Above  all,  itis  the  art  of  the  boy  and  girl  over-  burdened  by  the  troubles  of  their  sex  and  station,  for  as  Jane  Austen  wist-  fully  noted,  poetry  can  be  best  appre-  ciated  by  those  who  should  taste  it  most  sparingly.  It  was  a  strange  double  life,  and  small  wonder  if  it  comes  back  to  me  as  hallucination.  Each  morning  when  I  made  my  way  across  thetracks  from  the  passenger  station  to  the  goods  station,  I  said  goodbye  to  a  part  of  myself,  and  at  seven  that  evening,  when  I  returned  across  the  dark  railway  yard  and  paused  in  the  well-lit  passenger  station  to  see  the  new  books  in  the  bookstall,  he  rejoined  me,  a  boy  exactly  like  myself  except  that " b"no  experience  had  dinged  or  dented  him;  and  as  we  went  up  Mahoney's  Avenue  in  the  darkness,  we  chattered  in  Irish  diversified  by  quotations  in  Ger-  man,  French,  or  Spanish,  and  talked  of  the  Rhineland  and  4974616c79,  and  the  beau-  tiful  girls  one  could  meet  there,  and  re-  cited  Goethe's  poem,which  in  those  days  was  always  in  my  mind-the  per-  fection  of  the  poet's  dream  of  escape:  Kennst  du  das  Land,  wo  die  Zitronen  blith'n,  Im  dunkeln  Laub  die  Goldorangen  gliuth'n  ...  I  knew  I  often  hurt  my  mother  by  my  moroseness  and  churlishness  when  some  question  of  hers  brought  me  tum-  bling  down  from  the  heights  of  lan-  guage  to  the  English  that  belonged  only  to  the  office  and  the  warehouse  and  home.  When  my  first  wretched  effort  at  composition  appeared  in  a  boy's  paper  and  word  of  it  got  round  the  office,  everyone  was  astonished,  but  most  of  all  my  boss.  Sitting  at  his  high  desk  with  the  paper  open  before  him  and  a  frown  on  his  bulgy  forehead,  he  asked,  &quot;Did  you  write  this,  Native?&quot;  &quot;Yes,  [REDACTED];  I  said,  feeling  sure  I  had  done  it  again. Looking  for  models  of  fine  conduct  as  usual,  I  had  lit  on  a  Left  Wing  timekeeper,  who  knew  all  the  Italian  operas  by  heart  and  made  it  a  point  of  honor  not  to  take  off  his  cap  when  speaking  to  the  bosses.  Thinking  that  anyone  who  knew  so  much  about  Italian  operas  must  know  the  correct  thing  for  other  situations,  I  decided  not  to  take  my  cap  off,  either.  Even  then  I  should  probably  have  been  let  off  with  a  reprimand,  because  my  boss  realized  that  I  had  no  self-  confidence  and  went  about  imitating  everybody  in  the  wild  hope  that  I  might  accidentally  strike  on  the  right  thing.  But  with  my  bad  sight,  I  fell  over  a  hand  truck  and  injured  my  shin,  so  that  I  couldn't  walk  for  weeks.  On  the  rail-     " b"way,  bad  sight  was  more  serious  than  bad  manners,  because  it  might  result  in  a  claim,  and  transport  companies  have  a  thing  about  claims.  On  the  Saturday  night  I  was  sacked,  I  read  my  first  paper  before  the  Gaelic  League.  It  was  in  Irish,  and  the  sub-  ject  was  Goethe.  For  me,  my  whole  adolescence  is  summed  up  in  that  ex-  traordinary  evening-so  much  that  even  yet  I  can't  laugh  at  it  in  comfort.  I  didn't  know  much  about  Irish,  and  I  knew  practically  nothing  about  Goethe,  and  that  little  was  wrong.  In  a  truly  anthropomorphic  spirit,  I  re-created  Goethe  in  my  own  image  and  likeness,  as  a  patriotic  young  fellow  who  merely  wished  to  revive  the  German  language,  which  I  considered  to  have  been  grave-  ly  threatened  by  the  use  of  French.  I  drew  an  analogy  between  the  French  culture  that  dominated  eighteenth,  century  [REDACTED]  and  the  English  cul-  ture  by  which  we  in  Ireland  were  dominated.  While  I  was  reading,  it  was  suddenly  borne  in  on  me  that  I  no  longer  had  a  job,  or  a  penny  in  the  world,  or  even  a  home  I  could  return  to  without  humilia-  tion,  and  that  the  neighbors  would  say  again,  as  they  had  so  often  said  before,  that  I  was  mad  and  a  good-for-nothing.  And  I  knew  they  would  be  right,  for  here  I  was  committing  myself  absolutely  and  publicly  to  vague  words  and  vaguer  impressions.  I  could  barely  control  my  voice,  because  the  words  and  impressions  no  longer  mattered.  All  that  mattered  to  menow  was  the  actof  faith,  the  hope  that  somewhere,  somehow  I  would  be  able  to  prove  that  I  was  neither  mad  nor  a  good-for-nothing,  because  now  I  real-  ized  that  there  was  no  turning  back.
