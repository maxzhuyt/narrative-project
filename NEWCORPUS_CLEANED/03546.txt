He  had  talked  his  father  into  promising  a  week  before,  when  his  father  was  drinking,  and  had  reminded  him  several  times  since.  His  father  was  usually  good  for  his  word,  but  Andrew,  dressing  to  carry  his  morning  paper  route,  had  the  thought  that  his  father  would  surely  find  some  way  to  mess  it  up. He  recognized  after  a  moment  the  strange  bed  with  its  strange  damp  odor,  and  remembered  where  he  was-remembered  not  only  the  previ-  ous  day  but  glimpsed  his  entire  life-  and  came  fully  awake.  The  voices  were  from  the  kitchen,  and  he  lay  listening,  watching  the  line  of  dull  white  along  the  bottom  of  the  bedroom  door.  There  were  four  or  five  of  them,  and  laughter  was  around  everything  they  said.  Andrew  looked  across  the  room  toward  Howard's  bed,  but  heard  noth-  ing,  could  see  nothing  in  the  darkness. He  considered  whis-  pering  to  Howard,  considered  talking  to  him  to  somehow  straighten  out  in  their  own  way  this  confusion  that  had  them  listening  in  the  dark,  and  afraid.  But  Andrew  was  not  sure  of  the  idea,  nor  sure  of  Howard,  nor  of  himself,  and  he  could  not  bring  himself  to  speak.  There  were  both  men  and  women  in  the  kitchen.  Andrew  knew  Ward's  voice  the  way  you  never  forget  certain  voices  even  though  you've  heard  them  only  once  or  twice  (&quot;You  guys  want  some  pop  ...&quot;),  but  his  mother's  voice  sounded  different.
