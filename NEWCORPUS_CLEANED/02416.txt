My  cousin  glanced  toward  Leonid  and  then  said  to  her,  &quot;You  don't  even  know  what  you're  saying.&quot;  My  aunt  murmured  discreetly,  &quot;You're  doing  it  again,  Mama,&quot;  and  held  out  her  hand  forthe  ball  of  dough.  I  suddenly  felt  that  I  was  going  to  be  sick:  from  everything  I  had  eaten  and  drunk  at  that  table,  from  my  grandmother's  pies  and  my  uncle's  wine;  from  Grandmother's  humble  vulnerability;  but,  most  of  all,  from  the  fact  that  among  all  us  sinners  at  the  table  there  sat  one  real  knave.  His  arm  lay  across  my  cousin's  shoulders,  as  her  parents  could  very  well  see,  and  yet  neither  of  them  had  a  thought  of  objecting.  I  was  waiting  for  my  mouth  to  open  and  spit  out  the  words  like  machine-  gun  fire.  I  wanted  to  kill.  But  a  min-  ute  went  by,  then  five  minutes,  then  ten;  the  laughter  died  down,  and  they  were  all  talking  about  something  else.  My  grandmother  had  either  forgotten  the  insult  or  perhaps  had  not  even  con-  sidered  it  one.  I  could  perfectly  well  have  kept  quiet.  I  felt  like  a  traitor,  unable  to  look  them  in  the  face,  de-  spising  and  hating  myself,  Suddenly,  my  eyes  riveted  to  my  plate,  I  said,  said, &quot;I  see  you  already  know  your  way  around.&quot;  No  one  responded.  Not  one  of  them  could  understand  what  or  whom  I  was  after.  They  stared  at  me  in  surprise.  I  heard  my  voice  again,  this  time  loud-  er  and  bolder.  &quot;So  you  know  your  way  around  al-  ready?  You  know  who  you  can  talk  to,  don't  you?&quot;  Leonid  went  a  little  pale  and  stared  at  me.  &quot;That's  right,  I'm  talking  to  you.  You  know  who  you  can  laugh  at  in  this  house,  don't  you?&quot;  Inwardly  I  was  shrieking,  &quot;Get  away  from  this  table,  you  little  bastard,  you  scum-get  the  hell  out  of  this  house!  And  don't  come  back!&quot;  If  I  could  have  looked  at  myself  in  a  mirror  at  that  moment,  however,  all  I  would  have  seen  was  a  distorted,  pitiful  smile.  My  lips  were  trembling,  and  I  said  again  and  again,  &quot;You  know  your  way  around,  right?  You've  got  it  all  worked  out,  right?&quot;  I  almost  fainted.  Everything  went  dark.  My  grandmother  would  die  soon,  and  though  all  these  nasty  little  jokes would  naturally  cease  forever,  this  Leonid  or  whatever  his  name  was  would  remain  and  would  sleep  with  my  cousin.  We  would  all  remain  on  the  earth  for  a  time,  but  my  grand-  mother  would  be  no  more.  They  say  that  at  the  end  of  your  existence  your  whole  life  passes  before  your  eyes  and  you  remember  every-  thing  at  once,  only  to  forget  it  forever  in  the  next  instant.  If  you  return  to  earth,  as  abladeof  grass  or  awild  flower,  you  will  have  no  memory  of  the  fact  that  you  once  were.  You  will  not  remember  that  you  ever  suffered  injury,  that  once  you  fled  to  another  city  from  the  hurt,  that  sometimes  you  were  unable  to  defend  yourself  against  that  hurt  and  against  much  else  in  your  life.  The  grass  will  rustle  gently  in  the  wind,  and  that  will  be  all.  I  remembered  myself  as  a  child,  when  my  grandfather  was  alive.  He  would  never  have  permitted  someone  who  was  not  one  of  us  to  sit  at  our  table.  He  would  kiss  my  grandmother's  hand,  even  though  it  smelled  of  onions. She  would  say,  without  taking  offense,  &quot;What's  the  matter,  darling?  I  only  just  washed  my  hands&quot;-as  if  soap  could  wash  away  the  smell  of  cooking  for  a  big  family.  Another  moment  passed,  andLeonid  was  still  sitting  at  our  table. As  always,  circumstances  and  reasons-a  great  many  reasons-would  not  permit  it.  The  airplane  was  small  and  some-  how  did  not  look  serious.  But  it  hauled  itself  up  to  almost  inconceivable  heights  for  such  a  machine;  it  grunted  like  a  little  old  truck  as  it  climbed,  collapsing  into  air  pockets,  and  then  hung  sus-  pended  in  the  air  above  the  bluish  earth.  It  was  so  pure  up  there,  the  air  soclear  and  the  visibility  so  good,  that  what  from  time  to  time  occurred  on  earth  seemed  wildly  improbable.  My  being  vibrated  with  the  body  of  that  absurd  airplane,  and  my  spirits,  too,  vibrated  and  soared.  I  was  flying  home  to  [REDACTED],  to  my  native  city.  Wrong  again,  would  you  say?  I  was  born  in  one  city,  grew  up  in  another-where  was  my  home?  I  exulted-one  could  not  help  it  at  that  height-and  yet  I  was  exhausted,  too.  I  knew  quite  well  that  when  I  returned  to  earth,  feeling  better  dis-  posed  and  more  kindly  toward  every-  body,  they  would  think  that  it  had  been  outrageous  on  my  part  to  take  off  for  another  city  without  warning.  While  there  was  still  time  left,  I  took  a  note  pad  out  of  my  bag  and  started  writing  to  my  grandmother.  I  tried  to  make  the  letter  sound  a  bit  like  hers  to  me.  But  I  was  almost  positive  that,  as  usual,  I  would  forget  to  mail  it.  As  the  plane  was  coming  in  to  land,  and  the  tiny  houses  and  the  pine  trees  stretched  toward  the  sky,  I  pressed  my  face  against  the  window  and  breathed  so  hard  that  I  had  to  get  out  a  hand-  kerchief  to  rub  the  glass  clear  again,  I  searched  for  the  orange  fragments  that  were  mailboxes.  Where  were  they?  Something  shone  on  a  yellow  pillar,  but  it  was  only  a  pine  tree  on  whose  trunk  a  ray  of  sunlight  danced  deceit,  fully,  Where  was  there  a  mailbox?  Still,  I  knew:  All  over  the  world  were  scattered  the  little  orange  squares  of  mailboxes.  Please,  God,  don't  let  me  forget."
