Was  that  the  thunder  again?  We  all  heard  it-a  deep,  low  roll,  prolonged  and  heavy,  echoing  among  the  peaks.  The  song  broke  off;  the  shepherd  raised  his  head,  listening.  &quot;Another  avalanche  up  there.  est  le  saison.&quot;  He  threw  more  juniper  on  the  fire.  Paul  sat  up,  yawning,  glanced  at  the  door  and  then  at  his  watch.  &quot;Rain  or  no  rain,  we'd  better  be  on  our  way  down,&quot;  he  said.  He  pulled  on  one  boot,  reached  out  his  hand  for  the  second-and  then  came  that  other  sound.  I  don't  know  quite  how  to  describe  it;  it  began  as  a  jarring  shock  you  felt  rather  than  heard,  and  swelled  to  a  grinding  roar  that  filled           the  whole  valley.  The  shepherd  sprang  to  his  feet,  knocking  over  his  stool.  He  took  one  look  through  the  doorway.  &quot;Avalanche!  The  whole  lake's  coming  down  over  the  cascade!  Nom  de  Dieu,  d\xc3\xa9p\xc3\xa9chons-nous!&quot;  he  shouted,  and  bolted  out  through  the  door.  HE  next  few  minutes  live  in  my  memory  as  a  sequence  of  sharp  impressions:  Paul's  limping,  one-booted  gait  as  he  hustled  me  at  top  speed  across  the  slanting  meadow  toward  the  steep  bank  above  it;  the  shepherd  ahead,  scrambling  up  that  bare  incline,  agile  as  a  cat  in  his  sabots;  my  own  climb  after  him,  among  stones  that  broke  away  as  I  clutched  them  and  rivers  of  wet  shale  that  crumbled  like  sand  under  my  hobnailed  mountain  boots.  However,  we  did  manage  in  our  sauve-qui-peut  to  reach  the  top  of  that  almost  vertical  wall  where  the  path  came  out  of  the  beechwoods,  and  it  was  then,  as  we  crouched  breathless  a  hundred  feet  above  the  upper  rim  of  the  mead-  ow,  that  I  realized  there  were  only  three  of  us.  In  the  same  flash,  I  remem-  bered  Guy's  city  shoes  and  rubbers.  Glancing  down,  I  saw  him  standing  at  the  foot  of  the  steep  bank;  he  must  have  slipped  and  fallen,  for  he  was  coated  with  mud.  As  I  looked,  he  shook  his  shoulders  (how  I  recognized  that  shrug!  ),  wheeled  about,  and,  to  my  ut-  ter  stupefaction,  started  down  the  slop-  ing  meadow  in  the  direction  of  the  barn.  I  can  still  see  that  picture  of  the  barn  under  the  beech  tree  and  Guy  walking  toward  it;  he  was  not  even  running.  Beside  me,  Paul  had  lunged  to  his  feet.  I  don't  know  what  he  intended  to  do,  for  at  that  instant  we  saw  the  wall.  'That  is  precisely  what  it  looked  like-a  moving  wall  of  rocks  and  water,  bristling  with  pine  trunks,  that  rushed  down  the  valley  at  terrific  speed.  No  doubt  our  perspective  made  it  appear  higher  than  it  was  when  we  first  saw  it,  for  it  seemed  to  flatten  somewhat  as  it  approached.  The  grinding  roar-and  the  water  of  the  lake-came  with  it.  I  took  a  last  look  at  the  meadow-Guy  was  halfway  down  it-and  then  I  shut  my  eyes.  When  I  looked  again,  there  was  no  beech  tree  and  no  barn-only  tumbling  yellow  water  that  filled  the  valley  bottom  like  a  broad  river.  And  there  on  the  grass  at  the  very  brink  of  the  flood  lay  Guy.  He  said  afterward  that  the  wind  of  the  avalanche  had  thrown  him  to  the  ground,  like  the  blast  from  an  explosion,  when  he  was  half-  way  back  to  the  barn.  'The  yellow  cur-  rent  foamed  and  sucked  at  the  mead-  ow  grass  just  a  few  feet  below  him.  The  three  of  us  on  the  hillside  made  the  descent  in  record  time.  It  was  one  long  slide,  and  I  think  Paul  swore  all  the  way  down.  He  reached  his  friend  before  I  did,  so  I  didn't  hear  what  he  said  to  Guy  as  he  helped  him  to  his  feet,  but  I  was  in  time  for  the  reply.  &quot;I  was  going  back  for  my  coat;  it  had  my  notes  in  it,&quot;  Guy  said.  His  coat!I  saw  it  in  my  imagination  as  he  made  that  incredible  statement,  spilling  the  papers  as  it  whirled  down  the  gorges,  churned  to  a  rag  in  the  cur-  rent.  Guy's  next  remark,  though  banal  enough,  was  typical  in  its  implications.  He  glanced  from  the  peak  above  us  to  the  yellow  water  at  his  feet.  &quot;It's  of  no  consequence,  no  consequence  what-  ever,&quot;  he  said,  with  agesture  that  dis-  missed  the  mountain,  the  avalanche,  the  great  Count,  and  himself.  GUY  never  did  get  to  Foix  that  spring.  Perhaps  the  continued  rain  helped  to  dampen  his  Gascon  enthusiasm,  or  it  may  have  been  because  of  the  lost  papers,  or  the  fact  that,  as  he  admitted,  he  felt  &quot;rather  limp&quot;  after  his  experi-  ence  in  the  mountains.  Anyhow,  he  decided  to  postpone  going  to  Foix  until  a  more  favorable  season.  &quot;It's  just  as  well,&quot;  Paul  said.  &quot;If  you  did  go,  you'd  probably  find  the  record  office  burnt  down  and  the  archivist  dead  of  a  stroke.&quot;  As  it  was,  the  &quot;duel&quot;  of  the  two  Countshad  terminated  without  a  casualty,  so  in  one  sense  we  could  think  ourselves  lucky,  all  things  considered-  even  though  our  &quot;murderous&quot;  trip  to  the  Pyrenees,  as  Maman  insisted  on  call-  ing  it,  ended  in  defeat  for  everyone,  the  trout  fishers  included.  It  was  also  a  de-  feat  for  the  Armagnacs,  Paul  pointed  out  to  Guy.  &quot;I'm  convinced  that  your  friend  Gaston  Phoebus  had  nothing  to  do  with  it,  though,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;That  avalanche  wasn't  the  work  ofa  preux  chevalier.  Your  quarrel  was  with  the  Count's  bastard,  mon  wteux.  I've  thought  all  along  that  the  bastard  was  the  villain  of  the  piece.&quot;  Guy  took  the  teasing  in  good  part,  though  helooked  annoyed.  So  far  as  I  know,  he  never  duplicated  the  notes  that  the  mountain  had  taken.  He  said,  of  course,  that  he  intended  to  some-  day,  when  he  found  the  time.
