He  dreamed  his  usual  dream,  the  one  in  which  he  had  found  his  stuff  again  and  was  on  the  mound  at  Three  Rivers  throwing  seven  different  kinds  of  smoke.  The  sunshine  was  fragrant  and  the  grass  brilliant.  When  he  awoke,  feeling  refreshed,  the  funeral  was  over  and  the  coffin  had  already  been  wheeled  out.  Beryl  was  standing  in  front  of  him,  her  arms  folded,  looking  as  she  had  once  looked  on  bailing  him  out  of   the  Erie  County  Prison.  Magee  smiled,  rubbed  his  eyes,  and  then  realized  that  Coleman  and  Roxille  had  gone.  He  spun  around  in  time  to  see  the  little  boy  being  towed  by  his  mother  out  the  front  door  of  the  church.  Coleman  smiled  across  the  empty  pews  at  Magee,  who  saw  from  this  distance  that  the  design  shaved  into  the  side  of  the  little  boy's  head  was  not  two  eyeballs.  It  was  Eli  Drinkwater's  uniformnumber,  the  double  zero.  &quot;Poor  kid,&quot;  said  Beryl.  &quot;I  heard  what  he  asked  you.  God.  I  almost  lost  it.&quot;  &quot;I  know,&quot;  said  Magee,  scratching  his  chin.  He  could  not  seem  to  remem-  ber  what  his  reply  had  been,  or  if  he  had  said  anything  at  all.  &quot;So,&quot;  said  Beryl,  sitting  down  beside  him  and  taking  hold  of  his  throwing  hand. The  backs  of  her  hands  hadn't  aged  very  much  at  all,  and  Magee,  feeling  nostalgic,  watched  them  for  a  while  as  they  worked  him  over.  A  soft  lock  of  her  platinum  hair  brushed  against  his  cheek.  &quot;So.  What  are  you  going  to  do?  Buffalo? Her  face  was  neither  incredulous  nor  mocking;  she  only  bit  her  lip  and  wrinkled  the  bridge  of  her  nose.  Beryl's  nose,  though  small,  could  be  expressive  of  great  sadness.  &quot;Magee,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Matty.  Maybe  I'm  wrong  to  even  put  this  thought  in  your  head.
