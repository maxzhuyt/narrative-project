A  RIVERINE  TALE  HEN  I  was  a  young  scala-  wag,  the  river  used  to  flow.  We'd  scramble  down  through  the  thicket  to  theriverbank  and  take  our  clothes  off  and  run  in,  and  bathe  and  swim  and  play  water  hockey.  We'd  have  dirt-clod  fights,  chase  a  loose  pig  into  the  water,  hunt  for  crawdads,  and  have  contests  to  see  who  had  the  thickest  feet,  and  the  most  dirt  in  his  hair.  I  always  washed  myself  with  a  bar  of  hypo-allergenic  aloe  soap,  because  I  inherited  Pa's  sensitive  skin  and  Ma's  fondness  for  all-natural  skin-care  products.  I  carved  a  Styrofoam-battleship  soap  dish  to  keep  the  bar  of  hypo-allergenic  aloe  soap  afloat,  so  it  wouldn't  get  small  right  away.  After  washing,  I'd  leave  the  Styrofoam  battleship  dry-docked  be-  tween  two  outcroppings  of  schist  behind  the  diving  boulder.  Everyone  knew  where  I  hid  it,  but  it  stayed  put  because  people  respected  one  another's  property  in  those  days.  Hardly  anyone  bathed  alone  back  then.  The  average  person  did  not  associ-  ate  hygiene  with  privacy.  Some  pond  dwellers  took  to  solitary  laving,  but  most   folks  enjoyed  a  thorough  scrubbing  alongside  their  neighbors.  Swarms  of  freshwater  fish  swam  in  that  river.  We  had  aukfish  and  pi-  nochle  and  wab  and  dobrotail  and  buckfin  and  good  old  dumpike,  which  tasted  like  soft-shell  crab  if  you  baked  it  in  gray  river  mud  under  glowing  embers.  Late  afternoons,  you  could  make  out  hundreds  of  campfires  along  both  banks-the  smell  of  fish  frying,  anise,  and  malt  vinegar  mixing  with  that  gritty  ammonia  smell  of  night  earth.  Small  children  would  gather  plants  by  the  river,  and  the  older  boys  and  girls  would  turn  these  pickings  into  refreshing  sal-  ads,  adorning  each  other's  glowing  bod-  ies  with  necklaces  of  inedible  berries.  The  men  would  pull  streams  of  hot  milk  from  the  swollen  udders  of  cows  that  came  to  the  river  to  drink.  Broad-shouldered  townswomen  brought  out  platters  of  multigrain  bread  from  brick  ovens.  We  ate  together  naked,  on  super-absorbent  towels,  singing  songs  about  the  fecun-  dity  of  all  living  things.  I  accompanied  us  on  guitar.  Playing  guitar  in  the  nude  was  painful,  since  its  sharp  edges  dug           y  qrobeq  into  your  arms  and  thighs,  leaving  con-  spicuous  red  marks.  I  never  complained,  though.  People  didn't  complain  much  back  then.  We  used  riverside  herbs  for  treating  a  variety  of  illnesses  and  for  tuning  into  other  galaxies.  Powdered  ribbonwort  was  good  for  fungi  and  diseases  of  the  groin.  For  liver  ailments  and  pneu-  monia  you  boiled  the  ashes  of  the  acuma  fern  and  inhaled  the  steam  until  you  threw  up,  which  was  the  first  sign  of  re-  covery.  Tea  made  from  the  edgewater  rush  soothed  most  common  respiratory  problems  faster  than  the  leading  adult  nighttime  cold  medicine.  If  you  drank  it  while  in  good  health  you'd  come  down  with  a  nasty  flu  and  then  recover  again  within  minutes.  Different  herbs  put  you  in  touch  with  different  galaxies  and  star  clusters.  I  was  partial  to  ravenna.  Sprinkle  crushed  ravenna  leaves  on  your  tongue  and  within  minutes  your  pituitary  gland  would  vibrate  with  strong  radio  emis-  sions  from  the  Crab  nebula.  Ravenna  caused  temporary  blindness,  but  it  also  conferred  on  the  user  the  faculty  of  echo-  location  commonly  attributed  to  bats.  I  remember  jaunting  on  ravenna  one  night  up  in  a  tree  with  some  friends.  The  signals  came  fast  and  loud.  Bursts  of  indigo  alternated  with  waves  of  sidereal  symphonies. She  had  tremendous  lower-body  strength.  When  I  regained  consciousness,  I  asked,  &quot;What  happened?&quot;  &quot;La  petite  mort,&quot;  she  replied.  &quot;The  little  death.  You  fainted,  overcome  by  the  intensity  of  our  lovemaking.&quot;  We  cuddled  and  snuggled.  Harper  humored  me.  I  ran  my  tongue  in  figure  eights  around  the  two  dimples  just  above  her  lovely,  fruity  ass.  Harper  and  I  went  to  the  cove  once  a  week.  I  wanted  to  go  every  day,  several  times  a  day.  But  she  went  to  the  cove  every  day  with  a  different  person.  Once  I  saw  her  paddle  alone  past  Honorine's  Bend  on  a  red  inflatable  sea  horse.  In  the  fifth  week  of  our  acquain-  tance,  Harper  and  I  swam  out  to  the  cove  under  a  roiling  sky.  We  took  shel-  ter  ina  tiny  nook  and  clung  to  each  other  like  chimps.  The  clouds  broke  open,  and  sheets  of  water  exploded  down.  Lightning  struck  the  river,  creating  momentary  blue  plates  wide  as  forest  glades.  Thunderclaps  shook  the  air.  &quot;Cool!&quot;  Harper  said.  &quot;TheLord  is  doing  His  work.&quot;  Harper  came  from  a  transcendentalist  background  and  always  got  a  thrill  from  a  demonstration  of  God's  power.  &quot;Yes,&quot;  I  said,  burrowing  into  her  like  a  scared  animal,  &quot;and  I'm  trying  to  do  mine.&quot;  Goose  bumps  formed  on  her  hips,  making  it  easier  to  grip  her  in  all  that  confusion.  The  heavens  raged  for  a  hundred  days.  I  lost  patience  waiting  for  my  weekly  turn.  I  started  taking  different  people  to  the  cove.  I  took  Electra,  Meg,  Kezia,  Pasifa,  Stacey,  and  Thora-girls  I  had  grown  up  with,  now  brazen  young  women.  Sometimes  we  went  in  combi-  nations:  Thora,  Kezia,  and  me;  Meg,  Thora,  and  me;  Electra,  Pasifa,  Thora,  Clark  (Meg's  brother),  and  me;  Stacey,  Amber  (Thora's  mom),  Meg,  and  me.  Amber  came  just  that  one  time.  I  dis-  covered  that  I  could  love  a  lot  of  people  and  that  I  had  a  lot  of  love  to  give,  se-  quentially  and  simultaneously.  Harper  had  freed  my  mind.  The  sky  gave  itself  up  to  chaos.  The  river  overflowed  its  banks  and  flooded  the  towns.  People  drowned.  Houses  fell  and  washed  away.  Dead  ani-  mals-cattle,  fowl,  beast,  and  every  creeping  thing-floated  with  the  current  like  so  many  loose  logs.  The  water  rose  fifteen  cubits  high.  Then  a  great  wind  passed  over  the  89     earth.  Whirlpools  pocked  the  seascape.  The  storm  stopped.  The  water  subsided.  The  river  flowed  again  within  its  banks.  When  we  came  down  from  the  hills,  the  land  looked  freshly  shaved.  I  had  lost  Pa  and  Ma.  I  had  lost  many  friends.  I  went  back  to  the  two  out-  croppings  of  schist.  The  water  had  swept  away  the  Styrofoam-battleship  soap  dish  containing  my  bar  of  hypo-allergenic  aloesoap.  We  had  an  early  frost  that  year.  The  cold  was  hard  and  snowless.  The  river  froze  over,  and  people  walked  across  it  to  the  waste  places  on  the  other  side.  No  one  realized  that  the  winter  was  going  to  be  permanent.  Harper  came  to  my  fire  one  night  and  told  me  she  was  pregnant.  I  advised  her  not  to  have  a  child  so  late  in  the  mil-  lennium.  &quot;Fuck  you,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Ill  do  what  I  want.&quot;  She  went  ahead  and  had  the  baby,  a  boy  she  named  Forever.  He  was  not  my  baby.  He  was  Clark's  baby,  but  Clark  was  dead.  A  lot  of  people  were  dead.  Harper  managed.  I  married  Kezia,  and  we  huddled  to-  gether.  She  bore  us  seven  children.  Four  survived  childhood,  and  they  would  grow  to  be  giants.  Everyone  still  living  reproduced  of-  ten.  That  was  the  marvel  of  it:  you  could  have  all  the  warmth  in  the  world  taken  away  and  still  make,  furtively,  bushels  and  bushels  of  babies.  \xc2\xa2  "
