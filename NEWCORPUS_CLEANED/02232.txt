 b"WASTING  my  youth  on  the  banks  of  Dargal  Water  was  not  a  disagreeable  experience.  Sometimes,  of  course,  I  wish  I  had  squandered  myself  in  dance  halls  and  billiard  saloons,  but  at  the  time  I  was  the  same  as  everyone  else  and  mad  on  fishing.  When  I  was  courting  my  wife,  So-  phie,  it  was  often  along  the  banks  of  Dargal  Water  that  we  walked.  She  would  come  up  from  Glasgow  at  weekends,  after  we  had  known  each  other  long  enough  for  my  parents  not  to  consider  that  unduly  progressive.  We  would  wave  to  the  fishermen,  who  would  return  our  greetings.  These  were  the  fine  summer  dusks  of  the  nineteen-fifties.  Only  now  do  I  realize  that  my  friends  and  neighbors,  as  they  stood  there  with  rod  and  line,  or  waded  in  quick  water,  expected  me  to  return  to  them  once  I  had  married.
