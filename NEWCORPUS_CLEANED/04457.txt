 b"FROM  May  through  August  of  last  year,  everyone  I  met  on  the  mainland  said  to  me,  &quot;Salamis?  Is  that  where  you  live?  With  those  savages?  It  would  be  unbearable  enough  for  us,  but  for  an  American  .  .  .&quot;  None-  theless,  the  Greeks  are  very  proud  of  this  island,  or,  rather,  of  its  history.  W  hen  Athenian  friends  came  on  a  first  visit  to  my  house  at  the  northeast  end  of  Salamis  they  invariably  and  excitedly  pointed  down  to  the  straits  where  the  Persian  fleet  was  destroyed  and  over  across  to  the  Attic  mountains-those  luminous  orange  rocks  whence  Xerxes  and  his  secretaries  observed  this  most  dampening  of  Waterloos.  It  was  im-  possible  not  to  share  in  my  guests'  emo-  tion  and  see  with  them,  through  all  that  intervened:  twenty-five  centuries;  the  tiny  screaming  islet  of  Agios  Giorgios,  where  exiled  madmen  stood  along  the  shore  in  theirrags,  imploring  fishermen  and  passing  boats  for  cigarettes;  Niar-  chos'  drydocked  tankers  lined  up  in  the  Bay  of  Ambelakia  as  neatly  as  the  pleats  of  an  accordion;  and  (so  intermi-  nable  that  the  straits  seemed  turned  by  them  into  a  belt  conveyor)  the  gray  stream  of  battleships,  the  black  sinister  stream  of  submarines  flowing  from  the      naval  station  on  the  western  peninsula  of  Salamis.  I  looked  and  transpierced,  expanding  with  the  enthusiasm  that  comes  of  imag-  ining  righteous  wars,  glorious  victories,  the  collisions  of  great  cultures.  But  in-  evitably  my  friends  would  return  to  Athens,  taking  Herodotus  along  with  them,  and  I  would  be  deprived  of  all  but  the  reality  that  so  horrifies  the  Greeks.  Salamis  is  not  everywhere  as  unpic-  turesque  as  this  region  I  inhabited.  The  southwestern  lands,  owned  by  the  con-  vent  of  Faneromeni-whose  cells  were  all  season  staffed  with  holidayers-are  flooded  by  evergreens  and,  in  summer,  by  campers'  tents.  The  view  toward  the  mainland  is  good-especially  with  the  right  eye  closed,  because  otherwise  the  untroubled  Aegean  and  the  long  palm-  and-pine  promenade  of  Megara  Pevko  are  curtained  by  smoke  from  the  cement  factories  at  Elevsis.  But  full  south  lies  the  jewel  of  the  island,  its  capital-  Koulouri  (or,  also,  Salamis):  a  lovely,  Moorish,  blue-washed  town  all  of  closed  courts  and  walled  gardens  wherein  flowers  grow  lavishly  and,  indeed,  unex-  pectedly,  for  there  is  almost  no  water  on  Salamis;  it  must  either  be  collected  in  cisterns  during  the  rainy  months  or bought  for  three  cents  agallon  from  water  merchants  who  come  round  daily  with  donkey  carts.  Koulouri  rises  above  a  narrow  inlet;  I  call  it  an  inlet  because  I  have  been  told  it  is  such,  but  to  my  eyes  it  was  always  a  lake  surrounded  by  mountains.  During  a  summer  sun-  set  Koulouri  is  spectacular.  For  a  mile  along  the  shore,  caf\xc3\xa9s  spring  out,  and  as  the  nightly  wind  comes  up,  the  populace  descends  first  to  cool  itself  and  then  to  warm  itself  with  hot  drinks  and  the  narghile,  whose  coals  glow  brighter  as  the  sky  turns  red,  deepens  into  green  and  purple,  extinguishes  itself,  and  abruptly  flings  out  the  stars.  Very  exotic,  but  communications  be-  tween  Koulouri  and  the  mainland  are  poor,  and  since  I  was  obliged  to  go  frequently  to  Athens,  I  had  a  place  off  the  little  port  of  Kamatero.  Here  is  a  scraggly  brushy  region,  mostly  noth-  ing-a  scramble  of  space  and  peasant  shacks,  a  cathedral  high  on  the  hill,  a  few  summer  villas  belonging  to  Athe-  nians,  dwarf  evergreens  and  parched  brambles,  garbage  heaps,  and  the  colos-  sal  shattered  skeleton  of  the  Nazi  head-  quarters,  where  virgins  were  once  raped  and  murdered  and  to  which  lovers  now  retreat.  Between  sea  and  land  is  a  line  of  boulders,  thickly  tarred  by  the  tank-  ers;  and  although  there  is  a  fairly  pop-  ular  beach  in  the  vicinity,  one  always  comes  out  of  the  water  dirtier  than  one  went  in.  There  are  also  broken  and  de-  serted  henhouses  and  stone  huts  where-  in  live  hermit  dogs  and  cats,  the  Timons  of  Salamis,  who  have  escaped  the  poi-  soned  bread  of  the  police  and  the  malice  of  the  Salamites.  One  of  these  animals,  a  big  black  bitch  with  a  tan  mask  round  her  eyes,  the  most  disliked  animal  on  the  island,  gave  up  the  solitary  life  and  came  to  live  with  me  at  her  own  insistence. I  was  feeling  tri-  umphant  when  suddenly  two  strange  men  grabbed  my  arms  and  dragged  me  into  Melbo's  presence,  She  socked  me  in  the  face  several  times  before  Adonis  came  to  release  me.  &quot;No,  no!&quot;  he  shouted.  &quot;Not  here.  If  you  touch  him,  you  will  all  go  to  prison.&quot;  But  the  phrase  &quot;not  here&quot;  hit  me  more  power-  fully  than  Melbo's  fist,  and  I  guessed  there  had  been  talk  of  following  me  to  the  mainland.  Socrates  appeared  to  have  inferred  the  same  thing,  for  he  went  directlyto  Adonis,  took  him  by  the  el-  bow,  and  said,  &quot;For  apoliceman,  you're  very  careless  of  what  you  say.&quot;  Confused  and  embarrassed,  Adonis  let  Socrates  lead  him  to  the  car;  he  sat  down  unhappily  beside  Columbina,  who  was  hiding  her  face  and  wailing.  Soc-  rates  and  I  then  climbed  into  the  front  seat.  Though  the  motor  was  turned  on,  we  could  not  move,  because  of  being  encircled  by  the  enemy.  &quot;Olfred,  Adonis,  wait!&quot;  lamented  Phroso,  hu-  miliated  by  our  having  forgotten  her.  .  With  disgust  she  pushed  Columbina  to  the  floor  of  the  car,  sat  down  beside  the  Corporal,  adjusted  her  sunbonnet,  and  seemed  quite  pleased  at  the  prospect  ouf  a  drive  to  the  village.  &quot;Why  don't  you  move?&quot;  she  asked  Socrates,  and  when  the  reason  was  pointed  out  to  her,  she  ordered  Adonis  to  send  everyone  away.  Obedient  and  miserable,  he  leaned  from  the  window  and  shouted  that  he  was  giving  thedriverthe  order  to  go,  and  anyone  remaining  in  the  car's  path  would  be  knocked  down  mercilessly  and  killed.  The  way  cleared,  and  Socrates  drove  down  the  hill.  &quot;'Terrible  people!&quot;  complained  Phroso.  &quot;You're  lucky  to  be  leaving  this  island.&quot;  &quot;He's  lucky  to  be  leaving  it  alive,&quot;  Adonis  added  gloomily,  but  when  Phroso  laughed  at  his  wit  he  cheered  up  considerably.  I  myself  was  feeling  only  relief.  Halfway  to  the  village,  a  cloud  of  speeding  bicycles  passed  us,  and  Soc-           " b"rates  said,  &quot;'Those  are  the  boys  from  the  house.&quot;  And  indeed,  when  we  reached  the  harbor,  Melbo's  five  sons  and  three  of  their  friends  were,  along  with  their  bicycles,  already  waiting  on  the  ferry.  I  turned  to  Adonis,  but  before  I  could  speak,  he  jumped  from  the  car  and  quickly  drove  the  Eumenides  back  to  shore.  Demetrio,  who  studied  the  man-  ners  of  elegant  Frenchmen,  wheeled  his  bicycle  past  the  car,  dropped  back,  paused  at  the  open  window,  lowered  his  head,  and  spat.  It  was  a  momentary  renunciation  of  his  dreams,  and  I  grieved  for  having  caused  it.  The  ferry  had  been  waiting  only  for  us,  and  as  soon  as  we  had  driven  on  board  and  got  out  ofthe  car,  Phroso  went  ashore  and  waited  there  with  Adonis  to  wave  us  away.  &quot;Come  see  me  soon!&quot;  she  shouted  as  the  ferry  chugged  off,  and  then,  laughing,  she  added,  &quot;Send  me  a  postcard  to  say  when  you'll  come,  and  I'll  make  Adonis  meet  you  at  the  boat-for  safety!&quot;  The  ferry  moved  into  the  straits,  the  mist  spilling  back  and  receding  until  Agios  Giorgios  revealed  itself.  When  we  sailed  past  the  coast  of  that  little  island,  seven  skeletal  madmen  in  their  gray  denim  rags  came  running  to  the  shore  and  breast-deep  into  the  water,  pleading  for  cigarettes.  Leaning  far  over  the  rail,  I  handed  three  cigarettes  to  one  man  and  three  to  another;  I  had  none  left,  and  Socrates  did  not  smoke.  &quot;Sorry,  that's  all  I've  got!&quot;  I  called  to  the  five  disappointed  men,  but  they  were  paying  no  more  attention  to  me.  As  the  ferry  slipped  by,  they,  formed  a  circle  in  the  water  and  solemnly  shared  up  the  smokes. "
