It  was  a  color  I  would  never  have  considered  and  I  couldn't  have  guessed  that  Alan  would  either,  not  if  I  stayed  with  him  for  a  million  years.  &quot;I'm  leaving,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;I  went  to  American  Express  and  made  a  reser-  vation.  Only  three  days  more  of  me.&quot;  &quot;Are  you?'&quot;  he  replied.  &quot;Yes.  It  must  be  nearly  time  for  your  ticket  to  expire.  Well.  It  won't  be  the  same  without  you,  will  it?  All  on  my  own.&quot;  I  looked  at  Alan  tosee  if  he  was  sorry,  but  his  face  was  more  masklike  than  before  and  the  glow  of  his  cigarette  cast  light  only  on  his  smooth,  full  lips.  He'd  known  all  along,  then,  that  'd  go,  that  I'd  keep  faith  with  my  ticket.  He  must  have  been  waiting  politely,  hoping  I'd  leave  sooner,  leave  his  shop  and  his  orphan  life,  pack  quietly  and  depart  without  a  fuss,  spare  him  the  trouble  even  of  a  sad  parting.  And  when  I  was  gone  and  Alan  was  alone  with  Little  Dorrit,  he'd  hug  himself  to  himself,  and  vow  to  be  more  careful  next  time  and  find  someone  who  promised  even  less  trouble  than  I.  &quot;That  color  makes  a  real  differ-  ence,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;It  took  me  hours  to  find  the  right  shade.  And  it  wasn't  easy  painting  that  high  up,  tiptoe  on  top  of  chairs  and  such.  My  side's  aching.&quot;  &quot;I  can  imagine,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;You  don't  think  it's  too  dark,  do  you?&quot;  Alan  asked.  &quot;That  was  the  sub-  ject  of  much  inner  turmoil.&quot;  &quot;Too  dark?  It  may  bea  shade  too  dark  at  this  time  of  day,&quot;  I  said,  &quot;but  in  the  morning  it'll  be  something  else  -you'll  see.&quot;  I  squinted  my  eyes,  blurring  everything  together-Alan,  myself,  the  stairwell  as  it  had  been  and  the  way  it  was  now.
