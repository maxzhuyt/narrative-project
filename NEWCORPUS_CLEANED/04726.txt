FURTHER  NOTES  ON  FITZGERALD  AT  LA  PAIX  S  my  mother  once  put  it,  Perun  and  A  Scott  Fitzgerald  were  the  two  most  beautiful  creatures  we  ever  had  at  La  Paix,  our  twenty-eight  acre  place  on  the  northern  perimeter  of  [REDACTED].  Perun,  so  named  for  the  thunder  god  of  Russian  mythology,  was  a  wolfhound  with  curly  flanks,  re-  proachful  eyes,  and  a  long,  aristocratic  nose,  who  eventually  had  to  be  got  rid  of  because  he  killed  chickens.  Fitzgerald  also  had  destructive  possibilities,  though  from  the  point  of  view  of  our  family  they  were  far  outweighed  by  his  crea-  tive  ones.  There  were  two  houses  on  our  prop-  erty  and  we  lived  in  the  smaller  one,  built  by  my  father,  an  architect,  in  1925.  The  other-&quot;the  old  house,&quot;  we  called  it-had  been  built  by  my  grandfather  in  1885,  and  when  Fitzgerald  rented  it,  in  May,  1932,  he  must  have  felt  much  the  way  his  hero  Dick  Diver  did  when  he  vanished  into  upstate  New  York  on  the  last  page  of  &quot;'Tender  Is  the  Night,&quot;  the  novel  Fitzgerald  was  then  fight-  ing  to  a  finish.  To  the  once  famous  but  temporarily  eclipsed  author,  theold  house-it  would  be  his  residence  for  the  next  eighteen  months-must  have  seemed  like  the  end  of  the  line.  Not  that  it  lacked  a  literary  tradition.  Here  my  grandfather  had  edited  a  maga-  zine  called  the  New  Eclectic,  and  my  grandmother,  under  the  spell  of  Mrs.  Humphrey  Ward,  had  writ-  ten  historical  romances,  with  titles  like  &quot;Val-Maria&quot;  and  &quot;The  Royal  Pawn  of  Ven-  ice.&quot;  In  those  good  days,  life  at  La  Paix  had  a  gra-  ciousness  Fitzgerald  would  have  well  known  how  to  capture  with  an  image.  'The  velvet  lawns  were  embroi-  dered  with  flower  beds  of  coleus  and  geraniums  (one  of  them  spelled  out  &quot;LA  PAIX&quot;  ),  and  each  arch  of  the  long  porch  that  girdled  the  house  was  hung  with  a  basket  of  begonias.  In  summer,  my  grandfather  went  out  every  morning  before  breakfast  and  cut  a  dewy  rose  for  my  grandmother's  place  at  table,  a  gesture  that  to  me  has  always  seemed  totypify  the  era.  But  by  the  time  Fitzgerald  arrived,  the  sound  of  carriage  horses'  hoofs  had  long  since  faded  from  the  gravel  drives,  and  there  were  no  more  flowered  patterns  on  the  lawns  of  rougher  cut.  The  old  house,  with  the  red-brown  paint  bleaching  on  its  Victorian  gingerbread,  had  begun  to  disintegrate;  its  trembling  windows  sug-  gested  a  massive  looseness,  and  squirrels     from  the  surrounding  oaks  nested  un-  abashed  in  its  downspouts  and  mossy  eaves.  Yet  this  obsolescence  did  not  daunt  Fitzgerald.  &quot;I'll  take  it,&quot;  he  said  after  a  brief  reconnoitre  with  my  father.  &quot;I  never  was  interested  in  modern  plumbing.&quot;  There  was  always  great  excitement  in  our  household  about  a  new  tenant,  but  we  were  especially  excited  about  this  one,  not  only  because  he  was  a  writer  but  because  his  ten-year-old  daughter,  Scottie,  was  of  a  suitable  age  for  my  younger  sister,  Eleanor,  and  me.  (My  older  sister,  Frances,  was  less  affected  by  this  windfall,  having  by  then  abandoned  our  childish  games  for  interminable  heart-to-heart  talks  with  her  friends  about  boys  and  movie  stars.)  Fitzgerald  came  to  La  Paix  as  Tom  Buchanan  came  East  in  &quot;The  Great  Gatsby&quot;-&quot;in  a  fashion  that  rather  took  your  breath  away.&quot;  All  one  morning,  cars  swirled  past  our  house  and  out  of  sight  around  the  bend  leading  to  the  oak  grove  that  nearly  concealed  the  old  house  from  our  newer  one  on  the  hill.  Scottie  arrived  with  a  French  gov-  erness,  who  was  soon  superseded  when  Fitzgerald  hired  a  secretary.  The  inter-  viewing  of  the  candidates  for  this  post  occasioned  another  procession  of  cars  down  the  lane,  for  Fitzgerald's  require-  ments  were  unique.  He  wanted  (and  found)  alady  who  not  only  was  com-  petent,  attractive,  and  will-  ing  to  look  after  Scottie  but  was  of  a  type  with  whom  it  would  be  constitutionally  impossible  for  him  to  fall  in  love.  I  recall  that  I  was  jealous  when  Eleanor  made  friends  with  Scottie  before  I  did;  on  the  other  hand,  a  common  interest  in  football  gave  me  an  unexpected  rapport  with  Fitzgerald.  I  can  see  him  now  as  he  then  was-a  man  of  courteous  informality  in  his  ha-  bitual  sports  clothes  (he  was  the  first  person  I  ever  knew  who  wore  pink  shirts),  with  a  faint  smile  on  his  trench-  ant,  mobile  face,  and  eyes  that  were  neither  hard  nor  green,  as  has  sometimes  been  remarked,  but  gray-blue,  faraway,  and  full  of  the  pathos  of  burned-out  de-  sires.  His  conversation  was  taut  with  emo-  tion,  as  though  important  issues  hung  in  the  balance,  and  he  seemed  always  to  be  analyzing  or  appraising  as  he  talked,  in  a  voice  that  was  normally  tender  but  that  rose  at  times  to  an  abrupt,  scornful  &quot;My  God!&quot;  Physically,  he  was  both  delicate  and  compact,with  a  fine  nerv-  precision  abou  t  his  move  ments  a  nd  somethin  g  scrapp  y  ,  self-containe  d,  and  even  a  litt  le  b  elligerent  in  the  swagger  of  his  walk. We  children  would  roam  around  the  back  seat  of  the  Pierce  while  Fitz  -  gerald  sat  up  front  with  his  colored  A  chauffeur,  Aquilla,  whose  nose  was  so  .  flat  that  the  tip  of  it  almost  joined  his  upper  lip.  Aquilla's  short,  stocky  frame  was  packed  into  purple  pin-striped  suits,  and  his  passion  for  root  beer  was  evi-  denced  by  the  empty  bottles  that  littered  the  grass  around  the  old  house.  He  had  a  speech  impediment  that  made  him  sub-  stitute  &quot;s&quot;s  for  &quot;ths,  and  this  was  a  challenge  to  Fitzgerald,  who  devised  a  sentence  full  of  &quot;th&quot;s,  which  he  made  Aquilla  practice  over  and  over.  Fitz-  gerald  sympathized  with  his  lackey,  as  he  did  with  all  the  less  favored  among  men,  though  his  sympathy  did  not  pre-  clude  an  ironic  detachment.  (&quot;'The  steam  heat  brings  out  Aquilla's  bou-  quet,&quot;  he  wrote  in  his  notebook.)  'There  developed  an  easy  commerce  between  our  two  houses.  Eleanor  and  I  were  incessantly  down  at  Scottie's,  and  when  Fitzgerald  wanted  relaxation  from  his  labors,  he  would  come  out  of  his  study  to  chat  with  us,  or  mystify  us  with  card  tricks,  or  play  tennis  with  us  on  the  bumpy  grass  court.  He  was,  as  a  rule,  the  kindliest  of  companions,  though  sometimes  he  would  snap  at  Scottie  when  we  got  on  his  nerves.  How  well  he  knew  the  secret  of  extracting  d  rama  and  excitement  from  the  commonplace!  He  seemed  instinctively  to  quicken  life,  &quot;to  take  the  slag  out  of  it,  to  make  it  what,  in  our  dreams,  we  think  it  should  be.  &quot;For  awhile  after  you  quit  Keats,&quot;  he  once  wrote,  &quot;all  other  poetry  seems  to  :  be  only  whistling  or  humming.&quot;  Well,  if      you  were  a  child  who  happened  to  have  Fitzgerald  for  a  playmate,  you  might  have  felt  that  other  society  seemed  no  more  than  &quot;whistling  or  humming&quot;  after  you  left  him.  'The  days  were  so  interesting  that  Eleanor  and  I  often  went  back  to  the  old  house  for  more  entertainment  after  supper,  and  I  can  remember  Scottie  and  her  father  waving  us  goodbye  from  the  lighted  porch,  Fitzgerald  singing  &quot;Goodnight,  Sweet-  heart&quot;  in  a  weak,  rather  tuneless  voice  and  suddenly  breaking  into  a  little  fox-  trot  shuffle.  Less  often,  Scottie  was  allowed  to  visit  us  in  the  evening  (her  father  main-  tained  a  strict  surveillance  over  her  study  habits),  but  there  were  emergen-  cies,  such  as  the  night  he  got  a  hurry  call  from  the  Phipps  Clinic,  where  his  wife,  Zelda,  was  at  the  time  undergoing  psychiatric  treatment,  when  Scottie  had  to  be  deposited  with  us.  The  next  day,  Fitzgerald  wrote  my  mother  a  note  ex-  plaining  his  dilemma  and  closing,  &quot;With  many  thanks  and  a  sneaking  hope  that  sometime  your  well-ordered  life  will  meet  with  a  mild  emergency  from  which  we  can  extricate  you  ..  .&quot;  I  do  not  recall,  however,  that  his  hope  was  ever  grati-  fied.  All  the  emergencies  seemed  to  originate  in  his  quarter.  Occasionally,  on  summer  evenings,  Fitzgerald  himself  would  pay  us  a  neighborly  visit,  and  we  would  hear  the  crunch  of  his  saddle  shoes  on  the  gravel  lane  before  we  saw  his  dark  shape  approaching.  He  once  told  my  mother  that  reading  Thoreau  made  him  feel  he  had  missed  out  on  nature,  and  yet,  as  he  sat  there  on  our  hill-  slope,  rambling  on  in  his  nostalgic  cadences,  he  could  epitomize  in  a  casual  phrase  the  beauty  of  our  acres-the  dark  shadows  of  the  pines  athwart  the  moonlit  lawns,  the  scents  of  the  honey-  suckle  and  the  thorny  lemon  tree,  the  winking  fireflies,  the  mist  rising  from  |  the  pond,  whence  came  the  guttural  diatribe  of  the  bullfrogs.  His  spirit  seemed  to  merge  with  these  summer  delights.  As  a  rule,  though,  Fitzgerald  worked  in  the  evening,  and  he  was  more  apt  to  wander  up  to  our  house  during  the  day,  to  ease  the  weight  of  his  isolation-Zelda,  at  this  time,  being  more  often  at  the  Phipps  Clinic  than  at  home.  IT  was  a  crucial  period  in  Fitzgerald's  life,  a  time  of  stocktaking  and  con-  solidation,  and  his  talks  with  my  mother,  as  well  as  the  letters  (chronically  mis-  spelled)  that  he  sometimes  sent  her  to  amplify  a  point,  had  the  earnest  com-  pulsion  ofa  man  seeking  his  moral  and  intellectual  bearings.  He  was  swept  by  alternate  moods  of  dogmatism  and  self-  deprecation  in  his  almost  pedagogical  urge  to  communicate  the  lessons  he  had  learned.  He  needed  someone  to  hit  the  ball  to,  and  Mother  was  there  at  hand;  he  wrote  her,  &quot;Again  excuse  this  long  letter-couldn't  get  down  to  work  this  morning  and  simply  had  to  argue  about  something.&quot;  In  conversation,  Fitzgerald  was  one  of  those  people  who  break  down  your  defenses,  who  make  you  feel  there  is  nothing  you  cannot  talk  to  them  about,  just  as  they  are  quick  to  open  up  their  own  lives  to  you.  From  the  outset,  he  wanted  to  tell  my  mother  about  Zelda:  her  beauty,  her  brilliance,  her  physical  daring,  her  appeal  to  men-all  that  he  hadhad  and  lost  but  hadn't  quite  given  up  hope  of  regaining.  And  he  could  talk  objectively  about  his  drinking,  which  he  had  become  reconciled  toas  an  ex-  igency  of  the  creative  life,  the  classic  vice  of  authors,  though  he  admitted  that  it  was  asignof  defeat.  (Sometimes  during  a  visit  he  would  slip  into  our  dining  room  and  help  himself  to  a  glass  of  homemade  currantwine,  since  he  knew  the  exact  location  of  the  decanter  in  our  sideboard.)  While  he  was  reticent  about  his  novel  in  progress,  my  mother  could  see  that  he  was  excited  by  it  and  that  he  thought  it  might  be  the  best  thing  he  had  done  so  far.  But  at  the  back  of  his  mind  lurked  a  tragic  awareness  that  his  star  was  falling,  that  the  public  had  ceased  to  concern  itself  with  the  fast,  moneyed  society  which  was  his  material,  that  in-  terest  was  shifting  to  a  more  grass-  roots  kind  of  literature-to  the  works  of  Cather,  Sherwood  Anderson,  and  Dos  Passos.  Fitzgerald  accepted  the  fact  philosophically,  without  rancor.  And  he  was  generous  in  his  praise  of  other  writ-  ers,  especially  Hemingway,  whom  he  looked  up  toas  a  talent  of  a  higher  order.  One  day  when  he  came  to  call,  he  had  with  him  several  letters  from  Heming,  way,  one  of  which  delighted  him  be-  cause,  beneath  the  signature  &quot;Ernest,&quot;  Hemingway  had  written  in  brackets,  &quot;Christ  what  a  name.&quot;  Another  time,  he  sat  on  the  stone  coping  of  our  porch  and  read  aloud  his  own  celebrated  tribute  to  Ring  Lardner,  which  he  was  about  to  send  off  to  the  New  Republic.  I  might  add  that  some  of  the  books  Fitzgerald  lent  my  mother  were  after-  ward  passed  on  to  me.  Not  Proust,  that  highly  seasoned  dish  for  maturity,  which  I  watched  her  devour  with  gluttonous  appetite,  but  the  novels  of  Wolfe  and  Hemingway,  and  the  diary  of  Otto  Braun,  a  talented  German  youth  who   was  killed  in  the  First  World  War.  There  was  also  an  unexpurgated  edi-  tion  of  &quot;Lady  Chatterley's  Lover,&quot;  which  slipped  through  my  mother's  hands  and  was  read  surreptitiously  by  my  sister  and  me.  Fitzgerald  would  have  been  horrified  had  he  known,  for  there  was  in  his  nature  a  well-concealed  but  authentic  [REDACTED]  strain.  One  of  Fitzgerald's  avocations  dur-  ing  his  stay  at  La  Paix  was  Marxism.  To  keep  abreast  of  the  times,  he  felt  he  must  tackle  this  dogma  so  alien  to  him-alien  because  he  had  neither  the  historical  background  nor  the  acquaint-  ancewith  economic  misery  really  to  un-  derstand  it.  It  is  not  surprising,  there-  fore,  that  some  of  his  pronouncements  on  the  subject  had  the  newly  hatched  quality  of  those  of  a  hot-brained  campus  intellectual.  &quot;''The  logic  of  history  won't  permit  us  to  go  backward,&quot;  he  wrote  my  mother,  and,  in  a  different  context,  &quot;When  a  United  [REDACTED]  senator  after  his  election  has  to  look  up  the  principles  of  Marxism  by  which  one  sixth  of  the  world  is  governed,  it  shows  he's  a  pret-  ty  inadequate  defender  of  his  own  sys-  tem.&quot;  Fitzgerald  himself  was  con-  cerned  enough  with  those  principles  to  number  among  his  infrequent  visitors  at  theold  house  a  young  intellectual  of  Marxist  pretentions.  Yet  I  suspect  that  in  his  talks  with  my  mother  he  brought  up  Marx  primarily  to  épater  le  bourgeois;  the  self-made  man  in  him  was  mildly  contemptuous  of  us  as  privileged,  protected  folk  who  did  not  know  what  the  real  rough-and-tumble  was  all  about.  &quot;Please  believe,&quot;  he  wrote  my  mother,  &quot;that,  within  the  limits  of  the  frame  you  have  chosen,  I  know  of  no  children  bet-  ter  brought  up  than  yours.  My  some-  what  tactless  bursts  of  critisism  of  the  frame  itself  are  prompted  by  my  faith  in  a  sort  of  sixth  sense  that  I  have  about  the  way  the  world  is  going.&quot;  And  yet,  another  part  of  him  wistfully  envied  our  quietude,  our  rooted  stability,  our  con-  servative  values,  some  of  which  he  hoped  would  rub  off  on  Scottie.  &quot;'Thank  you  for  asking  Scottie  out  .  .  .&quot;  he  also  wrote.  &quot;I  like  it  when  she  goes  to  your  house  and  gets  a  sense  of  the  continuity  of  life  that  her  own  choppy  existence  hasn't  given  her.  I  want  her  to  be  pret-  ty  hard  but  if  she  has  to  be  a  condottiere  to  a  certain  extent,  I  like  her  to  know  that  all  people  don't.&quot;  My  mother,  in  her  conversations  with  Fitzgerald,  would  sometimes  allude  to  her  Presbyterian  upbringing,  and  while  religion  was  to  him  a  less  congenial  topic  than  some,  he  approached  it  with  the  frontal  honestyhe  reserved  for  all  seri-  ous  themes.  {}{Even  if  hehadn't  been  interested  at  all,  he  would  have  drawn  my  mother  out,  for  he  was  that  rare  com-  plement  to  a  goodtalker-a  good  listen-  er,  wonderfully  attentive  to  the  other  person's  point  of  view,  though  he  could  be  ruthless  in  his  opposition  to  it.)  Once  when  my  mother  asked  him  to  do  her  a  favor-she  had  in  mind  taking  him  to  hear  Dr.  Harris  Kirk,  of  the  Franklin  Street  Presbyterian  Church,  a  man  of  fine  literary  as  well  as  spiritual  gifts  Fitzgerald  anticipated  her  by  saying  that  there  were  two  things  he  positively  would  not  do  for  her:  go  to  a  dance  or  go  to  church.  As  a  writer,  Fitzgerald  was  sensitive  to  the  beauty  of  the  Bible,  and  he  could  grow  eloquent  over  the  majesty  of  Ecclesiastes  or  the  narrative  swiftness  of  St.  Mark.  But  religion,  he  said,  was  not  for  him;  he  could  not  reconcile  with  his  intellect  the  Catholic  faith  that  was  his  heritage.  &quot;I  simply  meant,&quot;  he  wrote  my  mother,  &quot;that  for  me  the  test  of  human  values  is  a  con-  formity  to  the  strictest  and  most  un-  flinching  rationality,  while  in  your  case  itis  based  on  standards  of  conduct.&quot;  He  thought  of  religion  as  primarily  the  woman's  sphere,  because  women  were  intellectually  less  rigorous  than  men,  al-  though  he  would  have  respected,  and  even  envied,  a  man  who  was  an  honest  believer.  The  fact  is  that  Fitzgerald  had  far  too  much  of  the  poet  in  him  ever  to  sneer  at  genuine  emotion.  Hisad-  miration  for  our  gardener-a  weath-  er-burned,  earth-smelling  Irishman,  stooped  with  years  of  bending  over  flower  beds-had,  I  think,  something  to  do  with  the  man's  true  Catholic  zeal.  When  the  gardener'swife  was  dying  of  cancer,  Fitzgerald  asked  after  her  often,  and  when  she  was  finallylaid  out  in  the  neat  little  &quot;best  parlor&quot;  of  their  cottage,  he  went  to  pay  his  respects,  perhaps  kneeling  for  a  moment  beside  her  coffin  to  say  a  prayer.  It  was  a  characteristic  act  of  kindness  on  his  part,  though  it  was  also  characteristic  that  he  went  with  only  a  topcoat  over  his  pajamas,  which,  together  with  bedroom  slippers,  con-  stituted  his  uniform  during  a  large  part  of  his  waking,  working  hours.  As  one  may  imagine,  there  was  never  any  dearth  of  conversation  with  Fitz-  gerald,  but  the  most  persistent  topic  of  all,  my  mother  found,  was  we  chil-  dren.  It  never  palled,  because  Fitz-  gerald,  with  his  dramatist's  eye,  be-  lieved  that  all  human  beings-even  pre-adolescents-had  limitless  dimen-  sions.  My  mother  has  told  me  that  one  day  he  came  up  to  our  house  to  de-  scribe  how  he  had  passed  Scottie  perched  high  in  a  tree,  with  me  seated  be-  neath  her  on  the  ground,  my  back  against  the  trunk,  both  ofus  reading,  completely  lost,  completely  at  peace.  He  said  it  was  one  of  the  most  beautiful  sights  he  had  ever  seen.  Then  he  went  on  to  contrast  this  oblivious  child's  con-  tentment  with  the  struggle,  and  even  bitterness,  that  might  one  day  come  be-  tween  us  as  man  and  woman.
