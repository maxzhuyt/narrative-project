When  the  Orator  returned  to  the  stoop  and  faced  the  men  and  women,  the  widow  Itupi  pulled  his  sleeve.  &quot;I  would  like  to  talk  to  them,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;A  few  words.&quot;  &quot;By  all  means,&quot;  said  the  Orator.  She  climbed  up  beside  him,  glancing  at  Stormalong,  who  blinked  as  though  he  had  just  looked  into  the  sun.  Then,  in  the  trumpet  tones  of  a  veteran  cam-  paigner,  she  said,  &quot;You  men,  you  just  made  a  wager  with  us.  You  said  if  a  girl  is  born  the  ladies  of  Tofua  they  will  get  the  vote.  Well,  can  you  deny  that  this  has  happened?&quot;  &quot;'There  is  a  male  child  there,  too,&quot;  one  of  the  Councillors  reminded  her  crisply.  The  widow  Itupi  shrugged.  &quot;Oh,  yes.  I  know.  Another  mouth  to  feed.  But  don't  you  bush  lawyers  get  tricky  with  me.  That  yelling  you  hear,  that  is   the  little  girl  reminding  you  of  your  promise!  Andnow  we  will  all  wait  here  while  you  dosomething  about  it.&quot;  She  stood  before  the  Council,  im-  placable  as  a  big  ship.  They,  recalling  the  ancient  island  dictum  which  states  that  the  quickest  way  to  end  an  argu-  ment  is  to  keep  your  mouth  shut,  shrugged  and  said  nothing.  But  Wil-  liame,  the  Chairman,  raised  his  flyswat  for  attention  and  said,  &quot;She  is  right  to  say  this.  It  is  as  she  says.  Now  I  will  tell  you  what  I  think.  I  say  the  women  have  half  won  their  bet  and  half  lost  it.  Therefore  we  must  half  honor  our  agreement.&quot;  Stormalong,  in  the  grip  of  a  sudden  idea,  rose  agitatedly.  &quot;Y\xc3\xa9s,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Williame  is  right.  Do  you  remember  what  King  Solomon  did?  He  took  this  child  and  said  he  would  give  half  to  this  woman  and  half  to  that  one.  So  let  us  give  half  a  vote  to  all  the  women  here  and  see  how  they  behave  themselves.  That  is  what  King  Solomon  would  have  saidto  you  ladies-to  you,  Mrs.  Manino,  and  to  you,  Mrs.  Itupi,  and  to  Miss  Rachel  and  Mrs.  Uili  and  Mrs.  Fa'amoemoe  and  old  Mrs.  Auivi  sitting  down  there  in  the  jeep.  You  vote  wise-  ly  today  and  perhaps  next  time  the  Council  will  give  you  the  full  vote.  But  if  you  disgrace  yourselves,  I  say  the  hell  with  you  and  that  is  the  end  of  the  matter.&quot;  This  was  the  longest  public  state-  ment  of  the  Corporal's  life,  and  he  sat  down  abruptly,  a  trifle  stunned  at  his  boldness.  The  Council  discussed  1\xc2\xbb  the  matter  while  Stormalong  savored  the  winks  and  smiles  the  women  were  directing  at  him.  He  noted  a  few  angry,  reactionary  voices  as  the  argu-  ment  proceeded,  but  these  were  stilled  when  the  midwife  appeared  at  the  sta-  tion  door  with  the  twins  wrapped  in  a  sail  and  held  them  aloft  like  royal  heirs.  The  men  looked  up  blandly,  but  this  sign,  together  with  the  chorus  of  ma-  ternal  clucking  from  the  women,  was  too  much  for  them,  and  they  passed  Stormalong's  motion  with  a  series  of  sad  and  silent  nods.  Stormalong,  in  the  act  of  lighting  a  cigarette,  caught  the  widow  Itupi's  eye  again,  and  turned  as  pale  as  chalk.  &quot;Can  we  have  our  candidates  back  now?&quot;  the  Orator  asked  the  widow.  &quot;'They  are  in  my  sister's  house,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Loosely  fettered  and  drinking  tea.&quot;  The  election  took  place  that  after-  noon,  beneath  a  high  copper  sky.  The  Progressive  won  hands  down;  the  can-  nibal  lost  his  deposit.  That  night,  the  widow  Itupi  invited  Stormalong  down  to  the  tide-rip  beach  for  dinner.
