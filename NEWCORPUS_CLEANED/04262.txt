We  all  gave  out  bursts  of  talk,  of  course,  and  it  may  be  that  it  is  only  be-  cause  Johnny  is  dead,  like  Robbie  Clarke,  that  the  bursts  he  gave  out  before  he  died  are  remembered  by  all  of  us  and  are  growing  in  our  heads  all  the  time.  But  I  don't  think  it  was  the  dying  that  makes  Johnny  what  he  is  to  us  to-  day.  It  was  his  being  so  obvi-  ously  and  undeniably  a  man  who  was  going  to  be  so  great  he  couldn't  possibly  die  young  or  by  accident  but  only  when  he  was  very  old  and  only  when  he  was  ready,  and  only  then  after  he'd  said  one  more  thing  to  a  world  that  sat  at  his  feet.  Coming  back  from  a  week-  end  Johnny  spent  with  us  at  the  house  in  Yardley,  the  first  and  only  time  he  met  my  mother,  we  stopped  at  a  broken-down  old  bar-and-grill  on  the  edge  of  Trenton,  and,  over  a  beer,  I  told  him  I'd  decided  to  bea  banker,  to  go  into  a  big  bank  in  [REDACTED]  and  ease  myself  gradually  up  to  the  top  of  it.  That  was  when  I  got  my  flash  from  him.  And  although  almost  all  of  his  words  have  gone,  the  feeling  of  those  words-how  I  felt  when  he  gave  me  a  pictureof  what  it  could  be  like  to  be  a  good  man  at  the  top  of  a  big  bank,  telling  the  talking  money  what  to  say-is  in  my  head,  is  growing  there,  and  sometimes  when  Ifeel  it  all  over  again  like  this,  I  know  I've  got  to  let  down  the  flaps  and  come  in  for  a  landing.  That's  a  peculiar  thing  about  the  flaps  on  an  airplane.  You  put  them  down  when  you're  tak-  ing  off,  and  they  help  you  to  get  off  the  ground.  And  when  you're  coming  in  from  being  high  in  the  sky  and  are  ready  to  land,  you  put  them  down  again  and  they  help  you  get  backon  the  ground.  Now,  when  the  traffic  light  ahead  of  us  changes  from  red  to  green,  I'll  start  talking  louder,  or  maybe  I'll  sing  a  song,  Lois,  so  you'll  wake  up  gently  with  my  generation  inside  you  as  we  start  into  the  long  hill  that  leads  down  to  Tenafly.
