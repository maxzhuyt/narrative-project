 b"AT  the  age  of  fourteen  and  a  half,  I  became  a  cad.  My  pitch  up  sent  me  to  be  educated  at  Winches-  ter,  a  college  (or,  as  you  Americans  would  have  it,  a  boarding  school)  older  than  Eton  and  likewise  designed  for  the  production  of  gentlemen.  But  Win-  chester  gentlemen  are  supposed  to  be  of  asuperior  species,  subscribing  to  the  school  motto  that  &quot;Manners  Makyth  Man&quot;  and  destined,  according  to  the  fourteenth-century  founder,  William  of  Wykeham,  Bishop  of  Winchester,  to  become  &quot;highly  educated  and  devoted  administrators  in  Church  and  State.&quot;  The  battles  of  these  gentlemen  in  later  life  are  not  of  the  rough-and-tumble  variety  first  practiced  and  won  on  the  playing  fields  of  Eton  but,rather,  battles  of  words,  won  or  lost  for  the  most  part  at  the  desks  of  Whitehall,  in  the  rostrums  of  the  Law  Courts,  or  around  the  committee-room  tables  of  the  Socialist  Party.  Winchester  pro-  duced  me  as  one  of  its  occasional  waste  products.  Winchester  has  a  language  of  its  own,  called  &quot;notions'&quot;-a  language  notable,  among  other  peculiarities,  for  its  distaste  for  the  definite  article.  Before  actually  becoming  a  cad,  I  was  allowed  a  fortnight  in  which  to  learn  this  lan-  guage,  under  the  instruction  of  a  T\xc3\xa9eg\xc3\xa9,  pronounced  &quot;Tee-jay&quot;  and  derived  from  the  French  &quot;prot\xc3\xa9g\xc3\xa9&quot;-himself  already  a  cad  aged  fourteen  and  three-  quarters-who  found  me  an  inept  pupil.                                  From  him,  and  from  a  book  (it  had  a  coat  of  arms  on  the  cover  and  contained  words  printed  in  Gothic  lettering)  called  &quot;Winchester  College  Notions,  by  Three  Beetleites&quot;  (one  of  these  Beetle-  ites  later  became  a  Socialist  statesman  ),  I  learned  that  my  family  was  my  &quot;Pitch  Up&quot;  (Pater,  Mater,  Frater,  Soror,  Nunky,  and  Nevy  ),  and  that  a  &quot;Cad&quot;  (subst.)  was  &quot;any  junior  who  has  a  regular  sweat.&quot;  To  sweat,  I  was  to  learn  soon  through  blood  and  through  tears,  was  to  serve  my  superiors,  the  prefects,  of  whom,  as  far  as  I  remem-  ber,  there  were  six.  I  had  become,  and  was  to  remain  for  the  next  four  years,  a  Wykehamist,  ambiguously  defined  in  the  book  as:  1.  A  member  of  the  School.  2.  The  School  paper  ofthat  name  insti-  tuted  in  1866.  3.  A  large  spider.  During  that  fortnight,  I  strove  to  learn  a  variety  of  other  notions.  Many  of  these  were  in  Latin.  &quot;Non  Licet  (adj.)&quot;  meant  &quot;Mlegal,  unwykehami-  cal;&quot;  &quot;Infra  or  Infra  Dig  (adj.)&quot;  meant  &quot;Scornful,  proud.  Ex.  'That  man  puts  me  off,  he  is  so  infra.&quot;  &quot;  Many  were  geographical.  I  learned  that  Salve  Diva  Potens  Corner  was  a  corner  in  Meads  by  Brolly  Tree,  where  some  poetic-minded  junior  had  carved  this  in-  scription  on  a  wall,  and  that  Brolly  Tree  was  a  tree  shaped  like  an  um-  brella  in  Salve  Diva  Potens  Corner.  I  learned  that  Adam  and  Eve  was  a  stream  flowing  past  Birley's  Corner  through  Dalmatia,  where  a  certain  Birley  was  supposed  to  have  been  drowned  while  bathing,  but  in  reality,  the  notions  book  explained,  he  had  &quot;lived  to  the  age  of  72,  dying  in  Man-  chester  in  1897.&quot;  I  learned  where  Mo-  abwas,  and  4974616c79,  and  Logie,  and  Ar-  cadia,  and  Paradises  Lost  and  Regained. It  appeared  from  this  book  that  other,  even  graver  ordeals  awaited  me.  I  might  be  subjected  to  sheepwash:  &quot;to  drag  a  man  out  of  bed  to  tub-room,  and  throw  him  into  a  tub  with  his  night-clothes  on;&quot;  or  I  might  be  toe-fit-tied:  pulled  out  of  bed  by  a  string  attached  to  my  toe;  or  I  might  become  thevictim  of  a  scheme:  a  &quot;highly  scientific  method&quot;  of  waking  a  cad  in  the  morning  by  means  of  a  rushlight,  which  burned  down  until  it  ignited  a  string,  re-  leasing  a  weight  of  books  ora  cup  of  water  onto  his  sleeping  head. I  wrote  my  pitch  up  long,  tedious  ac-  counts  of  my  finds,  in  letters  that  have  unaccountably  survived:  I  saw  a  starling's  nest  under  a  railway  bridge  which  was  un-get-at-able.  1  found  a  yellow  hammer's,  but  unfortunately  the  egg  had  a  young  bird  inside  and  bust  in  the  blowing.  I  haven't  found  a  hedge-sparrow  yet.  I  have  found  several  holes  which  I  think  are  sand  martins  but  have  nothing  to  dig  one  out  with.  I  saw  a  willow  wren  yesterday  and  have  been  trying  to  find  the  nest.  Later  my  interests  seemed  to  change.  &quot;Darlingest  Mum,&quot;  I  wrote.  &quot;I  am  getting  really  mad  on  butterflies  and  moths  and  caterpillars!  !!&quot;  These  old,  embarrassing  letters  re-  call  various  aspects  of  my  Wykehamical  life:  Mr.  Davidson  says  I  write  very  bril-  liant  and  powerful  English  but  what  1  say  is  not  worth  saying!!  He  compared  me  to  Mr.  Gladstone  in  this  respect.  We  had  a  divine  concert  last  night,  only  it  was  rather  spoilt  by  some  horrible  filthy  smoke  which  kept  pouring  down  the  chim-  ney  and  made  us  choke  and  cough.  How-  ever  after  a  time  we  got  accustomed  to  it  and  there  was  a  piece  of  Bach,  which  was  wonderful!  Terrific  excitement  about  the  Derby!  The  favourite  is  owned  by  the  father  of  a  boy  who  used  to  be  in  this  house.  An  awful  bounder  and  Liverpool  cotton  magnate.  I  hope  he  doesn't  win.  There  goes  the  bell  for  lunch,.  Raw  cat  and  cold  slimy  cocoa  shape.  Sometimes,  I  remember,  there  was  a  pudding  called  Rock  of  Ages.  Such  meals  were  supplemented  by  large  quantities  of  suction,  or  sweets,  which  we  bought  at  the  grub  store,  the  School  Shop.  Clothes  were  a  problem.  I  wrote  home  hastily,  &quot;It  is  a  bad  notion  to  wear  flannel  suits-isn't  it  sil/y-so  can  you  send  my  other  suit  by  return.  My  black  one  if  possible  only  please  let  it  down  and  mend  seat.  Do  be  quick  as  I  have  a  hole  in  my  brown  suit  and  nothing  to  wear.&quot;  My  pitch  up  were  not  bulky,  they  had  not  much  dibs  (were  not  rich,  had  not  much  dough  ),  and  dressing  me  for  school  was  costly.  The  great  sarto-  rial  occasion  of  the  year  was  the  cricket  match  against  Eton,  for  which,  since  they  lived  far  away  in  [REDACTED],  they  could  seldom  afford  to  come  down.  (This  was  a  pity,  since  my  sister  had  quite  a  cud  duck-a  pretty  face-and  this  would  have  been  good  for  my  pres-  tige.)  Nor  could  I  afford  to  dress  as  I  would  have  wished.  One  year,  how-  ever,  I  did  contrive-or  so  I  wrote- to  appear  at  the  match  as  &quot;a  study  in  mauve  and  brown,  in  my  light  brown  suit  and  brown  shoes,  soft  collar  with  mauve  stripes,  mauve  tie,  socks,  and  handkerchief.  I  am  sure  I  looked  a  most  awful  knut.&quot;  We  used  to  wear  straw  boaters  with  our  house  colors  around  them-colors  that,  together  with  a  galaxy  of  sports  colors,  had  to  be  laboriously  memorized.  The  only  one  I  now  remember  was  &quot;plum,  straw,  plum,  light  blue,  plum,  straw,  plum,&quot;  but  what  house  it  repre-sented  I  forget.  It  was  a  bad  notion  to  sport  a  speckled  straw  hat,  or  to  sport  a  satin  hatband  before  you  had  been  in  the  school  three  years.  To  tilt  your  hat  backward  was  to  sport  a  halo,  and  to  wear  the  hatband  inside  out  was  to  sport  a  firked  man's  notion.  (&quot;Firk.  [vb.]  To  expel.  Deriv.:  O.E.  fercian;  M.E.  fferke,  e.g.  Morte  d'  Arthur,  'ferke  to  the  far  lande  and  fetch  me  that  wa-  pene.'  &quot;)  When  we  went  home  for  the  holidays,  we  thankfully  discarded  these  hats  and  sported  our  cow  shooters  (&quot;[subst.]  A  billycock  hat,  a  bowler&quot;  ).  DID  not  make  many  friends  at  Winchester.  It  was  a  bad  notion  for  any  man  to  speak  to  a  man  in  another  " b'house,  which  limited  my  range;  and  it  seemed  to  be  rather  a  bad  notion  for  men  in  mine  to  speak  to  me.  I  was  not  good  at  raising  quills,  or  currying  favor,  and  was  often  seen  solo,  or  alone,  which  was  a  bad  notion  also.  One  of  my  diffi-  culties  arose  from  the  fact  that  only  second-year  men  might  sport  a  line;  that  is  to  say,  walk  in  the  street  three  abreast.  This  was  awkward  for  me  when  it  came  to  going  up  to  chapel  on  Sundays,  in  a  half-ceremonious  church  parade,  to  the  sound  of  the  bells.  Only  occasionally  was  I  able  to  save  face,  in  my  first  year,  by  persuading  another  man  to  walk  with  me,  or,  in  my  second,  by  receiving  re-  luctant  permission  to  join  the  line  of  two  other  second-year  men.  More  often  I  hid  alone  in  my  toys,  listening  to  bells  going  rotten  (one  bell  only,  at  long  in-  tervals)  for  the  first  five  minutes,  then  to  bells  going  double  (all  the  bells  to-  gether)  for  another  five,  and  emerging  only  during  the  last  five,  when  bells  went  single  (&quot;a  rapid,  monotonous  toll&quot;)  and  most  men  were  already  in  chapel.  Then  I  would  hurry  along  solo  and,  I  hoped,  unobserved,  to  reach  my  place  there  a  minute  before  bells  went  down  (stopped  ringing).  I  do,  however,  recall  one  friend-  who  has,  moreover,  remained  one.  He  was  both  jiggish  and  bulky,  and  later  became  a  renowned  writer  on  art.  But  at  this  time  he  was  not  much  more  re-  spected  than  I.  Like  me,  he  went  con-  tinent  a  lot,  and  I  remember  that  we  were  both  in  sickers  together  on  No-  vember  11,  1918,  when  the  armistice  was  signed,  thus  missing  a  whole  rem  (whole  holiday)  and  a  number  of  vic-  tory  celebrations.  Unlike  me,  he  rose  to  bea  prefect,  largely  because  he  had  been  in  the  schooltoo  long  for  this  promotion  to  be  avoided.  He  did  his  trap  up  tastefully,  with  Old  Masters  on  the  walls,  but  was  not  at  ease  in  the  role  of  authority.  Nearly  a  whole  half  (term)  went  by,  and  there  was  general  comment  on  the  fact  that  he  still  had  not  beaten  a  single  man  in  the  house.  Was  he  too  shy  to  do  so?  This  deficiency  must  have  begun  to  weigh  on  his  mind  a  little.  I  noticed  that  he  had  begun  to  grow  sterner,  more  aloof,  in  his  relations  with  me,  his  for-  mer  brother  in  misfortune.  Then,  one  day,  it  happened  that  I  omitted  to  hang  up  my  overcoat  on  en-  tering  the  house,  as  the  rules  demanded,  but  flung  it  hurriedly  and  untidily  down  on  a  bench.  He  noticed  this,  and  that  evening  summoned  me  to  library.  There  he  beat  me  with  a  ground  ash,  not  very  well.  His  vigor  and  authority  were  thus  vindicated.  Oddly  enough,  I  do  not  recall  that  I  resented  this  gesture  of  perfidy;  nor  do  I  resent  it  now,  in  retrospect.
