Take  in  fos-  ter  children  from  bad  homes  and  poor  countries.  Labor  to  cure  them  of  wounds  and  neglect.  Go  to  church.  Agree  to  believe  in  God.  Cut  her  hair  short,  stop  putting  on  makeup,  never  again  haul  her  breasts  up  into  a  wired  brassiere.  She  sat  down  on  the  bed,  tired  out  by  all  this  sport,  this  irrelevance.  What  made  more  sense  was  that  the  bargain  she  was  bound  to  was  to  go  on  living  as  she  had  been  doing.  The  bar-  gain  was  already  in  force.  To  accept  what  had  happened  and  be  clear  about  what  would  happen.  Days  and  years  and  feel-  ings  much  the  same,  except  that  the  chil-  dren  would  grow  up,  and  there  might  be  one  or  two  more  of  them,  and  they,  too,  would  grow  up,  and  she  and  Brendan  would  grow  older  and  then  old.  It  was  not  until  now,  not  until  this  moment,  that  she  saw  so  clearly  that  she  had  been  counting  on  something  happening,  something  that  would  change  her  life.  She  had  accepted  her  marriage  as  one  big  change  but  not  as  the  last  one.  So  nothing  now  but  what  she  or  any-  |  body  could  sensibly  foresee.  That  was  to  be  her  happiness,  that  was  what  she  had  bargained  for.  Nothing  secret,  or  strange.  Pay  attention  to  this,  she  thought.  This  is  serious.  She  felt  like  getting  down  on  her  knees.  Elizabeth  called  again,  &quot;Mommy!  Come  here.&quot;  And  then  the  others-Brendan  and  Polly  and  Lionel,  one  after  the  other-were  calling  her,  teasing  her.  &quot;Mommy!&quot;  &quot;Mommy!&quot;  &quot;Come  here.&quot;  It  was  a  long  time  ago  that  this  hap-  pened.
