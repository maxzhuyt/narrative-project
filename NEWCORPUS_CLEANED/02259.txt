My  theory  is  that  man  is  engaged  in  a  clandestine  love  affair  with  the  Angel  of  Death.&quot;  We  stopped  at  a  dilapidated  building  and  Morris  said,  &quot;Here  is  where  I  live.  In  this  house,  on  the  third  floor.  We  have  no  elevator.  I  hope  you  don't  mind  climbing  the  stairs.  Now  that  Tamara  is  in  the  true  world,  Necha  has  become  everything  to  me--a  wife,  a  sister,  a  mother.  Of  course,  it  is  all  platonic.  She  is  a  faithful  reader  of  yours.&quot;  We  went  into  a  dark  entrance  and  climbed  the  steps;  at  each  landing  Morris  stopped  and  panted.  He  pointed  to  the  left  side  of  hischest.  &quot;The  pump  refuses  to  function,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;It  has  been  doing  it  for  eighty  years.  How  long  can  it  go  on  pumping!  Enough  is  enough.&quot;  At  the  third  floor  Morris  rang  the  bell  of  his  apartment,  but  no  one  an-  swered.  He  said,  &quot;Either  she  is  not  home  orshe  doesn't  hear.  Wait.  I  have  a  key.&quot;  We  entered  a  narrow  corridor.  Morris  opened  the  door  to  the  living  room.  It  smelled  of  dust,  medicine,  and  something  rancid.  Over  a  torn  sofa  with  protruding  springs  hung  a  portrait  of  Lenchner-young,  with  curly  hair,  ablack  mustache,  and  shin-  ing  eyes,  which  looked  out  with  the  arrogance  and  the  complacency  of  those  who  have  found  the  truth  once  and  for  all.  Morris  Pintchover  re-  marked,  &quot;This  is  he.  What  Tamara  saw  inthis  faker  I  will  never  know.  But  she  defended  him  to  her  last  breath.  As  for  Necha,  she  was  a  real  martyr.  But  now  where  hasshe  run  around  to?  We  are  only  two  old  people  and  she  buys  food  as  if  we  were  ten.  Three-quarters  of  it  is  thrown  out.  She  has  a  kind  of  buying  mania.  How  much  can  we  eat?  A  piece  of  toast  and  a  glass  of  tea  is  enough  for  us  for  the  whole  day.  Thanks  to  God  and  to  Social  Security,  we  have  more  than  enough  of  everything.  Sit  down.  What  can  I  offer  you?'&quot;  &quot;Absolutely  nothing,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;Nothing,  eh?  A  man  must  want  something.&quot;  For  a  longwhile  we  sat  still.  Then  Morris  Pintchover  said,  &quot;You  were  right  then.  I  should  never  have  taken  them  in.  But  what  would  I  have  done  now  without  Necha?  She  is  the  only  person  who  really  knew  Tamara  and  witnessed  our  great  love.&quot;   "
