My  parents'  belief  was  that  I  &quot;played&quot;  a  lot  in  Farmer  Coles'  farm-  yard  and  particularly  in  his  cowshed.  True,  I  spent  a  lot  of  time  there.  It  was  the  most  serious  place  I  knew.  Chickens  laid  eggs,  cows  were  milked,  pigs  were  slaughtered  (their  screams  appall  me  now,  but  didn't  then),  cats  ate  mice,  dogs  ate  rats,  the  horse  pulled  a  wagon,  the  rabbits  became  stew,  the  geese  were  for  Christmas,  and  every-  thing  cultivated  in  the  garden  was  edible.  I  raked  the  hay  down  through  holes  in  the  loft  floor  into  the  cows'  and  horse's  mangers;  I  collected  eggs,  and  carried  milk  in  aslopping  pail,  though  I  never  milked  a  cow,  because  I  was  scared  of  them.  For  the  same  reason,  I  rode  a  hay  wagon  but  never  ahorse.  I  suppose  there  were  times  when  I  took  a  few  moments  off,  listen-  ing  to  the  swish  and  thud  of  the  cattle,  throwing  stones  at  the  ducks;  became  slack-mouthed  and  gormless  from  in-  haling  dung  and  hay  and  the  sweet,  icy  air  of  the  dairy.  Perhaps  it  was  these  moments  that  caused  Farmer  Coles  to  call  me  &quot;rum.&quot;  Perhaps  it  was  my  evil  eye,  careering  wildly  in  the  opposite  direction  to  its  twin  and  trapped,  from  the  age  of  three,  behind  supposedly  cor-  rective  glasses.  I  buried  these  glasses  again  and  again,  with  doglike  determination  but  human  reasoning,  in  the  rose  bed,  un-  der  the  laurel  bushes,  in  the  sandpit.  There  were  many  more  final  and  de-  structive  things  I  could  have  done  with  them-dropped  them  in  the  water  butt,  burned  them  on  the  bonfire,  trampled  them  to  death.  But  for  some  reason  I  preferred  burial,  as  less  final  and  more  ceremonious.  I  was  always  asked  (kindly,  I  think)  where  I  had  buried  them,  and  always  obediently unearthed  them,  though  often  they  were  bent  or  cracked  beyond  repair. I  also  had  a  large,  heavy  instrument  of  medieval  design  which  was  meant  to  exercise  these  wayward  eyes.  It  consisted  of  two  tubes  attached  to  a  kind  of  bridge  for  the  nose-into  the  end  of  one  tube  I  would  slot  a  slide  of,  say,  a  bird;  into  the  other  a  slide  depicting  a  cage. And  I  remember  the  Davis  Theatre,  Croydon,  where,  at  a  special  showing  for  the  clergy,  my  father  took  me  to  see  &quot;King  of  Kings.&quot;  The  Bishop's  children  were  kneeling  in  prayer  on  the  red-carpeted  staircase  and  my  fa-  ther  was  so  disgusted  by  the  whole  thing  that  he  bustled  me  out  before  the  Crucifixion.  That  was  Thornton  Heath. I  remember  vividly  the  excitement  of  arriving  at  these  tem-  porary  homes,  all  of  them  made  more  glamorous  by  the  thought  of  the  dis-  mal,  urban  house  we  had  left  behind.  One  of  the  first  things  to  do-though  strictly  under  my  mother's  supervi-  sion-was  to  strip  all  the  rooms  of  their  religious  bric-a-brac,  palm  crosses,  pho-  tographs  of  dead  bishops  and  live  cu-  rates,  and  put  each  roomful  of  junk  into  its  own,  labelled  cardboard  box.  Then  my  father  started  moving  the  furniture  and  my  mother  brought  in  flowers,  wild  if  necessary.  Sheun-  packed  her  cookbook-even  in  those  days  the  pages  were  loose  and  dog-  eared-and  put  on  her  apron.  On  Sundays  my  father  startled  a  congre-  gation  of  strangers,  and  my  mother  cut  the  dead  heads  off  somebody  else's  roses.  If  all  this  seems  to  indicate  that  the  three  of  us(I  remember  my  brother  only  once  on  these  visits,  when  we  mistakenly  found  ourselves  in  South-  ampton)  were  intimate  in  any  way,  a closely  knit  family,  I  am  pointing  in  the  wrong  direction.  Our  only  genuine  contact  was  through  my  mother's  cooking.  Perhaps  I  took  friends  to  these  places,  perhaps  I  didn't.  It  doesn't  matter.  It  is  the  place  I  remember:  a  conglomerate  of  Devonshire,  [REDACTED],  and  Norfolk,  of  unfamiliar  attics  and  cellars,  of  bushes  and  plots  of  ground  I  could  at  least  temporarily  call  my  own,  Southampton  must  havebeen  a  mis-  take,  because  it  was  another  city;  and  also,  very  grandly,  a  Deanery;  and  also  my  brother  was  indis-  putably  present.  The  summer  be-  gan  well,  by  my  waking  on  the  first  morning  to  find  a  travelling  circus  camped  at  the  bottom  ofthe  gar-  den,  an  elephant  chumping  the  hedge,  a  monkey,  hand  in  hand  with  its  owner,  walking  to  the  water  tap.  After  that,  for  two  shillings  each  we  sped  to  the  Isle  of  Wight  and  back  in  a  speedboat  called  Zip.  My  father  took  us  rowing  on  the  Solent,  but  the  tide  went  out  and  we  were  stranded  in  miles  of  mud;  my  father  lost  his  temper  and  called  my  brother  &quot;an  insolent  young  pup.&quot;  It  was  not  a  happy  day.  Young  people  (not  children)  from  Thornton  Heath  were  there,  girls  with  lipstick  and  tall  boys  in  blue  suits.  My  hair  was  clenched  back  with  a  huge  tortoiseshell  hairslide,  which  had  an  extraordinarily  intricate  mechanism  of  springs  and  levers  to  keep  it  in  place.  I  don't  remember  whether  the  young  people  were  staying  with  us,  or  whether  they  were  independent-  ly  in  Southampton-I  do  remember  the  deck  of  aboat  where,  by  some  miraculous  feat  of  clumsiness,  I  man-  aged  to  introduce  the  sharp  edge  of  this  hairslide  into  the  eye  of  one  of  the  tall  boys,  Basil  by  name. Sweetness  infected  me  for  years-oak  beams,  lattice  windows,  the  paintings  of  Margaret  Tarrant,  Flower  Fairies  of  the  Spring,  Summer,  and  Autumn,  lavender  bags  and  ram-  bling  roses  and  willow  pattern. What  can  you  think  about  dolls?  The  two  most  memorable  pic-  tures  of  my  childhood  were  a  Dor\xc3\xa9  en-  graving  of  Satan  being  cast  into  hell  and  a  picture,  in  the  Bible,  of  Solomon  ordering  the  baby  to  be  cut  in  half-  the  baby  dangling  by  one  foot,  the  soldier's  scimitar  at  the  ready.
