 b"THE  Communist  army,  to  which  Peiping  surrendered  in  January,  1949,  called  itself  the  Liberation  Army,  and  after  the  surrender  the  for-  eigners  remaining  in  Peiping  fell,  mere-  ly  for  convenience,  into  the  habit  of  speaking  of  events  as  having  occurred  &quot;pre-liberation&quot;  or  &quot;post-liberation.&quot;  My  own  experience  was  sharply  di-  vided  between  the  two.  Pre-liberation,  I  taught  English  at  the  National  Tsinghwa  University,  twelve  miles  from  Peiping,  and  had  a  house  nearby;  post-liberation,  I  wasn't  allowed  to  teach  English  anywhere,  and,  having  by  that  time  married  a  Chinese  girl,  lived  in  the  home  of  my  wife's  family,  in  Peiping.  The  story  of  Lao  Yu,  my  cook,  is  also  separated  into  two  parts  by  the  &quot;liberation.&quot;  Most  foreigners  in  Peiping  had  some  sort  of  servant  trouble  sooner  or  later.  Pre-liberation,  the  stories  of  these  trou-  bles  were  standard  conversation  at  any  gathering.  'The  servant  was  invariably  a  cook.  He  could  cook  French,  Rus-  sian,  Chinese,  or  Mongolian  food  to  perfection,  but  he  had  idiosyncrasies.  He  slept  with  carving  knives  under  his  pillow,  or  he  had  fainting  spells,  or  he  saw  ghosts.  Eventually,  he  went  berserk,  wielding  a  knife  or  a  meat  chopper.
