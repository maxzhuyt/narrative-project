  THE  SWAN  MAN  T  was  after  the  end  of  the  Second  World  War.  One  Sunday  morn-  ing,  the  heavy  guns  were  pulled  from  their  concrete  emplacements  at  the  end  of  the  village  and  set  on  car-  riages  one  behind  the  other  the  length  of  [REDACTED]  Road.  I  sat  with  the  other  twelve-year-olds  on  the  asphalt  outside  Judd's  shop.  A  gang  of  boys  in  long  trousers  stood  by  the  telephone  kiosk,  or  lounged  against  thecar  that  had  been  abandoned  there  three  years  be-  fore.  They  were  older  boys,  too  old  to  sit  on  the  ground  with  us.  But  they  watched  the  formation  of  the  convoy  with  a  wry  interest  similar  to  ours.  The  Canadians  were  leaving  [REDACTED],  taking  their  guns  and  their  liberal  hand-  outs  of  candy  and  cigarettes.  We  boys  never  had  much  to  offer  the  soldiers  apart  from  our  company.  We  ran  errands  for  them,  filched  small  articles  from  home  like  notepaper  or  firewood,  delivered  messages  we  only  half  understood  to  the  girls  that  worked  on  the  farm,  but  mainly  we  just  sat  around  their  billets  and  talked  about  airplanes,  especially  those  like  the  Jun-  kers  and  Dorniers  they  had  shot  down  at  the  time  of  the  raids.  Now  they  were  leaving,  and  we  wondered  where  we  would  be  able  to  go  of  an  evening,  what  we  should  find  to  do.  The  houses  where  the  Canadians  had  been  billeted  since  they  arrived  four  or  five  years  before  already  looked  strange  to  us-  exciting  now  that  they  were  being  locked  up  and  their  windows  boarded.  We  made  plans  to  force  a  way  into  one  of  them  and  make  a  camp  and  find  secret  places  to  hide  our  mes-  sages.  Perhaps  it  wouldn't  be  so  bad,  after  all,  once  the  soldiers  had  gone.  The  convoy  was  ready  and  about  to  move  off  toward  Portsmouth,  forty  miles  along  the  coast  road.  We  sat  astride  the  barrel  of  thebiggest  gun  to  have  our  photo-  graphs  taken.  And  then  we  went  back  to  our  pitch  outside  Judd's  shop  and  feigned  indifference,  as  the  big  boys  did,  while  the  guns  rolled  quietly  away.  We  wouldn't  wave;  we  wouldn't  shout  goodbye.  Alfie  Wheeler  started  to  cry,  and  we  ignored  him.  I  sat  in  the  sun  with  [REDACTED]  Ed-  wards  and  Bobby  Holden.  Alfie's  cry-  ing  made  it  difficult  for  us  to  speak. Those  times,  it  was  the  right  thing  to  do.  It  seemed  different  now.  For  a  long  time  we  sat  there,  know-  ing  he  was  sobbing  in  the  shop  door-  way  behind  us,  but  we  didn't  turn  round  to  make  faces  at  him  or  throw  gravel  in  his  face. In  my  imagination,  Sam  and  Beth  and  George  and  Mary  became  mem-  bers  of  the  same  family.  It  didn't  surprise  me  when  one  day  soon  after  all  the  cygnets  were  hatched  Sam  attracted  the  two  old  swans  and  their  babies  to  the  landing  stage  at  the  end  of  his  gar-  den. I  should  have  thought  this  a  terrible  waste  in  those  days  of  bread  rationing,  but  it  seemed  to  me  that  he  was  acting  as  the  head  of  a  family  should.  He  pro-  vided,  as  my  father  pro-  vided.  Every  morning  at  ten  o'clock  and  every  aft-  ernoon  at  five,  his  depend-  ents  came  to  the  landing  stage  and  he  fed  them  a  loaf,  The  weeks  went  by  and  it  became  a  loaf  and  a  half,  then  two  loaves.  The  cygnets  grew  to  nearly  the  size  of  their  parents;  adult  white  feathers  began  to  appear  on  their  backs.  When  the  summer  holidays  began,  I  sailed  my  raft  to  the  back-  water  every  day  to  watch  the  feeding.  Sam  took  to  wearing  a  white  summer  jacket,  and  Beth,  following  just  behind  him  with  her  curious,  paddling  gait,  had  changed  to  an  old-fashioned  dress  of  white  tussah  that  lifted  slightly  in  the  breeze  above  her  black-stockinged  legs  and  her  heavy  boot.
