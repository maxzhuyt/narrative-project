We  are  not  the  kind  of  people  who  are  looked  upon  as  weath-  er  vanes.  No  pollsters  ask  our  opinion,  and  no  large  bloc  of  voters  has  ever  waited  to  see  which  way  we  will  go.  Nevertheless,  we  take  our  politics  seriously.  My  wife  happens  to  be  a  Republican-one  of  the  things  she  inherited  from  her  father-and  I  am  a  Democrat.  We  are  residents  of  Bev-  erly  Hills,  [REDACTED],  and  belong  to  what  I  like  to  call  the  &quot;modest&quot;  income  group.  Nine  days  before  the  election,  an  unexpected  business  contingency  com-  pelled  us  to  fly  to  New  York.  We  knew  we  would  have  to  remain  only  a  week,  but  we  go  East  seldom,  and  we  decided  to  stay  an  extra  day  or  two;  that  is,  until  or  beyond  Election  Day.  How-  ever,  before  we  left,  my  wife  came  across  a  magazine  article  that  said  John  Quincey  Adams  was  elected  to  the  Presidency  by  one  vote  in  the  House  of  Representatives,  and  the  more  she  thought  about  it,  the  more  worried  she  became. So  we  wrote  out  our  applica-  tions  for  absentee  ballots,  and  I  gave  them  to  my  secretary  with  instructions  to  send  the  ballots  on  to  us  in  New  York  as  soon  as  possible.
