Against  the  dark  sky,  he  could  now  make  out  the  darker  shape  of  the  massively  piled  cone  of  the  bon-  fire  that  was  to  be  the  centerpiece  of  the  night.  And  as  he  came  nearer  still  he  began  to  smell  kerosene.  Micky  breathed  down  the  fumes  until  his  ris-  ing  exhilaration  became  unbearable.  Arching  his  back,  he  hurled  his  torch  high  into  the  dark  mass  in  front  of  him,  and  was  glad  it  was  done.  With  a  savage  speed  that  terrified  him,  the  little  flame  of  his  torch  ripped  away  in  every  direction,  like  something  alive,  spreading  its  blinding  stain  upward  and  outward  into  thenight.  Micky  forgot  his  pain.  Retreating  from  the  slamming  heat  of  the  fire,  he  watched  its  towering  shapes  and  the  sparks  that  flew  intothe  air  like  petals  as  great  spars  of  timber  in  the  white  middle  of  the  blaze  subsided  and  col-  lapsed,  cracking  like  whiplash.  Since  the  end  of  summer,  material  had  been  brought  for  the  fire:  old  lumber,  branches  of  trees,  broken  furniture-  anything  that  would  burn.  Micky  was  fascinated  by  the  giant  ribs  of  a  row-  boat  that  was  perched,  tossing  in  its  flames,  near  the  top  like  a  ghost  ship.  He  threw  his  trident  toward  it,  but  it  fell  short  and  was  lost  in  a  thick  wall  of  smoke  from  smoldering  tires.  It  was  time  to  get  out  of  sight;  al-  ready  he'd  hung  around  too  long.  Keeping  the  fire  between  him  and  the  hundreds  of  pairs  of  eyes  he  knew  must  be  looking  with  astonishment  from  the  bridge  at  what  he  had  done,  Micky  made  off  at  a  limping  run  toward  the  scrub  and  brambles  at  the  far  perimeter  of  the  ground.  He  knew  every  inch  of  that  rough  territory;  often  he  played  there  on  his  way  home  from  school.  There  was  a  bush  he  knew  of  where  he  could  hide-the  place  where  he  found  the  dead  seagull.  He'd  be  able  to  watch  them  all  from  there. There  was  nothing  the  police  could  do  to  stop  them.  More  and  more  planks  were  torn  away,  until  the  gap  was  wide  enough  for  ten  to  pass  abreast.  And  word  was  spreading  of  what  had  hap-  pened-over  the  bridge  and  along  the  High  Street  to  the  head  of  the  proces-  sion.  As  the  news  passed  back  along  the  line,  some  of  the  marchers  broke  ranks  and  started  to  run  ahead,  and  ignored  the  Master  of  Ceremonies,  who  ex-  horted  them  to  keep  in  their  places. The  fire  was  still  blazing  fiercely,  but  it  had  collapsed  from  its  stately,  thirty-foot  pyramid  into  a  shapeless  and  muddled  ruin.  As  the  people  threw  their  torches  into  it,  they  withdrew  and  gazed  ina  kind  of  hushed  and  sullen  trance.  The  band  stopped  playing.  A  few  fireworks  exploded  desultorily.  Knowing  himself  safe  now,  Micky  crept  out  of  his  hiding  place  and  sidled  into  the  milling  crowds.  In  the  sub-  dued  uneasiness  that  had  spread  over  the  ground,  he  was  beginning  to  feel  a  small  contrition  for  what  he  had  done,  but  he  knew  that  soon  the  spectacular  fireworks  display  would  start,  and  the  excitement  of  the  Carnival  would  grow  again.  The  best  things  were  still  to  come-the  judging  of  the  fancy  dress,  the  dancing,  the  prizefighting. Per-  haps,  after  all,  he  wouldn't  try  to  smash  up  Peter's  col-  lection;  setting  light  to  the  fire  was  revenge  enough.  He'd  find  Peter  now  and  talk  to  him.  Micky  could  see  the  shining  black  tophat  of  the  Master  of  Ceremonies  and  the  curve  of  his  whip.  Peter  would  surely  be  somewhere  near  him.  Then,  as  he  struggled  to  make  the  last  few  yards,  a  sudden  excitation  rippled  through  the  crowd  near  him,  and  a  tight  ring  of  people  formed  a  confused  and  spon-  taneous  scrum.  Perhaps  it  was  a  fight,  Micky  thought.  Every  year  there  were  fights,  and  his  father  had  always  dragged  him  away  before  he  could  see  more  than  the  first  few  blows.  &quot;He's  gone  berserk!&quot;  a  woman  shrieked.  &quot;Hold  him  down,  some  of  you  men,  before  he  can  hurt  anybody.&quot;  &quot;Don't  let  him  bite  off  his  tongue-get  a  gag  in  his  mouth,  quick. Others  came  to  help  and  cordon  off  the  surging  crowd,  and  Micky  found  himself  in  the  space  between  the  line  of  police-  men  linking  arms  and  the  figures  still  left  bending  or  kneeling  round  the  trouble,  whatever  it  was.  He  went  to  look.  It  was  Peter.  By  now,  the  convul-  sions  were  over,  and  he  was  lying  on  the  ground,  his  limbs  quite  rigid.  See-     ing  his  frightening  stillness,  his  terrible  pallor  in  the  glow  from  the  fire,  Micky  was  certain  he  must  be  dead;  nobody  alive  could  be  so  white,  so  motionless.  The  Master  of  Ceremonies,  who  had  taken  command  of  the  situation,  shout,  ed  his  orders  at  the  policeman  cradling  Peter's  head.  &quot;Put  a  coat  over  him.  Keep  that  gag  between  his  teeth.  There's  nothing  else  that  you  can  do  for  him,&quot;  Micky  heard  him  say.  Sick  with  fear  of  what  he  could  see,  Micky  couldn't  help  but  gaze  in  fasci-  nation.  He  was  struck  by  a  sudden  and  overwhelming  sense  of  dread  and  guilt.  Distraught  and  shaken,  he  tore  off  his  mask  and  wiped  his  eyes.  &quot;He's  all  right  now-sitting  up,&quot;  he  heard  somebody  say  as  he  started  to  fight  his  way  out  of  the  forest  of  bodies.  When  he  reached  the  dark  road,  he  stopped  for  a  moment  and  looked  back.  The  fireworks  display  had  begun,  with  rockets  bursting  into  exploding  yellow  stars  high  above  the  dying  fire.
