 EARLY  JOURNEYS  OT  long  ago,  I  came  across  a  N  travel  advertisement  that  set  me  to  thinking.  It  was  directed  at  what,  as  far  as  I  know,  is  a  com-  paratively  new  target  for  the  steamship  companies  and  the  airlines-children.  Or  at  least  it  was  aimed,  if  not  ex-  clusively  at  children,  at  parents  as  par-  ents,  instead  of  at  the  untrammelled  couples  usually  seen  skylarking  against  Old  World  backgrounds  in  travel  ad-  vertisements.  In  this  one,  there  were  a  couple  of  children  standing  in  some  sort  of  park  with  a  fine  old  castle  loom-  ing  above  it.  &quot;I  am  so  glad  Daddy  and  Mummy  took  us  with  them  this  time!&quot;  the  caption  ran.  &quot;It's  such  fun,  and  we  are  learning  such  a  lot,  too!&quot;  Well,  I  was  taken  on  a  number  of  trips  and  stayed  in  a  number  of  places  when  I  was  a  little  girl,  and  ever  since  I  saw  that  advertisement,  bits  of  those  jour-  neys  and  sojourns  have  kept  coming  back  to  me.  I  don't  know  exactly  how  much  Ilearned  on  my  trips,  but  I  was  glad  to  be  taken  along  on  them.  I  was  born  in  The  Hague,  and  my  first  journey  was  to  [REDACTED].  I  was  three  months  old,  and  don't  remember  a  thing  about  it.  My  second  trip,  also  to  [REDACTED],  took  place  three  years  later.  My  moth-  er's  best  friend,  who  had  married  a  Frenchman,  lived  there.  I  was  told  to  call  her  Texte  Tilly;  that's  all  I  re-  member  about  that  time.  On  my  third  visit  to  [REDACTED]-I  was  five-Je-  rome,  Tante  Tilly's  eld-  est  son,  and  I  were  cuffed  by  the  cook  for  eating  the  pointes  of  a  basketful  of  fresh  croissants.  A  couple  of  years  later,  I  went  to  [REDACTED],  with  my  family,  for  the  Christmas  season.  [REDACTED]  was  sheer  heav-  en,  being  full  of  marrons  glacés.  At  home,  I  was  allowed  no  more  than  a  nibble  of  that  luxury;  in  [REDACTED]  I  was  allowed  several  pieces  at  a  time.  In  [REDACTED],  also,  there  was  the  Palais  de  Glace,  an  indoor  skating  rink  where  le  five-o'  clock  was  served.  One  could  have  ices  brought  to  little  tables  in  the  midst  of  palms,  soft  lights,  and  music.  In  those  days,  I  loved  to  draw  and  paint,  and  in  the  Palais  de  Glace  I  filled  my  sketchbook  with  ladies  in  enormous  fur  toques  and  long,  tight  costumes  with  lace  jabots.  Wearing  bunches  of  violets  tucked  under  their  chins,  they  glided  over  the  ice  alongside  gentlemen  in  tweed  suits  and  chamois     gloves.  I  had  never  betore  seen  such  elegance,  and  to  skate  indoors  seemed  to  me  much  more  agreeable  than  to  skate  on  a  Dutch  canal  in  the  bitter  cold.  My  next  journey  was  to  [REDACTED].  I  went  one  summer,  with  my  father.  We  crossed  the  Channel  at  night  on  one  of  the  sturdy  old  Batavier  boats,  all  moist  mahogany  and  brass,  and  smelling  of  hot  oil  and  tar.  When  we  had  got  aboard,  I  sat  up  in  my  narrow  berth,  peering  through  the  porthole,  hearing  clinking  and  hissing  and  tooting  all  around  me.  As  the  boat  moved  away  from  the  wharf,  the  night  was  alive  with  trembling  green,  yellow,  and  red  lights.  Before  I  fell  asleep,  I  won-  dered  if  ships'  captains  were  ever  al-  lowed  to  take  their  wives  along.  I  was  certain  that  I  could  live  on  a  shipfor-  ever.  On  my  English  journey,  my  father  and  I  stayed  in  [REDACTED],  where  I  was  taken  to  seethe  sights.  An  elderly  lady  took  me.  Who  was  she?  One  of  the  family?  A  friend?  I  have  no  idea.  She  told  me  several  times  that  it  was  ex-  cellent  for  me  to  see  the  things  we  saw-the  Tower,  the  crown  jewels,  Westminster  Abbey,  the  Houses  of  Parliament.  But  I  did  not  stare  spell-  bound  at  these  wonders,  for  I  was  spellbound  staring  at  the  English  school-  boys,  with  their  blazers,  their  gray  trousers,  and  their  tiny  round  caps.  I  even  met  one  of  these  superior  beings,  at  his  parents'  house,  one  Sun-  day  in  the  country.  He  was  about  thirteen-a  handsome,  gentle  blond  boy,  dressed,  on  this  oc-  casion,  in  white  flan-  nels.  Blond?  His  hair  was  pure  gold  and  soft  as  silk.  §  After  a  long  lunch-  eon,  at  which  there  were  a  great  many  people,  and  two  footmen  serving,  this  boy  took  my  hand  and  told  me  he  would  show  me  around.  We  went  down  many  steps  to  a  cool,  cellarlike  room  with  a  stone  floor.  All  along  the  walls  were  shelves,  and  on  these  were  shallow  pans  filled  with  fresh  milk  set  out  to  cool.  The  boy  dipped  a  finger  in  one  of  them  and  licked  it.  &quot;Do  try  it,&quot;  he  said.  I  tried;  it  was  delicious.  We  went  on  dipping  and  licking  for  a  while.  In  one  wall  was  a  tiny  window-a  round  hole  cut  in  the  gray  stone.  I  looked  out  and  saw-and  I  see  it  now,  this  moment-a  miniature  landscape:  green  grass,  a  clump  of  trees,  cows  dozing  in  a  pool  of  shadow,  a  blue  sky,  and,  far  off  at  the  horizon,  a  yellow  rim  of  ripe  wheat.  I  went  to  [REDACTED]  a  couple  of  times  more,  and  those  times  are  mixed  up  in  my  memory.  I  do  remember  some  things.I  recall  my  surprise  that  Eng-  lish  villa  gardens  were  so  much  less  private  than  villa  gardens  in  [REDACTED]-  no  high  brick  walls  with  jagged  glass  on  top  of  them  to  keep  out  miscreants,  no  tall  gates  or  hedges.  I  remember  finding  a  small  spring  in  a  field,  and  lying  down  and  drinking  the  ice-cold  water;  this  was  at  a  picnic,  where-again  to  my  surprise-sheep  strolled  freely  around  the  picnickers.  And,  above  all,  Ire-  member  staying  up  late  one  summer  night,  sitting  on  a  terrace,  smelling  un-  known  flowers,  looking  at  immense  trees,  black  against  the  sky,  and  eating  cold  peaches.  I  had  never  eaten  fruit  late  at  night.  I  had  never  eaten  anything  late  at  night,  and  it  was  one  of  the  great  charms  of  that  holiday.  Probably  it  would  be  difficult  for  an  American  child  to  understand  how  I  felt,  but  I  had  never  had  the  freedom  of  the  kitchen,  or  snacks  when  I  wanted  them,  or  meals  alone  with  my  friends.  My  meals  at  home  were  on  the  dot,  and  supervised:  breakfast  at  eight,  with  brother  and  governess;  lunch  with  them  and  my  mother  at  twelve-thirty;  a  cup  of  tea  and  a  Petit-Beurre  after  school;  dinner  with  Mother,  Father,  governess,  and  brother  at  six.  In  [REDACTED],  the  food  was  as  varied  as  the  times  at  which  I  ate  it.  No  more  breakfasts  ofmilk,  bread,  egg,  and  apple,  but  toast,  fried  fish,  and  stewed  fruit.  Adieu  Petit,  Beurre!  Instead,  small,  moist  sand-  wiches  filled  with  watercress  or  cucum-  ber.  I  had  always  cared  more  for  salt  than  for  sweet,  and  after  tasting  shrimp  paste  and  anchovy  paste  I  knew  that  those  were  what,  in  future,  I  meant  to  live  on.  &quot;You  seem  to  have  thought  of  food  continuously,&quot;  my  mother  remarked  acidly  when  I  was  back  in  [REDACTED]  once  more,  and  this  was  almost  true,  but  not  quite.  There  was  one  other  thing  I  en-  joyed  tremendously  and  dwelt  on.  This,  I  remember,  was  on  my  final  childhood  visit  to  [REDACTED].  I  was  twelve,  and  my  father  took  me  to  various  art  museums.  I  had  always  enjoyed  going  to  them  in  [REDACTED],  but  this  was  something  new-  something  more  than  the  placid  enjoy-  ment  I  had  experienced  there.  This  was  a  revelation.  For  the  first  time,  I  saw  my  own  favorites-pictures  that  I  had  collected  copies  of  to  decorate  my  bed-  room,  and  that  were  to  me  the  most     beautiful  pictures  in  the  world.  They  were  the  portraits  of  faintly  smiling,  supremely  elegant  ladies  with  powdered  hair,  roses  at  their  bosoms,  and  lace  on  their  sleeves,  of  haughty  gentlemen  with  stars,  rosettes,  and  ribands  on  their  chests,  their  silken  legs  crossed,  one  hand  thrust  in  the  waistcoat,  the  other  fondling  the  smooth  head  of  a  dog.  They  were  picturesof  children  in  silk  and  satin  and  ruffles,  with  pale  sashes  around  their  waists,  and  buckled  shoes,  sitting  on  garden  seats  or  on  swings,  or  standing  beside  ponies.  How  lovely,  how  gay  they  all  were!  How,  I  won-  dered,  could  anyone  (my  father,  for  ex-  ample)  prefer  those  dreary  Dutch  por-  traits  of  dreary  Dutch  men  and  women,  always  dressed  in  black  with  white  col-  lars  and  cuffs?  HAT  visit  to  [REDACTED]  forms  the  last  but  one  of  my  memories  of  childhood  travel.  In  July  ofthe  follow-  ing  year,  which  was  1914,  Tante  Tilly  invited  me  to  visit  her  in  [REDACTED].  She  was  spending  the  summer  in  a  chalet  near  [REDACTED],  with  her  four  chil-  dren,  her  sister,  a  mademmoiselle,  and  a  cook.  Her  husband  was  to  join  them  later.  Now,  itis  impossible  for  a  child  from  the  lowlands  not  to  be  amazed  at  his  first  sight  of  [REDACTED]-the  slop-  ing  meadows  full  of  flowers,  the  chalets  and  farms,  the  garlanded  crucifixes  at  crossroads,  and,  above  them  all,  the  mountains,  snow-topped  even  in  mid-  summer.  Jerome  and  I  took  walks,  played  tennis  on  a  court  overlooking  a  deep  valley,  and  went  to  the  children's  balls  held  at  nearby  hotels,  and  after  two  weeks  I  was  bored  to  death.  Every  day  was  the  same.  Walks  that  went  up  and  up,  then  down  and  down.  I  didn't  really  care  about  tennis  or  about  dancing  the  polka.  I  wished  I  were  somewhere  else,  and  soon  enough  I  was.  On  the  second  of  August,  Switzer-  land  mobilized,  and  all  the  summer  vacationers  began  trying  to  get  back  to  their  own  countries,  Texte  Tilly  among  them.  We  packed,  and  even-  tually  all  of  us  departed  for  [REDACTED].  We  were  detained  there  awhile,  and  then  we  went  on  to  [REDACTED]-a  long,  un-  comfortable  journey,  which  I  remember  chiefly  because  we  seemed  to  spend  all  our  time  getting  in  and  out  of  trains,  or  waiting  for  them.  In  [REDACTED],  we  learned  that  Tente  Tilly's  husband  had  been  called  up,  and  that  no  one  had  any  idea  where  he  was.  The  next  few  weeks  were  frantic.  Daily,  the  news  from  the  front  became  worse;  the  German  Army  advanced             steadily.  The  French  government  left  for  Bordeaux.  Tante  Tilly  and  the  oth-  er  grownups  were  wild  with  anxiety  and  hardly  had  a  civil  word  for  the  children.  And  since  it  was  impossible  to  send  me  back  to  [REDACTED]  just  then,  I  was  taken  along  when  the  wholegroup  set  off  once  more,  this  time  for  a  tiny  village  about  sixty  miles  south  of  Poi-  tiers,  where  friends  of  Tante  Tilly's  owned  an  abbey,  to  which  they  had  al-  ready  fled.  They  had  urged  her  to  come  to  the  village,  and  had,  in  fact,  already  rented  a  house  for  her  there.  It  was  in  good  condition,  and  was  looked  after  by  a  caretaker  and  his  wife.  So,  after  another  tiresome  journey,  we  all  arrived  there  one  midnight  in  Sep-  tember.  I  woke  the  following  morning  in  a  large,  high  bed  with  a  green  curtain  around  it.  I  sat  up  and  looked  out  at  the  room.  Its  ceiling  was  very  low,  its  walls  were  whitewashed,  and  its  floor  and  massive  furniture  were  of  old,  dark,  shiny  wood.  There  was  one  window  but  no  view;  the  bronze  leaves  of  a  tree  hid  everything.  There  were  no  pictures  on  the  walls  or  ornaments  of  any  kind  except  a  crucifix  above  the  marble  mantelpiece  and,  on  a  table,  a  small  vase  filled  with  heliotrope.  I  thought  it  the  most  beautiful  room  I  had  ever  seen.  STAYED  almost  two  months  in  that  village,  and  all  the  time  I  was  in  a  state  of  complete  happiness,  of  ex-  alted  bliss.  I  did  not  know  why,  any  more  than  I  had  known  why  I  felt  so  dull,  so  bored,  in  the  country  of  Alps  and  conifers.  I  know  now,  though.  It  was  simply  that  I  happened  to  be  in  aplace  where  I  belonged-a  quiet  place  of  moss-covered  walls,  old  trees,  and  half-  open  gates  giving  glimpses  of  beauty.  I  loved  it  all:  the  house  itself,  with  its  white-shuttered  front  and  its  walled-in  garden  at  the  rear;  the  rooms,  almost  bare  but  made  beautiful  by  the  few  pieces  of  darkly  glowing  furniture  they  did  contain;  the  narrow  halls;  the  kitch-  en,  with  its  stone  floor,  and  its  oaken  beams  on  which  cheeses  lay  ripening  on  fresh  grape  leaves;  the  smells  of  bees-  wax,  apples,  and  drying  herbs.  And  the  garden-the  lovely,  sombre,  mute  garden  where  no  flowers  grew,  only  weeds  and  bushes  and  trees,  where  the  paths  were  moss-covered  and  hedged  in  by  boxwood  and  stone  statues  strangled  with  ivy  and  periwinkle,  and  where,  on  the  dark  earth,  a  few  yellow  leaves  lay  lightly.  Almost  daily,  we  children  and  Tante  Tilly  visited  the  abbey.  A  great  many  people-chiefly  women  and  children,  all  of  them  related-Ilived  there.  The  women  knitted  and  chattered,  wrote  letters  to  their  men  at  the  front,  or  picked  grapes  in  the  nearby  vineyard.  T  wo  of  the  youngest  ones,  hardly  more  than  girls,  were  always  laughing,  cry-  ing,  and  darting  hither  and  yon,  or  sud-  denly  plopping  down  on  a  bench,  un-  buttoning  their  blouses,  and  suckling  their  small  babies.  They  were  the  only  ones  who  took  notice  of  us  children.  They  would  go  with  us  for  walks  in  the  village,  or  would  help  us  arrange  flow-  ers  at  the  altar  of  the  church.  It  did  not  matter  to  us  that  the  older  women  bothered  with  us  very  little.  There  was  enough  to  occupy  us.  Plenty  of  books-old  ones,  beautifully  illus-  trated  with  pictures  of  ships,  butterflies,  or  theatrical  costumes;  copies  of  L'Z/-  lustration  of  many  years  before;  novels  in  English  and  German  as  well  as  in  French.  If  we  did  not  want  to  read,  we  could  play  the  piano,  dress  up  and  have  games  of  charades,  or  just  explore  the  abbey.  European  children  are  not  likely  to  be  impressed  by  the  oldness  of  their  surroundings,  and  the  oldness  of  the  abbey  did  not  impress  us.  We  knew  that  the  original  parts  of  it  had  been  built  somewhere  around  1100,  and  that  the  neighboring  bridge  was  older  still,  but  this  meant  little  to  us.  What  we  liked  was  the  new  part,  added  in  the  seven-  _|  teen-hundreds,  in  which  were  our  two  favorite  rooms-the  gun  room  and  the  ;  petit  boudoir.  The  gun  room  was  just  a  room  that  had  its  walls  hung  with  guns,  pistols,  and  bows  and  arrows,  and  had  lined-up  suits  of  armor  standing  |  below  these,  and  a  stuffed  bear  at  each  :|  side  of  its  door.  The  boudoir  had  more  charm.  It  was  a  small,  circular  room,  all  gold  and  crimson.  Here,  in  glass-  topped  tables  such  as  one  sees  in  mu-  seums,  lay  countless  trifles  under  lock  and  key:  miniatures  framed  in  pearls;  enamelled  snuffboxes  with  initials  in  diamonds  on  their  covers;  trinkets  of  ivory,  tortoise  shell,  or  wood;  and  mementos-medals,  ribbons,  flowers  or  hair  in  lockets,  a  fan,  a  discolored  glove.  At  other  times,  having  had  enough  of  the  things  of  long  ago,  we  would  go  out  and  roam  the  countryside.  We  must  have  made  an  untidy-looking  group  of  children.  At  the  head  would  be  fifteen-  year-old  Nino,  followed  by  his  two  younger  sisters.  Behind  them  would  '  come  their  cousin,  Marie-Charles,  aged  fourteen,  always  by  himself  and  smok-  ing  a  cigarette-wn  type  chic,  we  all  thought  him.  True,  his  black  school-  boy  apron  diminished  his  chic  somewhat,      but  the  tone  of  his  languid  &quot;Quo:?&quot;  when  one  asked  him  a  question  made  up  for  it.  Jerome  and  I  and  four  or  five  others  would  come  next,  and  far  be-  hind,  straggling  and  always  giving  up  in  the  end,  would  come  Alice,  who  was  four,  carrying  her  kitten.  The  kitten's  name  was  Clemenceau.  We  would  look  for  berries,  and  pick  cépes,  and  finally  rest  under  a  walnut  tree,  on  one  ofthe  white  stone  walls  that  divided  the  pastures.  We  would  shake  the  branches  of  the  tree  till  the  nuts  pelted  down,  and  we  would  crack  them,  and  peel  off  their  tough  outer  shells  and  their  wet,  dark  inner  skins,  and  eatthe  fresh  white  nuts.  We  would  sit  there  a  long  while,  swinging  our  legs,  talking,  singing  songs.  Finally,  a  mist  would  rise  and  the  sky  would  become  paler.  The  church  bell  would  start  to  ring,  and  it  would  be  time  to  go  home.  We  had  our  evening  meal-a  simple  one  of  thick  soup,  bread,  and  wine  and  water-at  a  round  table  on  which  stood  an  oil  lamp  that  left  the  room  in  deep  shadow  but  lighted  our  faces  softly.  We  went  to  bed  early,  and,  once  in  my  own  room,  I  would  work  on  some  sketch  I  had  made  that  day,or  read  in  a  book  I  had  found  downstairs,  and  the  silence  around  me  would  be  so  great  that  it  would  have  a  sound  of  its  own,  like  the  swishing  of  waves  far  off.  O  the  days  passed.  Not  too  long  a  dis-  tance  away,  the  war  was  going  on.  Twice  a  day,  the  tembour  de  ville  would  read  aloud  the  latest  dispatches  in  the  village  square,  and  in  the  evening  the  local  gazette  arrived,  but  none  of  the  really  important  news  interested  us  chil-  dren.  What  we  liked  was  the  news  that  Jerome  picked  up  from  the  stationmas-  ter,  who  would  always  be  full  of  it  after  a  train  of  wounded  had  returned  from  the  front.  Shivering  with  horror,  we  would  listen  to  stories  about  the  Ger-  mans  eating  babies  or  pulling  the  hairs  out  of  women's  heads  one  by  one.  When  our  elders  overheard  these  conversations  and  told  us  not  to  believe  such  things,  we  were  quite  indignant.  We  wanted  them  to  be  true.  And  then,  one  day,  coming  home  at  lunchtime,  we  found  Texte  Tilly  and  the  other  grownups  packing  frantically.  We  were  leaving  for  [REDACTED]  that  evening,  they  told  us.  [REDACTED]  was  safe  once  more,  and  the  sooner  we  got  back  there  the  better.  There  would  be  room  for  civil-  ians  on  a  certain  train,  and  it  was  a  chance  not  to  miss.  Was  I  sorry  to  leave?  Did  I  cry  by  myself  in  my  beautiful  room?  I  have  no  idea.  But  I  do  know  that  when,  the  next   ay,  I  was  back  in  [REDACTED],  I  found  myself  longing  to  return  to  [REDACTED]  and  my  family.  And  Texte  Tilly,  who  had  re-  ceived  one  or  two  anxious  letters  from  my  mother,  went  to  the  Dutch  Em-  bassy,  and  three  days  later  they  in-  formed  her  that  they  had  found  a  re-  liable  person,  himself  returning  to  [REDACTED],  who  would  be  willing  to  take  careof  me.  The  reliable  person  turned  out  to  be  the  kindest,  most  charming  man  imag-  inable-the  minister  of  the  Eglise  Wal-  lonne,  in  [REDACTED].  On  the  day  we  were  to  leave,  I  found  him  waiting  for  me  at  the  station,  wrapped  up  in  a  black  cape  and  several  shawls,  his  arms  full  of  magazines,  bonbons,  and  flowers  for  me.
