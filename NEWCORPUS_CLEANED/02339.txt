 b"WHEN  I  was  a  child  I  imagined  houses,  one  after  the  other,  in  which  I  lived  and  had  lived  in  other  ages.  They  were  mine,  and  yet  I  did  not  own  them.  I  did  not  have  to  believe  anything  about  them.  They  disappeared,  and  returned  if  I  remembered  them,  which  seemed  as  natural  as  the  going  and  coming  of  day.  But  halfway  through  my  twenties  I  found  that,  without  having  altered  the  precarious  economy  that  had  been  mine  since  college,  I  had  become  the  owner  of  a  house,  in  disrepair,  but  built  ofstone.  In  [REDACTED].  On  a  high  ridge,  looking  out  over  the  valley  of  a  river.  With  some  land  and  trees  and  barns.  The  surprise  of  having  it  never  altogether  left  me,  whatever  I  learned  about  it  and  remembered  of  how  I  had  come  to  it.  In  ancestry  of  that  acquisition,  I  think  first,  always,  of  Margie,  my  mother's  cousin,  one  of  the  few  mem-  bers  of  my  mother's  family  whom  I  ever  met.  Margie,  a  generation  older  than  my  mother,  had  taught  school  for  most  of  her  life.  She  visited  us  at  inter-  vals  and  spent  part  of  one  winter  with  us,  when  I  was  ten.  She  was  tall,  straight,  handsome-my  mother  said  she  was  statuesque.  A  woman  of  mea-  sured  words  and  imposing  character. While  she  was  with  us  she  supervised  my  sister  and  me  at  our  homework,  with  special  attention  given  to  my  un-  reliable  arithmetic.  She  left  long  be-  fore  spring,  for  [REDACTED],  where  she  spent  her  last  years,  and  I  never  saw  her  again.  The  year  after  the  end  of  the  Second  World  War  she  died,  and  to  my  mother's  astonishment  no  less  than  mine  her  will  named  me  as  her  sole  heir.  To  a  few  silver  spoons,  one  trunk  full  of  clothes,  and-after  the  expenses  had  all  been  paid-some-  thing  over  twelve  hundred  dollars.  I  was  nineteen.  My  mother  put  the  money  into  savings  bonds.  She  said  I  did  not  need  it  at  that  point,  and  she  was  right.  Then  there  was  a  friend  in  college,  a  French  boy  whose  father,  a  journal-  ist,  had  been  killed  in  [REDACTED],  in  the  Resistance,  toward  the  end  of  the  war.  His  companion  on  his  final  mission,  an  American,  undertook  to  keep  an  eye  on  the  man's  widow  and  two  children,  which  was  how  my  friend  happened  to  be  at  an  American  university.  The  year  I  graduated,  his  American  men-  tor  needed  a  tutor  for  his  own  nephew,  who  had  missed  a  year  in  school.  Through  my  friend's  recommendation  I  got  the  job,  and  the  following  sum-  mer  it  again  took  me  to  [REDACTED],  and  led  to  other  posts  of  the  same  kind, which  paid  little  but  occupied  only  the  mornings.  Having  got  to  Europe,  I  contrived  to  stay  there,  tutoring,  translating,  writing  for  the  English  radio,  earning  a  scant  living  and  saving  nothing,  but  managing  it  on  my  own  time.  And  after  exactly  five  years  abroad  I  was  in  southwest  [REDACTED]  one  July,  with  acar  so  old  that  bets  were  made  about  it.  That  part  of  the  country  was  still  referred  to  by  its  inhabitants  (and  sel-  dom,  yet,  by  the  Tourist  Bureau)  as  the  Quercy,  after  a  tribe  of  Gauls  whom  the  Romans  called  the  Cadurci.  It  is  a  region  of  rivers  that  flow,  for  long  curving  stretches,  beneath  pale  cliffs  dropping  from  upland  plateaus.  Small,  rapid  streams,  like  the  forested  Corr\xc3\xa9ze  and  Ve\xc3\xa9zere  to  the  north,  and  then  broader  currents,  great  shining  emanations  of  the  whole  terrain-of  the  caverns  underground  as  well  asof  the  hills  and  pastures  and  woods  under  the  sky.  These  rivers-the  Dordogne,  the  Lot,  and  the  Tarn-flow  westward  from  the  extinct  volcanic  peaks  of  the  Auvergne  to  join  the  Garonne  and  the  Gironde  near  the  Bordeaux  vineyards,  and  then  empty  into  the  Atlantic.  I  was  staying  in  a  village  on  the  south  shore  of  the  Dordogne,  a  river  that  flows  from  a  peak  in  the  Monts  Dore,  the  Golden  Mountains.  The  pension-hotel  had  been  a  castle  and  an  abbey.  The  walls  enveloped  an  ancient  church  with  a  famous  tympanum  carved  from  local  marble.  F\xc3\xa9nelon  had  lived  in  the  building  and  is  supposed  to  have  written  about  the  island  in  the river  just  below.  It  was  a  rainy  summer,  and  the  lush  woods  near  the  water  hung over  the  road,  green,  dark,  full  of  green  light  and  bow-  ing  green  dusk,  with  open-  ings  framing  sights  of  slop-  ing  bright  pastures  shining  from  inside  themselves,  red-  brown  cows  with  long  horns  grazing  belly-deep  in  grass  and  wild  flowers.  Walls  of  ruined  stone  buildings  loomed  through  the  foliage:  remains  of  shepherds'  huts,  vineyard  cabanmes,  pigeon  towers,  farmhouses,  barns.  The  nearest  large  city,  Li-  moges,  was  seventy  miles  to  the  north  over  steep,  nar-  row  mountain  roads.  In  " b'the  region  itself  only  the  few  main  arteries  were  paved.  Even  along  the  river  a  dirt,  and-gravel  surface  wound  through  water  meadows  and  woods,  with  holes  and  wash-  outs  insteep  places.  The  hotel  put  up  picnics  so  that  the  days  were  free  for  exploring.  The  oldcar  trundled  me  through  the  valley  and  em-  bodied  my  own  strangeness  in  that  time  and  country.  There  were  few  other  cars,  and  most  of  them  were  as  old  as  mine,  veterans  from  the  thirties  that  had  spent  the  war  jacked  up  on  blocks  in  sheep  barns  and  garages  in  market  towns,  next  to  pha-  etons  and  carts.  When  I  stopped  by  fields,  under  trees,  there  was  no  sound  of  motors  anywhere  but  only  the  whis-  per  of  the  river;  rustling  of  leaves  and  grass;  birdsong;  the  sounds  of  dogs  and  cows  in  the  distance.  In  the  after-  noons  flocks  of  sheep  and  family-sized  herds  of  cows-  fours,  fives,  sixes-took  the  road  in  their  good  time,  browsing  the  rich  foliage  over  the  ditches,  moved  along  by  children  or  by  old  men  or  women  with  long  sticks.  When  the  rain  stopped  for  over  a  day  there  was  haying  in  the  meadows:  cows  with  long  crocheted  face  masks  to  protect  them  from  flies  waited,  yoked  in  pairs  to  two-wheeled  blue  hay  wagons  with  slanting  racks  fore  and  aft,  for  the  load  to  be  built  behind  them,  and  they  started  home  as  the  shadows  stretched  out  across  the  newly  shorn  stubble.  They  had  the  right-of-way  of  the  winding  roads,  which  they  blocked  completely.  On  the  hills  the  cows  leaned  into  each  other,  shoulder  to  shoulder.  Bunches  of  hay  hung  in  the  trees  that  bent  over  the  road.  Later,  when  the  cows  were  in  their  barns  being  milked,  young  owls  appeared  on  the  shadowy  road  and  stood  staring  along  it,  as  the  nightin-  gales  began,  late  in  their  season.  The  smell  of  barns  drifted  even  through  the  market  towns  that  were  themselves  not  much  larger  than  vil-  lages,  and  in  the  evenings  cows  swayed  through  the  streets  guided  by  peasants  with  the  same  long  sticks.  Pigs  grunted  behind  arched  cellar  doors,  and  were  butchered  in  back  al-  leys,  with  groups  of  experts  standing                     around,  and  the  cobbles  running  blood.  The  farm  dogs  appeared  to  bea  random  mixture,  but  many  of  them  had  one  pale  and  one  dark  eye.  They  knew  their  jobs.  They  ate  soup.  The  language  on  the  farms  was  a  patois  descended  from  a  Languedoc  tongue  older  than  the  French  of  Tours  and  [REDACTED].  But  the  word  &quot;peasant,&quot;  with  its  heritage  of  poverty  and  of  scorn  from  other  classes  that  still  sap  and  abuse  the  peasantry,  had  been  replaced,  in  courteous  use,  by  the  phrase  &quot;coun-  try  people.&quot;  They  dominated  the  fairs  as  well  as  the  farms.  Even  in  town  they  sat  at  table  with  their  berets  on,  and  the  men  held  the  huge  wheels  of  bread  to  their  chests  and  cut  them  with  the  clasp  knives  from  their  pockets,  pulling  the  blades  toward  them.  Bread  swelled  in  the  soup,  and  when  they  had  finished  they  sluiced  out  the  bowls  with  wine  and  drank  it  off.  They  were  proud  of  what  they  called  their  polyculture:  they  grew  everything,  from  the  corn  and  wheat,  and  giant  forage  rutabagas  for  their  animals,  to  their  own  black  wine  and  their  firewood.  Their  cash  crops  were  milk  and  walnuts,  wool  and  lambs,  plums,  tobacco.  The  year  turned  through their  harvests.  It  was  common  for  families  to  have  land  scattered  here  and  there-plowland  and  pasture  and  woods-and  several  houses,  one  of  which  might  be  used  for  drying  to-  bacco  and  storing  corn  and  potatoes  and  old  furniture.  They  had  survived  the  German  Occupation  without  ser-  vility  or  shame.  In  the  Middle  Ages  the  quality  that  was  said  to  distinguish  the  Quercynois  was  dowucewr-some-  thing  more  than  gentleness  and  closer  to  its  literal  rendering:  sweetness. It  seemed  far  from  credible  that  I  could  buy-could  own-a  house  in  the  French  country-  side,  even  though  the  prices  then  were  low.  They  were  verylow. But  by  the  time  I  had  seen  them  I  had  begun  to  play  at  looking  for  a  house,  and  I  went  on  playing  at  it-somebody  al-  ways  knew  of  another  one. The  architecture  had  changed  little,  it  was  said,  between  the  time  of  the  Black  Prince  and  the  First  World  War,  and  almost  nothing  had  been  built  since  then.  Pretending  I  wanted  a  house  there  allowed  me  to  imagine  living  there,  rather  than  being  a  tour-  ist,  and  I  had  no  trouble  persuading  myself  that  it  was  a  relatively  harmless  fantasy.  Thoreau  said  he  hadhad  the  refusalof  several  farms,  which  was  all  he  wanted  of  them-and  perhaps  it  was,  finally.  The  search,  and  my  own  more  gen-  eral  curiosity,  led  up  out  of  the  valley  onto  the  plateau  above  it,  the  causse.  The  valley  life  had  risen  from  the  deep  soil  of  the  river  plain  and  had  some  of  its  softness.  The  soil  of  the  ceusse,  in  most  places,  was  a  thin  layer  of  red  clay,  hard,  and  full  of  chunks  of  limestone,  overa  bed  of  pure  limestone  that  stretched  south  some  thirty  miles  to  the  valleyof  the  Lot,  and  was  the  roof  of  a  network  of  caverns,  underground  rivers,  and  tunnels,  as  intricate  as  the  inside  of  a  loaf  of  bread.  The  stone  was  everywhere,  and  everything  was  built  of  it.  Freshly  turned  up  by  the  plow,  it  was  stained  by  the  rusty  shades  of  the  soil.  The  rain  washed  it  pale  gray  or  yellowish  white.  Broken,                 it  had  thebluish  stare  of  cracked  bones  in  a  butcher  shop.  It  weathered  to  darker  grays  like  long-dead  branches,  or  to  a  lighter  golden  gray,  and  li-  chen,  moss,  ivy  grew  over  it.  Walls,  and  the  ruins  of  walls,  knee-high,  waist-high,  shoulder-high,  ran  every-  where,  embraced  the  smallest  field  and  pasture,  pig  run  and  patch  of  garden,  skirted  huge  walnut  trees,  vanished  into  oak  woods.  There  were  so  many  stones  that  what  appeared  to  be  buildings  in  some  of  the  fields  turned  out  to  be  structures  of  solid  dry  masonry,  reared  in  order  to  get  the  stones  out  of  the  plowland.  Some  of  the  barnyards  were  slabs  of  solid  rock,  sloping  to  pools  with  a  little  stagnant  water  in  them  where  ducks  waded.  The  heat  shimmered  like  the  leaves,  and  the  middle  of  the  day  resounded  with  cicadas  and  the  tonkle  of  distant  sheep  bells,  and  smelled  of  box  and  hay.
