I  took  him  outside  the  building  into  the  stockade  and  hit  him  fifteen  lashes  with  a  strap  made  for  that  purpose,  at  the  end  of  which  he  said  that  he  would  go  to  his  work  and  would  not  make  any  more  trou-  ble  while  he  stayed  in  the  camp.  I  then  proceeded  to  take  outtwelve  more  of  the  ringleaders  and  hit  them  from  eight  to  fifteenlashes,  stopping  when  they  agreed  to  go  to  work  and  make  no  more  trouble.  .  .. The  world  that  lay  behind  the  house,  from  which  it  was  a  refuge  and  to  which  it  was  an  ornament,  became  visible;  and  the  house  itself  appeared  for  the  first  time  in  rela-  tion  to  the  events  of  political  and  finan-  cial  struggle,  lasting  from  the  end  of  the  Civil  War  through  the  beginning  of  the  century,  of  which  I  had  read  at  school.  But  by  that  time,  the  house  had  ceased  to  exist,  and  all  through  my  childhood  when  I  lived  there,  the  nineteenth-cen-  tury  world  of  which  it  was  a  part  was  no  more  alive  to  me  than  the  official  oil-  and-canvas  figure  of  my  great-grand-  father  in  the  full-length  portrait,  or  the  photograph  of  my  grandfather  in  the  small  oval  frame  on  the  living-room  wall.
