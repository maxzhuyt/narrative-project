THE  ROSE  AND  THE  WEED  N  a  country  garden  a  lovely  rose  looked  down  upon  a  common  weed  and  said,  &quot;You  are  an  unwelcome  guest,  economically  useless  and  un-  sightly  of  appearance.  The  Devil  must  love  weeds,  he  made  so  many  of  them.&quot;  The  unwelcome  guest  looked  up  at  the  rose  and  said,  &quot;Lilies  that  fester  smell  far  worse  than  weeds,  and,  one  supposes,  that  goes  for  roses.&quot;  &quot;My  name  is  Dorothy  Perkins,&quot;  the  rose  said  haughtily.  &quot;What  are  you-a  becetleweed,  a  bladderweed,  a  beggar-  weed?  'The  names  of  weeds  are  ugly.&quot;  And  Dorothy  shuddered  slightly,  but  lost  none  of  her  pretty  petals.  &quot;We  have  some  names  prettier  than  Perkins,  or,  for  my  taste,  Dorothy,  among  them  silverweed,  and  jewel-  weed,  and  candyweed.&quot;  The  weed  straightened  a  bit  and  held  his  ground.  &quot;Anywhere  you  can  grow  I  can  grow  better,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;I  think  you  must  bea  burglar-  weed,&quot;  said  the  disdainful  Miss  Perkins,  &quot;for  you  get  in  where  you  aren't  want-  ed,  and  take  what  isn't  yours-the  rain  and  the  sunlight  and  the  good  earth.&quot;  The  weed  smiled  a  weedy  smile.  &quot;At        least,&quot;  he  said,  &quot;I  do  not  come  from  a  family  of  climbers.&quot;  The  rose  drew  herself  up  to  her  full  height.  &quot;Pd  have  you  know  that  roses  are  the  emblem  of  old  England,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;We  are  the  flower  of  song  and  story.&quot;  &quot;And  of  war,&quot;  the  weed  replied.  &quot;The  summer  winds  take  you  by  storm,  not  you  the  winds  with  beauty.  \xc2\xabI've  seen  it  happen  many  times,  to  roses  of  yesteryear,  long  gone  and  long  for-  gotten.&quot;  &quot;We  are  mentioned  in  Shakespeare,&quot;  said  the  rose,  &quot;many  times  in  many  plays.  The  lines  aretoo  sweet  for  your  ears,  but  I  will  tell  you  some.&quot;  Just  then,  and  before  Miss  Perkins  could  recite,  a  wind  came  out  of  the  west,  riding  low  to  the  ground  and  swift,  like  the  cavalry  of  March,and  Dorothy  Perkins'  beautiful  disdain  sud-  denly  became  a  scattering  of  petals,  economically  useless  and  of  appearance  not  especially  sightly.  The  weed  stood  firm,  his  head  to  the  wind,  armored,  or  so  he  thought,  in  security  and  strength,  but  as  he  was  brushing  a  few  rose  petals  and  aphids  from  his  lapels,  the  hand  of  the  gardener  flashed  out  of  the  air  and  pulled  him  out  of  the  ground  by  the  roots  before  you  could  say  Dorothy  Perkins,  or,  for  that  matter,  jewelweed.
