People  who  knew  Millie  casually  inflated  their  acquaintance  in  order  to  make  themselves  a  part  of  the  tragedy  and  to  qualify  fordetails  Of  course,  nobody  knew  her  well;  her  husband  had  come  to  the  college  just  the  past  fall,  to  fill  in  a  sabbatical  leave  in  the  Economics  Department,  and  he  and  Millie  and  the  three  children  had  there-  fore  been  in  Spokane  less  than  a  year.  But  the  women  recalled  how  pleasantly  Millie  had  spoken  at  the  laundry  or  in  the  park,  or  when  she  was  out  collect-  ing  for  the  Chest,  with  the  little  girls  stringing  along  behind.  They  gathered  in  shoals  to  face  together  the  bleak  reality:  alive  and  healthy  one  day,  dead  the  next.  At  the  Tuesday  meeting  of  the  League  of  Women  Voters,  Mrs.  Horton  was  selected  to  make  the  semi-  official  condolence  call.  on  behalf  of  the  organization.  The  process  was  less  parliamentary  than  usual.  &quot;You  go,  Angela,&quot;  Mrs.  Titus,  the  president,  said  to  Mrs.  Horton,  and  the  mem-  bers  murmured  &quot;Yes,&quot;  as  they  did  to  motions  that  required  more  fervor  than  thought-like  votes  of  thanks  to  outgoing  officers.  Even  if  the  members  had  stopped  to  be  analytical,  Mrs.  Horton  would  still  have  been  the  logical  choice.  To  begin  with,  she  heard  things  straight.  (She  was  the  re-  corder  for  the  League  and  secretary  of  the  Planning  Commission.) In  addition  to  these  considerations,  Mrs.  Horton  was  large-boned  and  Scandinavian,  and  had  a  look  of  guile-  less  good  will  that  made  her  instantly  trusted.  And  she  was  thirty-five-still  young,  but  ten  years  older  than  Millie,  and  presumably  more  experienced  in  death.  Besides,  she  knew  Millie  better  than  the  other  women  did;  the  two  of  them  had  worked  together  on  the  League's  mailing  list,  and  once,  when  Millie  was  on  the  hospitality  commit-  tee,  Mrs.  Horton  had  shown  her  how  to  operate  the  forty-cup  coffee-maker  belonging  to  the  Democrats.  &quot;We're  counting  on  you,  Angela,&quot;  Mrs.  Titus  said,  a  little  jealously.
