It  is,  I  suppose,  rather  difficult  to  find  life  dull  when  one  is  the  only  boy  be-  tween  two  and  eighteen  on  an  eighty-  acre  estate.  The  Place  was  one  of  many  on  a  mountain  in  northern  New  Jersey.  Here,  between  1916  and  1921,  my  fa-  ther  was  kennel  manager  for  more  than  a  hundred  dogs,  and  my  parents  and  sister  and  Ilived  in  a  cottage  on  the  property.  Our  cottage  was  somewhat  isolated,  and  not  quite  in  the  center  of  those  eighty  acres,  but  it  was  so  situated  that  a  few  steps  took  me  into  fields,  or  orchards,  or  woods,  or  down  to  the  pond.  The  path  from  the  front  door  led  to  a  world  of  buildings,  and  I  moved  through  all  of  it,  freely  and  alone.  Close  to  the  cottage,  beyond  a  small  front  yard  and  to  the  left  of  the  path,  were  two  low  huts,  which  were  used  as  summer  kennels.  Just  beyond  them  was  the  Top  Kennel,  an  old  washhouse  for  carriages,  with  a  long  bay-win-  dowed  wing  added  to  it  for  kennels.  TheTop  Kennel  had  a  room  that  was  used  for  cooking  and  fixing  the  food  for  the  dogs,  and  two  or  three  steps  down  from  that  was  the  old  washroom,  a  cement-floored  area  with  box  kennels  around  the  sides.  It  was  also  the  place  where  bales  of  aromatic  shavings,  and  cedar  bedding,  and  the  show  crates  were  stored.  Upstairs  there  was  a  long  loft,  with  more  kennels,  which  were  seldom  used.  If  one  turned  left  here  and  went  down  the  hill  through  a  meadow,  one  came  to  the  Bottom  Kennel  and  the  pigpens.  If  one  did  not  turn  left  but  went  along  the  main  roadway  through  the  farmyard,  there  was,  on  the  left,  a  steep  bank  covered  with  sumac,  through  which  one  could  prowl.  On  the  right,  there  was  a  corncrib  and  then  a  long  wagon  shed,  full  of  mowing  machines,  wagons,  hayrakes,  hay  rigs,  dump  carts,  corn  planters,  seed  drills,  and  the  large,  smelly  tank  that  was  used  to  spray  the  fruit  trees. At  the  end  of  the  wagon  shed  was  the  narrow,  enclosed  carpen-  ter  shop,  where  Old  Alec  made  screens  and  did  cabinetwork  and  fixed  chairs,  and  took  care  of  all  the  other  minor  woodworking  jobs  on  The  Place.  In-  side  the  shop,  there  was  a  stairway  that  led  to  the  loft  of  the  wagon  shed,  where  all  the  impedimenta  of  the  garden-all  the  stakes,  pots,  fertilizer,  burlap,  and  assorted  tools-were  stored. The  icehouse  itself  was  nearly  always  unlocked,  but  the  damp,  smelly  sawdust  that  covered  the  large  blocks  of  ice  made  it  a  messy  and  unpleasant  place,  and  it  offered  very  little  in  comparison  to  the  pleasant  com-  plexities  of  all  that  surrounded  it. Or  else  one  could  go  up  the  driveway  from  the  Brick  Cottage,  and  on  up  a  steep,  winding  road  with  a  thick  belt  of  pines  and  clumps  of  wisteria  on  one  side,  and  on  the  other  the  stone  walls  and  thickets  of  honeysuckle  that  formed  one  side  of  the  gardens.  Up  at  the  Big  House,  there  were  two  or  three  temporary  summer  kennels  in  the  wooded  area  at  the  rear,  a  fountain  across  the  driveway  from  the  front  door,  a  long  rectangular  lily  pool  in  a  trellised,  vine-covered  enclosure  on  the  garden  side,  and,  down  a  path,  a  shed  where  the  large  power  mower  was  kept.  Through  all  of  this  I  roamed  at  will,  governed  only  by  discretion.  One  did  not  wander  around  the  vicinity  of  the  Big  House  while  the  family  was  there,  but  the  family  had  a  winter  house  in  Manbattan  and  a  summer  house  at  Quogue,  so  this  restriction  was  no  hard-  ship. Those  who  lived  on  the  mountain  were,  by  the  nature  of  their  world,  set  apart  from  those  who  lived  in  town.  At  least  once  a  week,  we  went  to  the  movies  in  town,  if  not  to  those  in  the  larger  town  seven  miles  away.  All  the  young  boys  sat  in  the  first  two  rows,  close  to  the  pianist  or  the  drum-  mer,  whichever  was  working. Chester's  wife,  Hannah,  was  a  dark,  pleasant,  quiet  woman,  a  woman  who  was  almost  invariably  re-  ferred  toas  &quot;sweet.&quot;  She  wore  per-  fume,  and  to  me  she  was  strangely  refined  and  very  feminine.  'The  apart-  ment  upstairs  always  struck  me  as  being  what  could  only  be  called  dainty.  It  had  about  it  a  cloistered  air,  and  to  this  day  I  associate  French  clocks  and  per-  fume  with  a  peculiar  distillation  of  femi-  ninity  and  daintiness,  forI  found  the  strange  mechanism  and  the  exotic  ap-  pearance  ofa  French  clock  to  be  the  intriguing  detail  of  Hannah's  rooms.  The  clock  was  of  glistening  gold  and  glass,  and  it  spun  airily,  and  was  small  and  very  silent.  DO  not  know  what  perfume  it  was  that  Hannah  used,  nor  do  I  know  what  sachet  it  was  that  identified  the  Big  House,  but  there  are  other  smells  that  come  through  most  clearly,  and  that  invoke  the  strongest  images.  Once  in  a  while,  gasoline  will  smell  as  it  used  to  when  a  tank  truck  would  pull  into  the  yard  of  the  garage  and  a  man  would  lug  five-gallon  cans  through  the  privet  hedge  and  dump  them  into  a  large  fun-  nel  that  he  had  set  in  the  opened  fill  pipe  of  the  underground  tank.  Sometimes  on  a  hot  day,  I  will  catch  the  scent  of  pines  as  the  pines  smelled  in  the  dense  belt  of  tepeed  limbs  that  ran  along  the  drive-  way.  Sometimes  when  a  large  lawn  has  been  newly  cut,  I  will  catch  again  the                    spacious  order  of  the  lawns  around  the  Big  House,  the  whole  two  or  three  acres  of  them  hedged,  bordered,  meticulously  edged,  with  an  occasional  rabbit  play-  ing  on  them  in  the  summer  evenings.  Even  now,  on  a  cold  day,  an  occasional  cigarette  will  smell  as  the  smoke  smelled  from  Jimmy's  cigarettes  when  he  took  my  sister  and  me  skating.  Always  there  were  lights,  and  smells,  and  silences  as  well  as  sounds.  There  were  few  cars,  and  we  were  well  away  from  the  nearest  road.  I  could  climb  an  ash  tree  that  stood  in  front  of  the  Top  Kennel,  and  only  a  catbird  and  the  chickens  would  disturb  the  si-  lence.  On  the  corner  of  the  chicken  runs  nearest  our  house  was  a  young  wild  " b"cherry  that  had  a  three-branched  crotch  at  its  top.  I  would  spend  as  much  time  as  an  eight-year-old  ever  spends  in  one  place,  sitting  peacefully  in  that  crotch,  and  from  the  height  of  fifteen  feet  or  so  I  would  maintain  a  foremast  look-  out  down  over  the  orchard,  or  pilot  an  airplane  aimlessly  through  the  sky.  Of  all  the  remembered  sounds,  there        are  only  two  that  have  survived.  In  summer,  after  I'd  been  sent  to  bed,  I  would  lie  in  the  late  dusk,  begrudging  the  loss  of  the  fading  day  and  hearing,  from  down  by  the  pond,  the  croaking  of  a  great  bullfrog  and  the  sound  of  some  creature-bird  or  insect  or  tree  toad,  I  have  never  known  which.  Now,  at  times  in  the  late  summer,  I  hear  the  49  same  creature,  and  I  begrudge  not  the  fading  day  but  the  fading  year.  SUPPOSE  that  there  were  minor  crises,  such  as  bruised  knees  and  skinned  elbows.  At  one  stage,  I  man-  aged  to  hammer  my  fingernails  so  often  that  I  lost  some  of  them  twice  over.  There  were  disappointments,  but  they  seldom  lasted,  for  there  was  always  The  Place  to  do  away  with  any  disappoint-  ment.  At  times  there  were  responsibil-  ities,  mostly  due  to  the  war.  One  is  either  born  a  livestock  man  or  one  is  not,  and  I  had  been  born  a  livestock  man,  which  means  not  only  that  one  likes  livestock  but  that  one  places  the  interest  of  the  stock  above  one's  own  interests.  In  1918,  Jimmy  was  away  and  it  was  im-  possible  to  get  help  to  replace  him,  for  the  only  people  who  could  be  hired  were  boys  just  out  of  school,  and  they  mani-  festly  preferred  sitting  on  the  strip  of  grass  across  from  the  Top  Kennel  to  the  job  of  cleaning  kennels.  Therefore,  at  the  age  of  nine  I  cleaned  kennels,  mak-  ing  the  rounds  unattended,  for  when-  ever  my  father  was  away  ata  show,  my  mother,  my  sister,  and  I  had  to  do  the  work,  and  when  my  father  was  home,  I  helped  him.  It  was  not  arduous  work.  It  was  a  little  difficult  for  me  to  carry  a  boxful  of  clean  shavings,  since  the  box  was  clumsy  for  one  of  my  height,  but  the  cleaning  was  simplicity  itself.  The  one  problem  that  I  had  to  solve  was  that  of  getting  rid  of  the  soiled  shavings  from  the  four  summer  kennels  that  were  situated  below  our  cottage.  'The  fences  around  all  the  kennels  were  the  same:  three  or  four  tongue-and-groove  boards  at  the  bottom,  then  a  four-foot  width  of  quarter-inch  mesh  wire,  then  a  tongue-and-groove  board  at  the  top.  One  of  these  fences  went  from  a  back  corner  of  the  cottage  down  through  the  orchard  to  the  woods,  turned  right  and  ran  parallel  to  the  woods,  turned  again  and  came  level  with  the  front  of  the  cottage,  and  then  turned  up  a  slope  and  joined  a  front  corner  of  the  cot-  tage.  Interior  fences  divided  the  area  into  half-acre  pens,  arranged  so  that  each  of  the  summer  kennels  provided  shelter  for  two  pens.  The  shavings  from  these  kennels  were  dumped  over  the  bottom  fence,  where  it  came  up  from  the  woods,  along  a  strip  of  rough,  wild,  wooded  ground.  The  fences  were,  of  course,  much  higher  than  I,  and  it  re-  quired  a  short  period  of  experimentation  for  me  to  work  out  the  most  expeditious  means  of  getting  the  shavings  over  the  fence  without  having  to  climb  over  to  retrieve  the  basket  in  which  they  were  carried.  I  learned  to  fill  the  basket  only  " b"half  full,  take  it  down  to  the  corner  where  an  interior  fence  joined  the  out-  side  fence,  and  then  balance  it  on  the  T  formed  by  the  boards.  'Those  fences  were  the  easiest  things  in  the  world  to  climb  over  at  any  point,  for  one  put  the  inner  side  of  one's  shoe  against  the  wire,  on  top  of  the  boards  at  the  base,  swung  up,  grabbed  thetop  board,  swung  the  free  leg  over  the  top  board,  and  strad-  dled  the  fence,  much  as  one  might  mount  a  pony.  Once  up  there,  I  could  take  the  basket,  dump  its  contents  over  the  fence,  drop  the  empty  basket  back  into  the  run,  swing  down  in  a  reversal  of  the  previous  procedure,  and  go  my  way,  all  in  no  time  at  all.  Cleaning  kennels  was  not  my  only  responsibility.  In  thosedays,  the  dog  biscuits  came  in  thick,  dark-brown,  hard  squares,  which  had  to  be  put  through  a  small  hand-turned  machine  in  order  to  break  them  up  so  that  terriers  could  eat  them.  This  machine,  made  of  wrought  iron  and  bolted  to  a  bench  in  the  cookroom  of  the  Top  Kennel,  had  a  hopper,  which  held  perhaps  four  bis-  cuits  at  one  filling;  two  drums  fitted with  spikes,  which  broke  up  the  biscuits;  a  crank;  and  a  chute.  On  weekdays,  when  the  dogs  were  fed  on  biscuits  soaked  in  a  stew  of  boiled  beef  or  boiled  fish,  the  biscuits  had  to  be  broken  fairly  fine,  which  meant  that  my  sister  and  I  had  to  turn  the  crank  together,  halting  the  grinding  whenever  the  hopper  had  to  be  refilled.  On  Sundays,  when  the  dogs-except  for  the  puppies,  show  dogs,  bitches  in  whelp,  and  so  on-were  given  dry  biscuit  and  water,  the  grinder  was  set  for  the  larger  size  and  I  could  turn  the  crank  alone,  while  my  sister  popped  the  square  biscuits  into  the  hop-  per.  I  suppose  that  it  was  only  as  often  as  I  was  hanging  around  the  kennels  at  feeding  time  thatI  helped  to  feed  the  dogs,  pick  up  the  empty  feed  pans,  wash  them  under  one  of  the  hydrants,  and  then  stack  them  on  acounter  to  dry,  but  it  was  a  job  with  which  I  was  fully  fa-  miliar.  'There  were  many  things  that  I  regarded  as  chores,  then  and  later,  but  cleaning  kennels  and  feeding  dogs  were  not  among  them.  On  one  occasion,  my  responsibilities  fused  into  something  that,  for  a  few   minutes,  verged  on  terror.  It  was  an  evening  that  began  with  a  somewhat  grim  chore  and  ended  with  a  minor  crisis,  'The  chore  arose  because  a  dog  had  died.  It  was  a  very  good  dog,  one  of  the  most  promising  that  the  kennel  had  produced,  and  this  was  a  kennel  that  did  a  good  deal  of  winning.  (It  should  have,  since  it  numbered  over  a  hundred  dogs.)  'This  dog  had  won  at  Southampton,  on  a  very  hot  day,  and  when  the  Cadillac  was  returning  that  evening,  the  dog,  alongwith  the  others,  had  been  given  water  in  [REDACTED].  It  had  been  taken  ill  the  following  day,  andhad  been,  of  course,  brought  into  the  cottage.  It  had  died  in  the  night,  with  its  head  on  the  bottom  step  of  the  stairs,  and  the  body  had  been  autopsied  late  the  following  day,  &quot;mechanical  pneumonia&quot;  being  given  as  the  cause  of  death.  That  night,  my  father  and  mother  went  to  the  movies,  and  for  some  rea-  son  or  other  they  left  my  sister  and  me  without  a  sitter,  perhaps  because  none  was  available.  'The  autopsy  on  the  dog  must  havebeen  performed  late  in  the  day,  because  it  was  Danny,  and  not  my  father,  who  buried  the  dog.  Danny  came  by  the  cottage  in  the  early  dusk,  with  the  body  in  my  express  wagon,  and,  while  I  do  not  remember,  I  as-  sume  that  I  had  been  told  to  help  him  take  it  down  to  the  gravel  pit.  The  body  was  wrapped  in  newspapers,  and  I  took  the  handle  of  the  wagon,  reso-  lutely  turned  my  back  on  a  glimpse  of  bluish  entrails,  and  pulled  the  wagon  down  the  road  to  the  woods  at  the  bot-  tom  of  the  orchard.  There  we  turned  left  and  went  along  the  wagon  road  to  the  gravel  pit.  The  sky  had  been  clouding  over,  and  as  we  reached  the  pit,  Danny  looked  up,  lifted  the  news-  papers  and  the  body  out  of  the  wagon,  and  told  me  to  cut  across  the  orchard  and  get  home.  I  reached  the  cottage  before  the  storm  broke,  and  at  some  point  after  dark  the  lights  went  out,  leaving  my  sister  and  me  in  blackness.  We  could  have  sat  there,  perfectly  safe,  until  our  par-  ents  came  home,  but  that  was  not  the  ,  way  my  sister  and  I  looked  at  things.  We  did  not  feel  it  right  that  we  should  merely  sit  in  the  dark  and  wait.  There  were  kerosene  lamps  upstairs,  but  it  was  pitch  black  in  the  house,  and  I  was  not  particularly  in  favor  of  going  up  those  stairs  alone.  My  sister,  who  was  all  in  favor  of  getting  a  lamp,  had  no  intention  of  going  upstairs  at  all.  Be-  sides,  the  chimney  of  a  kerosene  lamp  is  a  fragile  and  precarious  thing,  not  to  be  juggled  about  on  a  dark  stairway.  Therewas  no  flashlight,  and  we  were  " b"far  too  responsible  to  try  to  light  our  way  with  matches.  We  then  hit  upon  a  freakish  solution.  Not  too  long  before,  at  a  firemen's  carnival  held  on  the  school  grounds,  my  father  had  won  the  only  prize  he  ever  won  on  a  wheel  of  chance.  The  prize  was  a  brown  woolly  Teddy  bear  with  flashlight  bulbs  for  eyes.  The  batteries  were  past  their  prime,  but  they  were  not  yet  dead,  and  the  switch,  which  operated  when  one  pressed  a  certain  place  in  the  broad,  flattened  back,  was  still  in  working  order.  I  took  the  Teddy  bear,  dim-  ly  lighted  my  way  up  into  the  shadowy  rooms  above,  grabbed  a  lamp  with  my  free  hand,  and  came  back  down  the  stairs.  We  then  lit  the  lamp  very  care-  fully,  thereby  reducing  a  crisis  to  the  level  of  the  merely  unusual.  I  kept  the  little  toy  around  for  years  after  its  bat-  teries  had  gone  dead  and  it  was  useless,  for  it  had  served  me  well.  HERE  was  a  period  in  the  latter  part  of  1918  when  I  did  not  go  near  Danny,  or  the  Cadillac,  orthe  coach  barn,  forthe  Cadillac  was  con-  stantly  curtained  and  all  its  surround-  ings  were  under  a  quarantine  of  fear.  It  was  during  the  time  when,  in  the  spring  or  fall,  we  would  sometimes  assemble  on  a  grassy  bank  beside  the  school  build-  ing  and  wave  little  flags  while  we  sang  about  the  long,  long  trail,  and  the  home  fires  that  we  were  keeping  burning,  and  our  shrill  voices  piped  our  vehement  in-  tention  of  canning  the  Kaiser,  all  of  which  must  have  had  a  tremendous  ef-  fect  on  the  war  effort.  It  was  the  period  when  my  annual  gifts  of  St.  Nicholas  preached  the  bestiality  ofthe  Hun;  it  was  the  time  when  barrels  were  placed  beside  theschool  doors  for  the  collection  of  peach  pits,  to  be  converted  into  char-  coal  for  use  in  gas  masks.  It  was  the  time  when  the  militia  staged  a  sham  battle  on  the  Fourth  of  July,  using  the  football  field  beside  the  high  school  as  a  no  man's  land  and  leaving  a  confused  impression  of  two  lines  of  rifle  fire  twin-  kling  in  the  thickening  dusk.  One  line  finally  advanced  behind  dim  objects,  supposed  to  be  tanks,  and  overwhelmed  the  other  line.  It  was  in  the  winter  of  the  influenza  epidemic  that  the  curtains  on  the  Cadillac  were  fastened  down  to  stay  for  weeks.  The  station  wagon  be-  came  an  ambulance,  with  Danny  and  a  chauffeur  from  another  estate  as  its  crew,  and  the  parish  house  in  which  I  went  to  Sunday  school  became  an  emer-  gency  hospital.  A  friend  of  ours,  a  horse-  man  from  an  estate  ten  miles  or  soto  the  west,  was  stricken  with  the  flu  and  thenwith  pneumonia,  and  in  order  that  his  wife  and  two  small  daughters  might  be  closer  to  the  hospital  proper,  seven  miles  to  the  north  of  us,  they  were  tak-  en  in  to  live  with  us  for  six  weeks.  Still,  my  sister  and  I  went  to  school  every  day,  and  aside  from  the  fact  that  I  saw  the  familiar  gray  Cadillac  only  from  a  distance  and  Danny  not  at  all,  it  was  like  any  other  time.  So  it  went,  in  a  world  of  grass  and  trees  and  blossoms  and  snow.  All  the  world  was  a  golden  quietness,  and  the  long,  bright,  scented  years  passed,  and  then  a  man  died,  the  kennel  was  dis-  banded,  and  I  grew  older  in  other  places.  I  moved  out  upon  the  world,  and  the  world  moved  in  upon  me,  and  it  was  as  I  had  known  it  would  be,  for  never  again  was  the  world  to  be  quite  so  bright,  so  fair,  so  enchanted."
