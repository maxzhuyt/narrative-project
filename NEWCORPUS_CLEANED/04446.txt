If  they  got  the  taste  forthe  stuff,  they'd  be  pinching  it  all  the  time.  He'd  give  Piet  some  sugar  and  yeast  and  things  from  the  stores,  for  them  to  make  beer  with  tomorrow,  when  he  was  gone.  He  put  his  hands  deep  in  his  pockets  and  stretched  out  to  the  fire,  with  his  head  sunk  on  his  chest.  The  lyre  player  picked  up  his  flimsy  piece  of  wood  again,  and,  slowly,  what  the  young  man  was  feeling  inside  himself  seemed  to  find  a  voice;  up  into  the  night  beyond  the  fire  it  went,  uncoiling  from  his  breast  and  bringing  ease.  The  lonely  voice  of  the  lyre  went  onand  on,  as  if  it  had  been  made  audible  out  of  infinity  and  could  be  returned  to  infinity.  Nobody  spoke;  the  barriers  of  language  fell  with  si-  lence.  The  whole  dirty  tide  of  worry  and  planning  had  gone  out  of  the  young  man.  'The  small,  high  moon,  outshone  by  a  spiky  spread  of  cold  stars,  repeated  the  shape  of  the  lyre.  He  was  not  aware  how  long  he  sat,  justas  he  had  on  so  many  other  nights,  with  the  stars  at  59  his  head  and  the  fire  at  his  feet.  But  at  last  the  music  stopped,  and  time  be-  gan  again.  There  was  tonight;  there  was  to-  morrow,  when  he  was  going  to  drive  to  Fran-  cistown.  He  stood  up,  and  the  company  frag-  mented.  The  lyre  player  blew  his  nose  into  his  fingers.  Dusty  feet  took  their  accus-  tomed  weight.  'The  men  went  off  to  their  tents,  and  he  went  off  to  his.  Faint  notes  of  music  followed  them.  The  young  man  gave  a  loud,  ugly  animal  yawn-the  sort  of  un-  ashamed  personal  noise  a  man  can  make  when  he  lives  alone.  He  walked  very  slowly  across  the  sand;  it  was  dark,  but  he  knew  the  way  more  surely  than  with  his  eyes.  &quot;Piet!  Hey!&quot;  he  bawled  as  he  reached  his  tent.  &quot;You  get  up  early  tomorrow,  ech?  And  I  don't  want  to  hear  the  lorry  won't  start.  You  get  it  going,  and  then  you  call  me.  D'you  hear?&quot;  He  was  lighting  the  oil  lamp  thatPiet  had  left  ready  on  the  chest,  and  as  it  came  up  softly,  it  brought  the  whole  in-  terior  of  the  tent  with  it--the  chest,  the  bed,  the  clock,  and  the  coy,  smiling  face  of  the  seventeen-year-old  girl.  He  sat  down  on  thebed,  sliding  his  palms  through  the  silky  fur  of  the  kaross.  He  drew  a  breath  and  held  it  for  a  mo-  ment,  looking  round  purposefully.  And  then  he  picked  up  the  photograph,  folded  the  cardboard  support  flat  to  the  frame,  and  put  it  in  the  chest  with  all  his  other  things,  in  readiness  for  the  journey."
