 b"LIVE  albums  aren't  supposed  to  be  as  exciting,  as  immediate  as  the  actual  stage  performances  they  record,  but  (saints  be  praised!)  the  Bloomsbury  Group's  newest,  &quot;Live  at  the  Apollo,&quot;  is  a  shouting,  foot-stomp-  ing,  rafter-shaking  exception  to  this  rule.  Anyone  who  has  not  seen  John  Maynard  Keynes  doing  his  famous  strut,  or  Duncan  Grant  playing  his  bass  while  flat  on  his  back,  can  now  get  an  idea  of  what  he's  been  missing!  The  Bloomsbury  Group  has  always  stood  for  seriousness  about  art  and  skepticism  about  the  affectations  of  the  self-impor-  tant,  and  it  has  been  opposed  to  the  avowed  philistinism  of  the  English  up-  per  classes.  &quot;Live  at  the  Apollo&quot;  is  so  brilliantly  engineered  that  this  daring  Neo-Platonism  comes  through  as  un-  mistakably  as  the  super-bad  Blooms-  bury  beat.  A  few  critics  have  com-  plained  that  the  Bloomsbury  Group  re-  lies  too  heavily  on  studio  effects;  this  album  will  instantly  put  such  objections  to  rest.  The  lead  vocals  (some  by  &quot;Mister  White  Satin&quot;  Lytton  Strachey,  the  others  by  Clive  Bell)  are  solid  and  pure,  even  over  the  enthusiastic  shouts  of  the  notoriously  tough-to-please  Apollo  crowd,  and  the  Stephen  Sisters'  chorus  is  reminiscent  of  the  'Three  Bront\xc3\xa9s  at  their  best.  There  is  very  lit-  tle  &quot;dead  air&quot;  on  this  album,  even  be-  tween  cuts.  On  Band  3  on  the  flip  side,  there  is  a  pause  while  the  sidemen  are  setting  up,  and  if  you  listen  care-  fully  you  can  hear  Leonard  Woolf  and  [REDACTED]  Stephen  coining  withering  epi-  grams  and  exchanging  banter  with  the  audience  about  Macaulay's  essay  on  Warren  Hastings.  Very  mellow,  very  close  textual  criticism.  Lytton  Strachey,  who  has  been  more  or  less  out  of  the  funk-literary  pic-  ture  since  his  girl  friend  threw  boil-  ing  grits  on  him  in  his  Memphis  hotel  room  in  March  of  1924,  proves  here  that  his  voice  is  still  as  sugar-cured  as  ever.  In  his  long  solo  number,  &quot;Why  I  Sing  the  Blues,&quot;  he  really  soars  through  some  heartfelt  lyrics  about  his  &quot;frail  and  sickly  childhood&quot;  and  &quot;those  painfully  introverted  public-  school  years.&quot;  The  song  is  a  triumph  of  melody  and  phrasing,  and  it  provides  some  fascinating  insights  into  the  per-  sonality  of  this  complex  vocalist  and  bi-  ographer.  Much  of  the  credit  for  the  album's  brilliance  must  go  to  G.  E.  Moore,  who  wrote  &quot;Principia  Ethica,&quot;  the  group's  biggest  hit,  as  well  as  to  Lady  Ottoline  Morrell,  the  sound  technician  and  backstage  mama.  The  efforts  of  professionals  like  these,  combined  with  Bloomsbury's  natural  dynamism,  have  produced  that  rarest  of  rarities-a  live  album  that  is  every  bit  as  good  as  being  there.
