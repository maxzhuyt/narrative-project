The  only  sound  that  came  from  him  was  the  Vedic  hymns  he  sang  at  dawn;  his  voice,  though  cracked,  sounded  very  sweet.  His  wife,  Vijay,  was  left  to  rattle  around  by  herself  in  theirlarge  mansion,  with  nothing  to  do  except  bully  the  ser-  vants.  At  the  beginning  of  every  summer  she  had  the  carpets  rolled  up  and  stowed  away,  as  though  her  father-in-law  and  his  guests  were  still  there  to  be  kept  cool  amid  marble  floors  sprinkled  with  rose-  water;  and  every  winter  the  quilts  were  stuffed  with  new  cotton,  though  there  was  no  one  now  to  sleep  under  them  except  her.  The  old  man  on  the  roof  just  covered  himself  with  a  blanket,  as  threadbare  with  age  as  he  was.  She  spent  many  hours  sitting  on  her  ve-  randa,  looking  out  over  the  River  Jumna.  During  the  monsoon,  it  regularly  flooded,  and  one  year  they  had  had  to  go  up  and  down  the  streets  in  boats.  This  was  be-  fore  the  Ring  Road  and  the  fortifying  walls  and  theoverpass  were  built,  which  cut  off  the  view  of  the  river  from  their  house.  But  even  in  her  day  changes  were  taking  place. He  resisted  all  her  attempts  to  make  him  rise,  to  speak,  to  answer  or  assent  to  her  protestations  of  his  innocence.  At  last  realization  dawned  on  her.  She  said  in  a  cold  dead  voice,  &quot;Get  up.&quot;  He  obeyed,  and  when  he  glanced  at  her  face  it  was  closed  against  him  as  against  a  stranger.  &quot;Maji,&quot;  he  begged,  but  her  expression  didn't  change,  and  when  he  tried  to  take  her  hand  to  hold  it  against  his  cheek,  as  he  had  done  a  thousand  times  before,  she  snatched  it  away.  He  burst  into  tears,  but  she  continued  to  sit  rigid,  with  her  big  knees  planted  wide  apart  as  if  made  of  stone,  and  her  eyes  fixed  on  the  wall  above  him.  Although  she  said  nothing,  he  poured  out  his  confession.  He  did  not  try  to  jus-  tify  himself.  Yes,  it  was  the  girl  who  had  taken  the  initiative,  had  tempted  him,  but  he  had  followed  her  lead,  and  with  abandon.  Every  night  he  had  crept  from  under  his  mosquito  net  and  let  himself  out  of  the  house,  so  silently  and  secretly  that  neither  watchman  nor  sweeper  ever  caught  a  glimpse  of  him.  Silently,  se-  cretly,  he  ran  to  her  house,  three  lanes  away,  where  she  was  waiting  to  let  him  in.  While  the  household  slept,  two  sto-  ries  above  them,  they  had  done  what  they  did  together  right  there  on  the  gold  brocade  sofa  of  her  father's  drawing  room,  where  he  entertained  his  guests;  the  smell  of  the  kebabs  that  had  been  eaten  and  the  whiskey  that  had  been  drunk  still  lingered.  She  knew  where  the  bottles  were  kept,  and  they  fornicated  and  drank  whiskey  together,  till  the  household  began  to  stir  at  dawn.  This  wenton  night  after  night,  for  weeks  on  end-those  same  weeks  when  he  and  Vijay  had  walked  hand  in  handby  the  river  and  gone  shopping  in  the  bazaar.  And  there  was  more,  more-for  he  was  now  in  a  confessing  mood,  and  all  his  wickedness  came  rushing  out  of  him.  Sometimes  they  were  so  overwhelmed  by  desire  that  even  in  the  school,  after  her  class  with  him,  they  crept  into  a  cubbyhole  where  winter  clothing  was  stored  in  a  steel  trunk  and  lay  together  in  the  dark,  holding  their  breath  if  they  heard  voices  or  footsteps  passing  outside.  After  he  told  her  everything,  he  re-  mained  standing  before  her  with  his  headbowed.  He  did  not  dare  ask  for  her  forgiveness,  nor  did  she  offer  it.  She  re-  mained  sitting  there  as  before,  stone-  like-until  suddenly  she  rose  and  her  arms  flailed  as  she  beat  him  about  the  headand  shoulders.  He  put  up  his  hands  to  shield  himself  but  did  not  ut-  ter  a  sound.  When  she  had  finished  beating  him,  she  abused  him.  She  called  him  a  thief,  and  a  scoundrel,  who  had  in-  sinuated  himself  into  her  house  and  her  heart  for  what  he  could  get  out  of  her,  for  the  money  shespent  on  him,  the  jewelry  with  which  she  adorned  him.  When  she  said  that,  he  said,  &quot;No,  Maj,&quot;  in  a  still,  broken  voice.  &quot;And  you  laughed  about  me  with  her-  with  your  prostitute.  You  said  wait  till  you  see  what  theold  woman  will  buy  for  me.&quot;  &quot;No,  Maji,&quot;  he  said  again,  in  the  same  voice.  She  slapped  his  face.  &quot;Don't  lie  to  me!  All  this  time  you've  lied  to  me  and  I  believed  you.&quot;  &quot;I  never  lied  to  you,&quot;  he  protested,  but  she  slapped  him  again.  &quot;You  said  you  were  my  son,  that  you  loved  me  like  a  son.&quot;  &quot;It's  the  truth.  I  love  you  like  a  son.&quot;  &quot;Liar!  Liar!&quot;  Silently,  he  bent  down  to  his  three  bundles  and  untied  them.  He  took  out  everything  she  had  given  him-the  clothes,  the  gold-embroidered  slippers,  the  ornaments.  When  he  had  finished,  there  was  only  one  little  bundle  left,  and  he  tied  it  up  again.  The  rest  of  the  things  he  placed  in  a  pile  at  her  feet;  last,  he  took  off  his  new  watchand  laid  it  on  top  of  everything.  He  picked  up  his  re-  maining  bundle  and  went  to  the  door.  There  he  turned  around  to  her,  not  as  if  he  hoped  to  be  called  back  but  perhaps  for  just  one  word  from  her.  She  said  nothing  and  he  left,  the  mark  of  her  hand  still  red  on  his  cheek.  DURING  the  following  days,  there  were  times  when  she  wanted  to  call  him  back.  She  had  no  idea  where  he  had  gone,  and  when  she  went  tothe  school  to  find  out  it  was  as  if  he  had  never  been  there.  A  new  teacher  was  tak-  ing  his  class,  an  ugly,  squat,  pockmarked  man  whom  the  girls  teased  till  he  lost  his  temper  with  them.  Ankle  bells  still  tinkled,  drums  and  lyres  played,  but  now                all  this  was  unbearable  to  her.  That  day,  she  went  to  the  railway  station  and  picked  her  way  among  the  crowd  to  peer  at  the  figures  lying  asleep  on  the  plat-  form  with  their  cloth  bundles  of  food  and  their  water  jugs,  waiting  for  trains  that  had  not  arrived  or  had  not  departed.  But  Ram's  train,  if  he  ever  took  one,  must  have  started  long  ago,  and  he  had  already  reached  his  home  town  and  the  musicians'  alley  with  the  broken-down  house  where  his  mother  and  sisters  lived.  Or  he  was  still  on  the  train,  crammed  in  on  his  third-class  seat,  with  his  bundle  on  his  knees.  She  had  nothing  to  look  forward  to.  It  was  like  that  time  when  the  old  man  first  resigned  his  office  and  they  had  had  togive  up  the  perks  and  pleasures  of  power  that  she  had  begun  so  much  to  enjoy;  and  the  silent  years  that  fol-  lowed  and  her  lonely  solitude  as  he  withdrew  more  and  more.  Now  that  he  had  even  stopped  singing  his  Vedic  hymns,  the  silence  was  total;  whenever  she  went  up  there  she  found  him  perched  on  his  bed,  his  head  sunk  low  and  to  one  side,  like  a  sorrowing  bird.  Some  days  he  did  not  eat  his  food,  but  she  was  too  depressed  to  rally  or  scold  him.  There  were  times  when  shene-  glected  to  take  her  bath  and  sat  on  her  crumpled  bed  with  hergray  hair  hang-  ing  loose.  At  night  she  wandered  around  the  house,  and  when  she  went  upon  the  roof  she  found  the  old  man  lying  on  his  side  with  his  knees  drawn  up.  Shehad  no  thought  to  spare  for  him;  she  came  to  look  over  the  parapet  not  at  the  river  or  the  sky,  both  shin-  ing  and  beautiful,  but  over  the  other  side,  at  the  terrace  of  the  dance  school.  She  saw  the  white  mosquito  nets  stand-  ing  intheir  unbroken  rank.  There  was  no  movement,  no  sign  .of  life,  but  she  knew  that  under  each  net  a  figure  lay  stretched  out,  chastely  asleep.  But  one  night  there  was  movement.  A  net  in  the  middle  of  the  row  of  beds  stirred;  a  leg  was  cautiously  thrust  out;  then  a  man  emerged.  Although  every-  thing  was  floodlit  by  an  almost  full  moon,  she  was  too  far  away  to  make  out  whether  it  was  one  of  theold  teachers  or  a  young  one.  It  might  even  have  been  the  stocky,  pockmarked  Kathak  master  who  had  replaced  Ram.  She  watched  him  glide  past  the  other  beds-perhaps  he  was  only  going  to  the  toilet?  But  the  way  he  moved,  like  a  thief,  made  him  seem  intent  on  a  less  innocent  excur-  sion.  Once  he  stopped  stock-still-  maybe  one  of  the  sleepers  had  stirred-  and  stood  there  with  one  foot  raisedin  the  air.  Then  he  continued,  crouching  low.  She  wanted  to  shout  out  a  warn-  ing-was  it  to  the  others  or  to  him,  to  let  him  know  there  was  one  who  saw  what  he  wasup  to?  He  reached  the  stairs  leading  down  into  the  house  and  disappeared.  She  waited;  if  he  had  only  gone  to  the  toilet,  he  would  return.  She  waited  and  he  did  not  return.  She  continued  to  stand  by  the  par-  apet.  Her  eyes  were  still  fixed  on  the  row  of  white  nets,  but  in  hermind's  eye  she  was  following  the  progress  of  the  one  who  had  left:  out  of  the  house,  down  the  lane,  into  the  next  lane;  to-  night  he  would  have  to  be  very  careful,  because  of  the  moon.  When  he  reached  his  destination,  there  would  be  some-  one  to  let  him  in.  And  then  what?  She  let  her  imagination  roam,  beginning  with  what  Ram  had  told  her-the  bro-  cade  sofa  and  the  bottle  of  whiskey-  until  suddenly  she  was  plunged  into  her  own  ocean  of  memories,  and  it  was  not  the  girl  and  Ram  she  saw  but  herself  and  the  old  man-Prakash.  In  their  marital  bedroom,  with  the  door  locked  and  the  electric  fan  whirring  furiously  but  unable  to  cool  their  hot  bodies;  his  hand  was  over  her  mouth  to  muffle  her  laughter.  She  left  the  parapet  and  went  to  him  where  he  lay  curled  up  on  his  side.  When  she  touched  his  shoulder,  he  sat  up  immediately,  as  if  he  had  not  been  asleep  at  all.  She  said,  &quot;Why  didn't  you  eat  your  food  today?&quot;  There  was  some-  thing  in  her  voice  that  made  him  raise  his  drooping  head.  &quot;If  you  don't  look  af-  ter  yourself,&quot;  she  said,  &quot;what  will  hap-  pen  to  you?  And  to  me?&quot;  She  touched  his  face.  &quot;And  when  did  you  last  shave?&quot;  She  spoke  severely  but  bent  down  to  his  cheek,  brushing  the  gray  stubble  with  her  lips.  He  smiled,  toothless,  blissful.  She  sat  on  the  groundby  his  rickety  bed,  leaning  against  it;  they  talked  to-  gether  and  were  silent  together.  Al-  though  it  was  very  late  when  they  parted,  he  wasup  at  dawn,  and  she  heard  him  singing  again:  As  bees  pile  honey  upon  honey  O  Kama!  Thus  in  my  own  person  O  Kama!  Let  honey  flow  Let  lustre,  brilliance  flow,  and  strength!
