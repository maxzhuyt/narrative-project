SAVBVA  FANG'S  N  1942,  when  I  was  serving  as  a  I  secretary  to  Sawbwa  Fang  Yu-chi,  the  ruler  of  Mangshih,  I  came  to  the  conclusion  that  the  Shans  are  among  the  most  fortunate  people  on  earth.  The  Shans  are  members  of  the  simple  and  delightfully  relaxed  Mongoloid  tribe  that  makes  up  the  greater  part  of  the  population  of  the  Chinese  Shan  States,  of  which  Mangshih,  on  the  Burma  border,  is  the  largest,  and  they  can  afford  to  be  relaxed.  The  climate  of  Mangshih  is  so  mild  and  the  green  and  mountainous  countryside  is  so  fer-  tile  that  rice  and  fruits  grow  in  abun-  dance  without  much  care,  Cattle  and  hogs,  roaming  the  fields  at  will,  enrich  the  soil,  and  many  rivulets,  tumbling  down  the  hills,  turn  small  paddles  at-  tached  to  primitive  wooden  buffing  ma-  chines,  and  thus  spare  the  inhabitants  even  the  small  labor  of  polishing  their  own  rice.  All  this  bounty  of  nature  not  only  makes  the  Shans  leisurely  but  also  accounts  for  their  reputation  as  the  most  law-abiding  people  in  that  part  of  the  world;  crime,  in  such  a  sleepy,  providen-  tial  land,  would  strike  most  people  as  unnecessarily  hard  work.  I  had  been  among  the  Shans  for  about  six  months  when  I  became  involved  in  a  truly  rare  event-Mangshih's  first  criminal  case  in  more  than  thirty  years.  One  afternoon  in  early  autumn,  the  Sawbwa's  chauffeur  came  to  find  me  in  the  yamen,  a  rambling,  many-cham-  bered  structure  in  the  village  of  Mang-  shih,  which  served  as  a  combination  palace,  of-  fice  building,  court-  house,  dormitory,  and  dining  hall.  The  chauf-  feur  told  me  that  the  Sawbwa  was  suffering  from  a  cold  and  would  not  be  coming  to  the  yamen  that  afternoon  but  that  he  wanted  to  see  me  at  his  house  on  business.
