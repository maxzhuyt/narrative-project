Her  fur-  nishings  were  contemporary;  the  fab-  rics  were  sturdy  wools  or  linen,  the  broad  sofa  was  done  in  dark-brown  leather.  Roger  leaned  back;  he  blinked  and  then  sighed,  looking  up  to  the  ceiling.  Roger  had  been  talking,  as  he  rarely  did,  about  his  [REDACTED]  boyhood-that  house  full  of  books  near  the  quiet  James  River,  his  parents,  his  fragile  younger  brother.  &quot;I  sometimes  wish,&quot;  said  Roger,  &quot;that  I'd  taken  the  time  somewhere  along  the  line  to  have  learned  a  little  Greek.  It  seemed  to  give  Richard  so  much  pleasure.&quot;  &quot;But,  darling,  when  would  you  ever  have  had  the  time?&quot;  &quot;That's  just  it,  I  never  had  the  time.&quot;  &quot;I  wonder  what  has  happened  to  Ellen,&quot;  Karen  said.  &quot;Do  you  think  she's  still  put  away?&quot;  &quot;I'm  not  sure  I'd  even  want  to  know,&quot;  said  Roger.  &quot;I  forgot  to  tell  you  that  she  called  me  the  night  he  died.&quot;  &quot;Really?  No.&quot;  &quot;'Yes,  she  was  quite  hysterical.  I  think  she  was  angry  because  she  knew  I  was  Richard's  heir.&quot;  By  now  Roger  had  come  to  believe  that  this  was  in-  deed  the  case.  &quot;Yes,&quot;  he  said,  &quot;She  probably  thought  I  should  give  her  some  of  his  money.&quot;  In  the  large,  safe  room,  beneath  other  large  rooms  where  her  sons  were  all  sleeping,  Karen  shuddered,  and  together  she  and  Roger  sighed,  for  Richard's  pain  and  death  and  for  poor  lost  Ellen's  madness.  &quot;Here,&quot;  said  Karen,  &quot;have  more  coffee.  Poor  darling,  you  look  as  though  you  need  it.&quot;  &quot;You're  right.  I  do.&quot;  And  Roger  reached  out  to  stroke  his  big  wife's  smooth,  dark  cheek. "
