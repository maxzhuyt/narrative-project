There,  the  Rue  Raymond  Losserand-formerly  the  Rue  de  Vanves,  rebaptized  after  the  war  to  honor  a  hero  of  the  Resistance-  entersthe  city  and  serves  as  a  Main  Street  for  the  neighborhood.  The  dis-  trict  is  a  working-class  one,  and  nearby,  just  outside  the  city  limits,  are  the  al-  most  solidly  Communist  suburbs  of  Montrouge  and  Malakoff.  My  friend  told  me  that  in  the  midst  of  the  political  crisis  preceding  General  de  Gaulle's  ac-  cession  to  power,  on  a  certain  beautiful  Sunday-the  first  Sunday  in  June-  there  was  an  odd  multiple  celebration  in  her  neighborhood.  For  one  thing,  that  Sunday  was  the  F\xc3\xa9te  des  M\xc3\xa9res,  as  the  French  call  Mother's  Day,  and  all  the  florists  of  the  quarter  were  doing  a  rushing  business.  There  is  scarcely  ablock  in  the  hum-  bler  quarters  of  [REDACTED]  that  doesn't  have  its  tiny  florist  shop,  usually  with  a  few  geraniums  or  fuchsias  or  hydrangeas  standing  on  a  shelf  outside  the  window.  On  that  Sunday  morning,  close  to  noon,  the  Rue  Raymond  Losserand  and  the  side  streets  near  it  were  full  of  peo-  ple  of  all  ages,  many  of  them  returning  from  Mass,  carrying  potted  plants  or  cut  flowers  home  to  Maman  for  the  F\xc3\xa9te  des  M\xc3\xa9res.  That  Sunday  was  also  the  occasion  for  the  annual  f\xc3\xa9te  foraine,  or  fair,  that  is  held  in  a  Catholic  day  school  in  the  neighborhood.  On  a  side  street,  near  the  Porte,  is  the  residence  of  the  priests  who  teach  in  the  school-  I've  often  seen  their  black-  gowned  figures,  but  have  never  known  what  order  they  belong  to-and  the  school  itself  is  about  a  block  away,  on  the  Rue  Raymond  Losserand.  On  one  Sunday  each  year,  the  playground  and  some  of  the  ground-  floor  rooms  of  the  school  are  transformed  into  a  fair,  with  booths  where  home-  made  cakes  and  pat\xc3\xa9s,  as  well  as  other  articles,  are  of-  fered  for  sale  or  raffled  off.  The  children  of  the  school  always  put  on  a  dramatic  skit  during  the  afternoon,  under  the  supervision  of  the  p\xc3\xa9res,  and  even  those  youngsters  who  aren't  among  the  actors  dress  up  for  the  entire  day  in  masks  and  fancy  costumes.  So,  in  addition  to  the  lines  in  front  of  the  flower  shops  that  Sunday,  and  the  numerous  flower-bearing  inhabitants  of  the  quarter  thronging  the  streets,  there  was  a  constant  coming  and  going  of  priests  hurrying  between  their  resi-  dence  and  the  school,  many  of  them  carrying  chairs  or  tables  or  other  properties  for  the  show,  and  also  quite  a  crush  around  the  school  of  excited  boys  and  girls  dressed  like  Wild  In-  dians  or  Scottish  Highlanders  or  me-  dieval  queens,  or  just  wearing  wigs  and  false  noses.  The  children  were  fling-  ing  confetti  everywhere,  and  they,  the  priests,  and  the  passersby-all  who  had  anything  to  do  with  the  f\xc3\xa9te  foreine  and  a  great  many  who  hadn't-were  cov-  ered  with  it.  Inside  the  school  play-  ground,  waltzes  were  issuing  from  a  loudspeaker.  Quite  suddenly,  among  all  that  surg-  ing  gaiety,  there  was  the  sound  of  sing-  ing,  and  a  procession  came  in  sight  down  the  Rue  Raymond  Losserand  from  the  direction  of  the  Porte  de  Vanves.  It  was  composed  of  men  and  women  walking  four  abreast  (a  column  just  wide  enough  not  to  block  auto  traffic),  and  some  of  the  marchers  were  carrying  banners  inscribed  with  the  hammer  and  sickle  and  the words  &quot;Parti  Communiste  de  Montrouge&quot;  and  &quot;Parti  Communiste  de  Malakoff.&quot;  My  friend,  who  was  on  the  Rue  Raymond  Losse-  rand,  doing  some  Sunday-morning  shopping,  stood  on  the  sidewalk  and  watched  the  marchers  advance,  and  as  they  came  nearer  she  became  aware-more  aware,  probably,  than  most  of  the  French  people  present-  of  what  they  were  singing.  From  the  distance,  the  tune  had  sounded  like  &quot;Auld  Lang  Syne,&quot;  and  now  she  realized  that  it  was  indeed  &quot;Auld  Lang  Syne,&quot;  but  with  new  words-very  sim-  ple  words,  repeated  over  and  over-  to  fit  the  occasion.  &quot;Down  with  de  Gaulle,&quot;  the  Communists  were  singing.  &quot;A  bas  de  Gaulle,  &amp;  bas  de  Gaulle,  &amp;  bas,  &amp;  bas,  &amp;  bas.  A  bas  de  Gaulle,  &amp;  bas  de  Gaulle,  &amp;  bas,  &amp;  bas,  &amp;  bas.&quot;  The  new  words  fitted  the  old  music  perfectly. So,  what  with  the  beautiful  weather  and  the  catchy  tune,  it  wasn't  long  before  almost  everybody  on  the  street  was  joining  in  the  singing.  The  Mother's  Day  flower  buyers,  the  children  in  their  costumes  and  false  faces,  passersby  and  spectators-all  except  the  p\xc3\xa9res,  in  their  long,  black  confetti-splashed  habits-were  singing  the  strains  of  &quot;Auld  Lang  Syne.&quot;  Some  of  them  stopped  singing  rather  quickly,  realizing  that  by  joining  in  the  song  they  were  expressing  a  solidarity  that  they  didn't  entirely  feel.  And  the  p\xc3\xa9res  did  their  best  to  silence  the  children,  scolding  them  and  cuffing  some  of  them  when  they  caught  them.  But  plenty  of  peo-  ple-even  plenty  of  children-kept  right  on  singing.  And  then  a  shout  went  up  as  the  front  line  of  the  marchers  was  joined  by  a  woman  everyone  knew.  She  was     the  mother  of  one  of  the  flewristes-a  lady  florist  who  has  a  little  shop  quite  near  my  friend's  studio-and  she  was  singing  louder  than  anybody  else. The  mother,  who  lives  with  her  on  a  pension  of  some  kind,  had  been  helping  her  out  in  the  shop  that  morning,  as  she  always  does  on  particu-  larly  busy  days,  and  had  apparently  'become  overstimulated  either  by  the  fact  that  it  was  Mother's  Day  or  by  the  rush  of  business,  and  so  had  taken  time  out  to  visit  one  or  more  of  the  bars  in  the  quarter,  for  relaxation.  Neither  she  nor  her  daughter  is  a  Com-  munist,  but  suddenly  there  she  was,  at  the  head  of  the  marchers,  reeling  a  little,  helping  hold  up  one  of  the  Party  banners,  and  bawling  out  the  new  &quot;Auld  Lang  Syne.&quot;  The  fleuriste  was  terribly  distressed,  and  began  crying,  &quot;Ma-  man!
