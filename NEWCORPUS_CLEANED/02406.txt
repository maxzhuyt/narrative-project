Thomas  was  to  be  trans-  ferred  to  the  West  as  soon  as  his  af-  fairs  could  be  settled,  and  Matthew  was  to  leave  his  high  school  for  a  prep  school.  Jane  did  what  she  could  to  delay  this.  When  prospective  buyers  came,  she  gently  disparaged  the  house,  and  even,  a  few  times  after  rains,  poured  water  on  the  basement  floor,  then  took  herself  to  the  attic  for  having  borne  false  witness.  But  at  last  the  house  was  sold,  and,  clinging  to  Matthew  and  weeping  a  little-the  second  time  in  his  life  he  had  seen  her  weep-she  left  for  a  train  to  New  York  and  a  ship  to  [REDACTED].  The  brooch  remained  on  the  closet  shelf.  HEN  Jane  reached  the  cottage,  seven  years  before  Matthew's  wartime  visit,  she  found  Joseph's  books  scattered  on  tables  and  chairs  through-  out  its  few  rooms,  and  she  picked  them  up  and  put  them  in  a  closet  off  the  kitchen  and  locked  the  door.  Matthew  finished  school  and  went  to  college,  dutifully  writing  her  from  time  to  time,  and  Jane  promptly  re-  plied.  Like  Joseph,  Matthew  reported  (filling  space,  perhaps)  what  he  had  been  studying  and  reading,  but,  with  Joseph  in  mind,  Jane  often  wondered  whether  Matthew  was  telling  her  all  that  he  was  reading.  There  were  no  roses  growing  around  the  cottage  after  all,  so  she  planted  a  number  of  bushes.  She  coddled  them,  sent  photographs  of  them  to  Matthew  when  they  were  in  bloom,  sent  photo-  graphs  of  herself,  tiny  and  trim.  The  knot  of  hair  had  long  since  turned  white.  She  walked,  breathing  the  sea  air,  and  sewed  and  read  her  Bible.
