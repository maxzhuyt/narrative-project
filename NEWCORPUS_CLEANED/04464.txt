This  meant  that  there  were  at  least  eight  people  sitting  in  each  compartment  of  the  second-class  car-eight  people,  in  two  rows  of  four,  facing  each  other.  In  one  there  were  nine;  a  woman  was  holding  a  three-year-old  child  in  her  arms.  She  was  hardly  more  than  a  prop  for  the  child,  the  length  of  whose  curls,  fair,  turning  to  brown,  made  it  hard  to  tell  whether  it  was  a  little  boy  or  a  little  girl-a  little  boy,  probably,  from  the  sad  way  the  mother  looked  at  the  curls  and  touched  them,  as  if  she  were  think-  ing  that  soon  they  would  have  to  be  cut.  Her  husband  sat  beside  her-a  large  man  who  occupied  far  more  room  than  was  his  share.  The  span  of  his  hips,  however,  was  more  than  offset  by  the  slimness  of  a  dark  little  man  in  his  forties.  This  man  had  on  a  sleazy  striped  coat,  light-gray  flannel  trousers,  and  pointed  black  shoes,  badly  creased.  He  smoked  Nazionali.  He  got  the  most  out  of  each  drag-indeed,  out  of  each  ciga-  rette,  not  parting  with  ittillit  was  an  indecent-looking  stub. Beside  him,  next  to  the  window,  was  an  old  man  with  a  beard.  He  had  kept  his  thick,  coarse  brown  overcoat  on,  though  all  the  others  had  taken  theirs  off.  He  might  havebeen  a  shepherd,  or  a  defrocked  priest,  or  a  country  doctor.  He  held  his  hands-shiny,  old,  red  hands-clasped  in  front  of  him.  He  looked  fairly  robust,  but  this  may  have  been  due  to  the  thickness  of  his  clothing;  it  was  obvious  that  he  was  wearing  at  least  two  pairs  of  woollen  socks.  Most  ofthe  time  he  looked  out  of  the  win-  dow.  On  the  opposite  side  of  the  compart-  ment,  facing  the  old  man,  there  was  a  boy,  about  eighteen,  red-cheeked  and  with  smooth  black  hair-a  student,  judging  by  the  book  with  mathematical  figures  in  it  that  he  was  assiduously  reading.  Beside  him  there  was  a  sol-  dier,  who  seemed  perfectly  content  to  just  sit  and  wait;  he  had  the  ingenuous  look  of  a  recruit. Last,  in  the  corner     by  the  door  that  led  into  the  corridor,  and  opposite  the  woman  and  the  child,  sat  a  wide-eyed  man  in  his  early  thirties,  who  spent  most  of  his  time  looking  at  his  fellow-passengers  as  if  he  were  siz-  ing  them  up.  At  one  point,  he  took  a  pad  and  a  pencil  out  of  his  pocket  and  began  to  draw  the  mother  and  child.  She  looked  up  at  him,  understood  what  he  was  doing,  and  became  uncom-  fortable.  Sohe  stopped.  He  wasn't  an  artist,  because  the  drawing  wasn't  any  good.
