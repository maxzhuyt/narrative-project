Jacob  had  written  inviting  them  to  come,  and  they  had  been  glad  to  find  a  place  where  they  would  not  have  to  pay  anything  for  the  fortnight's  holiday  they  were  taking,  for  Benjamin  had  only  just  made  a  fresh  start  in  a  business  of  his  own-he  had  taken  over  the  assets  of  a  bankrupt  butter  fac-  tory-and  they  had  no  money  to  spare.  So  Sarah  saw  once  again  the  veld  and  the  sky,  the  camelthorn  trees  and  the  rock-littered  river,  the  railway  line  and  the  white  plastered  house  that  had  been  all  she  had  known  of  [REDACTED]  Africa  for  so  many  months.  Once  again  she  heard  the  hens  squawking  and  grumbling  in  their  pen,  the  creaking  of  the  windmill,  and  the  occasional  sound  ofa  train  ap-  proaching  or  receding  from  Dors  River  Station.  But  the  emptiness  and  blank-  ness  of  the  place,  the  loneliness  of  the  lives  the  Rosing  brothers  lived  no  longer  threatened  her;  she  was  no  longer  up-  set  by  them.  Instead,  she  felt  a  deep  compassion  for  the  country  itself,  as  if  it  were  a  sentient  being  that  knew  and  grieved  over  its  own  barrenness  and  pallor,  that  felt  the  ugliness  and  weight  of  the  rocks  that  lay  on  it,  the  harshness  ofthe  scar  the  Dors  River  cut  across  it,  the  fierceness  of  the  stroke  of  the  sun  each  day.  Of  Manny  she  thought  hard-  lyat  all.  At  night,  in  their  room,  she  let  her  husband  do  what  he  wished  with  her,  and  he,a  man  in  his  mid-thirties  who  had  always  been  awkward  and  shy  with  her  before,  was  roused  to  an  intensity  of  desire  that  only  her  passive  yet  total  compliance  could  have  provoked  in  him.  His  touch  never  made  her  tremble,  but  she  was  glad  she  was  able  to  satisfy  him  so  easily.  Her  attention  was  directed  elsewhere-where,  she  did  not  know.  But  she  thought  much  of  the  children  they  might  have.
