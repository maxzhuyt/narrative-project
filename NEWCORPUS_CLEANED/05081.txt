My  wife  and  I  had  asked  to  dinner  an  old  friend  whose  family  was  away  from  town.  I'll  call  him  Arthur  Daley.  He  is  a  precise,  considerate  man  of  forty-  eight,  peaceful,  a  Republican,  but  not  a  true  doctrinaire.  I  have  known  him  to  contemplate  splitting  his  ticket,  and  he  may  well  have  done  so  at  one  time  or  another.  But  he  is  also  a  man  of  con-  viction,  and  requires  no  excessive  prod-  ding  to  let  fly  a  peremptory  speech.  It  happens  that  on  Election  Night  a  year  ago  Arthur  was  excessively  prod-  ded,  by  a  fellow  named  Charlie  Fisher,  and  subsequently  spoke  at  length.  So  when  Charlie  called  up  this  Novem-  ber  3rd  and  asked  if  he  might  drop  in  that  evening,  I  told  him  he  might,  but  I  was  uneasy.  Charlie  is  a  bachelor,  round-  faced  and  somewhat  bald,  a  Demo-  crat,  and  ordinarily  a  man  of  at  least  partial  good  will,  but  on  that  unforgot-  ten  night  he  attacked  with  dazzlingly  sustained  invective  all  the  thirty-three  million  citizens  who  had  voted  for  Ei-  senhower.  Arthur,  listening,  demurred  and,  when  Charlie  turned  on  him,  gave  as  good  as  he  got,  except  that  his  adjec-  tives  were  less  vivid  than  Charlie's.
