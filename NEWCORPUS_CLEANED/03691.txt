Especially  the  east  side  was  bad-where  the  noreasters  hit.  That  side  had  hardly  any  mortar  left  in  it  at  all;  some  bricks  had  set-  tled  and  produced  the  sag.  And  that  was  the  side  he  started  patching  up,  sitting  on  the  roof  ridge.  Soon  he  would  be  done.  Soon  the  whole  house  would  be  in  tiptop  shape,  and  he  could  leave.  Where  would  he  go? For  a  moment,  he  stopped  working,  just  sat  astride  the  ridge,  his  gaze  directed  far  beyond  what  he  saw-the  elmed  main  street,  above  the  elms  the  white  church  steeples,  the  neighboring  houses  hatched  and  cross-  hatched  by  the  trees,  still  bare.  A  pretty  sight.  Its  simple  charm  was  enough  for  his  wife,  but  his  mind  skimmed  beyond  it,  toward  Genoa.  Well,  he  must  get  on  with  his  patching.  He  crouched,  then  stood  on  the  ridge  as  he  worked  up  toward  the  upper  portion  of  the  chimney.  It  was  clear  but  cold,  late  aft-  ernoon,  and  the  sun  was  about  to  set.  The  drop  in  temperature  made  the  furnace  go  on,  and  suddenly  he  felt  hot  air-or,  rather,  fumes-bathing  his  face.  He  welcomed  the  warmth  and  continued  working,  until  all  at  once  he  was  so  dizzy  he  had  to  drop  the  trowel,  grasp  the  chimney  with  both  hands,  and  hang  on.  It  gave.  He  had  the  top  half  of  the  chimney  in  his  arms.  With  chin  and  arms  and  chest  he  held  it  still,  but  it  was  heavy  and,  being  cleft  obliquely,  would  not  stay  put  without  him.  Nor  could  he  lift  it  and  rest  it  on  the  ridge.  Yet  he  must  get  away  from  the  fumes.  He  couldn't  let  it  drop  just  anywhere-there  might  be  someone  below.  Slowly  he  inched  back,  and  felt  the  top  half  of  the  chimney  coming  down  toward  his  hips,  thighs,  knees.  With  legs  flexed  and  arms  out-  stretched,  he  held  it,  reeled  with  it  on  the  ridge,  and,  directing  its  fall  toward  the  back  ofthe  house,  spun  with  it  down  the  side  of  the  roof  toward  the  edge,  until,  able  to  see  there  was  no-  body  below,  he  threw  it.  Then,  free  at  last  and  light-lighter  than  he  had  ever  felt-he  managed  to  stop  before  the  eaves.  There  he  lay,  flat  as  a  shin-  gle.  How  frail  and  fragile  the  house  was,  he  thought,  that  it  couldn't  even  stand  a  hug.  A  little  at  a  time,  he  moved  toward  the  ladder,  reached  it,  lifted  a  foot  over  the  top  rung,  and  found  the  next  one.  Sixteen  more  and  he  came  to  the  soft-  ness  of  the  ground.  He  went  round  to  the  kitchen,  staggered  into  the  living  room,  and  lay  on  a  sofa.  He  closed  his  eyes,  and  immediately  he  saw  himself  on  the  roof  again,  spinning  down  the  side,  getting  closer  and  closer  to  the  edge  but  never  over  it-he  couldn't  dump  the  chimney  or  himself.
