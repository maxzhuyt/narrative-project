His  work  is  difficult  to  clas-  sify,  and  he  has  been  called  variously  an  &quot;assembler,&quot;  an  &quot;expressionist-col-  lagist,&quot;  and  a  &quot;fruitcake.&quot;  Vemler  first  attracted  attention  when,  working  with  the  W.P.A.  in  [REDACTED]  in  the  thir-  ties,  he  made  a  series  of  sculptures  out  of  dust.  This  was  the  beginning  of  the  Catch-As-Catch-Can  School,  of  which  Vemler  was  founder,  leader,  and  sole  member.  The  chief  character-  istic  of  the  school  is  that  it  scorns  what  Vemler  calls  &quot;art-supply-store  stuff&quot;  and  employs  only  materials  that  are  readily  available-preferably  ephemeral  or  thrown-out  ones.  &quot;'Things  that  disin-  tegrate,  fall  down,  die,  run  away,  rot  and  stink-that  is  art  for  me,&quot;  Vemler  wrote  in  his  famous  manifesto,  &quot;Use  It!&quot;  With  his  &quot;Borrowings&quot;  series  in  the  fifties,  Vemler's  work  took  a  con-  troversial  turn,  and  eventually  such  pieces  as  &quot;29  Borrowed  Chickens&quot;  and  &quot;Borrowed  De  Soto&quot;  led  to  his  ar-  rest  and  imprisonment.  &quot;I  found  the  range  of  materials  severely  restricted  in  jail,&quot;  Vemler  has  said.  &quot;I  was  forced  to  think  in  a  completely  new  way  about  art. It  caused  the  guards  to  give  me  a  wide  berth.&quot;  When  he  was  paroled,  Vemler  continued  to  use  himself  and  his  per-  sonal  life  as  material  for  his  art,  and  works  like  &quot;Danse  Macabre&quot;  (1962),  which  incorporated  his  dog  Herb  and  his  wife's  bridge  partners,  brought  him  wide  acclaim,  at  least  in  taxidermic  circles. VEMLER  says  that  the  inspiration  for  his  Schneekunst  came  to  him  while  he  slept.  &quot;I  dreamed  I  was  a  little  boy  again,&quot;  he  explains,  &quot;walking  with  my  parents  on  a  winter  day.  They  were  holding  my  hands.  We  were  all  happy  and  laughing.  Suddenly  my  father's  hand  felt  icy  cold,  and  I  turned  and  saw  that  he  was  wearing  a  top  hat  and  carrying  a  broom.  When  I  woke  up,  my  bedroom  floor  was  covered  with  snow.  I  had  left  the  window  open.  After  breakfast,  I  was  taking  my  usual  stroll  around  the  yard,  when  all  at  once  it  hit  me-here  was  my  quarry,  my  Pietrasanta!  My  medium  was  all  around  me!  I  was  possessed  by  the  whiteness-the  szowness-of  this  snow.  I  ran  some  through  my  fingers,  and,  as  luck  would  have  it,  it  was  perfect  packing  snow-not  too  wet,  not  too  dry,  light  but  substantial.  I  fell  to  work.  After  what  could  have  been  no  more  than  half  an  hour,  I  stepped  back,  full  of  awe.
