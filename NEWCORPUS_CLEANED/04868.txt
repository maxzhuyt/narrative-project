 b"HECTOR BOUDREAU had  come down  to  the  boat  very  early.  Everybody  knew  that  it  would  be  too  rough  for  trawling-there'd  been  a  small-craft  warning  out  for  days-but  he'd  come  anyhow,  and  tinkered  with  the  engine  and  cleaned  out  the  tiny  cabin. The  pillow  cover  was  stained  with  greasy  fingerprints  and  smelled  strongly  of  hair  oil.  He  took  it  off  and  threw  it  to  the  dock,  and  dropped  the  pillow  in  the  sun. They  were  riding  the  wind,  hardly  moving  their  wings.  &quot;La-bas,&quot;  P\xc3\xa9rique  said,  pointing  at  a  boat  almost  out  of  sight  in  the  begin-  ning  dark.  &quot;Can't  tell  who  it  is,  this  far.&quot;  &quot;''They  must  about  turning  up  the  bayou  right  now,  this  minute,&quot;  Hector  said.  &quot;We  ought  to  be  up  there  with  them,&quot;  Archange  said.  &quot;We  going  to  get  there,&quot;  Hector  said.  &quot;We  got  the  wind  running  with  us.&quot;  &quot;All  the  same,  we  can't  gono  fast-  er,&quot;  his  father  said,  and  stretched  out  on  the  bunk  to  rest  his  leg.  &quot;And  I  wish  we  was  there  right  now.&quot;  P\xc3\xa9rique  laughed,  and  crinkled  his  little  eyes.  &quot;We  going  to  make  it.  Or  maybe  we  drown.&quot;  The  old  man  pulled  a  knife  from  his  shirt  pocket  and  began  to  clean  his  fin-  gernails.  Hector  motioned  P\xc3\xa9rique  to  take  the  wheel,  and  went  on  deck.  They  were  taking  a  little  spray;  there  was  a  little  trickle  running  out  the  long  slit  scuppers.  With  one  hand  for  balance  on  the  lifeline  that  circled  the  cabin,  he  stared  back  at  the  island.  &quot;Hey,  boy!&quot;  P\xc3\xa9rique  called.  &quot;Ain't  nothing  you  see  out  there  you  can't  see  from  in  here!&quot;  &quot;If  you  fall  over,&quot;  his  father  called,  &quot;there  ain't  going  to  be  any  use  even  trying  to  find  you.&quot;  &quot;Why  she  stay,  in  place  of  hiding  with  the  kids?  Why  she  didn't  save  herself  no  beating?&quot;  Hector  said  aloud.  Behind  them,  the  island  was  just  a  clump  of  trees  with  a  clump  of  storm-col-  ored  clouds  resting  right  on  top  of  them.  The  rain  had  begun  there,  too;  you  could  tell  by  the  color  of  the  haze.  The  trees  would  be  lash-  ing  around  like  crazy,  he  thought,  and  for  a  minute  he  saw  it  all  clear-the  peo-  ple  running  for  cover,  slam-  ming  doors  after  them,  put-  ting  in  the  bolts.  She'd  be  doing  that,  too.  He  could  see  her,  racing  up  the  steps.  She'd  have  stayed  outside  until  the  last  minute,  after  she'd  sent  the  kids  inside.  Maybe  she'd  even  stay  out  in  the  rain  for  a  while,  be-  cause  it  would  be  cool.  But  she  wasn't  one  to  do  that,  he  thought.  She'd  be  inside,  making  sure  the  covers  were  tight  around  the  furniture  and  that  the  kids  were  in  bed.  And  then  she'd  sit  down,  rocking  and  wait-  ing.  He  cocked  his  head  at  the  faintly  green-tinged  clouds  streaming  over-  head,  and  listened  to  the  wind  that  was  carrying  them.  And  suddenly  he  began  to  laugh.  He  was  still  grinning  when  he  turned  inside  and  closed  thecabin  door  behind  him."
