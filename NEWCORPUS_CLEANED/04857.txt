A  dozen  yards  beyond  the  cornice,  there  was  a  sharp  turn  to  the  right  again,  and  the  white  gallery  opened  out,  as  spacious  and  arched  as  a  man-made  tunnel.  At  once,  our  lamps  seemed  to  give  out  more  light,  reflected  on  walls  that  sparkled  like  frost.  Right  at  the  en-  trance  of  the  gallery,  on  a  flat  wall  space  between  two  folds  of  stalactite,  was  the  Stone  Age  sign,  bright  red  against  the  white  background.  Calcareous  water,  percolating  through  the  rock  for  many  centuries,  had  coated  the  wall  with  a  thin,  shiny  layer  of  calcium  carbonate,  in  this  particular  place  only  a  little  less  transparent  than  glass.  In  spite  of  the  seeming  freshness  of  the  red  marks,  they  had  been  there  for  ten  thousand  years  at  least,  perhaps  more,  and  they  con-  vinced  even  the  skeptlcal  Cabardés.  &quot;Authentique!&quot;  he  pronounced  grave-  ly  after  a  close  inspection,  tapping  the  glassy  coating  with  his  finger-  nail.  Paul  and  I  had  speculated  a  lot  about  those  marks.  We  had  seen  the  claviform  sign  in  other  caves,  but  where  we  had  seen  it  there  were  usually  other  signs  or  drawings.  And  what  was  the  mean-  ing  of  the  three  dots?  Had  we  perhaps  come  on  a  signpost  that  gave  some  indi-  cation  of  the  plan  of  the  cave?  Cabardés  seemed  inclined  to  believe  this  might  be  so.  &quot;A  club  and  three  dots,&quot;  Paul  mused.  &quot;One  dot  for  each  of  us.&quot;  Whatever  he  meant  by  that  remark,  it  appeared  to  displease  the  young  archeologist.  With  a  frown  and  a  shrug,  he  turned  away  from  the  wall  and  walked  ahead.  From  time  to  time,  he  paused  to  tap  with  a  hammer  cascades  of  stalactites  that  might  mask  open-  ings  in  the  cave  wall.  Each  tap  gave  back  a  sound  that  was  discouragingly  muted.  Finally,  holding  up  his  lamp  to  inspect  what  was  overhead,  he  said,  &quot;If  there  was  anything  on  the  vault,  it's  hidden.&quot;  &quot;Quite  so,&quot;  said  Paul.  We  had  gone  over  that  ceiling  pretty  carefully.  Thou-  sands  of  tiny  stalactites  hung  from  it,  like  nails  of  glass.  As  we  reached  the  mass  of  broken  rock  that  choked  off  the  gallery,       Cabardés  said,  &quot;So  this  is  the  end?  Hmm.&quot;  I  detected  an  echo  of  M. He  left  it  to  me  to  ask  about  digging  in  the  debris.  I  said  I  had  seen  just  such  a  slide  in  one  of  the  well-known  caves;  it  had  yielded  Stone  Age  tools  as  well  as  fossil  bones-  probably  brought  down  from  an  upper  gallery  that  was  no  longer  accessible.  By  way  of  answer,  our  expert  started  scrambling  up  the  rock  pile.  Halfway  to  the  top,  he  stooped  and,  setting  down  his  lamp,  tugged  at  something  dark  that  was  wedged  in  a  crevice.  It  came  free,  in  a  scattering  of  dried  clay. With  their  color  and  patina,  they  seem  to  have  an  existence  of  their  own;  I  never  really  thought  of  them  as  having  once  been  clothed  in  muscle  and  flesh. It  may  have  been  a  touch  of  cave  panic;  I  had  never  before  been  nervous  about  that  cornice,  but,  going  back,  I  really  won-  dered  whether  I  would  be  able  to  negotiate  it. Having  chalked  a  word  of  caution  about  falling  rock  on  the  threshold  for  the  benefit  of  future  visitors,  we  rejoined  Maman  and  M.  Boule.  Paul  parried  their  questions,  and  he  told  his  mother  jokingly  that  the  Ancestor  had  warned  us  to  stay  away.  M. But  after  stressing  some  of  the  hazards  of  cave  exploring,  he  did  not  forget  to  add  that  for  him  the  visit  to  our  cave  had  left  only  the  pleasantest  of  memories.  This  was  no  news  to  Paul  and  me,  for  when  we  came  out  of  the  cave  with  Cabardés  that  September  afternoon,  we  had  been  met  by  an  idyllic  picture:  Maman  in  her  rose-garlanded  hat  and  flowered  dress,  like  an  elderly  nymph  by  the  sun-flecked  pool,  and  the  admir-  ing  scientist  at  her  feet  beside  the  open  hamper.
