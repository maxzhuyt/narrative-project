Monroe  was  in  the  sitting  room  a  little  ahead  of  time,  too,  and  I  could  see  that  he  dis-  approved,  though  he  managed  a  smile  of  courtesy.  &quot;Why,  lovely-how  lovely  of  you!&quot;  Mrs.  Cowan  cried,  snatching  her  glasses  off  and  turning  a  sturdy  pink.  She  put  the  glasses  back  and  ex-  amined  the  bottle.  &quot;Frerch  cham-  pagne!&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Why,  my  goodness!  Mr.  Cowan-my  husband-we  had  French  champagne  at  our  wedding,  and  every  single  anniversary  as  long  as  he  lived  he  brought  it  home  and  we  had  it  together  in  celebration.  In  this  very  room.Well,  I  just  can  hardly-&quot;  &quot;Momma,&quot;  Monroe  said  quietly,  but  he  was  rigid.  &quot;You  know  that's  not  so.  You  just  know  it  is\xc2\xbb't,  Momma.&quot;     I  don't  recall  the  next  short  while  in  full  detail.  I  probably  tried  not  to  seem  to  take  much  notice.  For  Monroe,  the  little  speech  he  made  then  must  have  constituted  an  outburst,  though  for  someone  else  it  would  havebeen  just  pressing  a  sensitive  point.  I  remember  his  saying  that  she  had  often  been  mis-  taken.  His  father  had  never  brought  alcohol  of  any  sortinto  the  house,  whatever  he  may  have  done  at  the  club.  Nor  had  he  been  carried  back  from  the  cemetery-he  had  merely  been  lodged  there  overnight  in  a  vault.  There  was  more.  He  referred  once  or  twice  to  his  diary.  He  quoted  dates  in  conflict.  He  was  miserable-astonished,  even  outraged,  at  himself-but  plainly  helpless  in  the  powerful  gripof  facts.  I  remember  that  his  mother  waited,  and  that  finally  she  used,  quite  levelly,  the  words  &quot;contradict,&quot;  applied  to  him,  and  &quot;lying,&quot;  applied  to  herself,  and  that  they  crumpled  him.  He  got  out  a  cigarette,  though  he  didn't  light  it,  and  he  left  almost  at  once.  &quot;If  you  gentle-  men  will  join  me  here  at  five,&quot;  she  said  to  Lyman  and  me  in  the  most  formal  tone  I  had  ever  heard  her  use,  &quot;we  will  have  the  lovely  wine  in  honor  of  my  husband.  It  will  seem  like  old  times.&quot;  She  was  still  thinking  about  her  hus-  band  when  we  met  at  five.  She  said  it  wasn't  the  least  bit  like  Monroe  to  be-  little  his  father.  &quot;Mr.  Cowan  was  a-  ways  most  thoughtful  of  me.  He  loved  to  do  little  gallantries,  and  to  celebrate.&quot;  She  had  put  on  a  bright-blue  dress,  the  fire  was  crackling,  the  cham-  pagne  popped  and  bub-  bled,  but  the  occasion  was  more  thoughtful  than  fes-  tive.  She  kept  trying,  with  no  anger,  to  account  for  Monroe-not  for  his  out-  burst,  just  for  the  way  he  was.  I  guessed  that  she  was  trying  to  explain  him,  much  as  he  had  tried  to  explain  her.  She  thought  it  might  be  her  fault  how  (as  she  put  it)  down  to  earth  he  was.  Once,  when  he  was  a  little  boy,  he  had  said  he  could  see  the  wind.  &quot;I  kept  telling  him  all  he  could  see  was  the  trees  bowing  before  it  and  the  leaves  tugging  at  the  branches,  but  he  wanted  it  his  way,  and  he  was  im-  pudent,  and  I  spanked  him.  I  don't  know  what  got  into  me.  Maybe  I  marked  him  for  life.  You  can't  punish  a  child  for  imagining  things,  but  you  just  I  couldn't  have  impudence.&quot;  She  was  silent  for  a  while,  and  then  she  said,  more  to  herself  than  to  us,  or  perhaps  it  was  to  Monroe,  &quot;Why,  I  could  as  easy  have  said  that  about  the  wind  myself.  Things  are  just  what  you  make  them  out  tobe.  I  don't  see  harm  to  that-making  the  most  of  what's  handy  while  you  can.&quot;  She  added  that  the  bird  of  time  had  but  a  little  way  to  flutter.
