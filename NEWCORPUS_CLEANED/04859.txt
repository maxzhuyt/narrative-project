 b"IN  late  1942  and  the  first  halfof  the  next  year,  while  I  was  an  Air  Force  radio  operator,  I  came  slowly  and  rather  reluctantly  to  know  a  boy  whom  I'll  call  Glenn  Clayton.  The  slowness  and  reluctance  were  at  least  partly  ow-  ing  to  Glenn's  own  nature,  or  to  what  fora  long  time  it  seemed  to  be.  He  was  lonely,  but  he  was  also  suspicious,  and  ready  to  take  instant  flight  or  umbrage.  He  longed  to  make  friends,  but  he  didn't  at  all  know  how  to  go  about  it,  and  when  he  did  come  close  to  people,  he  always,  as  I  learned  eventually,  de-  serted  or  rejected  them  in  time,  though  he  kept  and  treasured  some  souvenir  of  each  of  them.  Glenn  was  an  airplane  mechanic,  and  he  did  his  work  with  a  kind  of  raging  efficiency  when  he  did  it  at  all.  He  joined  the  air-transport  squadron  I  was  with  just  before  it  set  out  for  Eng-  land  along  the  northern  route,  and  I  remember  him  first  only  as  a  whiskery  face-round,  fierce,  and  blue-eyed-  looking  down  from  the  top  of  a  double-  decked  bunk  in  a  barracks  in  Goose  Bay.  Pendent  there  in  the  smoke  and  the  dim  light,  the  face,  as  I  think  back,  seems  wistful  as  well  as  fierce,  though  it  hard-  ly  struck  me  or  anybody  else  as  wistful  at  the  time.  There  were  many  beards  among  us,  for  there  was  only  cold  water  in  the  barracks,  and  most  of  the  men  clipped  theirs  into  shapes  that  gratified  them,  but  Glenn's  stayed  untrimmed  and  bel-  licose.  No  one  knew  much  about  him.  He  was  reported  to  have  had  a  previous  term  of  service  in  the  Army.  Once,  he  re-  marked  that  he  had  slept  on  better  beds  in  jail.  He  was  generally  disliked.  He  had  been  demoted  earlier  from  staff  sergeant  to  private  for  some  piece  of  insubordination.  One  cold  day,  the  other  mechanic  assigned  to  his  airplane  sneaked  off  to  fish  through  the  ice  ofa  nearby  river,  and  when  orders  came  for  the  planes  to  be  ready  for  takeofi,  Glenn  tried  to  prepare  his  alone. He  almost  died. He  had  an  indecent  retort  or  a  sar-  donic  grin  for  almost  everyone  but  offi-  cers.  To  them  he  presented,  as  far  as  was  feasible,  only  deep,  disrespectful  silence. We  were  a  quiet,  peculiarly  assorted  group,  and  I  guess  he  was  figuring  us  out.  One  was  a  slow-spoken,  soft-  cheeked  youth  from  a  rich  [REDACTED]  farm;  one  was  a  shiny,  cricketlike  man,  a  salesman  who  longed  to  be  an  under-  taker;  one  was  a  violinist,  unknown  then  and  now;  I  was  a  teacher.
