He  takes  the  next  one,  and  re\xc3\xa9nters  the  expressway.  He  goes  back  to  her  exit  and  down  the  ramp.  He  comes  to  a  traffic  light.  There's  no  one  there.  Did  she  mean  here?  Is  he  on  the  right  side  of  the  ex-  pressway?  He  drives  back  toward  it,  under  it,  then  another  mile.  There  are  no  traffic  lights  on  this  side.  He  must  return  to  those  he  saw.  But  again  the  place  is  deserted.  Slowly,  in  despair,  he  drives  around  looking  for  her.  He  feels  inept,  a  fool,  mad.  For  a  moment,  going  back  and  forth  -away  from  and  toward  the  traffic  lights-he  feels  dead;  perhaps  he  is  dead,  died  instantly  in  an  accident,  died  without  knowing  it,  and  now  he  is  a  ghost,  condemned  to  drive  on  forever.  But  no-this  is  thecar  he  rented.  He  stops.  It's  almost  eleven-  nearly  two  hours  since  he  called  her.  He  must  find  a  phone  and  call  her  again.  This  direction  leads  back  to  the  expressway.  He  brakes.  He  must  back  up  to  the  traffic  light  to  turn.  As  he  does  so,  looking  back  through  the  rear  window,  he  sees  a  figure.  Per-  haps  that  person  will  tell  him  how  to  find  her  street.  It  can't  be  far.  Or  a  telephone.  He  backs  up  carefully  and  leans  out  the  window.  She  waves.  It  is  Anna.  He  hugs  her  as  a  shipwrecked  sailor  hugs  a  life  buoy.  Worried  about  him,  she  had  gone  home  to  ask  if  any  calls  had  come  in,  then  had  returned  to  this  spot.  She  drives  ahead  of  him  slowly.  It  is  a  short  distance  to  her  home.  The  children  are  asleep.  The  house  is  per-  fectly  quiet  and  still,  but  if  he  doesn't  focus  he  sees  the  road  advancing  to-  ward  him.  Slowly,  with  a  drink  in  his  hand,  he  rests.
