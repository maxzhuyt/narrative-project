Later  on,  when  the  raids  began,  I  was  given  a  tin  hat,  which  I  loved  because  it  made  me  feel  gloriously  safe,  although  it  was  heavy  and  gave  me  a  headache  and,  before  long,  a  rash  on  my  forehead.  The  helmet  was  the  kind  that  had  been  worn  by  infantry  soldiers  in  the  First  World  War,  and  by  the  look  of  the  leather  lining  it  had  seen  a  good  deal  of  service.  I  don't  know  whywe  wives  thought  it  necessary  to  wear  trousers  for  the  all-  night  watch-the  WAAF  officers  who  worked  with  us  didn't-but  we  did  wear  them.  Mine  were  made  by  Jae-  ger,of  gray  pin-stripe  worsted,  and  I  got  a  long-sleeved  red  Viyella  shirt  with  white  polka  dots  to  wear  with  them. Wherever  she'd  just  been,  the  air  was  filled  with  Schiaparelli  Shocking,  and  she  wore  gold  chain  bracelets  from  Cartier,  which  jangled  against  the  keys  of  her  ciphering  ma-  chine  like  a  carillon  when  she  was  doing  a  message. Audrey,  small  and  dark  and  half  Spanish,  had  blue  linen  trousers  she'd  bought  to  wear  in  Cannes. The  hours  we  spent  at  work  were  trying  to  all  of  us-chiefly,  I  suppose,  because  they  were  unaccustomed  hours,  involv-  ing  frequent  night  duty,  and  we  were  short  of  both  food  and  sleep.  The  can-  teen  in  the  Hole  provided  only  biscuits  and  tea  and  Nescaf\xc3\xa9,  and  when  we  were  off  duty,  sleep-day  or  night-was  al-  ways  interrupted  by  sirens,  if  by  nothing  worse.  The  day  watches  were  made  up  of  one  WAAF  officer  and  two  wives,  the  all-night  watch  of  one  officer  and  one  wife-an  arrangement  that  seemed  sil-  ly  to  us,  inasmuch  as  the  traffic  was  heaviest  at  night.  This  was  true  because  some  commanding  officers  had  a  passion  for  sending  the  most  trivial  messages  in  cipher,  and  these,  having  no  priority,  piled  up  and  caused  congestion  at  mid-  night  and  after.  Some  of  the  messages  we  handled  were  coded  or  decoded  by  machines,  some  by  reference  to  key  ciphers  in  books.  I  found  it  more  difficult  to  work  with  the  book  ciphers  than  to  operate  the  machines,  but  the  books  gradually  fell  into  disuse.  The  machines  were  like  typewriters  that  required  you  to  press  very  hard  on  the  keys.  They  were  fas-  cinating  things,  and  exactly  how  they  worked  remained  a  mystery  to  me.  If  you  were  to  sendan  outgoing  message,  it  was  given  to  you  in  plain  English,  and  you  copied  it  into  a  logbook  in  longhand.  Then  you  put  a  sheet  of  paper  in  the  machine,  pressed  a  trigger,  and  typed  the  message.  As  you  typed,  a  tape  cov-  ered  with  gibberish  came  out  of  the  ma-  chine.  This  you  cut  up  and  pasted  on  plain  paper,  which  went  to  Signals  to  be  sent  on  its  way.  As  for  an  incoming  message,  it  was  a  sheet  covered  with  gibberish.  You  pressed  a  reversetrigger  on  the  machine  and  copied  the  gibberish,  and  tape  trickled  out  with  the  message  typed  on  it  in  plain  English,  unless  someone-you  or  Signals  or  the  cipher  girl  at  the  other  end-had  made  a  mis-  take,  and  then  it  was  a  headache  to  straighten  out  the  message.  Every  mid-  night,  a  lever  on  the  machine  was  moved  in  a  prescribed  fashion,  and  this  changed  the  mechanism  so  that  there  would  be  a  fresh  cipher  combination  for  the  next  twenty-four  hours.  The  conditions  we  worked  under  were  not  good,  and  itis  a  wonder  to  me  now  that  a  group  of  women  confined  for  long  periods  in  a  restricted  under-  ground  space  kept,  on  the  whole,  even-  tempered  and  in  good  health.  We  were' b"allotted  two  very  small  rooms  in  the  Hole,  one  for  clerical  work  and  the  other  for  the  machines.  The  ventila-  tion  was  bad,  and  what  downdraft  there  was  never  seemed  to  reach  our  two  rooms  to  clear  the  air,  though  in  winter  it  made  the  passages  very  cold.  Natural-  ly,  smoking  was  forbidden.  Sixty-seven  steps  led  to  the  Hole  from  ground  level.  Going  down  was  like  de-  scending  into  the  bowels  of  a  ship,  for  there  were  gurgling  noises  issuing  from  pipes,  and  a  stuffy  smell  of  paint  and  close  air.  The  Operations  Room  was  no  legitimate  concern  of  ours,  but  the  one  of  us  whose  turn  it  was  to  go  to  the  can-  teen  always  went  the  longest  way,  which  took  her  past  the  Ops  Room  door,  to  have  a  peep.  You  could  tell  by  the  degree  of  tension  on  the  faces  of  the  officers  up  on  the  dais-some-  times  my  husband  would  be  there-and  the  degree  of  concentration  of  the  WAAF  girls  with  earphones  and  crou-  piers'  rakes  around  the  plotting  table  how  the  raids  were  going  that  night.  Besides  the  regular  officers  of  the  R.A.F.,  there  were  volunteers  from  all  walks  of  life-actors,  writers,  members  of  Parliament,  peers,  barristers  work-  ing  at  Bentley  Priory.  And  there  were,  of  course,  the  WAAF  girls  (whom  die-  hards  called  &quot;that  monstrous  regi-  ment&quot;),  their  femininity  bursting  out  of  their  ugly,  ill-fitting  uniforms;  the  girls  giggled  or  wept  when  bawled  at  by  officers  used  to  men  as  subordinates,  and  they  ran  down  the  passages  to  go  on  duty  like  a  hockey  team  going  onto  the  field.  The  majority  of  them  had  had  a  gentle  upbringing,  and  some  had  come  straight  from  school.  They  were  living  in  Spartan  conditions,  sleeping  ten  to  a  room  in  empty  houses  that  had  been  commandeered  for  hostels  and  fur-  nished  solely  with  iron  cots  and  tables  and  wooden  chairs.  During  the  hard  winter  of  the  phony  war,  when  for  weeks  drains  and  bath  pipes  in  the  hos-  tels  were  frozen,  some  of  the  girls  came  to  our  house  for  a  hot  bath  and  tea  be-  side  a  fire,  and,  when  spring  arrived,  to  sit  in  the  garden  and  relax.  I  was  sorry  for  them,  and  their  comfortless  exist-" b"ence  made  me  feel  inadequate  and,  by  comparison,  pampered.  Going  on  duty  at  night,  I  always  walked  through  the  house,  instead  of  picking  my  way  over  the  mud  and  rubble  to  the  covered  path.  I  think  it  was  in  the  vast,  marble-floored  hall-  bare,  damp,  dirty,  and  penetratingly  cold,  ill-lit  by  weak  naked  bulbs  where  the  once  magnificent  staircase  rose  up  into  darkness,  that  I  felt  most  strongly  that  none  of  what  was  going  on  was  real,  and  that  I  must  be  among  ghosts,  returned  and  bewildered. He  was  of  medium  height,  gray-haired  and  slightly  built,  and  in  his  long  career  in  the  R.A.F.  he  had  acquired  the  nickname  of  Stuffy. He  had  been  for  many  years  a  widower.  His  only  son  was  a  fighter  pilot,  and  his  stepdaughter,  Brenda,  worked  as  a  WAAF  cipher  officer  at  Bentley  Priory  all  the  time  I  was  there.  Quiet  and  withdrawn,  he  carried  his  natural  modesty  almost  to  the  point  of  shyness,  and  never  surrounded  himself  with  pomp  or  scurrying  aides. Per-  haps  it  was  easy  for  me  to  feel  the  un-  reality  of  the  present  because  at  that  time  the  faraway  past  of  the  neighbor-  hood  and  of  those  who  had  lived  in  it  and  made  history  of  their  own-the  Duke  of  Chandos,  Handel,  the  Mar-  quess  of  Abercorn-seemed  especially  real,  especially  close. We  understood  that  the  war  would  last  a  long  time,  and  that  there  would  come  an  inevitable  separation.  We  were  not  wrong. I  should  in  any  case  have  left  about  then,  because  the  day  of  wives  employed  as  cipher  clerks  was  coming  to  an  end.  We  had  filled  the  gap,  and  now  the  WAAF  officers  were  to  take  over  en-  tirely,  which  was  much  better,  since  they  were  subject  to  military  discipline  and  we  were  not.  But  though  we  could  have  stayed  away  from  our  appointed  watch  any  time  we  liked,  none  of  us  ever  did.  We  could  have  gone  home  early  or  arrived  late,  but  we  didn't.  I  handed  in  my  pass  and  it  wasn't  even  glanced  at,  so  what  I  had  done  with  the  photograph  was  not  discov-  ered.  The  sergeant  who  took  the  gas  mask  looked  at  the  nozzle  and  said,  &quot;Fat  lot  ofuse  this  would  have  been  to  you!&quot;  I  dearly  wanted  to  keep  the  tin  hat,  but  that  I  handed  over,  too.  Commissions  in  the  WAAF  were  offered" b"to  all  of  us,  provided  we  were  medically  fit,  but  I  decided  not  to  take  one.  I  wanted  to  be  with  my  husband  as  long  as  I  could,  and  I  thought  that  if  he  went  abroad  I  would  find  other  work,  and,  after  he  had  gone,  I  did.  ORE  than  fourteen  years  were  to  pass  before  I  saw  Bentley  Priory  again.  When  I  went  there,  for  a  cock-  tail  party,  it  was  in  May,  very  near  the  anniversary  of  the  day  King  Leopold  went  out  of  the  war.  We  were  the  guests  of  a  friend  of  my  husband's  who  had  stayed  with  us  in  Edgware,  and  with  whom  I  had  driven  home  from  Bentley  Priory  one  particularly  noisy  night.  But  now  his  official  car,  taking  us  back  over  the  same  route  up  Stanmore  Hill,  was  shiny  and  new-not  an  old,  camou-  flaged  bone-shaker.  The  evening  was  a  beautiful  one,  and  the  lilac  was  out,  and  the  pink  and  white  hawthorn,  too.  The  leaves  of  the  beech  trees  were  a  tender  green,  and  all  the  gardens  we  passed  were  gay  with  flowers-lupines  and  red  peonies  and  masses  of  rhododendrons,  red  and  mauve,  which  flourish  well  in  that  soil.  Bentley  Priory  is  still  the  headquar-  ters  of  Fighter  Command,  and  that  evening  I  imagined  that  the  grounds  would  have  been  tidied  up  by  then:  the  huts  removed,  perhaps,  if  they  were  no  longer  needed;  grass  sown  again  over  the  tragic  place  where  the  Czech  bomb-  er  crashed;  the  mud  and  rubble  flat-  tened;  the  rats  exterminated;  and  some  of  the  camouflage  paint  scraped  off  the  front  of  the  mansion,  eliminating  the  black  rivers-though  it  has  proved  to  be  an  almost  impossible  task  to  remove  that  wartime  camouflage  from  brick  build-  ings.  So  I  expected  the  place  to  be  changed  for  the  better,  but  I  was  total-  ly  unprepared  for  what  I  saw.  When  we  had  passed  the  guard  at  the  main  gate  and  were  going  up  the  drive,  our  host  said  to  me,  &quot;Shut  your  eyes,  and  open  them  when  I  tell  you.&quot;  And  when  he  told  me  to  open  them,  I  caught  my  breath  and  could  not  speak,  for  I  saw  the  beautiful  house  that  Sir  John  Soane  designed  as  it  must  have  looked,  except  forthe  center  tower,  when  the  first  Marquess  lived  there.  Elegance  has  returned  to  Bentley  Priory.  It  has  been  restored  to  its  for-  mer  beauty  and  splendor,  outside  and  in.  It  is  also  a  shrine  of  remembrance  now,  because  in  the  circular  music  room  hang  the  portraits  of  the  famous  pilots  whom  Churchill  called  &quot;the  few,&quot;  and  in  a  glass  case  in  the  hall  is  an  illumi-  nated  replica,  in  miniature,  of  the  Bat-  tle  of  [REDACTED]  Window  in  Westminster " b"Abbey.  The  grand  staircase  is  car-  peted,  and  the  balustrade  picked  out  with  gold  leaf,  as  it  was  long  ago,  when  elegant  hands  rested  there-a  lady's  or  a  Regency  fop's.  Later,  I  went  out  onto  the  terrace,  and  one  of  the  pilots  of  theBattle  went  with  me.  I  looked  at  the  lovely  lawns  sloping  down  to  the  woods,  and  at  the  view  of  distant  Harrow  Hill,  and,over  my  shoulder,  at  the  house  itself,  cream- colored,  stately,  standing  there  immeas-  urably  secure  in  the  soft  evening  light,  and  I  felt  quite  moved  to  think  that  out  of  so  much  neglect  and  decay  and  destruction  this  restoration  of  what  Lady  Blessington  called  &quot;the  most  sin-  gular  place  on  earth&quot;  had  emerged.  I  said  something  about  the  contrast  and  about  having  known  it  in  1940,  and  my  companion  said,  &quot;Oh,  were  you  in  the  WAAF,  then?&quot;  I  said  I  was  not,  and  told  him  what  I  had  been  doing  there,  and  he  said  he  had  forgotten  that  some  of  the  wives  did  that  job.It  did  not  surprise  me,  for  who  now  except  the  wives  would  re-  member?  It  was  so  long  ago.  &quot;Strange  times,&quot;  I  heard  him  say,  &quot;and  no  doubt  for  you  a  strange  experience.&quot;  We  do  not  like  growing  old,  or  being  reminded  that  we  are  doing  so  by  the  sight  of  youthful  men  in  uniform.  But  in  Norwich  Cathedral,  on  this  latest  an-  niversary  of  the  Battle  of  [REDACTED],  I  was  very  glad  that  I  had  not  missed  those  times,  and  that  I  had  worked  in  &quot;the  most  singular  place&quot;  during  a  period  that  is  now,  with  the  more  distant  past,  a  part  of  its  history."
