CHILD  LABOR  AND  THE  PRESBYTERIANS  N  view  of  the  fact  that  my  kindly  I  Presbyterian  clergyman  father  quit  preaching  from  pulpits  not  long  after  I,  his  fifth  and  youngest  child,  was  born,  in  order  to  devote  the  rest  of  his  life  to  pushing  anti-child-labor  legisla-  tion  through  Congress,  it  seems  rankly  paradoxical  to  me,  when  I  look  back,  that  my  own  labors  as  a  child  were  pro-  digious,  involuntary,  and  underpaid.  I  feel  that  I  was  made  to  work  like  a  dog  from  the  age  of  five  or  six  onward,  and  that  it  was  slave  labor,  forI  got  no  com-  pensation  other  than  my  weekly  allow-  ance.  This  amounted  to  five  cents  in  my  early  years,  and  although  it  was  gradually  raised  to  the  absolute  ceiling  of  one  dollar  a  week  by  the  time  I  was  fourteen  years  old  and  a  freshman  in  high  school,  one  couldn't  really  do  much  with  a  dollar  even  during  the  deflationary  administration  of  President  William  Howard  Taft.  It  was  this  Chief  Executive  who  signed  one  of  the  first  of  the  anti-child-labor  bills,  at  the  White  House,  with  a  gold  pen,  in  the  presence  of  an  anti-child-labor  group  that  included  my  father,  my  mother,  and  me.  In  a  photograph  of  this  cere-  mony,  which  I  still  possess,  I  am  not  sur-  prised  to  note  that,  for  a  theoretically  carefree  and  fun-loving  five-year-old,  I  am  looking  glum.  The  Presbyterian  multitude  I  grew  up  under  consisted  of  my  father  and  mother,  four  uncles,  four  aunts,  and  dozens  of  grown  first  cousins-many  of  them,  like  my  father  and  the  uncles,  Presbyterian  clergymen.  Everybody  ex-  cept  the  preachers  relaxed,  more  or  less,  on  Sundays,  and  nobody  worked  ex-  cept-during  mealtime-our  Scottish  |  Other-  &amp;_  housekeeper,  Miss  [REDACTED].  wise,  all  the  adults  worked  from  morn-  ing  till  nightevery  day,  and  insisted  that  the  children  be  raised  in  a  fashion  de-  signed  to  render  them  similarly  obsessed.  The  adults  were  convinced  that  work  was  habit-forming.  The  general  idea  was  that  a  child  would  be  given  small  doses  of  work  when  he  was  still  in  romp-  ers  so  that  he  would  eventually  become  a  helpless  work  addict  like  the  Old  Tes-  tament  laborers,  from  Adam  and  Eve  onward,  whom  these  Presbyterians  ad-  mired  and  emulated.  They  believed,  to  be  sure,  that  children  should  also  do  a  certain  amount  of  playing  on  weekdays,  but  they  couldn't  bear  to  see  a  child  do-  ing  nothing.  Like  most  children,  I  en-  joyed  daydreaming,  and  this  delightful  pastime  was  continually  being  inter-  rupted  by  some  grown  Presbyterian.  Finding  me  sitting  or  standing  at  a  window  doing  nothing,  my  mother  would  either  think  of  some  work  for  me  to  do  or,  if  she  couldn't  find  anything  in  that  category,  she  would  say,  &quot;For  goodness'  sake,  Son,  go  out  and  play!&quot;  Although  I  despised  work,  I  liked  many  forms  of  play,  but  there  were  times  when  I  wished  neither  to  play  nor  to  work.  And  that  was  known  as  idleness,  which,  like  so  many  other  things,  was  a  sin.  Since  I  didn't  become  very  proficient  at  any  kind  of  work  until  I  was  seven  or  eight  and  could  help  dry  dishes,  chop  kindling  wood  for  the  fireplace  and  for  the  coal-burning  kitchen  stove  and  the  coal-burning  furnace  in  the  cellar,  polish  silver,  or  something  equally  degrading,  my  earliest  job  around  the  house  was  that  of  running  errands.  At  five,  worn  out  by  a  day  at  kindergarten  and  as  much  outdoor  and  more  or  less  super-  vised  play  as  I  could  put  up  with,  I  couldn't  sit  down  for  more  than  five  minutes  at  a  time  without  being  drafted  for  at  least  an  indoor  errand.  My  busy  mother  ranged  over  our  rented,  three-  story,  red  brick  home  at  2805  P  Street,  in  Georgetown,  like  a  bee,  or  like  the  first  mate  of  a  ship,  and  she  was  inces-  santly  in  need  of  things  that  were  up-  stairs  when  she  was  downstairs,  or  vice  versa.  &quot;What  are  you  doing,  Son?&quot;  she  would  call  if  I  was  not  in  sight.  &quot;Nothing,&quot;  I  would  truthfully  reply,  not  having  yet  become  proficient  at  de-  ceit.  &quot;Well,&quot;  she  would  say,  &quot;run  up  to  my  room  and  see  if  I  left  a  ball  of  gray  yarn  up  there  with  my  knitting  needles  stuck  in  it.&quot;  If  my  mother  couldn't  find  any  other  work  for  her  own  hands  to  do  around  the  house,  she  would  knit.  Woollen  yarn  of  one  kind  or  another  played  a  not  unimportant  part  in  my  early  labors.  Before  I'd  got  the  hang  of  the  walking,  running,  and  climbing  that  was  necessary  in  order  for  me  to  do  in-  door  errands,  I  was  frequently  called  upon  to  hold  a  hank  of  yarn  in  my  outstretched  hands  while  my  mother  wound  it  into  a  ball.  I  can  think  of  noth-  ing  that  could  make  a  boy  feel  sillier  than  this  job  except  the  job  of  knitting  itself.  And  I  was  made  to  knit.  This  is  a  secret  I've  guarded  rather  closely,  but  there  comes  a  time  in  a  man's  life  when  keep-  ing  a  personal  secret  is  hardly  worth  the  effort;  and,  in  at  least  some  ways,  I  am  opposed  to  effort.  But  I  was  forced  by  schoolteachers  to  knit  my  way  through  the  early  part  of  the  First  World  War,  during  the  administration  of  President  Woodrow  Wilson,  who  was  even  more  vehemently  against  child  labor  than  President  Taft  had  been.  I  and  other  boys  my  age,  as  well    as  girls,  knitted  squares  of  gray  yarn  which,  according  to  the  principal  of  my  grammar  school  on  R  Street,  would  eventually  be  sewn  together  and  made  into  blankets-or,  as  my  mother  called  them,  &quot;comforters&quot;-for  the  use  ofold-  er  boys  who  were  getting  ready  to  go  to  [REDACTED]  and  fight  in  the  trenches.  The  fact  that  young  boys  as  well  as  girls  were  forced  to  knit  these  squares  during  school  hours  only  goes  to  indicate  the  in-  tensity  that  patriotic  frenzy  can  reach  in  time  of  war;  and  when  people  talk  to  me  these  days  about  the  horrors  of  a  thermonuclear  conflict,  I  am  not  sur-  prised  to  find  myself  shrugging  as  if  to  indicate  that  I've  known  worse  things  than  sudden  death.  HEN  I  was  six,  and  began  to  work  from  nine  until  three  in  the  first  grade  of  the  grammar  school,  out-  door  errands  were  added  to  the  indoor  errands  I  was  called  upon  to  do.  Initial-  ly,  in  this  field  of  endeavor,  I  encoun-  tered  some  good  luck.  My  very  first  outdoorerrand  was  to  trudge  from  T  wenty-eighth  and  P  Street,  where  we  lived,  to  the  manse  occupied  by  one  of  my  several  Presbyterian  clergyman  uncles,  my  Uncle  John  Roseboro,  and  ask  him  for  a  certain  newly  published  Greek  pamphlet  my  father  was  anxious  to  read.  The  manse  and  the  church  were  two  blocks  up  P  Street,  and  I  thus  had  to  cross  Twenty-ninth  Street  to  get  there.  I  was  given  explicit  instructions  by  my  mother  about  stopping,  looking,  and  listening  before  crossing  that  street,  but  I  was  nevertheless  run  over  by  a  man  on  a  bicycle-a  Mr.  Dodds.  Al-  though  Dodds  was  worse  off  after  this  accident  than  I  was,  he  having  gone  heels  over  head  over  his  handlebars,  whereas  I  was  only  run  over  by  his  rub-  ber  tires,  he  delivered  me  to  my  destina-  tion,  wiping  blood  off  his  forehead  and  limping.  There  a  great  fuss  was  made  over  me  by  the  Roseboros,  and  I  was  de-  livered  back  to  my  home  again  by  my  Uncle  John,  along  with  the  Greek  pamphlet. My  mother  would  follow,  or  sometimes  walk  abreast  of  him,  got  up  in  equally  formal  wear,  with  a  big  hat  and  prob-  ably  a  veil. My  older  brothers  were  stiffly  dressed  in  their  best  suits,  and  my  young  sister  and  I,  at  the  tail  ofthe  line,  were  similarly  uncomfortable-she  in  a  long  dress  and  I  in  a  Buster  Brown  col-  lar,  short  pants,  buttoned-up  jacket,  black  stockings  with  elastic  bands  hold-  ing  them  up,  and  my  valuable  small  feet  in  black  shoes.  After  the  walk,  we  could  relax  and  listen  to  my  father  read  Dickens  and  Mark  Twain  and  Jules  Verne  out  loud.  But,  for  some  reason,  nobody  tried  to  get  out  of  the  Sun-  day  clothes,  which  stayed  on  the  vari-  ous  bodies  until  bedtime.  'The  parade  was  frequently  enlarged  by  visiting  un-  cles,  cousins,  and  nomad  Presbyterian  preachers  who  were  in  W  ashington  to  go  sightseeing  and  eat  the  bought  chickens  that  Miss  [REDACTED]  cooked  for  those  Sunday  dinners.  By  the  time  I  was  fourteen,  I  was  hopelessly  addicted  to  work,  but  I  had  acquired  ideas  about  money.  I  quit  formal  education  forever  that  year,  got  a  job  as  an  office  boy  on  a  newspaper  at  fifteen  dollars  a  week,  and  began  to  spend  money.  I  have  been  spending  it  ever  since.
