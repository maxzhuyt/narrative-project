 A  ROLE  IN  MANILA  NLY  once  in  my  life  have  I  O  played  thepartof  a  hero.  I  did  so  deliberately,  and  at  the  same  time  quite  by  accident,  and  the  mem-  ory  of  that  performance  and  that  day  still  returns,  after  nearly  a  dozen  years,  to  fill  me  with  uneasiness  and  guilt.  It  was  soon  after  [REDACTED]  had  been  liberated  from  the  Japanese.  I  was  a  gunnery  officer  on  a  destroyer  that  stood  anchored  in  the  dirty,  silt-brown,  choppy  water  where  the  Pasig  River  pours  into  [REDACTED]  [REDACTED].  A  Reserve  officer,  I  had  been  in  the  Navy  five  years  and  was  profoundly  ignorant  of  all  naval  matters  except  gunnery  and  destroyers.  I  hung  over  the  ship's  rail  that  day,  watching  stores  being  brought  aboard,  and  occasionally  looking  along  the  shore  at  the  big,  spare,  beautiful  apartment  houses.  'They  had  flickers  of  black  above  each  window,  indicating  that  they  had  been  gutted  by  fire  during  the  fighting.  A  sailor  tapped  me  on  the  shoulder  and  said  the  captain  wanted  to  see  me  on  the  bridge.  &quot;Burdick,  you're  going  to  be  shore-  patrol  officer  this  afternoon,&quot;  the  cap-  tain  said.  He  was  young,  confident,  and  Annapolis-trained.  He  smiled.  &quot;'Take  two  men  and  hit  the  beach  around  thir-  teen  humdred.  Liberty  starts  then  for  all  ships  in  the  harbor.&quot;  &quot;Captain,  I've  never  done  shore-  patrol  duty,&quot;  I  said.  I  had  seen  shore-  patrol  officers  with  S.P.  brassards  on  their  arms,  sticks  in  their  hands,  and  45s  buckled  to  their  waists,  and  I  had  assumed  they  were  civilian  policemen  specially  recruitedfor  that  type  of  work.  Like  all  Navy  men,  I  instinctively  dis-  trusted  them.  &quot;Maybe  one  of  the  other  officers  has  had  shore-patrol  work  be-  fore,&quot;  I  suggested.  &quot;No,  Burdick.  You.  Alphabetical  order,  and  you're  first.&quot;  The  captain  smiled  again.  &quot;Get  two  men.  Report  to  the  senior  shore-patrol  officer  on  the  dock,.  There've  been  so  many  fights,  drunks,  and  general  trouble  that  the  regular  shore  patrol  needs  extra  help.  Take  along  a  .45.&quot;  I  went  over  the  ship's  roster  care-  fully,  and  picked  the  two  oldest  and  strongest  chief  petty  officers.  Both  hadhad  long  duty  in  the  Regular  Navy  and  I  knew  they  had  done  shore  patrol  in  ports  all  over  the  world.  One  was  Chief  Seed,  a  boatswain.  He  was  quiet,  scrawny,  but  very  powerful.  The  other  was  Chief  Trainor,  a  big,  pot-  bellied  signalman.  He  looked  exactly  like  a  medieval  friar  who  had  been   stripped  of  his  habit  and  clapped  into  a  chief's  uniform.  His  pate  was  bald,  with  a  thick  fringe  of  hair  around  it.  When  he  semaphored,  it  was  a  de-  light  to  watch.  He  wedged  his  belly  against  the  box  in  which  the  signal  flags  were  stored,  sethis  legs  apart,  and  wig-  wagged,  the  flags  moving  in  swift,  pre-  cise  motions  that  heightened  the  im-  pression  that  he  was  a  friar  engaged  in  a  ritual.  The  two  chiefs  and  I  went  ashore  and  reported  for  duty  to  the  senior  shore-patrol  officer,  who  was  a  lieuten-  ant  commander. I  was  on  my  third  doughnut,  and  be-  ginning  to  soften  toward  the  duty,  From  the  street  rose  a  sound  that  was  human  but  had  all  the  alarm  and  tension  of  a  siren.  It  was  small,  at  first,  shrill  and  distant,  but  in  a  few  seconds  it  grew  loud,  and  suddenly  it  was  in  the  U.S.0.  building  itself.  It  came  from  the  mouth  of  a  very  agitated  Filipino.  He  tore  through  the  building  at  top  speed,  his  eyes  bulg-  ing,  and  when  he  saw  the  brassard  on  my  arm,  words  rushed  out  of  him-  Tagalog,  English,  pidgin  English,  and  Spanish.  I  could  understand  none  of  it.  Chief  Trainor  took  him  by  the  arm  and  shook  him  hard,  and  at  once  his  words  came  out  smoothly  and  urgently,  all  English.  HE  Filipino  owned  a  dance  hall.  A  clean,  decent,  respectable  dance  hall,  the  largest  in  [REDACTED],  he  said.  Be-  cause  of  an  argument  over  a  girl,  sev-  eral  hundred  Marines  were  about  to  fight  several  hundred  Filipinos.  The  gutters  would  run  with  blood-and,  he  added,  the  most  beautiful  window  in  [REDACTED]  would  be  broken.  &quot;We  better  go,  Mr.  Burdick,&quot;  Chief  Seed  said.  &quot;There's  probably  only  a  half-dozen  people  down  there,  but  we  better  see.&quot;  The  chiefs  were  calm,  and  the  U.S.O.  girls  watching  the  scene  smiled  at  them  and  at  me  with  confidence  and  something  like  respect,  so  I  was  calm.  As  the  four  of  us  swung  out  the  door,  I  slapped  the  .45  on  my  hip  with  a  casual,  practiced  hand  for  the  benefit  of  the  girls.  The  Filipino  talked  frenziedly  as  we  trotted  along  the  street.  He  was  wear-  ing  long,  pointed  shoes,  a  pin-striped  suit  with  trousers  that  came  up  to  just  below  his  armpits,  a  shirt  of  soft  yel-  low,  and  a  black  bow  tie.  He  wanted  us  to  hurry  and  was  too  polite  to  de-  mand  it,  so  he  flew  around  us  as  we   went,  swooping  and  circling  in  a  des-  perate  mute  appeal  forspeed.  We  went  up  a  small  hill,  and  then  the  street  dropped  down  in  front  of  us  and  I  could  see  the  whole  scene  clearly.  Most  of  thebuildings  in  that  area  had  been  ruined  in  the  last  days  of  the  fight  ing;  there  were  black  smears  around  their  windows,  the  roofs  had  collapsed,  and  torn  wiring  and  bits  of  char,  cloth,  and  splintered  wood  lay  spilling  out  of  their  doors.  But  the  dance  hall  was  intact-a  three-story  building,  with  the  dance  floor  on  the  second.  The  windows  up  there  were  jammed  with  girls  looking  down  into  the  street.  And  the  Filipino  had  exaggerated  less  thanChief  Seed  thought.  There  were  almost  a  hundred  Marines,  soldiers,  and  sailors  drawn  up  into  a  clot  in  the  street,  and  they  faced  a  slightly  larger  clot  of  Filipinos.  We  paused  for  a  moment.  The  two  groups  looked  like  those  pseudopodal  objects  that  one  sees  under  microscopes.  They  were  homogeneous  but  made  up  of  tiny  units.  'They  roiled  slowly  and  heaved,  and  each  of  them  seemed  to  have  a  sort  of  membrane  that  held  it  together.  Each  putout  a  pro-  trusion,  like  a  smallneck,  and  the  two  necks  almost  touched,  but  they  were  not  yet  in  conflict.  The  Ameri-  can  clot  was  blue  and  white  and  khaki-  colored.  The  Filipino  clot  was  more  diverse,  and  a  fringe  of  ragged  boys  had  gathered  behind  it.  &quot;Hurry,  Captain,  hurry,&quot;  the  dance-hall  owner  said.  I  had  already  told  him  twice  I  was  not  a  captain.  &quot;The  window  will  be  broken.&quot;  On  the  ground  floor  ofthe  building  was  a  huge  plate-glass  window,  intact.  How  it  had  survived  the  fighting  I  can-  not  guess,  but  clearly  it  was  the  owner's     most  prized  possession.  Shining  and  very  clean,  it  dominated  the  entire  drab  street.  Behind  it  shimmered  colorful  life-size  pictures  of  the  dance-hall  girls.  I  knew  it  would  never  survive  the  mass  fight  that  was  brewing.  Some-  one  would  throw  a  fist,  a  rock,  or  an  opponent  through  it.  I  glanced  at  the  two  chiefs.  Their  faces  seemed  expressionless,  even  bored.  I  surmised  that  this  was  not  an  unusual  situation.  I  went  down  the  hill  toward  the  dance  hall,  pushed  along  the  side-  walk  there,  and  came  into  the  clear  area  where  the  necks  of  the  two  clots  almost  touched.  The  tip  of  the  Ameri-  can  clot  was  a  Marine  corporal.  He  was  big,  and  his  head  was  bent  forward.  He  was  speaking  in  a  low  voice,  making  his  words  very  clear.  &quot;Look,  if  a  girl  comes  in  a  dance  hall  she's  anybody's,  see?&quot;&quot;  he  said.  His  eyes  were  red.  A  flat  white  bottle  stuck  out  of  his  hip  pocket.  It  was  [REDACTED]  whiskey-cheap,  raw  bootleg  so  bad  that  the  American  authorities  had  begun  publishinga  daily  list  of  the  menwho  had  gone  blind  from  it.  The  Filipino  at  the  tip  of  the  other  mass  listened  silently.  His  expression  did  not  change,  but  once  or  twice  his  right  hand  flicked  over  his  chest  and  then  snapped  back  to  his  side  in  a  gesture  so  intense  and  threatening  that  it  made  my  stomach  tighten.  The  two  clots  wriggled,  made  encouraging  sounds,  surged  forward,  and  then  fell  back.  I  walked  over  to  the  leaders.  &quot;Look,  Corporal,  you're  creating  a  public  nuisance  here,&quot;  I  said  firmly.  &quot;Now  shove  off,  and  I  won't  arrest  you.  We'll  just  forget  it.&quot;  The  Marine  turned  his  head  slowly  and  looked  at  me.  His  red-rimmed  eyes  were  hard,  and  I  knew  instantly  that  he  was  impervious  to  both  authority  and  reason.  He  smiled  a  tight,  tough  smile  and  shook  his  head.  &quot;Lieutenant,  you  just  run  along  and  you  won't  get  hurt,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;'This  is  private.  You  try  to  get  tough  and  you'll  be  mobbed.  You  just  amble-go  along.&quot;  I  shivered,  or  felt  that  I  did.  I  knew  what  had  happened.  I  had  already  ex-  hausted  my  authority.  I  had  depended  on  my  insignia  and  the  chiefs',  and  on  the  Marine's  training  and  military  disci-  pline,  and  none  of  them  had  worked.  I  felt  cold,  as  if  I  had  been  literally  de-  frocked.  At  the  same  time,  I  became  aware  of  something  else:  the  air  be-  tween  the  two  clots  was  so  dense  it  actually  seemed  to  smell  of  menace.  Patience  and  time  were  running  out,  and  in  a  moment  the  two  masses  would  surge  together.  'The  protrusions  would  snap  at  each  other,  and  then  the  clots  would  break  their  membranes  and  swirl  together  in  rage.  Distinctly,  and  for  the  first  time,  I  realized  that  this  was  dangerous-that  someone  might  even  be  killed.  &quot;I'm  not  fooling,  Corporal,&quot;  I  said,  and  my  voice  came  out  scratchy  and  uncertain.  &quot;You  shove  off.&quot;  He  grinned  wickedly,  with  utter  con-  fidence,  and  turned  back  to  the  Filipino.  He  started  to  utter  precise,  low-pitched  words  again.  I  looked  at  the  two  chiefs.  Their  faces  were  still  expressionless,  but  as  they  caught  my  eye  they  both  grinned  a  little.  Chief  Trainor  shook  his  head  slightly,  as  if  at  a  puzzling  joke.  &quot;'The  gook  has  got  a  knife  in  his  coat,&quot;  Chief  Seed  said  quietly  to  me.  &quot;When  he  takes  it  out,  the  fight  will  start.&quot;  I  became  aware  that  between  my  shoulder  blades  was  a  hot,  itching  spot,  and  I  felt  as  if  a  huge  drop  of  sweat  were  being  squeezed  out  of  it.  Chief  Seed  winked  at  me  and  stepped  back,  and  I  felt  a  sudden  relief.  The  chiefs  had  been  trying  to  tell  me  something.  The  wink  was  a  signal,  and  it  called  a  blurred  but  perfectly  recognizable  pic-  ture  to  the  top  of  my  mind.  The  picture  was  a  montage  of  any  number  of  mov-  ies  I  had  seen.  In  its  foreground  were  a  good  tough  man  and  his  two  friends,  and  facing  them  were  a  bad  tough  man  and  As  friends.  I  knew  what  to  do.  I  winked  at  the  two  chiefs.  Their  grins  vanished,  their  faces  became  expressionless  again,  and  I  turned  back  to  the  Marine,  utterly  assured.  &quot;All  right,  Corporal,  I  gave  you  your  chance,&quot;  I  said,  and  my  voice  was  as  tough  as  his.  He  looked  at  me  quickly,  with  sur-  prise.  I  smiled,  confident  because  of  the  chiefs'  grins  and  Chief  Seed's  wink,  and  because  of  the  after-image  of  the  black-and-white  picture  still  in  my  mind's  eye.  Calmly  I  undid  the  button  of  the  flap  that  held  my  .45  in  its  hol-  ster.  Deliberately  I  took  the  gun  out.  I  whipped  it  up  and  brought  it  down  flat  against  the  side  of  the  corporal's  head.  In  just  the  way  I  had  known  it  would  happen,  his  eyes  rolled  up,  his  mouth  went  open,  and  he  collapsed,  falling  slowly  to  the  ground.  He  was  unconscious  before  he  hit  it,  but  I  had  already  seen  the  immense  astonishment  on  his  face.  I  moved  forward  and  swung  the  gun  again,  but  this  time  I  was  experi-  enced  and  steadier.  The  Filipino's  hand,  reaching  for  his  knife,  stopped  &amp;  with  its  fingers  just  at  his  lapel,  and    he,  too,  fell  to  the  ground  unconscious.  I  shifted  the  gun  in  my  hand,  held  it  at  hip  level,  and  flicked  off  the  safety.  I  swung  the  muzzle  in  a  casual  half  arc  toward  the  Marines.  &quot;All  right,  now.  Clear  this  street,&quot;  I  said  crisply.  &quot;In  thirty  seconds,  clear  the  street  or  ...&quot;  I  moved  the  gun  menacingly.  The  Americans  stared.  They  were  not  frightened,  but  immediately  there  was  the  sound  of  feet  shuffling.  Men  moved  backward  out  of  the  clot;  the  membrane  broke  and  they  became  sol-  diers  and  sailors  and  Marines  again,  drifting  off  down  the  street.  I  smiled,  and  it  was  the  easy,  con-  fident  smile  of  the  Western  sheriff  halt-  ing  a  lynching  mob  at  the  door  of  his  jail.  I  turned  to  the  Filipinos.  This  time,  I  did  not  even  speak.  I  merely  waved  the  gun.  The  Filipinos  pushed  back,  and  the  clot  of  them  dissolved,  and  in  thirty  seconds  the  street  was  empty  except  for  the  two  unconscious  men  at  my  feet,  the  chiefs,  the  dance-  hall  owner,  and  the  girls  watching  us  from  the  windows.  I  looked  up  at  them,  stuck  the  gun  back  in  the  holster,  and  closed  the  flap.  I  took  outa  pack-  age  of  cigarettes  and  lit  one,  and  held  the  pack  out  to  the  chiefs.  &quot;Jesus,  Mr.  Burdick,  that  was  close,&quot;  Chief  Trainor  said.  His  voice  was  parched  and  tight.  &quot;I  never  saw  a  crowd  as  mean  as  that  one.  'That  was  awful  close.&quot;  &quot;What  do  you  mean?&quot;  I  asked,  star-  ing  at  him.  &quot;Just  like  any  mob,  wasn't  (ck,  &quot;No,  it  wasn't,  Mr.  Burdick,&quot;  Chief  Seed  said.  &quot;I  never  thought  we'd  get  out  without  them  tearing  us  to  pieces.  I  was  trying  to  tell  you.  My  God,  man,  you  got  guts,  I'll  say  that!&quot;  Chief  Trainor's  face  was  pale,  and  for  the  first  time  I  noticed  that  there  was  a  sheen  of  sweat  on  the  skin  beneath  his  eyes.  His  whole  fat  body  was  trembling  in  a  fine,  almost  invisible  way.  He  nodded  agreement  with  Chief  Seed.  &quot;Awful  close,  Mr.  Burdick,&quot;  he  mut-  tered.  &quot;Awful  close.&quot;  Instantly,  I  was  racked  by  a  terrible  belated  spasm  of  fear.  The  chief's  wink  had  been  a  warning,  and  I  had  com-  pletely  misinterpreted  it.  I  had  gone  to  the  edge  of  the  abyss  and  returned,  and  now  I  realizedthat  it  had  been  an  acci-  dent.  My  hands  began  to  tremble,  and  I  dropped  the  cigarette.  I  did  notlook  at  the  chiefs.  N  a  few  minutes,  an  Army  ambu-  lance  came.  I  don't  know  who  had  sent  for  it.  I  watched  the  Marine  and  the  Filipino  hauled  away.  Then  I  went  back  to  the  dock  and  made  a  report  to  thesenior  shore-patrol  officer.  Later,  I  went  to  the  hospital  where  the  two  men  had  been  taken,  and  an  impressed  and  respectful  colonel,  a  surgeon,  told  me  that  both  of  them  would  live,  but  that  the  doctors  had  had  a  hell  of  atime  patching  up  the  Filipino.  He  laughed  softly.  &quot;You're  a  pretty  tough  S.P.  officer,  Lieutenant,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;They  don't  usually  come  in  that  badly  chewed  up.’)  I  stared  at  him  without  speaking.  I  wanted  to  see  the  two  men-to  assure  myself  that  they  were  safe  and  would  live-but  I  could  tell  from  the  sur-  geon's  eyes  that  he  would  not  under-  stand.  I  turned  away,  and  at  once  I  had  the  feeling  that  guilt  was  literally  pouring  into  me,  like  a  burning  juice  in  my  stomach.  When  I  got  back  to  the  ship,  I  found  that  Chiefs  Trainor  and  Seed  had  al-  ready  spread  word  ofthe  episode,  and  that  day,  and  all  through  the  rest  of  my  tour  of  duty  aboard  the  destroyer,  I  moved  in  an  aura  of  respect  that  made  me  uncomfortable.  The  tough  eggs,  the  rowdies  in  the  crew,  made  excuses  to  speak  to  me.  The  captain  said  nothing,  but  helooked  pleased  whenever  he  saw  me.  I  grew  unhappy  and  restless.  I  was  a  hero  who  did  not  deserve  to  be  one-  to  whom  the  memory  of  his  splendid  moment  brought  more  distaste  than  pleasure.  And  when,  finally,  the  fight-  ing  was  over  and  I  was  due  for  dis-  charge,  I  left  the  ship-and  my  role-  with  intense  relief.
