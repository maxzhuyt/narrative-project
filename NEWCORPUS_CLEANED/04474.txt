 b"IF  you  have  ever  driven  an  automo-    bile  in  New  York  City,  or  if  you  have  been  even  casually  observant  while  riding  in  a  taxicab  or  walking  along  the  sidewalks  of  the  city,  I'm  sure  you  have  seen  those  yellow,  conical  objects  that  are  placed  from  time  to  time  on  the  pavements  of  certain  streets  and  intersections  by  traffic  policemen.  They  look  like  the  castoff  hats  of  overgrown  gnomes.  They  are  about  three  feet  tall,  the  round  bottoms  have  a  diameter  of  eight  or  ten  inches,  and  the  tops  are  pointed.  In  a  cluster  of,  say,  a  couple  of  dozen  gnomes'  hats  at  a  complicated  intersection  such  as  [REDACTED]  Circle,  five  or  six  are  usually  lying  on  their  sides,  having  been  bowled  over  by  motorists  who  were  unable  or  unwilling  to  keep  from  sideswiping  them  or  run-  ning  into  them  head  on.  Everything  about  the  gnomes'-hats  system  is  in-  genious.  The  largest  cluster  of  gnomes'  hats  I  know  of  is  the  one  that  is  planted  ev-  ery  afternoon  before  the  rush  hour  on  one  of  the  main  approaches  to  the  George  [REDACTED]  [REDACTED].  You  drive  north  up  the  Henry  [REDACTED]  Parkway  (sometimes  called  the  West  Side  High-  way  ),  and  after  turning  right  at  the  sign  that  says  &quot;George  [REDACTED]  [REDACTED]&quot;  you  make  a  sharp  right  turn  and  then  a  sharp  left  turn.  Your  view  of  these  turns  is  unobstructed-in  other  words,  they  are  not  what  are  called  &quot;blind&quot;  turns-and  although  the  traffic  is  sup-  posed  to  stay  in  the  one-way  lanes,  there  are  no  gnomes'  hats  on  these  turns.  Then  you  go  uphill  for  a  while  and  then  downhill,  passing  under  the  bridge  in  order  to  be  on  the  right-hand  side  of  it  when  you  start  going  across  it  to  New  Jersey.  The  foundations  of  the  bridge  make  the  sharp  right  turn  at  the  bottom  of  this  hill  a  blind  turn,.  You  start  mak-  ing  this  turn  and  all  at  once  you  are  in  the  middle  of  the  abundant  garden  of  gnomes'  hats  to  which  I  have  referred.  Once  you  have  been  in  this  one,  other  concentrations  of  gnomes'  hats  seem  like  nothing. After  the  sharp  right  turn  that  brings  you  into  this  cluster  of  gnomes'  hats,  you  go  uphill  a  short  distance  and  try  to  go  around  another  blind  right  turn.  If  you  get  around  it,  you  are  then  on  the  bridge  and  going  toward  New  Jersey.  The  gnomes'  hats  are  planted  around  both  these  blind  turns  and  all  up  and  down  the  one-way  traffic  lanes  that  lead  from  the  lower  turn  to  the  upper  one. But  I  only  wish  to  discuss  here  the  cluster  the  policemen  plant  early  in  the  afternoon  on  ordinary  days  in  and  around  the  two  blind  curves  at  this  particular  ap-  proach  to  the  bridge.
