In  a  few  minutes,  she  discovered  that  I  had  not  matriculated  or  even  passed  my  School  Certificate,  that  I  had  hardly  been  to  school  at  all,  that  I  had  no  languages  (I  had  succeeded  in  learning  no  French),  and  could  not  even  type.  On  beingasked  what  I  had  done,  I  mentioned  the  theatre  and  play-  writing,  but  the  first  she  regarded  with  suspicion,  and  the  second  she  utterly  dis-  missed  as  an  occupation  at  all.  As  there  was  a  stream  of  candidates  behind  me,  all  looking  as  though  they  had  passed  their  School  Certificates,  she  announced  that  the  only  vacancies  in  the  Service  were  for  cooks  and  stewards,  and  that  she  did  not  think  that  I  would  be  suit-  able.  I  opened  my  mouth  to  say  that  I  could  cook,  but  her  watertight  attention  was,  I  saw  thankfully,  on  the  next  girl.  My  dislike  of  community  life  boiled  up  in  my  throat,  and  I  left.  It  seemed  too" b"much  for  the  Wrens  to  accept  me  at  such  great  mutual  sacrifice.  I  went  back  to  the  country  and  told  my  family  that  the  Wrens  did  not  seem  to  want  me  much,  and  it  was  decided  that  I  should  not  beof  use  for  anything  until  I  had  learned  to  type.  The  next  morning,  I  went  to  get  some  shoes  repaired.  'The  cobbler  worked  in  a  little  hut  at  the  end  of  the  village.  He  was  a  charming,  tragic-look-  ing  man,  and  a  very  good  cobbler.  He  was  deaf  and  dumb,  but  he,  too,  could  read  and  write,  and  he  had  a  slate  on  which  one  wrote  one's  requirements.  He  would  then  examine  the  shoes  and  tell  one  how  long  the  job  would  take  and  how  much  it  would  cost.  He  and  I,  that  morning,  stretched  our  smiles  to  embrace  the  entire  greeting,  and  then  I  wrote  my  needs-new  soles  and  heels-  on  his  slate.  He  turned  my  shoes  absent-  ly  in  his  hands-he  was  not  really  look-  ing  at  them-and  suddenly  seized  the  slate,  wrote  furiously  upon  it,  and  pushed  it  into  my  hands.  It  read,  &quot;You  lovely  girl  like  princess  me  like  marry  you.&quot;  Then  there  was  a  space,  and  then    I  looked  up.  His  face  was  lit  with  intention.  He  pushed  the  pencil  into  my  hand  and  took  the  slate  and  carefully  rubbed  it  clean  for  my  reply.  I  wrote,  &quot;You  very  kind.  Cannot  marry  any-  body  must  learn  typing  in  [REDACTED]  for  war.&quot;  He  read  it  like  lightning,  but  his  face  changed  only  slowly,  like  the  sun  going  in,  Then  he  shrugged  his  shoulders  gently  and  wrote,  &quot;Tuesday  12/6.  Don't  get  bombed.&quot;  I  wrote,  &quot;Thank  you  will  try  not  to  get  bombed.&quot;  He  smiled  with  great  sadness,  and  underneath  my  writing  put,  &quot;Goodbye  Goodbye,&quot;  and  I  stroked  the  words  on  the  slate  with  my  fingers  to  say  it  back.  Walking  home,  I  saw  how  little  I  knew.  And  it  was  suddenly  clear  to  me  how  little  I  understood  of  anything  that  I  thought  I  knew.  Even  being  able  to  type  would  not  have  helped  me  particu-  larly  with  the  cobbler  and  his  feelings,  which  either  meant  that  education  as  I  had  thought  of  it  wasn't  education  at  all,  or  that,  at  its  best,  it  was  a  prelimi-  nary  to  something  that,  so  far  as  I  could  see,  was  going  to  continue  for  the  whole  of  my  life."
