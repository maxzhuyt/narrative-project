There  were  big,  low  houses,  bats  flying  in  the  semi-darkness,  horned  trees  pointing  at  the  sky,  and  Venus-fat,  bright,  and  alone.  &quot;Look,&quot;  he  said,  pointing  at  the  planet  with  his  stick.  They  came  to  Shelly's  house.  &quot;Well,  Gelb,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;Been  a  pleasure,&quot;  he  said,  drawling  like  a  cowboy. He  would  write  a  poem  in  three  parts  ...  The  beginning  of  the  descent  from  the  plateau  was  a  steep,  wooded  tunnel,  a  street  with  no  houses.  It  was  very  dark.  In  winter  Michael  walked  down  this  street  unafraid,  not  even  thinking  of  fear.  In  the  spring  and  summer  he  understood  nature  worship.  The  hillside  came  alive  with  vegetation  and  shadows  and  odors.  A  friend  of  his  swore  that  he  had  seen  a  huge,  low  white  animal  of  some  sort  there,  cross-  ing  in  front  of  his  headlights  one  night.  Michael,  who  usually  jumped  at  the  cracking  of  a  branch,  walked  down  the  street  composing  the  poem.  He  was  at  the  bottom  of  the  green  tunnel,  among  houses  again,  having  forgotten  to  be  afraid.  Suddenly,  he  felt  light  and  hap-  py;  he  could  do  anything.  He  skipped  down  the  smooth,  steep  streets.  At  the  top  of  his  block,  someone  was  having  a  back-yard  party:  people  were  standing  around  a  lighted  swim-  ming  pool,  their  faces  glowing  green,  and  a  man  was  leading  them  in  snappy  songs,  shouting  &quot;Hey,  hey&quot;  on  the  beat.  Michael  imagined  he  was  coming  back  from  years  at  sea,  with  nobody  expecting  him.  Ghostlike,  he  drifted  down  through  the  neighborhood,  look-  ing  in  lighted  windows.  His  own  house  seemed  strange  and  small.  With  an  in-  tense  joy,  he  saw,  through  the  glass  doors,  his  father  and  brother  sitting  and  watching  television.  He  joined  them  without  a  word,  and  sat  until  late  watching  an  extra-inning  baseball  game.  He  dreamed  all  night  about  Shelly  Eisenpreis,  only  she  was  some-  one  else.  "
