Three  of  them  had  crew  haircuts,  and  all  four  were  dressed  as  any  Westerner  might  dress  on  a  warm  day.  The  pueblo  of  San  Leandro  was  only  five  miles  from  Castellano,  New  Mexi-  co,  where  the  Bonds  lived,  and  the  In-  dians  came  to  town  for  much  of  their  shopping.  It  was  not  unusual  for  them  to  stop  and  visit.  Some  of  them  were  Dr.  Bond's  patients,  and,  as  so  readily  hap-  pens  between  Pueblo  Indians  and  white  people  of  a  sympathetic  disposition,  he  and  his  wife  had  developed  friendly  re-  lations  with  yet  others.  Charlie's  rela-  tionship  with  the  San  Leandro  people  had  begun  when  he  was  twelve  years  old,  when  the  Bonds  first  settled  in  Cas-  tellano,  and  it  was  far  more  intimate  and  less  self-conscious  than  his  parents'.  He  had  quickly  learned  never  to  in-  quire  into  Pueblo  secrets,  but  in  years  of  association  he  had  picked  up  enough  to  know  that  Agapito  was  the  Tur-  quoise  Cacique-that  is,  one  of  the  two  highest-ranking  priests  in  the  hierarchy-that  Juan  Rael  was  impor-  tant  in  a  society  the  function  of  which  he  did  not  know,  and  that  Eddie  Sota,  recently  re-  turned  from  a  hitch  in  the  Navy,  was  Aga-  pito's  nephew  and  had  started  training  to  fol-  low  in  his  uncle's  path.
