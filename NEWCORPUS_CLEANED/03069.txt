I  often  imagine  you  in  trains  sitting  opposite  each  other  saying  the  odd  word,  then  getting  off,  the  two  of  you  carrying  the  baby's  cot.  What  bliss!  Tell  me,  is  she  pretty,  is  she  soft,  your  lady,  or  does  she  have  a  whim  of  iron?  I  did  ask  you  once  if  she  had  blueeyes  and  you  professed  not  to  know.  You  must  know.  It  is  not  that  I  am  a  lover  of  blue  eyes;  the  question  simply  cropped  up.  You  must  have  seen  them  in  all  lights,  and  at  all  moments,  maybe  even  in  childbirth.  On  these  numerous  train  journeys,  do  you  ever  think  of  me?  I  know  you  do.  I  am  certain  of  it.  I  can  feel  you  thinking  of  me  even  now.  You  may  carry  the  thought  through.  You  may  get  in  touch  with  me.  Yes,  he  died,  that  young  doctor.  I  am  glad  thatI  didn't  make  love  to  him.  I  would  not  care  to  have  made  love  to  a  man  who  is  dead.  Yes,  it  got  killed  between  us,  you  and  me.  Contravened.  That  is  a  fact,  a  bitter  fact.  It  wasn't  that  it  didn't  happen;  oh,  it  did.  Your  face  and  mine,  your  voice  and  mine.  Eve-  ning.  Milking  time.  The  cows  lowed.  We  were  in  the  byre.  The  moon  came  up.  Our  faces  shone.  I  could  have  eaten  the  stars.  One  should  be  thank-  ful  for  a  moment,  ever  grateful,  and  not  be  plaintive  like  this.  Yes,  it  is  nothing  short  of  a  miracle,  the  way  you  met  me,  more  than  halfway.  The  way  you  came  out  of  your  innerness  and  complexity  and  came  to  me,  and  I  told  you  things,  nonsense  things.  I  told  you  about  the  one  wooden  sweet,  which  was  mixed  up  with  all  the  other  sweets  in  a  carnival  assortment.  This  wooden  sweet  had  bright  wrapping  like  all  the  others,  and  more  than  once  I  got  it  and  I  didn't  know  whether  to  be  glador  sorry,  and  neither  of  us  could  tell  why  it  was  in  the  box  in  the  first  place,  whether  it  was  some  sort  of  joke  or  the  like.  And  you  told  me  about  having  to  undress  at  the  doctor's  and  having  a  dirty  vest  on  and  being  scolded  by  your  mother.  Whereas  in  reality  she  was  ashamed.  In  reality.  It  is  when  I  think  about  you  suffering  that  I  cannot  bear  it.  I  think  of  you  crying.  You  cried  lest  you  could  never  see  me  again,  and  I  said  you  always  could  and  that  I  would  always  understand  and  be  wom-  anly  and  be  patient.  King  Lear  says  women  must  be  kind,  or  comfortable,  or  something  to  that  effect.  That  is  what  I  must  be-kind  and  womanly.  I  know  what  I  will  do,  I  will  talk  to  your  friend  Chat,  the  friend  you  first  brought  here.  I  will  tell  him  some  little  pleasantries  and  he  will  pass  them  on  to  you,  and  you  will  be  touched,  regaled.  I  will  do  that  now.  Oh  God,  he  told  me.  You  have  gone  away  to  a  new  country-a  young  country  at  that.  Gone  with  your  fam-  ily.  I  knew  it.  It  seems  she  did  some-  thing  silly  like  her  sister,  something  ex-  treme.  I  was  mad  to  think  that  she  would  give  you  up,  that  we  would  all  share.  Oh,  how  cracked  one's  thoughts  get.  You  did  the  right  thing,  the  only  thing.  Yes,  Iwill  see  you  when  you  are  old,  just  as  I  visualized  it,  in  the  white  trenchcoat  with  the  perforations  under  the  arms,  and  you  will  be  con-  valescing.  I  suppose  you're  married.  Oh  God,  send  me  some  word,  some  sign,  some  token.  Tell  me  if  you  are  lost  to  me.  Tell  me  how  you  are.  It  has  all  been  for  nothing.
