Martha,  when  she  was  not  driving,  studied  a  soft-cover  guidebook  and  Spanish  grammar.  She  practiced  inces,  santly,  at  first  cultivating  the  Castilian  lisp. We  saw  the  markets,  the  Basilica  of  Guada-  lupe,  the  National  Palace,  and  the  Tif-  fany-glass  curtain  at  the  Bellas  Artes.  &quot;After  all,  we  are  tourists,&quot;  Martha  said.  After  a  week  of  tourism,  we  started  south  again.  The  mountains  rose  and  dropped  away  rhythmically.  The  corn-  fields  climbed  the  mountainsides  like  stairs,  the  Indians  grew  smaller  and  more  reserved,  and  the  mountain  farm-  ers  usually  stayed  on  their  narrow  fields.  We  did  not  see  them  moving  from  town  to  town  except  on  market  days.  It  was  on  a  market  day  that  we  drove  into  Oaxaca.  The  city  lay  compact  be-  tween  the  folds  ofthe  surrounding  mountains.  A  river  arched  across  one  end  of  the  town  through  a  Spanish  aqueduct  and  meandered  through  fields  poison-green  with  alfalfa  before  it  lost  itself  in  a  blaze  of  sand  and  stone  in  the  lower  valley.  Before  we  dropped  down  the  final  mountain  slope  and  followed  the  river  into  the  town,  I  took  over  the  wheel  from  Martha.  It  seemed  only  fair  to  leave  her,  a  landscape  painter,  free  to  look  about.  She  and  Jack  had  come  to  this  place  to  work.  At  inter-  vals,  I  stopped  the  car  so  they  could  see  everything,  especially  the  great  butter-  yellow  oxen,  yoked  by  their  horns  to  wooden  plows,  turning  the  earth  for  the  nextsowing.'The  farmers  of  Oax-  aca  are  busy  all  year  long;  they  can  raise  twelve  crops  in  twelve  months  without  exhausting  their  soil.  A  little  later,  over  cocktails,  I  di-  vulged  this  fact,  gleaned  from  the  guidebook,  to  Jack  and  Martha.  We  were  sitting  at  a  table  on  the  sidewalk  outside  our  hotel,  under  its  arched  portico.  &quot;Is  that  a  lot  of  alfalfat&quot;  Jack  asked.  &quot;In  New  [REDACTED],  we  usually  get    two  cropsa  year,&quot;  I  replied.  &quot;Three  are  phenomenal.&quot;  &quot;And  you  hear  that  Mexicans  are  lazy!&quot;  Jack  said.  &quot;What  do  the  farmers  do  for  recrea-  tion?&quot;  Martha  asked. It  seems  there  is  a  feeling  that  the  dead  are  never  far  away-that  they  can  share  the  joys  of  the  living  family.  Especially  dead  chil-  dren.&quot;  &quot;Well,  if  there's  a  saint's  day  while  we're  around,  we  ought  to  try  to  join  the  festivities,&quot;  said  Jack. 'Maybe  there's  one  that  makes  tequila  and  runs  up  a  little  pottery  on  the  side.&quot;  I  sipped  the  second  Margarita.  &quot;I'd  like  tequila  better  than  pottery-mak-  ing,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;I've  seen  an  awful  lot  of  pottery.&quot;  HE  first  two  weeks  in  Oaxaca  were  idyllic  for  me.  I  rose  when  the  donkeys'  feet  padded  on  the  cobble-  stone  street  beneath  my  window  on  their  way  to  the  market.  I  showered,  then  drank  the  chocolate  and  crunched  the  roll  the  maid  brought  up  to  me.  I  wandered  about  the  town,  I  inspected  the  churches  and  their  treasures,  I  visited  the  museum  and  the  post  office,  I  went  to  the  hairdresser's,  I  ate  lei-  surely  lunches,  and  I  went  to  the  out-  door  market.  I  left  my  notebook-I   did  have  one  along  with  me,  out  of  habit-at  the  hotel.  The  whole  south  side  ofthe  market  was  stalls  filled  with  pottery.  I  stayed  on  the  north  side,  where  the  baskets  were,  or  wandered  down  the  central  aisles  where  the  curanderas  sat  beside  their  little  heaps  of  herbs.  My  friends,  meanwhile,  were  busy.  Jack  had  shuthimself  into  an  inner  room  of  the  hotel  and  begun  to  produce  the  first  of  a  series  of  Mexican  abstrac-  tions.  Martha  drove  to  the  top  of  a  hill  and  painted  every  day.  Sometimes  Jack  and  I  met  at  lunch;  if  Marthawas  un-  usually  hotor  tired,  she  now  and  then  came  in  for  the  meal.  The  rest  of  the  daylight  hours  I  spent  alone.  Inevitably,  I  acquired  the  siesta  habit.  I  wakened  at  four,  and  while  Martha  and  Jack  showered  and  rested  I  revisited  the  market.  Gradually  my  observation  periods,  morning  and  evening,  lengthened  and  began  to  blend.  The  pattern  of  the  mar-  ket  day  emerged  for  me.  The  people  who  were  there  early  in  the  morning  seldom  lingered  throughout  the  day.  |}  The  food  and  flower  peddlers,  the  sell-  ers  of  meat  and  fish,  and  the  herb  vend-  ers  went  home  early.  The  weavers  went  home  for  their  siestas,  leaving  their  stalls  shuttered  and  locked,  but  returned  to  their  looms  in  the  late  afternoon.  Only  the  pottery  sellers,  in  whom  I  was  firmly  not  interested,  stayed  throughout  the  day,  rolling  themselves  in  their  shawls  or  ponchos  to  sleep  through  the  worst  heat.  I  have  never  seen  more  completely  uninterested  people  than  those  pottery  sellers. Take  or  leave.&quot;  I  grew  to  recognize  faces  in  other  parts  of  the  market,  but  the  pottery  sellers  lived  behind  fagades  as  blank  as  their  bowls.  &quot;Are  you  two  going  to  paint  all  the  time?&quot;  I  demanded  crossly  one  evening.  I  was  getting  bored.  &quot;Why  not?&quot;  Jack  asked.  &quot;That's  what  we  came  here  for.  Aren't  you  hav-  ing  a  good  time?  Aren't  you  resting?&quot;  &quot;I've  about  caught  up  on  my  rest,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;T'd  like  to  see  something.&quot;  &quot;Where  would  you  like  to  go?&quot;  &quot;What  about  seeing  a  native  vil-  lage?&quot;  Martha  asked.  &quot;We  can  drive  out  early  in  the  morning  and  be  back  by  noon,  for  lunch  and  a  nap.  Look.&quot;  She  spread  on  the  tabletop  the  Chamber  of  Commerce  map  with  which  thehotel  had  supplied  us.  &quot;There's  one.  It  can't  be  more  than  ten  miles...  or  is  that   ten  kilometres?  Here's  a  good  road  going  straight  to  it.&quot;  So,  the  following  morning,  after  an  early  and  light  breakfast,  we  started  for  the  village.  The  map  showed  it  with  a  drawing  of  a  pot-I  was  not  to  escape,  it  seemed.  The  road  was  good  for  the  first  mile.  Then  the  pavement  ended  and  sand  began.  I  took  the  wheel,  as  the  one  having  most  experience  with  country  driving.  We  plowed  along  a  narrowing  track  that  wound  across  the  plain  in  the  direction  of  the  river  while  the  sun  grew  hotter.  I  watched  the  temperature  gauge  on  the  dashboard.
