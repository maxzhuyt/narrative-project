 b"ONE of  thethings  that  horrified  my  American  wife  when  she  lived  for  a  year  or  so  the  life  of  the  small  Italian  town  where  I  was  born  was  the  sight  of  the  dead  birds  ex-  hibited  each  autumn  on  the  stands  of  the  market  place.  These  market  stands  of  Vicenza  are  always  gay  with  awn-  ings  and  country  produce.  But  in  the  fall,  besides  being  filled  with  the  usual  eggplants  and  zucchini  and  peppers,  they  are  adorned  with  bunches  of  small  birds,  strung  up  by  their  beaks  and  hang-  ing,  about  a  dozen  to  the  bunch,  from  hooks  in  the  supporting  poles  of  the  stands.  Some  of  them  have  been  plucked  naked;  others  still  have  their  feathers,  caked  and  matted  with  blood.  And  the  venders,  when  they  have  a  moment  to  spare,  sit  and  pluck  birds,  and  make  lit-  tle  piles  of  down  near  their  feet.  Some-  times  a  stir  of  wind  or  the  rush  of  boys  playing  hide-and-seek  among  the  stands  will  raise  a  cloud  of  feathers  that  goup  noses  and  into  hair  and  eyes,  and  stick  on  the  clothes  of  shoppers  or  on  the  nearby  monuments  of  statesmen.  Then  there  will  be  pandemonium-the  shop-  pers  swearing,  the  boys  shouting,  and  the  venders  threatening  them  and  try-  ing  to  subdue  the  flutter  of  dead  feath-  ers  with  their  capes  or  aprons.  Such  a  scene  is  gay,  for  us  who  are  of  the  region;  for  my  wife  it  was  plain  horror.  &quot;But  these  are  larks  and  swal-  lows-songbirds!&quot;  she  would  exclaim.  &quot;They  aren't  ducks  or  pheas-  ants.  Why,  you  would  have  to  kill  dozens  of  these  poor  birds  to  get  a  plateful!&quot;  What  particularly  dis-  turbed  her  was  the  Ital-  ian  indifference  to  nice  distinctions-our  pagan,  ism,  she  called  it-that  kept  us  from  seeing  the  difference  between  eating  a  lark  and  eating  a  guinea  hen.  How,  she  would  ask  indignantly,  could  we  sentimentalize  over  St.  Francis  calling  the  birds  his  sisters  and  then  fill  our  plates  with  finches?  Fortunately,  I  had  already  found  that  one  of  the  ways  to  help  my  wife  ac-  cept  our  strange  customs  was  to  project  them  into  the  past  by  means  of  the  old  stories  told  in  my  family.  Nothing  was  quiteso  forbidding  to  her  once  it  had  assumed  the  picturesqueness  of  the  past.  And  so  it  was  with  the  birds;  she  learned  at  last  to  take  a  long,  long  view  of  them, and  even  to  enjoy  tales  of  those  great  bird  barbecues  that  had  taken  place  in  my  mother's  childhood  and  my  own.  IN  the  Venetian  countryside,  in  the  time  of  my  grandparents,  a  barbe-  cue-an  errosto-of  at  least  seventy  or  eighty  birds  was  held  several  times  a  week  in  the  autumn.  A  good  arrosto  of  birds  of  passage  was,  in  our  local  tradi-  tion,  an  expression  of  high  civilization  and  delicate  cuisine.  Above  all,  the  birds  had  always  to  be  killedin  free  flight,  by  gunshot,  and  not  taken  in  net  traps,  for  a  trapped  bird,  battering  its  wings  against  the  net,  dies  slowly,  and  the  bile  it  secretes  in  its  torment  makes  its  flesh  bitter.  A  connoisseur,  perfectly  able  to  tell  the  difference  at  first  taste,  would,  out  of  concern  for  sportsmanship  as  well  as  flavor,  refuse  any  bird  taken  by  snare.  In  my  grandmother's  kitchen,  the  fire  for  the  arrosto  was  built  on  hot  ashes  on  an  enormous  stone  hearth,  and  was  fed  with  a  sweet-smelling  wood,  light  and  well  dried.  If  the  plucked  birds  were  very  plump,  they  were  emptied  of  their  entrails;  otherwise  they  were  threaded  intact  on  the  spit,  with  two  squares  of  salt  pork  andtwo  leaves  of  fresh  sage  between  each  pair.  As  the  spit  began  to  turn,  the  birds  were  sprinkled  with  salt  and  then  lightly  basted,  drop  by  drop,  with  pure  olive  oil.  'The  fire  had  at  first  to  burn  slowly  and  then  be  built  up  gradually  so  that  the  birds  cooked  thor-  oughly,  without  burn-  ing,  and  only  the  head  and  feet  became  dry  and  crusty.  (When  birds  were  perfectly  done,  it  was  expected  that  nothing  should  re-  main  of  them  on  the  diners'  plates  except  the  beaks.)  'The  mixture  of  hot  oil  and  juices  that  dripped  from  them  as  they  cooked  was  caught  in  a  narrow  copper  pan,  as  long  as  the  spit,  called  a  leccarda.  'This  pan  had  been  filled  with  pieces  of  corn-  meal  polenta,  made  at  least  a  day  in  advance;  unless  the  birds  were  accom-  panied  by  polenta  fried  in  their  drip-  pings,  they  were  not  complete.  In  the  doorway  that  led  from  the  dining  room  to  the  kitchen,  thechildren  would  cluster. There  were  two  spits  in  that  kitchen:  one  that  had  to  be  turned  by  hand,  and  an  elegant  self-  revolving  spit  controlled  by  a  spring  similar  to  a  watch  spring;  the  spit  even  gave  a  tiny  chime  of  warning  whenever  the  spring  needed  rewinding.  For  an  hour  or  so,  the  women  in  the  kitchen  would  all  be  intent  on  the  fire,  birds,  polenta,  and  the  precise,  rhythmic  turn  of  the  spits.  Finally,  my  grand-  mother  would  call  out,  &quot;Grego,  I  think  we  are  ready!&quot;  At  that,  my  grandfa-  ther,  Gregorio,  would  leave  whatever  he  was  doing  and  plow  his  way  through  the  children  in  the  doorway,  through  the  women  in  the  kitchen,  to  the  hearth.  And  I  can  still  picture  him-already  an  old  man-bending  with  extreme  atten-  tion  over  the  fire  and  pulling  delicately  at  the  foot  of  a  bird  until  the  leg  came  off.  He  would  pop  the  leg  into  his  mouth,  and  after  a  moment  he  would  say  grandly,  &quot;Yes,  we're  ready!&quot;  Then  the  ladies  took  off  their  aprons  and  ran  to  wash  their  hands  and  put  their  hair  in  order. The  men  poured  themselves  wine,  saying  that  wine  as  an  ap\xc3\xa9ritif  was  as  good  as  any-  thing  else  to  give  the  doctor  a  slap.
