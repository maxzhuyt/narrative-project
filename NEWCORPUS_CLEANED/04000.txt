   A  SUMMER  ON  THE  DALES  I  :VERYONE,  I  suppose,  has  his  own  picture  of  lumberjacks--  men  like  tall  doors  reflectively  gnawing  on  six-inch  nails,  violent  primi-  tives  in  close  touch  with  danger  and  epic  struggle,  a  muscled  horde  of  bright  checked  shirts  loping  into  town  on  pay-  day  while  the  citizens  shutter  their  win-  dows.  When  I  reported  for  duty  at  a  logging  camp  in  the  high  northeast  of  [REDACTED],  just  after  the  last  war,  the  man  who  greeted  me,  the  foreman,  was  the  size  of  my  old  Sunday-school  teach-  er,  barely  making  it  out  of  the  gnome  category.  &quot;I'm  the  ganger,&quot;  he  said  in  the  slow  and  rounded  accents  of  the  North.  &quot;You'd  better  come  in.&quot;  A  drab  scarf,  perhaps  knitted  for  the  troops  during  the  war  by  one  of  those  patriotic  but  in-  ept  women,  swathed  him  to  the  hips.  He  carried  a  small  paper  bag  of  sweets  and,  since  these  were  still  rationed,  his  face  contorted  with  self-sacrifice  as  he  offered  me  one  as  a  gesture  of  hospitality.  The  ganger  led  me  into  the  camp's  common  room,  acentral  social  building  with  sleeping  huts  angling  away  from  it.  The  room  was  occupied  by  ordinary-  sized,  ordinary-looking  men  sitting  in  attitudes  of  orderly  comfort.  It  was  a  Sunday  afternoon,  and  the  room  exuded  a  sleepy,  Sunday  calm.  The  meagre-  ness  of  the  coals  in  a  fireplace  at  the  far  end  of  the  room  accounted  for  the  ganger's  scarf.  Horse  brasses,  ranged  along  a  neat  hearth,  augmented  the  glow.  The  only  leg  art  on  showwas  in  buskins  and  belonged  to  a  demure  oleo-  graph  of  &quot;The  Boyhood  of  Raleigh.&quot;  There  was  a  gardening  program  com-  ing  in  on  the  radio.  At  that  time,  there  were  always  gardening  programs  on  the  B.B.C.  [REDACTED]'s  postwar  householders,  clubbed  by  austerity,  were  constantly  being  exhorted  to  turn  their  postage-  stamp  gardens  into  miniature  dust  bowls  through  overplanting.  The  only  status  symbol  that  counted  then  was  food  par-  cels  from  the  Commonwealth  or  Amer-  ica,  and  their  recipients  lay  around  for  days  with  the  doors  locked,  paralyzed  by  richness.  Several  of  the  lumberjacks  said  &quot;How  d'you  do?,&quot;  enunciating  clearly  and  with  courtesy.  One  of  them,  a  cloth  cap  pulled  down  to  the  bridge  of  his  nose,  rose  to  take  my  suitcase.  The  ganger  said,  &quot;This  is  Arnold  Thwaite.  You'll  have  acabin  with  him.  For  a  few  days,  like.&quot;  The  cloth  cap  was  lifted  fractionally  to  give  me  almost  the  only  naked  view   I  ever  had  of  the  Thwaite  forehead.  He  wore  the  cap,  I  discovered,  in  bed,  at  work,  during  meals,  in  the  bath.  He  explained  to  me  once  that  there  was  a  broody  relation  between  rheumatism  and  light  rays  on  the  skull.  Our  cabin  was  at  the  end  of  a  line  of  hutments.  Beyond  it,  the  Dales,  an  un-  dulant  plateau  of  moorland  cut  with  deep  valleys,  swooped  away  in  a  gray  and  purple  succession,  fragmented  dark-  ly  on  their  leeward  sides  by  plantations  of  mixed  trees.  I  have  heard  complaints  since  that  isolation  is  difficult  to  achieve  in  [REDACTED],  and  perhaps  it  is,  but  that  lumber  camp  must  have  been  the  near-  est  place  to  nowhere  on  the  whole  cramped  island.  Civilization  was  a  five-  house  village  thirty  miles  away,  and  three  of  those  houses  were  empty.  Thwaite  placed  my  suitcase  carefully  on  a  rack  in  our  cabin  and  said,  &quot;  °'E's  a  hard  man,  that  ganger.  You  want  to  watch  him.&quot;  I  nodded,  unconvinced.  ''They  reckon  you  were  a  miner.&quot;  Indeed  I  had  been,  but  not  by  choice.  During  the  war,  a  proportion  of  con-  scripts  had  been  chosen  to  fend  off  the  Fascist  hordes  by  becoming  miners.  It  was  compulsory,  an  honorable  alterna-  tive  to  uniform,  but  my  younger  dreams  of  glory  had  not  included  the  risk  of  having  half  a  county  settle  on  me  as  I  wormed  along  an  eighteen-inch  seam.  In  my  four  years  as  a  miner,  I  had  learned  to  overcome  the  claustrophobia  that  had  previously  kept  me  out  of  tele-  phone  kiosks,  and  to  tolerate  pit  ponies,  whose  aim  in  life  was  to  smash  my  in-  steps.  Buttonlike  scabs  had  formed  along  my  vertebrae,  and  I  had  a  blue  scar  aboveone  eyebrow.  I  wasn't  a  real  miner  like  those  born  to  it,  but,  accord-  ing  to  me,  I  was  entitled  by  service  to  borrow  some  of  their  reputation  for  rough  and  arrogant  endurance.  &quot;What  you  doing  here?&quot;  Thwaite  asked.  &quot;'There  isn't  the  money  in  this,  you  know.&quot;  &quot;I  was  sent  here.&quot;  He  understood.  During  and  after  the  war,  the  elderly  economists  running  the  country  had  divided  industry  into  &quot;essential&quot;  and  &quot;non-essential&quot;  cate-  gories. They  were  a  little  bleary  about  the  eyes,  stiff-gaited,  older-looking  in  the  gray  morning.  'There  were  twelve  of  us,  including  the  ganger  and  the  driv-  er. In  time,  we  arrived  in  the  middle  of  five  acres  of  semi-gutted  woodland,  from  which  the  big  stuff  had  been  hacked  down  and  dragged  out  piecemeal  to  feeda  war  effort  that  already  seemed  part  of  an-  other  life.  The  trees  left  were  more  than  half  grown,  an  accidental  mixture  of  species,  but  mostly  silver  birch.  We  were  supposed  to  clear  the  lot  and  leave  the  ground  ready  for  timber  to  be  planted  as  a  systematic  crop.  The  ganger's  yellow  paint  crosses  on  the  fragile  striated  bark  marked  the  trees  we  were  to  leave  as  a  windbreak  for  the  new  growth.  The  gang  climbed  down  from  the  truck  to  stretch.  The  men  looked  sus-  piciously  at  the  dew  spidering  wetly  across  their  boots.  They  moved  slowly  to  the  tools,  each  man  taking  his  own  axe  and  tucking  it  under  his  arm.  The  spare  one  was  for  me,  and  I  swung  it  round  my  head  in  dangerous  circles,  full-armed,  a  bit  lightheaded  at  the  sky  and  the  space  about  me.  I  yelled,  &quot;Tim-  ber!&quot;  and  sent  up  a  clatter  of  scared  wood  pigeons.  The  ganger  said,  &quot;You  ought  to  be  in  a  Tarzan  picture,&quot;  and  we  trooped  off.  I  slashed  at  the  brittle  tendrils  of  blackberry-an  amusement  and  a  de-  laying  exercise  for  myself-as  they  plodded  steadily  uphill  to  the  top  of  the  stand.  An  hour  later,  I  was  slower,  and  the  morning  air  labored  in  my  lungs.  The  muscles  in  use  were  different,  and  I  seemed  to  be  sweating  more  than  I  had  down  in  the  pit,  where  mineral  and  ani-  mal  moisture  mixed  in  a  natural  cle-  ment.  I  waited  for  a  birch  to  fall  and  watched  Arnold  Thwaite,  still  wearing  a  sort  of  woolly  overjacket,  attack  the  odd  tree  in  line  that  should  have  been  mine.  Beyond  him  were  the  others,  _  wizened  against  the  silver  and  green.  The  men  seemed  to  work  like  smooth  mechanical  toys,  raising  the  axe  heads  with  a  slight  lift  of  shoulder  and  arm,  moving  very  little.  Each  time  a  blade  fell,  it  was  exact  and  calculated,  and  white  chips  of  wood  fell  and  gathered  in  neat  piles.  I  slogged,  driving  the  blade  blindly,  as  I'd  been  used  to  driving  a  pick  a  mile  underground,  full  of  animal  hate  for  the  shiny  coal  and  lying  on  my  guts  in  the  wet.  I  used  the  same  wrench  of  the  haft  and  the  same  grunts.  By  noon,  the  only  sound  I  wanted  to  hear  was  the  ganger  calling  &quot;Now  then,  we'll  have  summat  teat!&quot;  I  was  yards  behind  by  then,  even  al-  lowing  for  the  clearings  in  the  section  tactfully  allotted  to  me.  'The  rest  of  the  gang  moved  remorselessly  on,  leather  men,  ash  handles,  bright  steel  blades.  Their  tree  stumps  were  white  and  clean,  as  if  thev  had  been  cut  by  a  dental  surgeon.  Mine  looked  like  the  work  of  drunken  beavers.  Nobody  shouted  &quot;Timber!&quot;  The  men  said,  conversa-  tionally,  &quot;Heyup,&quot;'  or  &quot;Mind  your  back,&quot;  or  &quot;By  giney,  there's  a  maggie's  nest  in  that  one,&quot;  and  dropped  trees  with  the  precision  of  El  Greco  painting  an  eyelash.  That  evening,  fatherly  men  helped  me  climb  onto  the  truck,  and  the  ride  back  to  camp  was  a  light  coma  as  I  experimented  with  varieties  of  muscular  discomfort.  I  was  almost  asleep  at  the  supper  table  when  Arnold  said  placidly,  'They'll  be  playing  dominoes  in  here.  It  gets  a  bit  noisy.  You  have  a  bath,  lad,  and  get  to  bed.  T'll  see  you  get  your  cocoa.&quot;  In  the  big  tin  bath,  I  wondered  briefly  about  drowning  and  then-a  drastic  revision  of  my  earlier  scorn-  understood  how  men  likethis  could  tear  towns  apart  for  their  leisure.  The  leisure  arrived  that  Saturday  evening.  I'd  been  asleep  with  lichen  in  my  hair  for  two  hours  when  Arnold  ambled  in  and  shook  me  awake.  &quot;We're  off  down  the  club.  Ganger  says  you  can  come  if  I  keep  an  eye  on  you.&quot;  They  were  waiting  for  me,  unrecog-  nizable  around  the  truck,  aneat  group  of  men  approaching  middle  age.  I  saw  two  or  three  caps  like  Arnold's,  the  ganger  in  an  old-fashioned  trilby,  lots  of  double-breasted  dark  suiting  with  horizontal  creases  in  the  trousers  where  they  had  been  folded  all  week.  I  was  all  shoulders  in  a  semi-zoot  job,  bought  with  black-market  clothing  coupons,  which  had  sent  the  mining-town  maid-  ens  into  a  medium  transport.  I  wore  black  shoes  with  thick  crépe-rubber  soles  and  I  had  an  orange  handkerchief  looped  from  the  top  pocket  of  the  jacket.  The  ganger,  gently  disapproving,  borrowed  my  handkerchief  on  the  journey,  and  I  never  saw  it  again.  The  club  was  in  a  chapel  town  about  fifty  miles  away.  Its  official  title  was  the  Howkerdale  Working  Men's  Social  and  Debating  Club,  a  device  common  enough  in  [REDACTED]  to  get  around  li-  censing  difficulties.  During  the  war,  when  drink  was  scarce,  the  club  system  had  the  advantage  of  restricting  what  ale  was  available  to  throats  accustomed  to  it.  The  Howkerdale  Club  was  reason-  ably  typical-a  long  room  with  a  bar  at  one  end  and  a  small  stage  at  the  other.  A  privilege  of  club  members  was  enter-  tainment  as  they  drank.  The  ganger  signed  everybody  in,  and  we  wedged  through  the  usual  phalanx  of  clients  who  preferred  to  do  their  drinking  in  the  posture  of  Guardsmen,  bolt  upright,  the  pint  jars  rising  and  falling  like  a  salute.  A  table,  ours  by  right  of  custom,  was  empty  at  the  side  of  the  small  stage.  Each  logger  gave  the  ganger  a  pound,  and  throughout  the  evening  we  set  off  in  turn  tothe  bar  with  a  large  tray.  Drink  was  still  in  short  supply  and  ordering  was  simple.  &quot;We'll  have  ale  till  he  runs  out.  Then  we'll  have  owt  he's  got,&quot;  Arnold  explained.  One  method  of  keeping  the  ale  for  the  regulars  who  had  arrived  early  was  the  bartender's  refusal  to  serve  anyone  without  a  glass.  I  was  warned  to  keep  the  tray  high  in  the  air  and  to  dodge  if  I  saw  some  late  pirate  climb  on  a  chair  in  my  path.  There  wasn't  much  talk  at  our  table,  but  when  a  solidly  built  soprano  climbed  onto  the  stage  and  handed  the  pianist  a  sheaf  of  music,  the  ganger  said,  &quot;Order,  please.  Let's  have  some  order.&quot;  The  pianist  went  into  a  showy  rip-  ple,  and  the  soprano,  oratorio-trained,  warmed  up  on  &quot;There'll  Be  Bluebirds  Over  the  White  Cliffs  of  Dover,&quot;  a  chauvinistic  piece  of  one-octave  opti-  mism  left  over  from  the  war.  When  she  had  finished,  applause  rattled  the  glasses  on  our  table,  and  Arnold,  cau-  tious  and  cloth-capped,  held  on  to  mine  as  well  as  his  own.  The  ganger  dabbed  his  eyes  and  then  closed  them  in  quiet  ecstasy  as  the  soprano  swelled  up  for  a  brass-lunged  version  of  &quot;'The  Trumpet  Shall  Sound,&quot;  which  had  me  glancing  at  the  door  for  some  millennial  answer  to  her  summons.  High  notes  lodged  like  sparrows  in  the  rafters  and  hung  there  chirping  with  a  life  of  their  own.  The  tray  had  been  back  and  fortheight  times.  I  took  my  turn  with  &quot;Oh,  My  Beloved  Father&quot;  as  background  and  then  sank  deaf  and  soggy  into  my  chair,  sealed  off  in  a  cloudy  dimness  of  liquor  and  tobacco  fumes.  A  man  on  the  stage  recited  &quot;Gunga  Din,&quot;  with  actions.  The  ganger  said,  &quot;I  like  that.  Makes  you  appreciate  this  port  stuff.&quot;  He  meant  the  thick,  prewar  wine  we  were  drinking,a  choice  forced  on  us  when  the  beer  ran  out.  The  next  round  was  atype  of  homemade  black-currant  cordial,  and  a  young  woman  played  &quot;Rustle  of  Spring&quot;  on  the  gap-toothed  piano.  Arnold  climbed  onto  the  stage,  bowed  to  her,  waited  for  the  right  ripple,  and  dived  in  with  a  high  baritone  version  of  &quot;Roses  of  Picardy,&quot;  while  I  poured  my  cordial  into  his  glass.  'The  applause  heaved  like  the  sea  at  my  eardrums,  and  the  room  shrank  and  expanded  in  time  with  my  breathing.  It  was  closing    time,  and  a  fight  broke  out  at  the  door.  Here  my  reflexes  started  to  work.  This  was  the  dangerous  time,  remem-  bered  from  Saturday  nights  in  the  pit  villages.  All  it  took  was  an  accidental  shove  or  a  sidewise  look  and  the  fight  could  be  with  your  best  friend.  Nobody  ever  interfered.  Drinking  was  a  man's  business,  and  most  of  the  colliers  had  worn  heavy  boots  for  it.  Even  a  light  kicking  was  a  hospital  job.  I  crept  away  to  the  outhouse  and  put  my  fingers  down  my  throat.  I'd  seen  these  loggers  work,  and  enough  drink  had  been  portered  on  the  big  trays  to  inflame  an  army.  I  was  the  newcomer  and  I  wanted  any  argument  to  find  me  at  least  half  sober.  Arnold  bumped  into  me  at  the  doorway  of  the  jakes.  &quot;I  thought  as  I'd  find  you  here.  Ganger's  waiting.&quot;  They  were  all  at  the  truck,  ignoring  a  four-handed  brawl  nearby.  The  ride  back  and  the  truck's  leaky  canvas  added  a  catalyst  of  fresh  air  to  the  drink  fumes,  and  nobody,  including  the  ganger,  walked  quite  straight  when  we  arrived  back  at  camp. Their  voices  were  suddenly  shrill  with  irritation  and  liquor.  Arnold  stopped  singing.
