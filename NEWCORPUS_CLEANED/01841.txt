When  Eda  spoke,  it  was  in  Spanish.  Her  voice  was  low-a  little  like  a    man's.  I  didn't  understand  what  she  said.  The  girl  said,  &quot;We  are  sorry  to  disturb  you.  Were  you  sleeping?  We  are  sorry  if  you  were  sleeping.&quot;  &quot;Let  me  get  my  money,&quot;  I  said.  After  I  paid,  I  said,  &quot;Please  thank  the  translator.&quot;  &quot;He  does  good  work,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;He  loves  English.  He  loves  it  like  his  own  language.&quot;  Two  nights  later,  the  girl  took  me  to  a  discotheque.  In  50657275,  the  dis-  coth\xc3\xa9ques  are  pitch-black,  so  no  one  can  see  who's  with  whom.  We  sat  at  a  table,  and  as  we  sat  there  I  thought  that  this  must  be  the  kind  of  darkness  people  talk  about  when  they  talk  about  the  amazing  darkness  of  underground  caverns.  After  we  had  danced  a  couple  of  times,  the  girl  sat  very  close  and  began  telling  me  a  story.  She  said  it  was  a  story  about  her  Aunt  Eda.  She  said  that  when  Eda  was  nineteen  she  fell  in  love  with  a  doomed  man  and  never  fell  in  love  again.  He  was  a  twin,  and  he  died  in  a  car  crash.  When  he  died,  his  twin  brother's  hair  went  white  overnight.  White  as  snow.  The  twin  brother  asked  Eda  to  marry  him,  but  Eda  said  no,  she  just  wouldn't  be  able  to  bear  it.  The  twin  brother's  name  was  Jorge-George  in  English.  Jorge  loved  Eda.  Really,  he  loved  her  more  than  his  brother  had  loved  her.  But  Eda  couldn't  see  that,  or  didn't  want  to  see  it,  she  was  so  blind  with  grief.  For  nearly  twenty  years,  Jorge  wrote  Eda  letters-hun-  dreds  and  hundreds  of  letters-but  re-  ceived  no  replies.  One  day,  still  many  years  ago,  Jorge  was  swimming  at  La  Herradura  and  swam  out  too  far.  Eda  was  in  her  garden  in  Jesus  Maria  and  she  saw  him,  saw  his  face  and  saw  the  water  and  the  things  he  must  have  been  seeing  under  the  water:  gray  fish  and  seaweed  and  a  muddy  darkness.  She  knew  that  he  was  drowning  and  she  rushed  to  the  beach  in  a  taxi.  When  she  got  there,  Jorge's  body  was  already  spreadout  on  the  sand.  That  night,  in  a  dream,  she  saw  his  face  and  she  saw  him  running  toward  her,  and  in  his  hand  was  another  letter,  freshly  written.  -PETER  GORDON        "
