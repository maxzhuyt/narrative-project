THE  HIGH  PRIEST  OF  FRIVOLITY  OWE  my  life  to  a  series  offor-  I  tunate  coincidences.  It  is  remark-  able,  too,  that  an  eleven-year-old  can  realize  the  cold  fact  that  he  can  be  killed  at  any  time,  and  yet  not  falter.  In  the  winter  of  1944-45  I  thought  of  death  almost  the  way  one  thinks  of  firewood:  there  was  nothing  special  about  it.  It  was  a  thing  beyond  my  control,  and  there  was  no  point  in  fearing  it,  for  it  had  as  much  probabil-  ity-no  more,  no  less-as  drawing  a  bad  card  and  losing.  The  Germans  were  roundingup  Jews  in  our  country  town  that  fall,  and  when  my  parents  were  arrested  I  made  my  way  to  Budapest  to  stay  with  rela-  tives.  There  were  certain  apartment  buildings  in  the  city  that  were  desig-  nated  protected  houses;  in  these  the  wealthier,  more  secular  Jews  lived,  those  who  had  managed  to  establish  contact  with  the  embassy  of  some  neutral  country.  The  less  well-off-and  the  Orthodox  Jews,  with  their  beards  and  black  hats-ended  up  in  the  ghetto,  along  with  their  wives,  their  peyis-  wearing  sons,  and  their  large-eyed  daughters.  Their  place  was  there,  near  the  rabbinate  and  most  of  the  syna-  gogues.  They  must  have  felt  that  this  arrangement  was  proper-as  did,  no  doubt,  the  diplomats  of  those  neutral  countries.  Arrow-Cross  men,  the  Hun-  garian  Nazis,  who  ventured  into  the  ghetto  through  the  wooden  fence  found  a  free  hunting  ground  within.  Their  bullets  spared  only  those  they  didn't  feel  like  shooting.  Immediately  after  the  liberation  of  the  city,  I  looked  into  a  café  at  the  corner  of  Klauzal  Square.  It  was  piled  to  the  ceiling  with  corpses.  It  was  not  easy  to  break  the  icy  ground  to  dig  the  mass  graves.  The  building  in  which  our  Aunt  Zsuzsa  had  her  apartment-studio,  on  Pozsonyi  Road,  came  under  Swiss  protection.  She,  Uncle  Arnold,  and  their  daughter,Klara,  could  have  moved  to  the  adjacent  building,  which  was  under  Swedish  protection-rumor  had  it  that  Swedish  protection  was  more  effective  than  Swiss-but  they  decided  to  stay  in  their  own  apartment,  which  was  anice  one,  although  eighty  people  were  living  in  it  by  then.  Every  evening,  all  the  furniture  that  could  not  be  slept  on  was  piled  in  one  corner.  There  were  not  enough  beds  or  mattresses  to  go  around,  but  at  least  everyone  had  a  rug  under  him.  We  slept  in  close  proximity;  it  was  a  continuous  pajama  party,  and  there  was  always  somebody  to  talk  to.  We  were  allowed  to  leave  the  building  for  two  hours  every  morn-  ing.  On  the  front  entrance  hung  a  yellow  star  and  a  sign  that  said  the  house  was  protected  by  the  Swiss  gov-  ernment.  While  Communists,  mem-  bers  of  the  Resistance,  and  braver  souls  in  general  were  hiding  in  all  parts  of  the  city,  constantly  on  the  move  and  carrying  false  papers,  the  more  re-  signed,  and  perhaps  more  timid,  middle  class  hoped  to  ride  out  the  worst  in  these  protected  apartments.  In  our  apartment,  beautiful  long-  legged  young  women  in  ski  boots,  pants,  and  heavy  sweaters  with  Norwegian  motifs,  their  abundant  hair  combed  flat,  stood  around  and  smoked  cigarettes.  They  laughed  at  things  I  didnot  understand-but  Klara  understood.  Not  at  all  like  their  small-town  counter-  parts,  these  women  were  cynical,  enig-  matic,  radical,  and  refined.  They  spoke  of  French  Surrealists,  German  Expres-  sionists,  and  Russian  abstractionists  as  old  friends.  They  were  modern  danc-  ers  and  could  stretch  their  bodies.  For  us,  the  children,  they  sang  the  &quot;Inter-  nationale&quot;  and  Russian  revolutionary  songs.  I  was  in  love  with  all  of  them,  but  especially  with  Zsuzsa,  who  was  around  thirty  then.  I  would  have  loved  to  do  something  heroic  for  her.  She'd  call  to  me  in  a  drawl,  &quot;Will  you  come  and  escort  me,  my  cavalier?&quot;  I  would  have  escorted  her  to  Hell  itself.  I  turned  fiery  red  when  Zsuzsa  walked  into  the  bathroom  once  and  saw  me  naked.  For  her  sake  I  was  willing  to  eat  with  a  thick  novel  clamped  under  each  arm,  to  learn  to  hold  my  elbows  in.  Half  the  occupants  of  the  apartment  had  soon  been  shot,  and  thrown  into  the  Danube.  That  made  more  room.  Those  who  were  frightened  moved  down  to  the  cellar,  but  Zsuzsa  could  not  sepa-  rate  human  dignity  from  hygiene.  It  was  out  of  the  question  for  her  to  hide  from  incendiary  bombs,  cannonballs,  and  artillery  shells  in  the  gloom  and  stench  of  the  cellar,  the  cramped  confusion  of  people  who  hadn't  washed  recently.        31  Human  dignity  was  worth  more  than  safety,  she  would  say.  Not  wanting  to  get  lice,  we  didn't  go  down  to  the  cellar  even  when  the  siren  sounded.  The  most  sensible  place  for  us  chil-  dren  to  play  turned  out  to  be  the  roof  terrace,  where  every  morning  the  sun  was  bright,  even  in  the  January  cold  of  twenty  degrees  below  zero  Centi-  grade.  With  a  few  pails  of  water  we  created  a  splendid  ice-skating  rink.  With  the  metal  taps  on  the  heels  of  our  shoes  we  could  slide  grandly  across  the  shiny  surface.  We  stared  at  the  sky  when  the  bombers  came,  and  watched  their  bellies  open,  and  followed  the  fall  of  the  bombs  to  see  where  they  hit.  Only  the  plumes  of  dustor  flame  would  indicate  whether  they  were  explosive  or  incendiary  bombs.  Then  we  watched  the  smoke  rise.  On  the  street  below,  Germans  shouted.  The  Russians  were  already  near  the  city,  but,  in  the  neighborhood,  the  Arrow-  Cross  men  were  still  carrying  on  their  massacre  of  Jews  and  of  Christian  deserters.  The  word  &quot;kill&quot;  appeared  in  every  notice  posted.  Its  meaning  was  clear:  you  would  be  shot  on  sight  and  left  lying  where  you  fell.  All  through  this  period,  intermittent  rifle  shots  were  heard  in  the  streets.  Identification  papers  no  longer  meant  anything;  one's  fate  was  determined  only  by  drunkenness,  fear,  a  moment  of  empathy  or  antipathy.  People  were  still  being  shot,  but  the  armbanded  shooters  sensed  that  they  would  notbe  able  to  finish  off  every  Jew.  Perhaps,  too,  they  could  not  work  themselves  into  a  manhunting  mood  every  day.  To  fill  the  rolling  Danube  with  the  wounded  bodies  of  old  women  and  little  girls  left  something  to  be  desired  as  decorative  art.  And  even  these  help-  less  people,  of  whom  the  Hungarian  Nazis  were  permitted  to  kill  as  many  as  they  liked,  expressed,  if  only  by  the  look  in  theireyes,  a  protest-a  protest  repeated  and  strengthened  by  the  gaze  of  witnesses,  who  watched  with  some  compassion  as  silent  Jews  in  winter  coats  were  escorted  to  the  lower  wharves  of  the  Danube.  It  must  have  occurred  to  the  Hungarian  Nazis  that  if  the  Russians  reached  the  outskirts  of  Buda-  pest-and  the  constant  booming  of  artillery  indicated  that  they  were  on  their  way-it  was  not  likely  that  they  would  stop  without  entering  the  city.  And  if  the  Russians  entered  the  city,  many  things  would  be  instore  for  the  Hungarian  Nazis-things  that  did  not  32  include  medals.  An  unpleasant  thought.  The  lust  to  killleft  them  and  flared  up  again,  alternately.  To  fire  at  the  Rus-  sians  was  dangerous;  to  shoot  the  Jews  was  safe,  like  shooting  clay  pigeons.  Life  is  good  luck,  death  is  bad  luck.  People  in  the  apartment  were  taken  away  one  night;  bychance  they  were  taken  from  the  adjacent  room,  not  from  ours.  Next  day,  in  the  bright  winter  morning,  I  am  skating  on  the  roof.  From  a  machine  gun  in  a  Rus-  sian  fighter  plane  overhead,  bullets  crackle  on  the  ice.  A  streetcar  rattles  by,  carrying  ammunition  to  the  front,  which  is  only  five  blocks  away.  I  watch  the  Germans:  military  fuss,  boyish  activity  around  the  weapons.  Do  they  really  believe  they  can  repelthe  Rus-  sians?  The  Germans  are  intelligent,  but  they  don't  see  what  is  obvious.  And  these  Arrow-Cross  men-they  arethe  trash,  the  ones  who  flunk  school,  who  are  talented  only  at  torturing  cats.  A  child  grows  up  and  learns  how  child-  ish  adults  are.  Learns  that  mass  mur-  derers  are  not  evil,  but  only  rotten  kids  with  runny  noses;  rotten  kids,  even  if  they  are  fifty  years  old.  An  armed  fourteen-year-old  takes  unarmed  grownups  to  the  bank  of  the  Danube.  And,  instead  of  disarming  him,  they  do  as  he  says.  Most  victims  consider  inevitable  what  ought  to  be  fought.  The  attitude  of  a  gardener  to  the  hail  falling  on  his  flowers.  Just  as  a  farm  animal  grows  accustomed  to  the  slaughter  of  its  mates,  man  grows  accustomed  to  the  slaughter  of  his  fellow  human  beings.  You  cannot  be  shocked  every  half  hour.  As  we  children  were  standing  on  the  roof  terrace,  the  sound  of  rifle  shots  came  from  several  places  in  the  neigh-  boring  streets.  A  man  was  checking  another  man's  papers:  an  armed  person  checking  an  unarmed  one.  The  armed  one  did  not  like  the  face  of  the  unarmed  one,  so  he  made  him  stand  against  the  wall  and  shot  him.  And  the  people  taken  to  the  Danube  had  to  line  up  in  a  row  facing  the  river.  Then  came  the  round  of  fire,  from  behind.  Yet  even  all  this  violent  death  could  not  eclipse  the  icy  beauty  of  that  bright  winter  morn-  ing.  In  the  shadow  of  our  doom,  bread  was  more  breadlike,  jamwas  more  jaralike,  and  I  took  pleasure  in  making  firewood  out  of  furniture. By  giving  them  nothing,  I  insulted  them.  Unfortunately,  the  bad  poets  were  in  the  majority,  and  they  took  their  re-  venge.  They  became  journalists,  and  at  their  papers'  expense  they  came  to  see  my  shows,  and  then  they  denounced  me  in  their  reviews.  They  loved  my  girls  and  the  way  they  danced  almost  completely  naked,  lit  from  below  the  revolving  glass  stage,  but  in  the  papers  I  was  accused  of  pornography.  The  reviewers  roared  at  my  jokes,  then  declared  them  offensive  to  the  regent  of  the  country.&quot;  Arnold  Kobra  got  out  of  his  chair  and  went  to  the  window.  He  pointed.  &quot;That  was  my  hotel.  Now  it  is  a  house  of  torture.  W  hat  can  you  do  when  such  idiots  are  turned  loose?  They  give  our  words  a  background  of  machine-gun  fire.  There  is  no  connection  whatever  between  our  behavior  and  our  being  shot,  and  pushed  into  the  Danube.  They  shoot  those  who  resist  and  those  who  don't.&quot;  &quot;Mostly  those  who  don't,&quot;  I  put  in.  &quot;You  can  learn  something  from  all  this,&quot;  Arnold  said,  &quot;and  the  next  chap-  ter  will  be  yours.  I  wonder  what  you'll  do  with  your  dual  status  as  Jew  and  citizen.  Will  you  try  to  scratch  the  Jewishness  off  like  a  scab?  Will  you  try  to  be  one  or  the  other,  or  neither?  The  God  of  our  fathers  has  now  shown  us  his  other  face.  He  gives  us  life,  but  he  gives  us  death,  too.  At  times  he  does  this  in  the  right  proportion,  bringing           MARCH  9,  1992  death  to  old  people.  But  when  he  brings  it  indiscriminately,  wholesale,  to  children-well,  I  can't  go  along  with  that.  I  remain  a  God-fearing  man,  but  I  still  say  that  God  wasn't  paying  attention.&quot;  WE  had  an  unexpected  visitor  in  the  protected  house:  our  old  governess,  Nene.  She  brought  a  small  oval-shaped  medallion  on  a  chain.  It  was  a  figure  of  Mary.  Nene  asked  us  to  wear  it.  We  should  convert  to  Catholicism,  she  said.  If  we  did  that,  or  at  least  declared  our  intention  to  do  that,  she  would  take  us  to  a  convent  where  converted  Jewish  children  were  being  hidden.  We  thanked  Nene  for  her  offer  but  declined.  To  remember  her  friendship,  however,  we  did  accept  the  medallion  of  Mary,  even  though  we  wouldn't  wear  it.  We  conferred,  and  decided  that  if  we  were  taken  away  with  a  group  of  other  people  from  the  building-which  would  mean  being  shot  on  the  banks  of  the  Danube-then  at  the  edge  of  the  nearby  park  we  would  throw  down  our  backpacks  and  run,  each  in  a  different  direction.  Even  if  they  fired  at  us,  some  of  us  would  get  away;  the  guards  would  not  leave  the  column  of  hun-  dreds  of  Jews  to  pursue  a  few  children.  The  following  day  we  almost  had  the  chance  to  put  our  plan  into  action.  At  dawn,  five  Arrow-Cross  men  and  a  policeman  broke  into  our  room,  yelling  at  us  to  get  dressed,  turn  over  all  our  weap-  ons,  including  kitchen  knives  and  pocket-  knives,  and  everything                                   of  value,  then  line  up  on  the  sidewalk  in  front  of  the  building.  There  was  a  rabbi  with  them.  He  urged  us  to  obey,  saying  that  that  was  the  wisest  thing  to  do,  that  we  should  surrender  all  our  valuables-our  necklaces,  heirlooms,  even  wedding  rings.  Pulling  on  my  socks  slowly,  I  could  see  Rebenyak's  redcap  downstairs,  already  out  front.  Rebenyak  was  the  bad  boy  of  the  build-  ing;  he  hadwanted  to  join  our  group,  but  we  wouldn't  accept  him.              THE  NEW  YORKER  We  looked  at  one  another,  wondering  whether  the  time  had  come  to  carry  out  our  plan,  and  whether  to  let  Rebenyak  in  on  it.  Just  then,  two  more  men  arrived.  One  was  wearing  a  policeman's  uniform,  the  other  a  German  officer's.  They,  too,  yelled,  but  not  at  us-at  the  Arrow-Cross  men  and  their  police  escort.  They  ordered  everyone  back  inside.  Perhaps  these  two  were  Com-  munists  in  disguise,  or  Jewish  actors.  In  any  case,  they  played  their  roles  far  better  than  the  &quot;rabbi&quot;  had  played  his.  There  we  were  again,  upstairs  in  our  room,  sitting  in  our  coats,  confused  by  the  turn  of  events.  IN  the  street,  the  day  after  New  Year's,  a  barricade  of  cobblestones  was  growing  higher.  Uncle  Arnold  was  ordered  outside  to  carry  stones  along  with  other  old  Jewish  men;  the  younger  men  had  long  since  been  taken  away.  Rows  of  cobblestones,  six  deep:  such  a  formidable  wall!  Surely  the  T-34  tanks  that  had  come  all  the  way  from  Stalingrad  would  be  stopped  by  these  few  pebbles.  From  their  build-  ing's  entrance  the  children  watched  the  old  men,  who  were  bent  over  in  the  freezing  cold  as  they  labored  with  hammers  and  fire  irons  to  separate  the  stones  stuck  together  with  pitch,  and  then  carried  them  to  add  to  the  puny  obstruction  in  the  middle  of  the  street.  Young  men  in  hunting  boots,  black  pants,  and  green  shirts  supervised  the  work,  pressed  the  old  men  to  move  faster.  One  of  the  young  fellows  had  a  fancy  whip,  like  a  hansom  driver's,  and  with  it  he  would  hit  the  old  Jews  in  the  neck.  There  was  no  denying  that  they  could  have  worked  faster.  It  must  have  been  the  young  man  with  the  whip  who  outraged  an  elderly  gentleman  from  the  building  next  door,  where  Christians  lived.  It  does  happen  that  the  old  will  side  with  the  old,  even  across  religious  barriers.  The  elderly  gentleman  produced  a  shotgun,  fired,  and  wounded  the  young  man.  The  Arrow-Cross  men  thought  it  was  a  Jew  who  did  this,  What  else  could  they  have  thought?  They  began  to  shoot  wildly  in  all  directions.  The  twenty  barricade  builders  scattered,  ran  for  cover;  Arnold  left,  too,  but  he  walked  rather  than  ran  so  as  not  to  draw  attention  to  himself.  At  the  main  entrance  to  theapart-  ment  building  only  I  still  stood;  the  other  children  and  the  adult  doorkeeper  on  duty,  a  very  old  Jew,  had  run  upstairs  at  the  first  sound  of  gun-  fire.  I  opened  the  heavy  boarded-up  door,  and  Arnold  jumped  inside.  I  tried  to  shut  and  lock  the  door  before  it  could  be  forced  open  by  the  tall,  arm-  banded  young  man  following  Arnold.  Two  pushed  from  the  inside-an  eleven-year-old  and  a  sixty-year-old-  but  the  young  man  won,  sticking  the  tip  of  his  boot  in  the  crack  of  thedoor.  He  stood  before  us,  his  pistol  drawn.  He  was  taller  than  Arnold,  and  his  lips  were  quivering  with  injured  dignity:  these  Jews  would  slam  the  door  in  his  face,  just  like  that?  A  faint  smile  from  Arnold,  the  smile  of  the  loser.  The  young  man  put  his  pistol  to  Arnold's  temple  and  pulled  the  trigger.  Arnold  fell,  and  the  blood  from  his  head  flowed  across  the  dirty,  pink  fake-  marble  stone  of  the  lobby.  The  young  man  now  took  aim  at  the  child.  I  did  not  understand-would  this,  then,  be  death?  More  in  wonder  than  in  fear,  I  stared  into  the  tall  man's  face.  The  man's  anger  subsided;  he  lowered  his  pistol.  After  all,  it  was  only  a  child.  Magnanimity  got  the  better  of  him.  He  left.  ON  the  night  of  January  17,  1945,  we  watched  one  Russian  tank  and  then  another  roll  over  the  barri-  cade  the  old  men  had  built.  The  German  soldiers,  who  had  been  lying  behind  the  barricade  clutching  their  machine  guns,  fled  to  the  park.  That  night  we  slept  not  on  the  floor  of  the  living  room  but  in  the  hallway,  farther  from  the  fighting,  and  we  kept  sneaking  to  the  window  to  peek  out.  In  the  light  of  the  Katyusha  rockets  we  saw  scenesout  of  the  newsreels,  but  this  time  close  up  and  without  the  customary  frame  of  the  movie  screen.  And  once  again  we  sang  the  &quot;Internationale.&quot;  Aunt  Marta,  a  Communist,  had  taught  us  the  song,  and  told  us  that  we,  too,  should  be  Communists,  because  that  was  the  only  party  that  had  been  outlawed;  all  the  rest,  simply  in  being  legal,  had  col-  laborated  in  some  degree  with  the  authorities.  &quot;Learn  Russian,  children;  you'll  need  it,&quot;  she  said.  Later,  in  1949,  in  the  same  ski  boots  she  had  worn  through  the  winter  of  the  city's  siege,  Aunt  Marta  tried  to  flee  across  the  frontier.  The  Communist  border  guards  fired  a  few  warning  shots,  one  of  which  hit  her.  She  died  in  the  hospital.  But  on  that  night  in  1945  we  did  not  fall  asleep  until  four  in  the  morning.  We  drifted  off  knowing  that  we  would  wake  up  liberated.   
