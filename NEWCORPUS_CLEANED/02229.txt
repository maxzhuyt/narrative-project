The  poverty  of  the  Haitians  was  such,  especially  when  one  left  [REDACTED]-au-Prince,  that  it  bruised  the  tourist's  heart.  In  the  hills,  bare-  foot  old  women  in  black  rags  squatted  on  the  ground  in  frontof  their  shacks,  smoking  brown  cigarette  ends  held  be-  tween  their  thumbs  and  forefingers. I  took  strong  medicines  to  relieve  the  bronchial  spasms,  and  they  all  had  side  effects.  If  the  nights  were  divided  into  sleepless  episodes,  the  mornings  were  united  in  exhaus-  tion  and  fog.  I  would  get  up,rather  late,  heart  pounding  from  ephedrine  (which  seems  to  be  the  common  de-  nominator  in  asthma  remedies),  and  a  new  day  with  a  new  husband  would  begin.  The  [REDACTED]-au-Prince  pension  we  stayed  in  was  called  Mon  R\xc3\xa9ve.  There  were  two  barefoot  houseboys  attached  to  it,  and  they  were  called  (as  if  they  were  characters  in  a  jungle  version  of  a  Maeterlinck  play)  Oxyg\xc3\xa9ne  and  T\xc3\xa9l\xc3\xa9phone.  My  new  husbandand  I  would  leave  the  pension  and  its  strong  black  coffee,  which  did  not  help  my  palpitations,  and  set  out  to  explore.  There  was  plenty  to  do.  We  used  to  visit  a  Yugoslav  who  owned  a  large  house  at  the  edge  of  the  city.  He  seemed  old  to  me  at  the  time,  but  now  I  realize  that  he  was  merely  middle-  aged.  His  house  had  window  frames  without  panes  and  door  frames  with-  out  doors,so  that  the  warm  air  could  circulate.  Also  circulating  through  these  various  openings  was  an  elabo-  rate  menagerie.  I  remember  a  small  green-and-red  parrot  whose  wings  had  been  cruelly  clipped  by  a  former  owner.  It  could  not  leave  the  ground.  This  parrot  could  usually  be  found  describing  fond  circles  around  the  feet  of  its  present  owner.  There  was  a  six-  foot  iguana,  which  I  never  saw  but  which  was  reputed  to  visit  the  Yugo-  slay  every  morning  at  the  bottom  of  his  garden.  There  were  three  goats,  which  walkedaround  the  dining-room  table,  begging  for  food  or  paper  nap-  kins  from  the  guests  at  lunch.  There  was  a  particularly  ugly  turkey,  which   answered  when  spoken  to.  &quot;You  are  beau-ti-ful,&quot;  the  man  would  say,  and  then  he  would  add,  &quot;You  ere  my  beauty,&quot;  punctuating  the  words.  The  turkey  would  shake  its  head  toward  the  sky  and  then  acknowledge  the  compliment  with  a  long,  guttural  re-  ply.  The  man  who  spoke  to  animals,  whose  name  I  have  long  ago  forgot-  ten,  had  a  tall,  pale-blond,  no-longer-  young  wife,  alsoa  Yugoslav,  and  a  brand-new,  blond  and  white,  delicate  baby,  whose  skin  had  a  pale-blue  cast,  like  watered  milk,  and  who  slept  in  an  all-white  cot  under  mosquito  netting.  The  Yugoslav  was  an  honorary  houn-  gan,  or  voodoo  priest.  He  could  go  where  no  other  white  man  was  al-  lowed.  At  times,  without  the  slightest  persuasion,  my  husband  would  follow  him,  and  they  would  disappear  into  the  hills  late  at  night  for  secret  ceremo-  nies.  They  went  where  the  sound  of  drums  came  from,  and  my  husband  would  reappear  at  dawn,  adding  yet  another  aspect  to  the  already  white  nights.  Often  we  would  leave  Mon  R\xc3\xa9ve  to  go  to  the  [REDACTED]  d'Art.  I  know  that  it  was  a  lovely,  beigy  house  with  steps  leading  to  its  entrance,  and  I  think,  but  am  not  certain,  that  it  stood  in  the  middle  ofa  garden.  I  do  remember  the  [REDACTED]  d'Art  jeep  vividly.  It  was  painted  all  over,  by  different  painters'  hands,  mostly  in  riots  of  flow-  ers,  and  it  could  be  seen  regularly  in  the  streets  of  [REDACTED]-au-Prince.  The  man  in  charge  at  the  [REDACTED]  d'Art  was  De-  Witt  Peters,  and  he  was  solely  responsible  for  the  renaissance  of  Hai-  tian  art. His  [REDACTED]  d'Art  was  opened  in  1944.  It  pro-  vided  artists  with  en-  couragement  and  tech-  nical  assistance.  It  also  gave  them  training. Then  came  Rigaud  Benoit,  a  cab-  driver,  who  brought  a  vase  he  had  decorated  with  pink  roses.  (The  same  delicate,  Persian-  looking  roses  appear  in  his  paintings  to  this  day.)  Others  came  along,  among  them  the  very  talented  Castera  Bazile,  who  was  Peters'  houseboy.  DeWitt  Peters  wrote  in  his  journal  about  his  first  encounter  with  the  art  of  the  one  acknowl-  edged  genius  among  the  painters.  One  day  in  the  autumn  of  1943,  as  he  was  driving  through  Mont  Rouis  (a  coastal  village  between  [REDACTED]-  au-Prince  and  Saint  [REDACTED]),  he  saw  two  gaily  decorated  door  panels  on  the  porch  of  a  roadside  bar.  The  name  of  the  bar,  posted  on  a  shingle,  was  Ici  la  Renaissance.  Peters  found  out  that  the  name  of  the  paint-  er  was  Hector  Hyppolite,  and  that  he  was  an  itinerant  housepainter  and  houngan  who  lived  in  Saint  [REDACTED].  There,  he  had  been  close  to  starva-  tion  for  fifteen  years,  but,  with  left-  over  house  paint  and  brushes  he  had  made  of  chicken  feathers,  he  had  nev-  ertheless  managed  to  paint  crude  and  luminous  paintings  of  tropical  birds  and  flowers,  still-lifes  of  fruit,  and  por-  traits  of  voodoo  gods.  Hyppolite  was  a  mystic  who  had  been  advised  in  a  dream  that  a  white  man  would  discov-  er  him,  so  he  readily  followed  Peters  to  [REDACTED]-au-Prince,  where,  in  less  than  three  years,  in  a  white  heat,  he  painted  more  than  a  hundred  pictures.  They  are,  of  course,  uneven.  But  some  of  them  are  splendrous,  and  today  Hyppo-  lite  is  considered  one  of  the  great  nat-  ural  painters  of  modern  time.  (He  died  of  a  heart  attack,  suddenly,  in  1948.)  By  1950,  Hyppolite's  paintings  were  enjoying  a  fame  unmatched  by  that  of  any  other  Haitian  artist.  There  was  a  greater  dimension  to  his  work:  it  was  flamboyant,  darkly  inspired,  and  there  was  something  almost  combative  in  its  naivet\xc3\xa9.  In  1950,  if  one  felt  like  buying  a  Haitian  primitive  it  was  logical  and  wise,  almost  inevitable,  to  think  in terms  of  an  Hyppolite.  At  the  [REDACTED]  d'Art,  there  were  two  for  sale.  One  was  a  still-life,  a  predominantly  white  picture,  very  flat,  of  a  watermelon  and  other  fruit,  and  the  pink  of  the  melon  looked  warm  and  tender,  like  ablush  under  rice  powder.  I  wanted  that  one.  The  other  was  of  the  god  of  the  sea,  Agou\xc3\xa9,  standing  all  dressed  up  but  barefoot,  with  his  wife,  or  mistress,  Maitresse  Erzulie,  in  what  appears  to  be  a  flower  garden  (appears,  because  its  greens  are  very  blue).  Agou\xc3\xa9  wears  a  naval  uniform,  red  and  or-  ange.  Both  colors  bring  out  the  dark  brown  of  his  skin.  He  carries  not  a  trident  but  a  sceptre,  like  a  cane,  in  his  left  hand.  Maitresse  Erzulie  wears  a  long  dress,  also  red  and  orange,  and  a  yellow  turban,  and  she  is  equally  bare-  foot.  At  their  feet  lies  an  anchor.  This  painting  is  immensely  joyous  and  bright:  red  and  green  and  orange.  It  is  also  rather  crude,  but  powerful,  par-  ticularly  if  one  compares  it  with  the  tender  still-life  I  wanted.  Without  a  single  breath  of  hesitation,  my  hus-  band  chose  &quot;Agou\xc3\xa9  and  Maitresse  Er-  zulie,&quot;  and  I  did  not  attempt  a  single  word  in  favor  of  the  still-life.
