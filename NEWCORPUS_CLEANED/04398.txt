 b"EARLY  in  1949,  soon  after  the  E  Communist  armies  took  Peiping,  old  Mr.  Yu,  my  Chinese  father-  in-law,  died,  leaving  my  wife,  Aimee,  and  her  two  brothers  and  eight  sisters  the  joint  owners  of  the  Yu  family  man-  sion,  on  the  east  side  of  the  city.  At  the  same  time,  they  inherited  the  family's  dilapidated  ancestral  temple,  which  stood  on  the  shore  of  Peiping's  north-  ernmost  lake,  and  the  Yu  family  burial  plot,  somewhere  in  the  countryside.  This  was  only  a  small  part,  Aimee  told  me,  of  what  her  family  had  owned  when  she  was  a  girl.  There  had  been  villas  and  farming  land  in  the  country,  other  houses  in  the  city,  and,  above  all,  gold-bars,  cups,  chains,  and  bracelets  of  gold  in  a  multitude  of  sizes,  and  the  bulk  of  it  in  one-ounce  boat-shaped  in-  gots,  sometimes  called  &quot;shoes,&quot;  since  they  resembled  the  small  silk  shoes  worn  by  Chinese  women  with  bound  feet.  In  Aimee's  youth,  all  this  gold  had  been  kept  in  the  Peiping  mansion  (neither  banks  nor  paper  money  were  to  be  trust-  ed),  concealed  in  pots  under  the  floor  tiles  and  courtyard  flagstones  or  hidden  in  secret  recesses  within  the  walls;  and  it  would  all  still  be  there,  Aimee  la-  mented,  had  her  father  not  been  such  a  staunch  patriot.  Mr.  Yu  had  been  at  one  time  the  Chief  Justice  of  the  Chinese  Supreme  Court,  and  when,  about  1934,  the  gov-  ernment  asked  all  Chinese  citizens  to  turn  their  private  hoards  of  gold  in-  to  national  currency  to  help  resist  the  Japanese  aggressors,  he  had  piled  all  the  gold  he  could  find  into  his  horse  carriage  (he  owned  an  automobile  but  refused  to  ride  in  it)  and  taken  it  off  to  a  central  bank,  where  the  amazed  presi-  dent  had  converted  it  into  freshly  printed  one-yuan  bills.  The  habits  of  tradition  were  not  en-  tirely  disregarded,  though,  because  old  Mr.  Yu  had  carried  the  new  bills  back  home  again  for  safekeeping.  This  move,  however,  was  to  no  avail.  As  a  result  of  inflation  and  the  advance  of  the  Japanese,  the  bills  had  been  ab-  ruptly  withdrawn  from  circulation  within  two  years,  leaving  Mr.  Yu  a  poorer  but  wiser  patriot.  Through  the  years,  the  Yu  children  from  time  to  time  had  found,  at  the  bottoms  of  old  chests  and  in  the  backs  of  drawers,  reminders  of  their  father's  fol-  ly.  I  found  some,  too.  As  an  American,  I  had  lost  my  job  teaching  English  at  a  national  Chinese  university  after  the     Communists  came  to  power,  and  I  had  moved  with  Aimee,  the  fourth  Yu  daughter,  into  the  mansion,  where  we  occupied  a  suite  of  rooms  that  had  been  Mr.  Yu's  private  library  and  study.  One  day,  I  came  across  acache  of  faded  notes,  and  on  another  occasion  Idis-  covered  a  pack  of  a  hundred  yellowed  bills  still  bound  in  their  original  wrapper. On  his  deathbed,  Mr.  Yu  had  felt  it  necessary  to  assure  his  children  that  they  were  not  destitute. Several  tall  pine  trees  grew  close  beside  the  temple's  dilapidated  main  gate,  and  its  stone  steps  were  strewn  with  needles  and  pine  cones  that  had  fallen  in  the  storm.  Picking  our  way  through  them,  we  found  an  old  man  in-  side  the  gate  who,  when  we  questioned  him,  offered  to  lead  us  to  Aunt  Chin's  quarters.  His  wrinkled  face  looked  curi-  ously  soft  and  hairless,  and  I  wondered  if  he  was  a  eunuch.  He  led  us  through  a  series  of  courts,  none  of  them  in  very " b"good  condition,  past  a  central  prayer  hall  with  a  yellow  tiled  roof,  through  a  gate  of  faded  turquoise,  and  into  a  side  courtyard  filled  with  what  looked  like  the  remains  of  a  vegetable  garden.  At  the  north  end  of  the  garden,  its  white-  papered  windows  facing  south,  stood  a  neat  building  containing  perhaps  three  rooms,  and,  to  its  side,  a  kind  of  green-  house.  Next  to  this  greenhouse  was  a  grove  of  thorny  bushes.  &quot;Is  anyone  home?&quot;  Aimee  called,  after  theold  man  had  left  us.  There  was  a  sudden  rustling  of  thorny  branches,  and  Aunt  Chin,  look-  ing  somewhat  dishevelled  and  carrying  a  pan  of  small  yellow  berries,  pushed  herself  into  view.  &quot;You've  come,&quot;  she  said  to  us.  &quot;We've  come,&quot;  Aimee  answered.  Aunt  Chin  asked  about  us  and  other  members  of  the  family,  and  we  gave  her  what  news  we  had,  but  we  said  nothing  then  about  the  old  mansion.  Later,  we  all  sat  down  to  lunch  in  Aunt  Chin's  sitting  room,  which  she  had  furnished  with  her  own  tables  and  chairs.  Although  I  remembered  them  well  from  the  mansion,  they  looked  as  if  they  had  sat  in  that  room  for  many  years.  Aunt  Chin  had  hung  three  paint-  ings  on  the  wall;  far  from  being  the  elaborate  blue-and-green  palace-filled  landscapes  she  had  displayed  in  the  old  house,  these  were  of  a  pomegranate  on  a  branch,  a  pair  of  crabs,  and  a  sleep-  ing  cat.  Aunt  Chin's  companion  carried  our  food  over  from  the  temple's  main  kitchen.  As  long  as  we  were  in  a  Buddhist  temple,  Aunt  Chin  said,  it  was  only  proper  that  we  eat  a  Buddhist  vegetarian  meal.  We  were  served  mush-  rooms  and  tree  ears  (a  kind  of  fungus)  and  bean  curd,  and  there  were  pickled  plums,  and  lotus  seeds  boiled  in  sugar  water,  and  eggs  cured  in  lime,  and,  as  a  last  course,  caramelized  sweet  pota-  toes.  While  Aimee  and  I,  who  had  been  enduring  Elder  Sister's  cooking,  ate  as  if  we  had  forgotten  the  taste  of  food,  Aunt  Chin,  looking  surprisingly  well,  talked  about  her  new  life.  &quot;This  used  to  be  an  herb  garden,&quot;  she  said,  indi-  cating  the  garden  outside  the  windows.  &quot;During  the  dynasty  it  supplied  medici-  nal  herbs  to  the  palace.  Did  you  notice  that  I  have  begun  to  recultivate  the  plots  nearest  the  house?&quot;  I  had  noticed  when  we  came  in  that  some  dandelion-  like  weeds  were  growing  in  neat  rows  near  the  doorstep,  but  it  hadn't  occurred  to  me  that  they  were  herbs,  and  it  cer-  tainly  hadn't  occurred  to  me  that  Aunt  Chin  was  cultivating  them.  &quot;The  monk " b" who  tended  this  garden  is  very  old,&quot;  Aunt  Chin  went  on,  &quot;and  spends  most  of  his  time  these  days  in  the  Buddha  Hall-'meditating,'  he  says-but  I've  persuaded  him  to  teach  me  all  he  can  remember  about  herbs.  He  gave  me  his  old  herb  and  medicine  books,  too,  since  he  doesn't  use  them  any  more,  and  he  gave  me  the  original  chart  of  the  garden.  Wait,  I'll  show  you.&quot;  Aunt  Chin  bustled  into  a  side  room  and  came  back  in  a  moment  with  a  large  roll  of  stiff  paper,  which  she  opened  on  the  desk.  The  paper  was  di-  vided  into  squares  and  rectangles,  each  containing  a  picture  of  the  sun  and  a  crescent  moon,  with  numbers  and  vari-  ous  mysterious-looking  symbols  under  them.  &quot;This  is  a  planting  chart,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;It  will  take  me  several  years,  but  with  the  help  of  this  and  theold  monk  and  the  books,  I  think  I  can  replant  much  of  the  garden.&quot;  &quot;But  why  do  you  want  to  do  that?&quot;  Aimee  objected.  &quot;It's  too  much  work  for  you,  and  who  wants  all  those  herbs,  anyway?&quot;&quot;  &quot;People  seem  to  goon  dying  just  as  they  always  did,  in  spite  of  all  the  mod-  ern  chemical  medicines  they  keep  stuffing  into  themselves,&quot;  Aunt  Chin  said.  &quot;My  herbs  may  notkeep  people  alive,  but  they  won't  kill  them. Gossip  and  cards  and  cats  had  been  her  only  occupation  for  years,  and  yet  here  she  was,  ejected  from  her  home,  thrust  into  a  world  strange  and,  I  would  have  guessed,  inhospitable  to  her,  learning  what  looked  to  me  very  much  like  a  new  trick. When  I  fin-  ished,  she  looked  neither  saddened  nor  surprised.  &quot;It  is  too  bad  that  the  garden  is  gone,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;It  was  very  beauti-  ful,  but  it  was  an  old  garden. Aunt  Chin  looked  at  me.  &quot;I  may  not  see  you  again,&quot;  she  said,  &quot;and  gold  is  a  poor  gift  to  remember  me  by.&quot;  She  turned  to  her  companion.  &quot;Putsome  of  that  new  herb  tea  into  a  jar  and  wait  for  me  here,&quot;  she  said.
