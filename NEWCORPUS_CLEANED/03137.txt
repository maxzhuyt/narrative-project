Master  Sal-  tonstall,  a  student  here  at  Blessed  Monstrance  Junior  High,  was  at  home  in  the  evening  when  he  received  a  per-  son-to-person  call  from  President  Rich-  ard  Nixon,  in  [REDACTED].  The  Chief  Executive  told  Packy  that  he  wanted  to  congratulate  him  on  the  fine  record  achieved  this  year  by  the  Blessed  Mon-  strance  football  team,  for  which  young  Saltonstall  served  as  manager  and  wa-  ter  boy.  Master  Saltonstall  thanked  the  President  but  told  him  that  it  had  been  a  disappointing  campaign  for  the  Monsters,  who  finished  with  a  1-8  won-and-lost  record.  &quot;Besides,&quot;  he  add-  ed,  &quot;the  football  season  has  been  over  for  weeks  and  weeks  around  here,  sir.&quot;  When  asked  by  Mr.  Nixon  if  he  could  be  of  any  service  in  some  other  field,  Packy  admitted  that  he  had  been  having  some  recent  difficulties  with  algebra.  The  President  offered  assistance,  stating,  &quot;I've  always  been  an  algebra  nut,  in  my  own  small  way,&quot;  and  within  a  matter  of  minutes  Master  Saltonstall  had  presented  him  with  a  challenging  problem  involving  two  rival  canoeists  paddling  upstream  at  different  rates  in  a  rain-fed  brook  that  is  also  threatening  to  run  dry  be-  cause  of  a  mysterious  underground  leak.  Mr.  Nixon  copied  down  the  ques-  tion  and  then,  after  a  pause,  told  Packy  Saltonstall  that  he  would  call  back  shortly.  The  re-  turn  call  came  an  hour  and  thirty-seven  minutes  later,  and  the  President  cheerfully  gave  his  young  friend  the  answer:  7.2631  miles.  &quot;I've  always  been  an  algebra  nut,  in  my  own  small  way,&quot;  the  President  said,  chuckling,  &quot;but  just  to  make  sure,  I  got  some  help  from  the  Bureau  of  the  Budget.&quot;  Master  Saltonstall  in-  cluded  the  President's  an-  swer  in  the  math  home-  work  he  handed  in  the  next  day,  and  was  dis-  appointed  when  it  came  back  marked  incorrect.  The  right  answer,  accord-  ing  to  Packy's  algebra  teacher,  Miss  Grogan,  should  have  been  eleven  gallons.  Partly  as  a  result  of  this  error,  Saltonstall  received  a  D-mi-  nus  on  his  homework  paper,  but  he  told  reporters  nonetheless  that  he  would  &quot;probably  never  forget&quot;  Mr.  Nixon's  surprise  call.
