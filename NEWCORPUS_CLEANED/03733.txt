The  I.B.M.  gadgetry  in  the  bowels  of  the  [REDACTED]  was  put  to  work  and  came  up  with  two  cards,  mine  and  one  other.  The  man  from  the  State  Department  did  his  best  to  make  the  job  sound  attractive,  but  at  that  point  my  wish  was  to  slip  out  of  uniform  and  go  home.  By  home,  I  did  not  mean  Budapest.  I  should  never  have  told  Father  about  it.  His  reaction  was  startlingly  violent.  &quot;The  chance  of  a  lifetime,&quot;  he  said,  waving  his  arms.  &quot;You  passed  up  the  chance  of  a  lifetime.  Why,  you  would  have  been  hailed  as  the  Savior  of  the  Country.  My  God.  You  could  have  been  knee-deep  in  countesses.  You  could  ...  ah,  never  mind.&quot;  About  an  hour  later,  he  had  one  of  his  minor  seizures,  and  I  felt  sure  I  had  helped  to  bring  it  on.  The  seizures  were  not  too  different  from  epileptic  fits-frightening,  until  we  learned  how  to  cope  with  them.  Luckily,they  did  not  occur  often;  sometimes  months  went  by  without  one.  He  was  fairly  good  about  taking  his  medicine,  although  he  never  wholly  accepted  the  doctors'  unanimous  view  that  he  had  cerebral  arteriosclerosis.  He  attributed  his  illness  to  the  beating  in  the  lobby.  He  settled  down  to  visiting  his  favorite  doctor-a  Hungarian,  of  course-once  a  month.  Before  long,  his  reports  of  these  visits  ossified  into  a  ritual  whose  words  and  accents  never  varied:  &quot;Doctor  says  I'm  in  pretty  good  shape,  considering.  He  says  I  ought  to  find  some  kind  of  hobby  to  fill  my  time.  What  would  I  want  with  a  hobby?&quot;  The  fact  was,  he  did  have  a  hobby,  which  afforded  him  fanatical  joy.  He  kept  track  of  Hungarians  who  had  left  the  old  country  and  achieved  a  measure  of  distinction  elsewhere.  Poring  over  the  \xc3\xa9migr\xc3\xa9  press  and  the  gossip  columns  of  all  the  New  York  papers,  he  amassed  an  astonishing  fund  of  information.  He  could  list  all  the  husbands  of  all  the  Gabor  sisters  and  their  mother.  He  could,  and  would,  tell  you  what  Eugene  Ormandy's  real  name  had  been,  and  Tony  Curtis's  and  Leslic  Howard's  and  Sir  Alexander  Korda's  ancestors.  He  had  a  particular  fondness  for  Nobel  Prize  winners  like  de  Hevesy,  von  B\xc3\xa9k\xc3\xa9sy,Szent-Gyorgyi,  and  Wigner.  He  had  known  Arthur  Koest-  ler's  family  in  Buda-  pest,  and  everybody  else's  second  cousin  in  some  provincial  town  or  other;  hadn't  they  run  a  feed  store  in  Csongrad  or  practiced  law  in  Szombathely?  I  do  not  believe  that,  aft-  er  forty  years  in  the  United  [REDACTED],  Father  could  have  told  you  who  Robert  E.  Lee  was,  or  the  difference  between  [REDACTED]  and  the  [REDACTED].  But  he  really  kept  track  of  his  Hungarians.  About  the  time  of  the  Oppenheimer  hear-  ings,  I  had  dinner  at  my  parents'  apartment  with  a  cousinof  mine,  who  is  a  scientist.  After  dinner,  my  cousin  and  I  were  having  our  coffee  while  Father  dozed  in  an  easy  chair  behind  us.  We  talked  about  the  hearings,  Edward  Teller,  one  of  Father's  eminent  Hungarians,  had  just  testified.  My  cousin,  though  a  good  friend  and  collaborator  of  Tel-  ler's,  was  not  altogether  happy  about  the  testimony,  and  said  so.  Then  he  left  the  room  for  a  while.  Father,  his  eyes  still  half  closed,  said,  &quot;Teller  could  have  been  mistaken.  Anybody  can  be.  But  don't  sell  Hun-  gary  short.  Did  you  ever  hear  of  Isadora  Duncan?  The  dancer?&quot;  &quot;Was  she  Hungarian,  too?&quot;  &quot;Don't  be  silly.  But  it  took  a  Hun-  garian  to  seduce  her.&quot;  &quot;Now,  now,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;I  read  her  book,  too.  Lots  of  people  seduced  Isa-  dora  Duncan.  Americans.  Russians.  Goatherds  in  Greece.&quot;  &quot;I'm  talking  about  the  first  time.  It  took  a  Hungarian.  Plenty  of  people  had  tried  before,  including  the  French.  Ever  hear  of  Auguste  Rodin?  The  sculptor?  He  tried.  Nothing  doing.  It  took  a  Hungarian:  Oszkir  Beregi.  Teacher  of  mine  at  dramatic  school-  the  Academy.  It's  all  in  her  book.  Great  actor,  Beregi.  Still  alive.  Great  Hamlet,  great  Romeo.  Do  comedy,  anything.  If  Edward  Teller  made  a  mistake,  you  may  be  sure  it  was  an  honest  one.&quot;  Then  he  nodded  off  to  sleep  again.  "
