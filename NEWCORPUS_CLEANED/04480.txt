Apart  from  religion,  her  taste  ran  to  cheap  novelettes,  though  she  studied  and  discussed  them  rather  as  though  they  were  reports  of  police-court  cases.  &quot;Ah,  woman!&quot;  she  would  say  im-  patiently  to  Mother.  &quot;Sure  he  was  a  fool  to  have  anything  to  do  with  her. Great  as  was  the  disgust  caused  in  the  neighbor-  hood  by  these  goings  on,  no  one-not  even  the  priests-could  interfere  with  the  girl  while  Teresa  stood  by,  but  she  took  her  payment  in  gossip.  &quot;Wisha,  wait  till  I  tell  you,&quot;  she  would  say  hum-  bly,  coming  in  and  throwing  off  her  coat,  and  then  for  an  hour  or  more  we  got  it  all,  as  if  it  were  a  novelette.  &quot;But  if  he  felt  that  way,  Mrs.  Delaney,&quot;  she  would  say,  putting  her  hand  flat  on  Mother's  knee,  &quot;why  did  he  use  those  words  to  her?&quot;  Teresa  had  an  extraordinarily  strik-  ing  face,  with  high  cheekbones  and  sunken  eyes,  thick  lips  like  apoet,  and  a  toothless  mouth.  I  remember  with  ex-  traordinary  vividness  the  hasty,  recol-  lected  shuffie  of  her  feet  in  some  foot-  gear  that  never  seemed  to  fit,  the  ragged  old  coat  that  she  clutched  together  across  her  breast,  and  the  bowed  gray  head  that  would  suddenly  be  raised  to  reveal  the  most  enchanting  smile  in  the  world.  Before  God,  I  am  not  exaggerat-  ing!  Sometimes  it  comes  before  me  in  the  early  hours  of  the  morning,  and  it  seems  as  if  all  the  suffering  and  delight  of  humanity  were  in  that  one  strange  smile.  Teresa  had  been  for  some  time in  the  Big  House,  as  she  euphemistically  called  the  Lunatic  Asylum,  and  some-  oneonce  told  me  that  while  she  was  there  she  wept  almost  without  ceasing.  The  doctors  called  it  acute  melancholia,  but  what  do  doctors  know  of  what  may  be  revealed  to  people  like  Teresa?  What-  ever  it  was,  it  had  left  no  trace  on  her  character  except  when  she  saw  suffer-  ing  inflicted  on  an  animal,  and  then  she  went  stark,  staring  mad;  screamed  in  the  middle  of  the  street,  and  stamped  off  to  complain  to  the  police  or  the  So-  ciety  for  the  Prevention  of  Cruelty  to  Animals.  They  all  knew  she  was  only  an  old  maid  who  had  spent  a  lot  of  her  time  in  the  Lunatic  Asylum,  and  so  they  paid  little  heed  to  her.  Yet  that  woman  who  had  been  in  the  asylum  was  the  sanest  woman  I  have   ever  met-and  the  finest.  Sometimes  when  I  think  of  her,  I  see  her  holding  a  poisoned  cat  or  a  starving  dog,  talking  to  it,  her  face  all  lit  up,  and  think  how  thin  and  vulgar  that  window  was  in  the  church  that  was  supposed  to  show  the  Good  Shepherd.  Wherever  sickness  or  death  was,  there  would  come  the  knock,  and  Teresa  would  be  standing  outside  with  her  ragged  old  coat  clutched  about  her,  asking  humbly,  &quot;Is  there  anything  I  can  do  for  you,  Ma'am?&quot;-ready  to  scrub  or  cook  or  nurse  or  pray,  to  hold  the  hand  of  some  dying  man  in  hers  or  wash  his  poor  dirty  body  when  the  life  was  gone.  If,  when  everything  was  over,  they  gave  her  a  shilling,  she  would  be  too  humble  to  re-  fuse  it,  unless  she  knew  they  were  as  poor  as  herself,  and  then  she  pushed  it  away  with  a  gentle  smile  and  said  dryly,  &quot;You'll  be  wanting  that  yourself,  Ma'am.&quot;  Yet  what  she  was  so  gently  rejecting  might  be  her  own  dinner  for  the  same  day.  When  I  grew  up  and  realized  that  the  woman  I  had  been  dealing  with  was  a  saint,  I  understood  both  her  terrible  pride  and  her  terrible  abnegation.  She  cannot  have  been  an  easy  woman  to  get  on  with,  because,  though  she  and  Mother  adored  one  an-  other,  and  up  to  the  day  of  Teresa's  death  shared  their  last  confidences  and  their  last  pennies,  there  were  long  spells  " b"of  not-speaking,  when  both  of  them  were  as  hurt  as  schoolgirls.  Teresa,  as  I  say,  had  a  passion  for  novelettes,  which  she  fed  to  poor  Moth-  er,  who,  though  she  could  never  resist  a  love  story,  no  matter  how  bad,  or  deny  a  tear  to  suffering  virtue,  had  a  natural  distinction  of  taste.  When  as  a  child  in  the  orphanage  she  suffered  from  somnambulism,  the  nuns  found  her  one  night  wandering  the  corridors  in  her  nightdress,  reciting  &quot;Farewell,  a  long  farewell  to  all  my  greatness.&quot;  I  doubt  if  Teresa  knew  a  single  poem,  but  she  had  a  fierce,  combative,  masculine  intellect,  even  if  it  didn't  always  func-  tion  in  an  orthodox  way,  and  would  have  had  no  hesitation  in  putting  down  the  Pope  himself  in  the  matter  of  doc-  trines  she  disapproved  of.  She  told  Mother  that  the  Church  was  going  to  hell,  with  all  the  vulgarians  who  were  being  raised  to  the  altars,  and  com-  mented  so  bitterly  on  the  difference  between  her  two  namesakes-Theresa  of  Avila,  a  woman  of  intellect,  whom  she  venerated,  and  Th\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xa9se  of  Lisieux,  who  from  Teresa's  point  of  view  was  only  a  stage-struck  child. But  he  couldn't  resist  standing  at  the  door  in  his  shirtsleeves,  or  talking  to  the  other  men,  or  digging  in  the  little  garden.  Within  a  few  weeks  Nora  Farrell  locked  the  door  on  him.  He  got  in  through  the  window.  She  did  it  again  and  he  started  to  break  down  the  door.  By  this  time  Father  was  beginning  to  say  Hennessey  was  &quot;no  balk,&quot;  which  from  Father  meant  that  he  was  more  of  a  man  than  he  had  seemed.  When  the  spring  flowers  started  to  shoot  in  the  little  front  garden,  Nora  poured  buckets  of  red-hot  cinders  on  them.  Hennessey  affected  to  believe  that  the  plants  merely  needed  water,  and  dragged  buckets  of  it  from  the  back  yard,  spilling  them  all  in  the  kitchen,  till  he  flooded  the  old  woman  out.  She  took  him  to  court,  but  he  defended  him-  self  manfully,  and  the  magistrate,  be-  ginning  to  get  suspicious  of  the  gallant,  bonneted,  deaf  old  lady  who  was  always  in  trouble,  gave  the  decision  in  his  favor.  Defiantly  Nora  shouted  as  she  left  the  court,  &quot;The  Corkies  has  me  bet  at  last!&quot;  To  the  end  she  remained  a  loyal  Tipperary-woman. The  figureof  this  woman  she  had  not  spoken  to  in  more  than  twenty  years  must  have  warned  theold  woman  what  was  in  store  for  her,  but  she  kept  a  contemptuous  silence.  Teresa  washed  and  cleaned  the  room  and  then  sent  for  the  priest.
