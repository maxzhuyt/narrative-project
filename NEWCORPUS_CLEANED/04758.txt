THE  PICTURE  Is  ALEXANDER  arrived  at  M  Hardpan  Farm  late  one  July  afternoon,  shakenby  her  train  trip  from  New  York.  The  three  hours  had  seemed  more  like  six.  Oh,  those  gorges  beside  the  roadbed,  those  ravines,  those  cliffs!  The  thing  was  that  such  steep  declivities,  which  had  always  hitherto  delighted  Miss  Alexander's  painter's  eye,as  having  the  sharp,  airy  quality  of  a  Japanese  print,  now  seemed  to  have  added  a  dark  dimension  she  did  not  yet  know  what  to  do  with:  they  kept  seeming  to  yawn  wider,  to  ex-  pose  a  menacing  facet  up  to  now  con-  cealed.  How  would  one  paint  that?  Miss  Alexander  kept  wondering  all  the  way  up  in  the  train.  She  hoped  that  this  sofar  unsolved  artistic  problem  presaged  a  new  development  in  her  technique.  Mrs.  Harley  and  Miss  Hughes  passed  her  on  their  way  out  to  walk,  as  she  stood,  newly  arrived,  in  the  hall  of  the  small  sanitarium  in  the  country  that  the  Doctor  ran  for  eight  patients.  Dying  of  curiosity,  they  took  in  the  thin  face,  the  huge  scared  eyes,  the  dark  crest  of  hair,  the  shrinking  figure.  Mrs.  Harley,  whose  social  reflexes  had  been  set  at  an  early  age  in  [REDACTED]  drawing  rooms,  swam  forward  and  took  Miss  Alexander's  hand.  &quot;You're  Miss  Alexander,&quot;  she  declared.  &quot;We've  all  been  looking  forward  so  to  your  ar-  rival.&quot;  Miss  Hughes  took  over  Miss  Alex-  ander's  limp  hand  and  gazed  earnestly  into  her  eyes.  &quot;I'm  terribly  glad  you've  come  to  it,&quot;  she  said.  Miss  Alexander,  who  had  no  social  reflexes  whatsoever  and  had  not  been  aware  of  coming  to  anything  but  the  country  for  a  rest,  shrank  still  more.  &quot;I'm  afraid  there's  no  question  what  her  type  is,&quot;  Mrs.  Harley  said  sadly  as  the  two  of  them  turned  up  the  road  to  the  Goose  Farm.  &quot;She'll  be  put  on  the  ground  floor  with  the  introverts,  all  right,&quot;  Miss  Hughes  agreed.  Mrs.  Martin,  the  housekeep-  er,  who  had  gone  to  fetch  the  Doctor  from  his  study,  now  re-  turned  and,  while  the  little  white-haired  psychiatrist  shook  Miss  Alexander's  hand,  carried  her  bags  upstairs  to  the  coom  with  the  balcony.  &quot;I  want  you  to  get  a  real  rest,  now,&quot;  the  Doctor  said  sharply.  &quot;Ido  need  one,&quot;  Miss  Alex-  ander  said  politely.  Her  eyes  wandered  past  his  face-pink,  glittering  glasses,  white  mus-  tache-to  the  dim  living  room  beyond:  ugly,  yellow-ochre  sofa,  round  Victo-  rian  table,  rug  in  ochre  and  gamboge  and  rose  madder;  one  beautiful  thing,  a  china  pitcher  on  a  window  sill.  &quot;Well,  now,  give  yourself  what  you  need,&quot;  he  said,  peering  at  her  over  the  tops  of  his  glasses,  dissatisfied.  Miss  Alexander  gave  him  a  half  smile.  She  felt  good  will  toward  the  Doctor,  to  whom  a  worried  friend  had  sent  her  in  May,  but  she  never  seemed  to  have  anything  to  say  to  him.  &quot;Say  anything  that  comes  into  your  head,&quot;  he  had  adjured  her;  but  nothing  came-noth-  ing,  that  is,  that  could  be  put  into  words.  She  had  neverbeen  much  of  a  talker,  and  besides,  the  Doctor  wanted  her  to  talk  about  the  past.  Miss  Alexander  was  against  hashing  over  the  past.  The  past,  she  felt,  was  a  sort  of  dunghill  up  from  which  sprang,  perhaps,  one's  art;  but  it  was  wrong  to  go  fiddling  about  with  the  dung.  Those  hours  of  total  silence  with  the  Doctor  had  come  high,  too,  and  Miss  Alexander  had  been  on  the  point  of  giving  them  up  when  he  had  made  the  suggestion  that  she  come  and  get  a  rest  in  the  country,  To  that  Miss  Alexander  could  respond,  and  did,  with  every  plucking  nerve  in  her  body.  &quot;Oh,  yes!&quot;  she  had  cried,  and  the  Doctor,  encour-  aged,  had  gone  on,  &quot;I  expect  you'd  like  somegood  walks,  some  good  nights'  sleep.&quot;  But  she  felt  unable  to  reply.  She  did  not  live  in  a  world  of  good  walks  and  nights'  sleep.  She  only  stared  at  him  and  figured  out  what  colors  would  go  into  his  skin  tone. As  she  looked,  she  had  the  sensation  that  the  ring  of  woods  was  about  to  open  and  expose  a  hidden  horror;  she  turned  before  it  could,  and  went  back  into  the  bedroom.  There  was  not  very  much  to  unpack.  Four  cotton  dresses  soon  hung,  faintly  swaying  in  a  little  breeze,  in  the  open  closet.  Four  dresses  should  last  ten  days,  and  ten  days  was  a  good  rest  in  the  country.  Miss  Alexander  took  a  sketch-  book  and  three  pencils  and  went  down  to  a  garden  she  had  seen  from  above.  As  she  had  observed,  several  deck  chairs  stood  about,  facing  into  the  deep  shadows  of  the  trees  that  lined  the  drive-  way  back  to  the  barn.  She  had  not,  how-  ever,  seen  that  one  of  them  was  oc-  cupied.  As  Miss  Alexander  passed  this  chair  a  woman,  middle-aged,  with  mouse-colored  hair,  stopped  fanning  herself  with  a  palm-leaf  fan  toask,  &quot;Are  you  the  new  patient?&quot;  Miss  Alexander  had  not  thought  of  herself  as  a  patient;  she  seldom  thought  of  herself  as  anything  except  an  artist;  she  could  not  summon  a  reply.  &quot;I'm  in  the  collective  unconscious,&quot;  the  woman  said,  and  relapsed  into  fan-  ning  herself.  What  could  she  mean?  Big  words  dismayed  Miss  Alexander.  She  sat  down  in  another  deck  chair,  at  some  distance,  and  opened  her  sketchbook.  The  tree  immediately  in  frontof  her  was  an  oak,  massive  and  intricate;  against  the  gray  stone  wall  under  it  shone  the  green  and  ruby  grapes  of  nightshade;  beyond  lay  peach  and  apple  orchards.  She  began  to  indicate  the  tortuosities  ofthe  tree:  with  tentative  and  hesitant  strokes,  as  though  waiting  for  her  hand  to  be  guided.  This  was  her  standard  approach,  and  her  hand  always  was  guided.  She  had  suggested  no  more  than  a  trunk,  a  nimbus  of  branches,  when  a  huge  and  sav-  age  animal  thrust  its  faceinto  hers  and  breathed  outa  burning  blast.  She  would  have  screamed,  if  she  could  have.  Her  sketchbook  fell  to  the  grass,  her  pencils  were  scattered,  before  Miss  Alexander  realized  that  the  animal  was  a  dog-a  rough-coated,  yellow-  ochre  dog. She  was  not  accustomed  to  dogs,  but  terror  was  only  half  of  what  she  felt  for  this  one.  The  other  half  was  love,  or  at  least  a  desire  to  stick  her  tongue  out,  back  at  that  pink  slavering  tongue.  The  animal  had  by  now  sub-  sided  onto  its  haunches,  panting  in  the  hot  afternoon  with  its  mouth  hanging  open;  it  bobbed  gently  up  and  down  with  its  rapid  inhalations.  It  did  look  so  kind  and  foolish,  a  great  silly,  gazing  at  her  with  water  running  off  the  end  of  its  tongue,  that  she  wanted  to  laugh;  but  then  the  mouth  opened  a  little  wider,  and  evil  peered  at  her  around  the  sharp  teeth.  She  gathered  herself  for  flight,  but,  in  another  moment,  the  dog  smile  was  sweet  and  silly  again.  Cautiously  she  picked  up  her  sketch-  book  and  one  of  the  pencils,  without  moving  her  eyes  from  the  ambiguous  beast;  how  could  one  indicate,  in  paint-  ing,  the  alternate  existence  of  good  and  evil?
