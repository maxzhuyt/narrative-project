b"THE  ISLAND  T  was  the  beginning  of  November,  I  early  in  the  afternoon,  the  wind  from  the  north,  the  sky  clear,  the  tide  high-his  last  chance,  he  thought,  to  sail  to  the  island.  All  summer,  he  had  tried  to  reach  it,  but  the  weather  had  never  been  right-not  on  any  day  he  was  free  to  sail,  at  any  rate.  Either  the  wind  was  against  him,  or  there  wasn't  enough  of  it,  or  the  tide  was  too  low,  or  theseatoo  rough,  or  it  rained.  He  had  tried  many  times,  and  not  this  summer  alone.  Once,  quite  re-  cently,  he  almost  reached  it-oh,  came  within  a  thousand  feet-but  then  he  gave  up  because  of  the  late  hour.  He  knew  his  wife  would  worry,  and  when  he  got  back  she  had  in  fact  called  a  friend,  and  was  about  ready  to  drive  out  with  her  to  look  for  him.  If  only  she  would  stop  being  anxious  about  him.  She  made  him  feel  like  a  child.  Poor  girl,  he  thought,  she  had  no  other  man.  He  ..  .  did  have  another  woman,  and  he  was  going  to  see  her  the  next  night.  His  boat  was  small,  not  even  ten  feet  long.  It  weighed  less  than  a  hun-  dred  pounds.  But  it  wasn't  just  a  board.  It  was  shell-shaped,  so  that  one  could  get  in  it,  sit  comfortably,  and,  if  the  sea  wasn't  rough,  sail  around  with-  out  getting  wet.  When  he  bought  it  two  summers  before,  he  had  visions  of  going  to  the  open  sea  and  sailing  along  the  coast,  calling  at  different  ports,  only  to  find  that  he  couldn't  even  get  out  of  the  bay,  though  that  wasn't  very  extensive-about  four  miles  long  and  two  miles  wide.  Not  enough  sail,  he  soon  realized,  and  the  centerboard  too  short.  Not  drawing  deep  enough;  the  boat  didn't  point  well  but  drifted.  If  the  wind  was  dead  against  him,  no  matter  how  much  he  tacked  he  was  more  likely  to  lose  ground  than  to  make  headway.  But  if  the  wind  was  right,  as  today,  then  there  was  no  reason  he  shouldn't  reach  the  island.  He  would  just  run  with  the  wind,  and  as  for  returning  ...  Oh,  well,  he'd  find  some  way;  at  worst,  he'd  just  sail  on  to  a  point  on  the  shore  south  of  where  he  kept  the  boat  and  call  his  wife  to  come  with  the  car  and  fetch  him.  She  would  have  to  walk  to  the  beach  parking  lot  where  he  had  left  it,  but  that  wasn't  far.  The  car  was  a  large  old  convertible,  and  the  boat  was  small  enough  to  be  loaded  over  the  seats  and  trunk.  That,  indeed,  was  the  way  he  carried  it  out  to  the  beach  each  spring  and  brought  it  home  late  in  the  fall,  With  luck,  though,  he  wouldn't  need  to  bother  her.  Surely,  with  the  wind  behind  him  he  would  reach  the  island  in  an  hour,  and  that  would  give  him  three  hours  in  which  to  get  back  before  dark.  The  island-one  could  see  it  from  the  beach  where  he  stood-was  a  thin  strip  of  land  a  few  hundred  feet  long  and  maybe  thirty  wide.  At  high  tide  it  was  completely  submerged.  It  really  wasmore  a  shoal  than  an  island,  but  it  was  called  an  island  because  a  cen-  tury  ago  it  had  been  one,  with  houses,  even  a  shop.  Living  there,  the  fisher-  men  were  hours  nearer  the  fish  they  caught  than  if  they  had  lived  in  the  vil-  lage  proper.  Then,  little  by  little,  the  sea  claimed  more  and  more  of  it  until  there  was  only  a  vestige  left.  He  had  heard  that  there  were  still  bricks  to  be  found.  He  wanted  to  land  and  explore.  More,  he  wanted  to  reach  it  because  the  island  was  just  outside  the  bay,  on  the  open  sea.  It  was  strange  to  go  there  now  at  the  beginning  of  November,  when  the  sailboat  season  was  over.  There  had  been  no  frost  yet,  but  the  water  was  cold.  It  wasn't  even  a  weekend,  when  there  might  have  beenmotorboats  out.  Except  for  two  trawlers  plying  back  and  forth  across  the  bay,  there  were  no  boats  in  sight. He  thought  he'd  drive  part  of  the  way.  &quot;Perhaps  all  the  way,&quot;  he  said  to  himself,  taking  a  sandy  little  road  that  skirted  the  inlet.  A  sign  warned:&quot;Cars  Will  Get  Stuck.&quot;  He  disregarded  it,  and  at  the  end  ofthe  road,  only  a  few  hundred  feet  away  from  the  boat,  he  didget  stuck.  And  not  in  sand-in  mud.  What  a  fool,  what  a  dreadful  fool,  he  thought.  From  bad  to  worse.  Worse  than  yesterday.  And  he  could  so  easily  have  walked,  waited  for  the  tide  to  rise,  and  rowed  back.  With  jack       and  boards,  he  tried  to  shift  the  rear  of  the  car  to  firmer  soil,  but  the  weight  of  the  car  toppled  the  jack  over  the  wrong  way,  and  the  wheel  sank  deeper  and  deeper  in  the  mud.  And  the  tide  was  coming  in.  In  an  hour  or  two  his  car  would  be  swamped.  He  was  almost  ready  to  walk  back  around  the  inlet  to  the  nearest  house  and  call  some-  one  at  a  service  station  to  come  and  haul  him  out,  expensive  and  depressing  though  that  was,  when  he  saw  a  small  motorboat  crossing  the  inlet  toward  him.  A  frail-looking  older  man  with  white  hair  landed  and  came  over.  &quot;You're  stuck,  are  you?&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Well,  you're  not  the  first  one.&quot;  That  was  already  some  comfort.  &quot;Yes,  I  came  to  get  my  boat.  That  little  one  over  there.  Yesterday  evening,  I  got  stuck  with  it  in  the  mud  flats  and  had  to  leave  it.&quot;  &quot;Oh,  is  that  yours?  I  saw  it  carly  this  morning. He  looked  at  the  frail  man  and  felt  very  pessimis-  tic.  And,  indeed,  though  they  pushed  and  pulled  and  raised,  the  car  was  in  no  better  position  than  before.  &quot;Don't  think  I  should  call  a  garage?&quot;  he  said,  with  some  anxiety. Parked  just  in  from  the  road  on  a  small  asphalt  circle  were  the  older  man's  cars,  two  white  Cadillacs;  not  the  latest  models-in  fact,  one  of  them  quite  a  few  years  old  -but  so  clean  they  seemed  almost  new.  He  looked  at  his  battered  Chevy  with  the  boat  on  it.  Two  different  modes  of  life.  Things  never  did  go  very  smoothly  with  him,  or  hardly  ever.  He  wondered  about  the  white-haired  man.  Did  things  go  smoothly  with  him?  Probably,  he  thought,very  probably.  If  notwith  him,thenwith  whom?  By  compari-  son,  his  own  life  seemed  all  in  dis-  array.  Tonight,  for  instance,  he  had  a  date  with  a  woman,  but  did  he  want  to  see  her?  No,  not  at  all.  Well,  tonight,  he  thought,  he  had  a  good  excuse  not  to-  his  leg  hurt,  and  he  was  stiff  and  bruised.
