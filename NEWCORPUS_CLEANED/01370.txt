His  Highness  started  out  in  the  morning  from  the  site  of  the  Siva  Temple  (where  he  offered  obeisance),  threaded  through  theold  town  of  Brahmpur,  and  arrived  at  the  top  of  the  great  earthen  ramp  that  led  gently  down  from  the  top  of  the  mud  cliffs  to  the  sands  on  the  south  bank  of  the  Ganges.  Every  few  steps,  a  crier  announced  the  Raja's  advancing  presence,  and  rose  pet-  als  were  flung  into  the  air  to  his  greater  glory.  It  was  idiotic.  However,  it  was  of  a  piece  with  the  Raja's  conception  of  him-  self  and  his  place  in  the  world.  The  procession  halted  at  the  top  of  the  ramp  under  the  shade  of  the  great  pipal  tree,  and  the  Raja  looked  down.  Below  him,  as  far  as  the  eye  could  see,  lay  an  ocean  of  tents-khaki,  haze-  enveloped-spread  outalong  the  sands.  Along  with  the  semi-permanent  pon-  toon  bridge  across  the  Ganges,  there  were,  at  present,  five  bridges  of  boats,  effectively  cutting  off  all  downriver  traffic.
