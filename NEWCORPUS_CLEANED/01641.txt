His  mother  onlygoes  as  far  as  the  footpath  at  the  end  of  the  village,  because  she  can't  manage  the  steep  climb.  Their  cottage  is  really  two  small  " b"flint-and-tile  cottages  turned  inge-  niously  into  one.  Ethel  has  a  room  on  the  ground  floor,  with  a  door  giving  onto  the  long  lawn,  because  she  finds  stairs  tiring.  Paula  works  in  a  room  upstairs,  where  one  wall  is  lined  with  books,  and  she  sleeps  in  a  narrow  room  that  looks  out  onto  the  churchyard.  Behind  the  house  is  a  public  footpath,  and  a  handsome  row  of  sycamores  and  then  the  cricket  field  and  the  pink  villas  of  the  newer  part  of  the  village.  In  the  afternoons  and  some  evenings  the  two  ladies  sit  in  a  sitting  room,  which  is  almost  luxu-  rious,  for  it  is  furnished  with  one  or  two  treasures  Paula  brought  down  from  [REDACTED]  after  she  sold  her  flat  there.  There  is  a  long,  tall  gilded  mirror  on  one  wall,  a  chaise  longue,  and  a  cabinet  with  one  or  two  pieces  of  china  in  it.  The  chairsare  pleas-  antly  low-seated,  the  win-  dows  are  long  and  look  out  across  the  garden  hedge  to  the  public  footpath,  and  it  is  pleasant  to  hear  the  gate  slam-  ming  at  the  end  of  it  and  to  see  who  has  gone  by.  Quite  a  number  of  distinguished  vis-  itors  come  to  the  house,  especially  one  or  two  professors  from  Paula's  univer-  sity,  and  then  is  the  time  for  Ethel  to  show  her  gift  of  bringing  in  large  glasses,  like  globes,  containing  her  spe-  ciality:  well-iced  and  powerful  gin-  and-vermouths.  When  she  comes  into  the  room  she  always  catches  the  ends  of  the  sentences  she  hears  and  repeats  them  as  if  to  give  the  impression  that  she  has  not  been  out  of  the  room.  If  she  hears  a  visitor  saying,  &quot;In  my  opinion,  the  film  is  a  disasterto  anyone  who  has  read  the  book,&quot;  she  glides  in  saying,  &quot;'To  anyone  who  has  read  the  book,&quot;  to  join  the  conversation.  When  her  son  is  there  and  perhaps  says,  &quot;It  was  three-one  at  halftime,&quot;  she  will  eagerly  repeat,  &quot;''Three-one  at  halftime.&quot;  She  is  making  up  for  the  time  she  was  in  hospital  for  all  those  years  and  heard  nothing.  She  is  particularly  quick  to  pick  up  Paula's  exclamations  in  the  garden  when  Paula  finds  a  climbing  rose  has  gone  wild  and  says,  &quot;Nothing  to  be  done  with  them.  Cut  them  back  or  they  go  on  regardless,  with  ideas  of  their  own.&quot;  Ethel  says,  with  fervor,  &quot;With  ideas  of  their  own.&quot;  When  someone  said,  &quot;[REDACTED]  has  the  finest  collection  of  pictures  in  Europe,&quot;  she  repeated,  &quot;'The  finest  in  Europe,&quot;  as  if     she  had  been  visiting  there  all  her  life.  Occasionally,  at  weekends,  Ethel's  son  comes  down  to  stay  with  them.  He  has  turned  into  a  plump  and  dressy  young  man  with  the  habit  of  making  a  hissing  noise  between  histeeth  when  he  is  bored.  On  Saturdays  there  is  sometimes  a  sharp  bang,  like  a  shot,  from  the  gate  to  the  footpath  at  the  side  of  the  house.  The  gate  slams  loudest  when  the  children  from  the  pony  club  ride  through.  &quot;Iwish  that  woman  from  the  rid-  ing  school  would  take  her  horses  some  other  way,&quot;  says  Paula.  &quot;Her  horses  some  other  way,&quot;  says  Ethel  as  she  limps  into  the  room.She  adds,  &quot;The  noise  interferes  with  Paula's  work.
