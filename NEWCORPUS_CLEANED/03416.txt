ADRIA  HE  little  boy,  gone  to  4974616c79  with  his  family  to  spend  the  Christ-  mas  holidays  at  his  grandparents'  home,  was  put  in  the  housekeeper's  room  and  slept  on  a  cot  next  to  her  bed.  Her  name  was  Adria,  and  she  came  from  near  the  very  town  she  and  the  Adriatic  were  named  after.  She  was  a  handsome  woman  in  her  early  forties,  with  straight,  glossy  black  hair  knotted  into  a  chignon,  dark,  flashing  eyes,  and  a  mouth  that  seemed  always  ready  to  put  in  a  word,  or  laugh,  or  kiss.  One  evening,  he  was  taken  by  his  cousins  to  the  circus,  and  so  went  to  bed  later  than  usual.  A  few  minutes  after  he  had  put  out  the  light,  it  was  switched  on  again,  and,  wincing,  he  saw  from  be-  tween  his  lashes  Adria  coming  in.  She  loosened  her  chignon  in  front  of  the  mirror  and  let  her  hair  drop  to  her  shoulders  and  below  them.  It  covered  so  much  of  her  back  she  seemed  to  be  wearing  a  shawl.  Then  she  went  to  sit  on  her  bed  and  began  undress,  ing.  Intently,  he  watched  her  unfasten  her  stockings,  peeling  them  off  her  thighs.  These  were  huge,  as  in  a  Mo-  digliani,  except  that  their  color  wasn't  at  all  the  brick  red,  the  burnt  sienna,  the  terra-cotta  ofa  Modigliani  nude  but  white,  nun  white.  A  moment  later,  she  noticed  he  was  looking  at  her.  &quot;You  are  watching  me,  eh,  you  little  scamp,&quot;  she  said,  and  switched  off  the  light.  This  was  the  first  erotic  experience  that  he  could  remember-a  visual  one.  Now,  grown  up  and  married  and  the  father  of  a  child  nearly  as  old  as  he  had  been  when  he  had  shared  the  bedroom  with  Adria,  he  still  felt  a  warm  sympa-  thy  for  her  because  of  that  carly  experi-  ence,  and  because  of  her  dismissal  sever-  al  years  later-the  circumstances  of  it,  the  way  it  happened.     HIS  grandfather  was  a  law  profes-  sor,  an  energetic  little  man  with  a  goatee,  who  was  reputed  never  to  have  lost  a  case. Some  of  the  lawyers  had  been  or  had  come  near  to  being  failed  by  him  in  an  oral  exam-  ination,  and  even  this  they  would  relate  with  a  certain  gusto,  as  though  they  considered  it  an  honor  to  have  had  some  sort  of  scrape  with  such  a  man. Because  he  liked  to  do  everything  connected  with  the  law  by  himself  and  used  his  assistants  as  little  more  than  errand  boys,these  young  men  were  not  of  the  first  order. One  couldn't  think  of  it  without  her.  She  was  one  of  those  persons  whose  pres-  ence  cannot  pass  unnoticed,  at  worst  a  gossip  and  at  best  a  pills  whose  very  exuberance  insures  its  ultimate  downfall  or  depar-  ture  but  which  has  more  than  one  life  in  it,  so  that  though  one  is  tempted  to  say  &quot;What  of  it  now,  poor  soul?,&quot;  on  in-  quiring  one  finds  the  person  well  placed,  central  again,  ever  central,  in  another  situation.
