I  heard  my  brothers  getting  up  in  the  other  room.  They  were  shouting  and  laughing  and  hitting  each  other  with  pillows.  Daddy  moved  his  head  a  little  and  some  soap  got  on  his  pillow.  I  wiped  it  off,  then  went  up  along  the  side  of  his  cheek  tohis  sideburns.  I  went  quickly  over  the  left  side  of  his  face.  There  was  still  nothing  outside,  and  I  was  praying  the  van  would  come  soon.  Music  started  on  the  radio,  and  Daddy  told  me  to  turn  it  off,  but  I  pre-  tended  I  didn't  hear  him  and  kept  shav-  ing  away.  All  the  black  and  gray  hairs  made  funny  little  patterns  on  the  blade,  along  with  the  soap.  I  wiped  it  carefully  on  the  end  of  the  towel.  He  said,  Turn  the  radio  off,  son.  I  said,  Ah  please,  Daddy.  Are  you  listening  to  your  father?  he  said.  Turn  that  mess  off  right  now.  I  reached  across  and  flipped  the  radio  off.  Just  then  I  heard  the  van  in  the  lane  and  he  heard  it,  too.  It  turned  in  at  the  gate  and  made  a  squishy  sound  as  it  went  through  the  puddles.  I  could  see  by  the  way  Daddy's  fore-  head  creased  that  he  was  wondering.  I  told  him  it  must  be  the  postman  com-  ing  early,  and  I  pretended  to  look  out  the  window  and  said,  Aye,  it's  a  red  van,  it  must  be  the  post.  I  turned  the  radio  on  again  so  he  wouldn't  hear  any  sounds  or  van  doors        or  the  poles  being  loaded  or  any  other  noise  that  might  happen.  But  he  told  me  to  turn  the  radio  off,  no  ifs,  ands,  or  buts.  I  started  shaving  his  chin,  and  then  I  moved  up  tohis  mustache,  and  I  thought  I  should  have  washed  my  hands  better,  because  maybe  there  was  still  the  smell  of  wood  and  preservative  on  my  fingers.  The  blade  touched  against  his  top  lip,  but  it  didn't  bring  any  blood.  He  wrinkled  his  forehead,  and  he  looked  like  he  was  thinking  about  something  very  closely.  They're  very  early,  he  said.  ye.  This  is  the  earliest  I  ever  heard  them.  The  doors  of  the  van  closed  with  a  bang,  and  I  coughed  a  loud  cough.  Daddy  stirred  his  back  against  the  pil-  lows  and  said  how  it  must  be  a  package  of  some  sort,  but  for  the  life  of  him  he  couldn't  imagine  who  would  be  sending  a  package.  I  don't  know,  Daddy,  I  said.  He  asked  me  to  help  run  his  fin-  gers  over  his  face,  so  I  lifted  his  arm  up.  We  started  first  on  the  neck,  then  the  cheeks,  then  the  sideburns,  down  to  his  chin,  where  he  rubbed  the  lit-  tle  hollow  between  his  chin  and  his  mouth.  You  missed  a  part,  he  said  to  me.  Shall  I  shave  it?  No,  run  downstairs,  he  said.  See  about  that  package.  I  bolted  down.  Mammy  was  still  in  the  courtyard  when  I  got  outside.  Her  hair  was  blowing  sideways  in  the  wind.  She  had  tucked  the  money  away  in  her  apron.  Shewas  biting  her  lip  and  star-  ing  up  the  lane.  The  van  was  gone.  My  brothers  opened  the  window  upstairs  and  they  were  roaring  down,  but  I  didn't  hear  what  they  were  saying.  Mammy,  I  said.  Aye?  He  thinks  there's  a  package.  She  bit  her  lip  even  harder  and  said,  Tell  him  it  was  amistake.  I  said,  O.K.  She  looked  at  me  for  a  long  time,  then  she  turned  and  went  across  the  yard,  taking  small  steps  through  the  puddles.  I  looked  at  the  trees  behind  the  mill.  They  were  going  mad  in  the  wind.  The  trunks  were  big  and  solid  and  fat,  but  the  branches  were  slap-  ping  each  other  around  like  people.
