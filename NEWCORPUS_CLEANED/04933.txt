 b"OUR  three  children-Polly,  ten,  Martha,  twelve,  and  Tom,  six-  teen-have  radios,  a  television  set,  and  many  other  things  designed  to  amuse  or  instruct  or  fill  in  the  time.  During  the  week,  of  course,  they  go  to  school,  and  on  several  days  after  school  they  attend  meetings  or  have  music  or  dancing  lessons.  Weekends  are  taken  up  with  Sunday  school,  sports  events,  home  chores  (light),  and  seeing  their  friends.  So  I  am  considerably  surprised  when,  on  a  rainy  Saturday  morning,  I  am  con-  fronted  with  the  question  &quot;W  hat  can  we  do  today?&quot;  &quot;Oh,  I  don't  know,&quot;  I  usually  say.  &quot;Just  amuse  yourselves.&quot;  Sometimes  this  works  and  sometimes  it  doesn't. Once  in  a  while,  when  no  friends  show  up,  the  question  arises  again,  about  three  o'clock  in  the  afternoon,  and  then  with  a  special  urgency.  &quot;W  hat  can  we  do?&quot;  On  a  recent  rainy  Saturday  afternoon  when  the  question  was  put  to  me-this  time  by  Martha-I  was  at  my  desk  in  the  living  room,  writing  letters  on  the  typewriter.
