A  year  gone.  In  a  few  weeks,  there  would  be  subways,  work,  meeting  people  in  front  of  places.  Banks,  buses.  All  the  months  of  chaos  to  sort  out.  The  first  time  Kate  made  it  to  the  din-  ing  room  for  breakfast  was  the  morning  she  was  leaving.  A  few  ta-  bles  away,  a  waitress  brought  a  glass  of  milk  and  a  doughnut  to  a  child  in  pink.  At  the  end  of  the  meal,  the  child  stood  up  and  reached  slowly  and  jerkily  for  a  cane  hooked  over  the  next  chair.  It  was  theold  woman  who  had  come  to  Kate's  door  by  mis-  take  the  first  after-  noon.  ATE  went  to  the  front  desk  to  pay  her  bill.  &quot;But  I  thought  it  was  a  package,&quot;  she  said,  surprised.  She  had  not  exactly,  as  the  brochure  advised,  availed  herself  of  many  activities  at  slight  extra  charges.  &quot;It  is,&quot;  the  man  said.  &quot;But  there  are  surcharges.&quot;  He  smiled  at  Kate.  &quot;If  you're  with  a  group,  your  time  here  is  tax-exempt.&quot;  Kate  smiled  back.  &quot;I'm  not,&quot;  she  said,  and  wrote  a  check.  On  the  bus  ride  home,  the  sun  came  out.  Really  a  pretty  reasonable  bill,  Kate  thought,  though  she  knew  she  had  no  idea  what  things  cost.  But  she  had  been  away,  and  was  moving  back.  She  had  found  a  window  seat,  and,  staring  out,  didn't  care  that  the  dazzle  of  the  bright  red  and  yellow  leaves  was  a  signal  of  death.
