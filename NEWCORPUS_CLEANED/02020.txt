 b"THE  cities  of  Kimberley  and  Bloemfontein  lie  about  a  hun-  dred  miles  apart.  Considering  that  there  is  little  between  them  but  parched,  pale  veld,  wire  fences,  and  tin-roofed  farmhouses,  one  would  think  there  would  be  a  fair  amount  of  coming  and  going  between  the  two  places.  Perhaps  this  does  take  place  nowadays.  When  I  lived  in  Kimberley  as  a  boy,  however,  the  two  towns  stood  aloof  from  one  another.  There  were  many  reasons  for  this.  Chief  among  them  was  the  fact  that  the  whites  of  Kimberley  were  almost  wholly  En-  glish-speaking,  then;  Bloemfontein,  the  former  capital  of  the  Boer  Repub-  lic  of  the  Orange  Free  State,  was  \xc2\xab&quot;Dutch&quot;-Afrikaans-speaking.  The  result  was  a  gulf  that  nothing  could  bridge.  Moreover,  Kimberley  people  nursed  a  secret  resentment  against  Bloemfontein,  simply  because  it  was  bigger  and  better  endowed:  it  was  a  provincial  capital,  which  Kimberley  was  not;  it  was  the  seat  of  the  coun-  try's  Supreme  Court;  it  housed  a  col-  lege  of  the  University  of  [REDACTED]  Africa.  Not  that  the  effect  of  these  advantages  should  be  exaggerated.  If  you  did  go  to  Bloemfontein,  what  did  you  find  there?  A  few  more  cinemas  than  in  Kimberley,  two  or  three  more  depart-  ment  stores,  a  zoo,  a  few  &quot;historic&quot;  buildings  of  granite,  dating  from  the  days  of  the  Boer  Republic,  the  same  heat,  the  same  flat  countryside  all  around  (even  more  denuded  of  trees  than  it  was  around  Kimberley)-  nothing  to  make  you  feel  that  the  journey  had  been  worthwhile.  Better  by  far,  if  you  were  vacation-bound,  to  turn  your  car  northward,  to  the  big-city  excitements  of  [REDACTED],  or  southward,  to  the  spectacularly  heaped  and  fractured  coastlines  of  the  [REDACTED].  At  some  time  during  my  adoles-  cence,  however,  the  towns'  two  small  Jewish  communities,  each  of  which  consisted  of  about  a  hundred  and  twenty  families,  decided  that  they  should  do  more  for  one  another  and  with  one  another  than  they  had  done  in  the  past.  As  faras  the  young  people  were  concerned,  this  meant  that  a  group  from  each  town  visited  the  other  for  a  long  weekend,  turn  and  turn  about,  every  six  months  or  so.  The  arrangement  came  to  an  abrupt  end  after  a  couple  of  years,  in  the  circum-  stances  I  shall  describe  below,  but  while  it  lasted  it  made  a  welcome  change  for  anyone,  like  myself,  who  belonged  to  either  group.  Boys  and  girls  were  put  up  in  each  other's                 houses;  a  program  of  events  of  various  kinds,  from  lectures  to  rugby  matches,  was  arranged;  and  in  the  free  time  between  these  events  there  was  ample  opportunity  for  driving  about  in  mo-  torcars  or  walking  about  the  streets  lateat  night,  playing  Ping-Pong,  gos-  siping,  smoking,  flirting  publicly,  pair-  ing  off  privately,  and  some  drinking.  Almost  all  of  it  was  as  innocent  as  it  was  intense-most  childish  perhaps  in  its  most  nervously  grownup  moments:  the  kind  of  thing  that  similar  groups  of  prosperous,  middle-class,  secular-  ized  young  Jews  in  Europe  would  no  doubt  have  been  doing  during  those  very  years  if  the  Nazis  had  not  been  rounding  them  up  and  murdering  them.  In  thecourse  of  one  such  weekend  a  picnic  was  arranged  at  a  place  called  Riverton,  on  the  Vaal  River,  about  fifteen  miles  outside  Kimberley.  A  bus  was  hired  to  take  out  those  who  could  not  get  a  lift  in  a  private  car.  There  must  have  been  about  forty  in  the  party  altogether,the  oldest  perhaps  nineteen  years  of  age,  the  youngest  thirteen;  about  half  were  visitors  from  Bloem-  fontein.  On  the  way  out  people  talked  to  one  another  and  showed  off  as  they  usually  did;  from  time  to  time,  though,  they  also  put  their  heads  solemnly  to-  gether  and  exchanged  whispers  and  glances  and  meaningful  nods.  They  were  talking  about  a  scene  that  had  taken  place  after  a  dance  the  previous  night.  Two  of  the  Kimberley  boys,  one  named  Brian,  the  other  Peter,  had  got  drunk  at  the  dance  andhad  then  gone  to  the  house  of  one  of  the  girls  in  the  group.  There  they  had  behaved  so  badly  that  the  girl  had  finally  told  them  to  clear  out.  The  scene  that  fol-  lowed  was  noisier  and  nastier  still.  The  girl's  parents  became  involved;  the  two  youngsters  were  manhandled  out  of  the  house.  Alternately  yelling  insults  and  weeping,  Brian  stood  in  the  lamplit  street,  which  a  few  minutes  before  had  been  as  tranquil  as  only  a  small-town,  residential  streetlate  at  night  can  be.  He  knew  why  he'd  been  treated  like  that,  he  cried:  people  liked  to  push  him  around;  they  thought  of  him  as  a  cripple;  they  despised  him  because  of  it;  but  he'd  show  them  all  one  day;  and  so  forth.  The  scene-with  all  its  varying  proportions  of  melodrama,  misery,  ex-  citement,  and  pleasure  for  everybody  involved-ended  as  such  scenes  usu-  ally  do.  After  much  expostulation  and  explanation,  more  tears  and  expres-  " b"sions  of  defiance  and  apology,  everyone  dis-  persed  into  the  darkness.  Now  it  was  all  over,  or  would  have  been  all  over  but  for  two  things.  The  first  was  that  peo-  ple  had  begun  to  suspect  that  Peter  had  what  would  nowadays  be  called  a  serious  drinking  problem.  He  was  a  silly,  giggling  schoolboy,  nothing  more,  fully  six-  teen  years  old,  with  a  pair  of  jaunty  shoulders  and  a  face  ravaged  by  acne.  That  he  might  also  be  on  the  way  to  becoming  a  &quot;real&quot;  drunk  -a  grownup  drunk,  a  helpless  drunk-was  no  longer  impossible  to  con-  ceive,  as  it  had  been  a  few  months  before,  but  merely  implausible:  too  grandiose,  somehow.  As  things  turned  out,  this  wonder  or  suspicion  among  some  of  those  who  knew  himwas  not  at  all  a  piece  of  ado-  lescent  sensationalism.  Much  of  the  next  fifteen  years  or  more  of  Peter's  life  was  to  be  spent  either  in  a  state  of  drunken-  ness  or  in  various  insti-  tutions  for  the  treatment  of  alcoholism.  Then  his  irreparably  damaged  liver  finally  gave  up  the  struggle.  As  for  Brian-bespectacled,  bony-  browed  Brian,  whose  left  hand  clutched  his  hip  as  he  walked  and  whose  frail  chest  was  somehow  a  little  closer  to  his  chin  than  one  would  ordi-  narily  expect  chest  and  chin  to  be;  Brian,  with  his  prominent,  flattish  nose  and  lizard-like  smile,  his  curi-  ously  harsh  voice  and  grunting  laugh,  which  always  seemed  to  be  shaken  out  of  him  in  small  bursts  of  sound,  as  if  he  were  reluctant  to  let  it  go;  brown-haired,  faintly  freckled,  sar-  donic  Brian,  who  was  older  than  almost  everyone  else  in  the  group,  and  who  looked  even  older  than  his  years,  partly  because  of  his  stooped  walk  and  partly  because  of  a  habitual  weariness  and  wariness  around  the  eyes-Brian  was  quite  a  different  case.  His  friends  cared  about  him  far  more  than  they  ever  would  about  poor  Peter.  Whether or  not  Peter  drank  too  much  or  why  he  drank  too  much  were  subjects  for  gossip  rather  than  real  concern.  There  are  always  people  like  Peter  whose  claims  to  attention  are  easily  put  by,  without  their  acquaintances  even  real-  izing  that  that  is  what  they  are  doing.  There  are  also  people  of  whom  the  exact  opposite  is  true.  Though  they  may  not  seem  to  wish  it,  or  even  to  know  it,  their  presence  penetrates  and  changes  and  appears  to  enlarge  the  possibilities  open  to  the  company  they  keep.  Brian  was  one  of  these.  He  was  not  a  &quot;leader&quot;  but  something  more  interesting.  He  roused  your  curiosity.  You  wanted  him  to  share  his  experi-  ences  with  you. It  was  a  knack  or  gift  he  had,  and  like  all  gifts  it  was  irreducible,  beyond  explanation,  as  much  part  of  him  as  the  texture  of  his  skin  or  the  posture  of  his  body  or,  for  that  matter,  his  habit  of  wearing  his  shirtsleeves  only  half  rolled  up,  to  just  below  the  elbows.  Why  should  the  movement  of  his  body  in  his  shirt,  even  the  buttons  on  one  particular  blue,  coarse-woven  shirt,  still  be  vivid  in  my  memory  after  so  many  years?  The  question  is  impos-  sible  to  answer.  The  detail  is  simply  lodged  among  many  others  in  my  mind.  For  a  long  time  my  older  brother  and  he  had  been  close  friends,  which  meant  that  though  I  saw  him  often  I  never  had  a  direct  relationship  with  him;  they  were  the  ones  who  decided  whether  or  not  I  would  be  allowed  to  join  their  games  or  conver-  sations.  Then  they  drifted  apart,  and  I  saw  much  less  of  him.  But  between  us  there  remained  something  of  the  close-  ness  and  distance  of  the  earlier  rela-  tionship.  I  was  more  than  just  another  junior  to  him,  and  he  showed  it  in  the  way  he  looked  at  me  and  talked  to  me.  Or  did  not  talk  to  me.  He  and  I  sometimes  used  to  walk  part  of  the  way  " b"home  together  after  school.  Strangely  enough,  given  his  vivacity  at  other  times,  what  I  remember  best  about  those  walks  was  their  unembarrassed,  almost  intimate  silence.  It  was  as  if  he  did  not  mind  my  seeing  just  how  fa-  tigued  an  ordinary  day  at  school  could  make  him  feel.  His  face  would  be  set,  his  walk  more  labored  than  usual,  his  forehead  somehow  more  prominent.  My  other  memories  of  him,  though,  are  quite  different  in  kind,  and  I  still  cherish  them.  Once,  when  he  and  my  brother  were  having  an  insults  match  -trading  the  worst  things  they  could  say  about  each  other-Brian  fell  silent  and  then  brought  out  his  coup  de  grace:  &quot;Well,  whatever  I  am,  you're  what  comes  out  of  my  arse.&quot;  It  was  the  end  of  the  match.  My  brother  conceded  defeat  by  collapsing  helplessly  into  laughter.  Brian  merely  smiled  like  a  lizard. The  child  ran  off,  his  lips  still  moving  over  the  phrases.  There  was  nobody  else  at  school  or  outside  it  about  whom  one  could  have  told  such  stories.  There  was  no  one  else  whose  words,  whose  gaze,  whose  demeanor,  even  when  he  was  fooling about,  were  marked  by  such  a  curi-  ously  watchful  restraint  and  detach-  ment-a  distancing  of  himself  from  what  he  was  doing,  which  made  the  activity  seem  all  the  more  intense  to  the  people  around  him.  This  capacity  or  quality,  I  have  no  doubt,  sprang  in  part  from  the  fact  that  he  had  suffered  from  a  severe  and  prolonged  illness  as  a  child.  He  had  had  polio  at  the  age  of  about  eight,  and  had  almost  died  of  it.  For  weeks,  to  make  matters  worse,  the  disease  had  been  misdiagnosed  as  tick  fever.  Then  there  had  followed  many  months  in  bed,  endless  physiotherapy  sessions,  a  trip  to  the  United  [REDACTED]  just before  the  outbreak  of  the  war,  where  his  distraught  (and  wealthy)  parents  had  taken  him  in  search  of  some  kind  of  miracle  cure.  In  the  end  the  degree  of  disability  with  which  the  disease  had  left  him  was  not  as  severe,  appar-  ently,  as  had  at  first  been  feared:  he  suffered  from  a  permanent  weakness  of  the  spine  which  showed  itself  in  the  way  he  walked  (his  hand  supporting  his  hip)  and  the  posture  of  his  body.  He  had  lost  a  year  or  more  of  school  and  had  fallen  out  of  my  brother's  class  into  the  one  immediately  above  my  own.  He  had  difficulty  in  walking  for  long  distances  or  in  standing  for  long  periods;  he  was  also  barred  from  all  organized  sports.  On  the  other  hand,  because  of  the  physiotherapy  and  hydrotherapy  courses  he  had  been  through,  he  was  supposed  to  be  a  pow-  erful  swimmer. He  had  stood  in  the  mid-  dle  of  a  street,  outside  the  house  of  a  girl  he  was  especially  fond  of,  and  lacerated  himself  and  everyone  around  him  by  yelling  out  unsayable  things  about  his  own  body  and  about  what  he  thought  to  be  the  attitude  of  others  toward  it. We  hadn't  gone  far  when  we  suddenly  heard  cries  from  a  boat  that  was  just  a  few  yards  away.  It  was  approaching  directly  toward  us,  at  right  angles  to  the  bank.  It  hit  the  bank  prow  first.  Several  boys  we  knew  leaped  up  from  it  with  an  agitated  clatter  and  rumble  of  oars  and  rowlocks.  Behind  them,  a  person  reared  up  within  the  boat;  then  we  saw  that  he  had  risen  up  in  that  fashion  because  theothers  were  hauling  and  pushing  him  upright;  then  we  saw  that  it  was  Brian.  Amid  shouts  and  gasps,  he  was  stumblingly  carried  onto  the  bank  and  laid  down  on  it.  He  died  there,  in  front  of  us.  His  face  was  turned  to  one  side.  It  was  blue  in  color.  It  had  an  intent,  inward  ex-  pression,  as  if  he  were  listening  to  a  voice  with-  in  him  that  might  have  been  lost  if  he  had  been  distracted  by  the  efforts  of  the  people  working  over  him,  or  by  the  crowd  that  gathered  at  a  little  distance  to  gaze  at  the  scene.  I  had  wit-  nessed  only  one  death  before,  also  that  of  a  boy  -someone  from  school  whom  I  knew  slightly.  I  had  come  on  him,  around  a  corner,  seconds  after  he  was  knocked  off  his  bicycle  by  a  speeding  Army  truck,  and  then  " b"carried  by  it  for  many  yards  before  being  thrown  off  it.  He  had  been  so  badly  smashed  up  that  it  was  those  parts  of  his  body  which  were  still  undamaged  that  looked  uncanny  or  bizarre.  Brian  was  not  like  that.  Only  his  color  and  the  intent-  ness  of  his  expression  seemed  to  tell  one  that  there  was  no  more  to  be  done  in  this  case  than  there  had  been  in  the  other.  What  could  be  done  was  done.  People  worked  over  him  for  I  don't  know  how  long  -hours,  it  seemed.  Much  of  the  work  was  undertaken  by  one  of  the  visitors  from  Bloemfontein,  himself,  ironically  enough,  a  champion  swimmer,  the  holder  of  a  junior  [REDACTED]  African  title.  He  was  the  one  who  had  held  Brian  up  in  the  water  after  he  col-  lapsed.  Peter  and  Brian  had  been  swimming  together;  it  was  Peter'svoice,in  fact-not  Brian's  -that  had  made  the  young  man  realize  that  something  serious  was  amiss.  An  ambulance  and  a  doctor  were  summoned  from  Kimberley;  someone-I  don't  know  who-also  telephoned  Brian's  parents.  While  the  amateur  helpers  waited  for  the  profes-  sionals  to  arrive,  they  continued  to  toil  away  at  their  task.  Those,  like  myself,  who  had  no  expertness  in  first  aid,  wandered  away  and  came  back-once  because  there  was  a  cry  of  &quot;He's  com-  ing  round,  he's  coming  round!&quot;  It  turned  out  to  have  nothing  in  it,  but  I  remember  that  when  I  heard  the  cry  I  thought  &quot;Of  course  he's  coming  round!,&quot;  as  though  Brian  and  I  had  all  along  had  a  silent  understanding,  one  quite  private  to  ourselves,  that  he  would  do  so.  I  remember  also  sitting  with  three  or  four  others  on  a  thick  willow-tree  root  that  grew  out  of  the  ground.  It  was  near  the  river,  which  was  now  sullenly  empty  of  boats  and  bathers.  During  that  time,  all  of  us,  I  think,  got  to  know  how  gray  the  dust  was,  how  grooved  was  the  root,  how  hot  the  sunlight,  how  stricken  in  appearance  faces  could  become,  how  lit-  tle  breath  there  seemed  to  be  in  any-  one's  chest,  how  dark  it  was  when  you  put  your  hands  over  your  eyes.  The  ambulance  men  and  the  doctor  eventually  arrived.  'They,  too,  set  to  work,  but  they  were  able  to  do  no  more  than  the  others.  Brian'sparents  ar-  rived  just  after  the  effort  had  been  abandoned  and  the  body  had  been  car-  ried  into  the  ambulance.  The  doors  of  the  ambulance  had  just  been  closed  when  their  car  came  to  a  halt.  The  doctor  went  to  them.  Brian's  mother  cried,  &quot;My  boy,  my  baby  boy!,&quot;  and  his  father  clapped  his  hands  loudly  together  in  front  of  his  chest.  Then,  and  only  then,  it  seemed,  was  it  over:  truly  irrevocable.  We  got  in  the  vehicles  that  had  brought  us  to  the  river  and  returned  to  Kimberley.  None  of  the  visitors  from  Bloemfontein  stayed  for  the  funeral,  which  had  to  be  postponed  until  after  the  inquest.  (Its  finding  was  that  Brian  had  not  drowned  but  had  died  of  coronary  fail-  ure.)  No  one  couldbring  himself  to  suggest,  the  following  year,  that  there  should  be  another  exchange  between  the  young  people  of  the  Jewish  com-  munities  in  the  two  towns;  indeed,  no  further  visits  of  that  kind  took  place  while  I  still  lived  in  Kimberley.  Today,  forty  years  after  the  events  I  have  described,  Kimberley  is,  as  Bloemfontein  always  was,  largely  Af-  rikaans-speaking;  the  Jewish  commu-  nities  of  both  towns  are  smaller  than  before.  Amid  the  silent  cypresses  of  the  Kimberley  Jewish  cemetery,  tomb-  stones  have  now  appeared,  alongside  Brian's,  on  which  are  inscribed  the  names  of  others  among  those  who  went  to  Riverton  that  day.  Brian  was  the  forerunner.  With  his  high  spirits  and  his  detachment,  his  frailty  and  his  strength  of  will,  his  laughter  and  the  element  of  grimness  in  him,  he  had  always  seemed  the  most  grown-up  among  us.  His  death  made  it  seem  as  if  he  would  have  that  advantage  over  us  forever.  But  one  by  one  we  are  at  last  catching  up  with  him."
