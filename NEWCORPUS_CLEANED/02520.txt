I  sit  down  on  the  brick  walk  and  Ms.  White  soars  over  a  clump  of  fading  lemon  lilies  and  comes  to  stretch  beside  me  and  put  her  head  in  my  lap.  She  curls  around  me,  humming.  The  peonies  in  this  garden  are  too  shaded  and  therefore  do  not  bloom.  I  must  arrange  now  to  have  them  moved  in  September  or  so  much  will  be  lost.  In  September  summertime  ends,  floats  away  on  little  puffs  of  milk-  weed,  and  the  mindless  geese  will  fly  over  the  trees  so  crowded  with  leaves  that  we  cannot  see  the  geese  but  only  hear  them  in  their  chaos  of  communi-  cation;  they  come  and  go,  come  and  go,  dragging  their  invisible  net  over  the  land,  gathering  the  last  scraps  of  summer,  scattering  the  first  olive-green  leaves  among  the  returning  finches  un-  til  you  cannot  tell  finch  from  leaf,  leaf  from  finch.  We  will  put  up  storm  windows  and  wear  heavy  sweaters  in  the  house,  and  the  soup  will  be  piping  hot.
