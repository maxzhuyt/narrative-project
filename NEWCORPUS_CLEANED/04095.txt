For  the  first  hour  or  so,  she  couldn't  believe  that  he  meant  what  he  was  saying,  but  finally  she  spoke  as  though,  even  if  she  couldn't  quite  believe,  she  would  try  to  understand.  &quot;What  will  I  do,  Rob-  ert?&quot;  she  kept  saying.  &quot;What  will  I  do  when  you  are  gone?  I  can't  believe  that  this  is  the  last  time  I  will  ever  see  you.&quot;  'T  here  must  be  good  Catholic  boys  that  your  cousins  know,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Go  out  with  them. We've  got  to  stop  now.&quot;  Oh,  he  was  good.  Goodness  and  Righteousness  those  were  his  middle  names  to-  night.  Saving  this  innocent  young  girl  from  a  life  of  sorrow.  If  he  loved  himself  any  more  for  what  he  was  doing,  he  thought,  he  wouldn't  be  able  to  live  with  him-  self.  He  told  her  that  he  had  to  go,  but  she  seemed  not  to  hear  and  remained  on  his  lap.  Then  all  ofa  sudden  she  got  up  and  went  to  the  shower  and  came  back  with  his  jacket.  It  had  dried,  but  it  was  wrinkled.  &quot;Here  is  your  coat,  sir.  Look,  it's  all  wrinkled.  We've  been  sitting  here  so  long.  If  it  wasn't  so  late,  'd  press  it  offfor  you.&quot;  &quot;That's  all  right,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;I  have  to  have  everything  cleaned  and  pressed  before  I  go  back  to  school  anyway.&quot;  He  stood  up  very  slowly  to  take  the  coat  from  her,  and  though  he  wanted  to  throw  his  arms  into  it  and  rush  from  the  apartment,  he  put  it  on  very  slow-  ly,  and  buttoned  it  up  method-  ically,  and  then  after  a  few  minutes,  when  theyboth  just  con-  tinued  to  stand  there,  he  unbut-  toned  it  again.  'The  only  sound  in  the  house  was  the  rain  beating  against  the  gutter.  At  last,  she  started  walking  to-  ward  the  hall  to  the  door.  He  waited  until  she  was  almost  there  before  he  started.  When  they  got  to  the  door,  she  reached  up  to  take  the  night  latch  off,  and  suddenly  she  stopped  and  put  her  arms  around  his  waist  and  laid  her  head  on  his  chest.  He  put  his  arms  about  her,  and  they  stood  there  in  the  quiet  for  a  few  minutes,  neither  speaking;  he  was  thank,  ful  that  she  hadn't  started  crying.  She  reached  up  with  one  hand  for  the  night  latch,  and  then  she  turned  the  door-  knob,  squeezing  him  at  the  same  time  with  her  other  arm,  and  then  she  opened  the  door.  He  paused  for  a  few  seconds  before  he  stepped  out  into  the  hall.  Slowly,  they  walked  together  to  the  stairs.  &quot;I  can't  believe  that  I'll  never  see  you  again,  Robert.  Let  me  look  at  your  face.  I  can't  believe  that  I'll  never  see  you  again.&quot;  He  started  down  the  first  step  and  turned  around  so  that  he  couldlook  at  her.  He  went  down  another  step.  &quot;'They  won't  believe  me  when  I  tell  my  cousins  that  you  are  gone  for  good.  They  won't  believe  it.  They  thought  that  we  would  be  engaged  soon.&quot;  He  went  down  another  step,  and  then  another,  to  the  landing.  After  one  more  flight,  she  wouldn't  be  able  to  see  him,  and  he  would  finally  be  alone  again.  &quot;I  couldn't  believe  when  you  came  to  Ann's  tonight  to  collect  me  that  we  would  say  goodbye  like  this.  I'll  always  think  of  you,  Robert-there  can't  be  anyone  else.  And  now  I'll  never  see  you  again.&quot;  He  continued  very  slowly  down  the  stairs.  He  wanted  to  take  them  two  at  a  time,  even  jump  the  whole  flight  at  once,  justto  get  out,  but  he  car-  tied  through  to  the  end.  He  walked  down  one  step  at  a  time,  very  slowly;  the  only  noise  in  the  building  was  the  sound  his  shoes  made  against  the  mar-  ble.  When  he  got  to  the  bottom,  he  heard  the  apartment  door  shut.  He  opened  the  outside  door  and  walked  out  into  the  rain.  He  felt  that  he  would  have  been  justified  in  running  to  the  subway,  but  he  knew  she  would  be  watching  from  the  window,  so  he  put  his  collar  up,  bent  his  head  slightly  to  shield  it  from  the  rain,  and  walked  very  slowly  down  the  street  to  the  subway  station.
