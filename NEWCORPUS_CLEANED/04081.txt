This  [REDACTED]  was  the  Rue  de  Rivoli  in  the  dog  days  of  1911,  when  my  family  paused  there  on  the  way  home  to  New  York  from  a  holi-  day  in  Europe.  I  was  nearly  seven.  In  my  mind,  Europe  proper  was  a  place  where  people  spoke  German,  like  Friu-  leins,  and  were  equally  disagreeable.  The  word  &quot;Fraulein,&quot;  to  me,  had  a  strong,  specific  connotation  of  nurse-  girl;  it  was  yearsbefore  I  learned  that  it  meant  &quot;Miss&quot;  and  could  be  applied  to  an  unmarried  woman  not  in  service.  Frauleins  had  been  my  immemo-  rial  enemies-evil  bailiffs  interposed  between  my  parents  and  me.  My  fa-  ther  and  mother,  I  was  sure,  would  have  com-  plied  with  all  my  wishes  had  successive  Friuleins  not  prevented  me  from  presenting  my  petitions  in  person.  They  were  like  the  wicked  doorkeep-  ers  of  a  benign  sultan.  Without  their  officious  intervention,  I  could  have  been  with  my  par-  ents  twenty-four  hours  a  day,  and  they  would  have  been  charmed  to  have  me.,  There  were  two  incidents  in  that  1907  trip  thatI  do  remember,  and  they  both  involved  a  Friulein-Martha.  In  Wiesbaden  that  summer,  Martha  dragged  me  at  unforgettable  speed,  my  feet  touching  the  ground  only  occasion-  ally,  through  what  I  remember  as  a  mile  of  streets,  to  see  a  tall  man  in  a  white  uniform  and  a  cape  sitting  in  a  white  automobile  on  some  hideous  Platz,  sur-  rounded  by  a  cheering  crowd.  He  was,  she  told  me  in  hysterical  shrieks,  the  Kaiser.  Martha  ranked  in  the  Frauleini-  an  dynasty  about  like  Caligula  among  Roman  emperors.  She  was  one  of  the     \-  worst.  In  Nuremberg  that  same  sum-  mer,  she  had  packed  me  off  to  the  tor-  ture  chamber  in  the  old  keep  to  see  the  Iron  Maiden,  a  hinged,  hollow  figure  lined  with  spikes.  The  guide-lecturer  said  that  prisoners  were  put  inside  and  then  squashed  and  blinded  simultaneous-  ly.  I  understand  now  that  the  Maiden  was  a  fake,  but  it  was  a  German  fake.  Then  they  showed  me  the  mouth  of  the  well  down  which  the  torturers  used  to  drop  the  broken  bodies.  The  guide  held  a  lighted  candle,  and  Martha  lifted  me  in  her  arms  and  let  me  look  down.  Then  he  chucked  a  stone  in,  and  we  waited  an  impressively  long  time  before  we  heard  it  hit  the  water.  &quot;It  happens  to  naughty  children,  too,&quot;  Martha  said.  She  bought  me  a  miniature  Iron  Maiden  as  a  souvenir.  My  wars  against  the  Frauleins  left  me  with  a  blurred  recollection  of  in-  justice  and  struggle,  like  the  collective  memory  of  the  Irish,  for  whom  I  have  an  anomalous  sympathy  even  when  they  are  at  their  most  difficult.  The  Fraulein,  I  have  often  thought  since,  was  a  re-  markably  effective  device  for  siphoning  off  from  the  parents  the  hostility  that  ana-  lysts  assure  us  is  the  parents'  due.  Careful  bootleggers  in  [REDACTED]  during  prohibition  used  to  pass  suspected  alcohol  through  an  old  felt  hat,  on  the  theory  that  the  denaturing  agent  would  remain  there.  The  Fraulein,  in  the  seg-  ment  ofa  segment  of  society  that  I  was  young  S  in,  fulfilled  the  function  of  the  felt  hat.  As  full  of  discharged  hostility  as  the  hat  of  fusel  oil,  she  shifted  to  another  job  when  the  child  outgrew  her,  leav-  ing  it  to  its  parents  as  harmless  as  a  baby  rattlesnake  milked  of  venom.  I  credit  to  this  system  the  patience  that  a  number  of  elderly  Germanistic  friends  of  mine  still  manifest  with  regard  to  their  mothers.  To  the  child  who  began  conscious  life  under  the  rule  of  a  Friu-  lein,  even  the  least  bearable  mother  will  always  appear  an  angel.  The  device  seems  to  me,  looking  back,  more  sensible  than  thatof  the  kind  nanny,  described  in  middle-class  English  novels.  The  nannies  siphon  off  not  the  hostility  but  the  affection,  leaving  the  heroes  of  the  novels  incapable  of  liking  anybody  else  forthe  rest  of  their  lives. Fraulein  could  build  a  whole  day  of  peace  around  this  pros-  pect,  keeping  us  in  line  during  the  morn-  ing  by  threatening  not  to  take  us  if  we  weren't  good,  then  making  us  have  a  nap  after  lunch  as  preparation  for  the  treat. Friu-  lein  and  I  had  the  sodas,  but  they  were  not,  in  my  opinion,  up  to  the  standard  of  the  West  Side.  Trying,  at  this  long  distance  in  time,  to  appraise  the  trouble,  I  do  not  think  that  Rumpelmayer's  had  a  genuine  soda-fountain  pump  to  gas  them  up  with.  I  suspect  them  of  being  parfaits  slopped  overwith  a  bottle  of  Perrier.  Boys  likedtheir  sodas  very  gas-  sy  then,  with  the  sharp,  metallic  bite  that  reminded  me  of  the  smell  of  a  bi-  cycle  shop.  My  sister,  I  suppose,  was  allowed  to  have  some  of  the  ice  cream  out  of  my  soda.  Then  the  waitress  brought  the  bill.  Fraulein,  who  had  ex-  pected  they  would  cost  ten  cents,  like  the  ones  at  home,  did  not  have  enough  French  money  with  her  to  pay  it.  There  was  a  conference,  made  more  memo-  rable  because  Fraulein  spoke  no  French  and  they  trusted  nobody  who  spoke  German.  The  allegorical  statue  of  [REDACTED]  among  the  monuments  to  the  cities  of  [REDACTED]  in  the  Place  de  la  Concorde  was  permanently  draped  in  black  and  knee-high  in  funeral  wreaths.  La  revanche  was  in  the  air,  like  humid-  ity.  Finally,  Friulein  and  the  manage-  ment  reached  a  Konkordanz:  she  was  to  go  back  to  the  hotel  and  change  some  Austrian  kronen-Rumpelmayer's  wouldn't.  She  was  to  take  Norma,  who  was  already  howling,  with  her,  and  I  was  to  be  left  as  collateral.  She  explained  the  deal  to  me  before  she  left.  I  did  not  like  it;  I  proposed  we  leave  Norma.  She  said  patiently  that  Rumpelmayer's  wouldn't  go  for  it--the  reason  they  were  releasing  her  at  all  was  to  get  Norma  out  of  there.  I  demanded  a  sup-  plement  of  pastry,  on  the  principle  of  extra-danger  pay,  but  said,  perhaps  mendaciously,  that  the  manage-  ment  would  advance  no  further  credit.  So  there  I  sat,  for  what  seemed  the  second  seven  years  of  my  life,  my  appre-  hension  steadily  growing,  while  she  walked  the  couple  of  hundred  yards  back  to  the  Régina  and  returned.  Be-  fore  she  made  it,  I  had  decided  a  dozen  times  that  she  wouldn't.  When  at  last  she  reappeared,  she  asked  me  not  to  tell  my  parents  what  had  happened,  and  I  blackmailed  her  out  of  an  éclair.  It  was  a  prophetic  trauma.  I  have  spent  a  large  portion  of  my  life  since  in  situations  that  repeated  the  quarter  hour  at  Rumpelmayer's,  waiting  for  a  loan  or  an  advance  to  bail  me  out.  No  matter  how  sure  I  have  been  that  the  money  would  arrive,  I  have  always  had  the  same  anxiety.
