When  Nikolay  Viktorovich  came  home  from  the  hospital,  his  wife  would  ask,  &quot;Well,  how  are  our  boys  today?&quot;  They  had  no  children,  and  so  that  is  how  the  two  of  them  referred  to  the  wounded  young  soldiers.  And  Nikolay  Viktorovich  would  laugh  as  he  told  his  wife  funny  stories  about  life  in  the  lit-  tle  hospital.  But  the  Germans  had  not  entirely  forgotten  about  the  little  building  in  the  depths  of  the  park.  One  day,  Nikolay  Viktorovich  was  calledto  the  Town  Council  medical  department  and  asked  to  produce  a  list  of  all  the  wounded  under  his  supervision.  Niko-  lay  Viktorovich  felt  anxious  as  he  com-  piled  the  list,  but  the  official  on  duty  did  not  even  read  it.  He  threw  it  care-  lessly  into  a  file;  evidently  it  was  just  a  bureaucratic  formality.  The  Germans  continued  to  ad-  vance.  The  military  bulletins  in  the  newspapers  sounded  more  triumphant  than  ever,  and  Nikolay  Viktorovich  tried  not  to  read  them.  There  were  rumors  that  the  sanato-  riums  would  soon  be  reopening,  and  that  not  only  colonels  and  generals  but  also  the  German  intelligentsia  would  be  coming  for  treatment.  It  appeared  that  there  were  some  people  who  had  had  well-educated  Germans  billeted  to  them,  Germans  who  seemed  to  be  afraid  of  Hitler  and  Himmler  and  who  seemed  to  disapprove  of  the  horrors  reported  by  people  living  near  the  Gestapo  headquarters.  All  in  all,  then,  life  wasn't  really  so  very  different  from  the  life  they  had  known  before.  Once  again,  Nikolay  Viktorovich  found  himself  delighting  in  the  comfort  of  his  house  and  the  beauty  and  charm  of  his  wife;  once  again,  he  was  sure  that  he  had  been  right  toprefer  Elena  Petrovna  Kseno-  fontova's  name-day  party  to  the  meet-  ing  of  the  revolutionary  circle.  But  now,  just  as  Nikolay  Viktoro-  vich  was  about  to  go  home,  intending  to  relax  fora  while  and  then  have  some-  thing  to  eat  before  going  with  his  wife  tosee  a  performance  of  Gerhart  Haupt-  mann's  &quot;The  Sunken  Bell,&quot;  a  car  had  crunched  over  the  gravel  and  up  to  the  little  building  in  the  park.  Out  of  it  had   stepped  a  stout  man  withhigh  cheek-  bones  and  a  snub  nose,  with  gray  eyes  and  fair  hair;  he  could  have  been  a  So-  viet  district  agronomist,  or  the  man-  ager  of  ashop,  or  someone  giving  a  talk  on  social  insurance  at  a  meeting  of  a  domestic  workers'  Party  committee.  It  was  clear,  however,  from  the  peaked  cap,  the  belt,  the  armband,  the  shoulder  straps  on  his  gray  uniform,  and  the  swastika  and  iron  cross  on  his  chest  that  he  was  a  Gestapo  officer,  equal  in  rank  toa  Wehrmacht  colonel.  As  always,  Nikolay  Viktorovich  looked  tall  and  sleek;  his  face  had  a  handsome  glow,  his  hair  was  an  ele-  gant  shade  of  silver,  and  his  eyes  were  almost  indecently  expressive.  Beside  this  runt  of  a  potbellied  German,  a  manwho  looked  as  if  he  had  been  cobbled  together  out  of  some  kind  of  waste  matter,  Nikolay  Viktorovich  re-  sembled  a  distinguished  landowner  who  enjoyed  the  favors  of  Fortune;  he  could  have  been  taken  for  an  impor-  tant  Russian  aristocrat  or  maybe  some  foreign  duke.  But  that  is  not  how  it  was  at  all.  &quot;Sie  sprechen  Deutsch?&quot;  &quot;Jawobl,&quot;  Nikolay  Viktorovich  re-  plied.  When  he  was  a  boy,  he  had  learned  German  from  a  governess  named  Augusta  Karlovna.  &quot;Heavens!&quot;  he  said  to  himself,  real-  izing  what  grace,  what  coquettish  ea-  gerness,  what  a  passionate  desireto  seem  nice,  sweet,  and  obedient  he  had  infused  into  his  cooing  &quot;Jawo//&quot;  The  German,  on  hearing  the  voice  of  the  gray-haired  aristocrat  and  look-  ing  him  quickly  up  and  down  through  almost  divinely  omniscient  eyes-the  eyes  of  a  being  whose  actions  were  performed  in  a  higher  realm,  where  nothing  existed  but  life  and  death-  immediately  understood  what  kind  of  man  he  was  talking  to.  The  stout,  stocky  Gestapo  officer  had  before  him  the  task  of  destroying  vast  mountains  of  human  flesh.  There  were  thousands  of  human  spirits  that  he  had  to  break-to  cleave  or  shatter,  to  bend  or  fragment:  yes,  there  were  Catholics  and  the  Ortho-  dox,  there  were  fighter  pilots,  princes  who  were  passionate  monarchists,  Party  functionaries,  inspired  poets  who  had  trampled  over  every  exist-  ing  convention,  nuns  whose  spiritual  frenzy  had  led  them  to  renounce  the  world.  When  life  is  under  threat,  ev-  erything  cracks  and  splits  apart,  every-  thing  turns  upside  down,  sometimes  slowly,  sometimes  with  obstinate  re-  sistance,  sometimes  yielding  withan  ease  that  makes  you  want  to  laugh.  But  the  result  is  always  the  same,  and  the  exceptions  only  prove  the  law.  Like  children  in  front  of  a  Christmas  tree,  people  pushed  and  shoved  as  they  reached  out  to  grab  the  simple,  crude  little  toy  that  a  Gestapo  Santa  Claus  was  now  offering  them,  now  snatching  away.  ...  Yes,  everyone  wants  to  live-  Shmulik  from  the  ghetto  no  less  than  Johann  Wolfgang  von  Goethe.  It  was  not  a  complicated  matter,  and  the  Gestapo  bureaucrat  explained  everything  concisely  and  clearly,  with-  out  a  single  coarseor  cynical  word,  and  he  even  came  out  with  a  few  sen-  tences  that  were  not  strictly  necessary:  about  how  all  civilized  people  under-  stand  that,  when  it  comes  to  matters  of  worldwide  historical  importance,  there  can  be  only  one  morality-the  morality  of  state  expediency.  In  Ger-  many,  doctors  had  understood  this  long  ago.  Nikolay  Viktorovich  listened,  nod-  ding  quickly  and  obediently,  and  the  look  in  his  eyes  was  that  of  an  atten-  tive  pupil,  a  pupil  determined  to  mem-  orize  as  accurately  and  conscientiously  as  possible  everything  that  his  teacher  says.  Or  perhaps  it  could  better  be  described  as  a  look  of  servility,  an  ex-  pression  of  obsequious  devotion  to  strength.   Examining  this  sanatorium  doc-  tor,  this  well-groomed  aristocrat,  the  Gestapo  bureaucrat  thought  good-  naturedly  that  he  didn't  really  have  the  right  to  laugh  at  him,  after  all.  This  man  had  been  exposed  to  over-  whelming  temptation;  he  had  been  enslaved  by  his  many  years  of  sweet  living  in  the  wonderful  climate  of  a  spa  town,  among  flower  beds  and  bub-  bling,  health-giving  Russian  water.  No  doubt  he  had  many  well-tailored  Russian  suits  and  an  apartment  full  of  expensive  antique  furniture.  And  no  doubt  this  apartment  was  well  stocked  with  delicacies.  No  doubt  he  regularly  ate  Russian  caviar  that  he  had  sto-  len  from  the  sanatorium  stores.  No  doubt  he  collected  fine  crystal,  or  am-  ber  cigarette  holders,  or  walking  sticks  with  ivory  handles.  ..  .  And  there  was  no  doubt  at  all  that  he  had  a  beauti-  ful  wife.  The  stocky,  thick-necked  German,  this  manwho  looked  as  if  he  had  been  cobbled  together  out  of  some  kind  of  waste  matter,  was  by  no  means  sim-  pleminded.  His  work  had  to  do  with  the  human  soul,  that  mystery  of  mys-  teries;  when  it  came  to  clarity  of  vision,  and  one  or  two  other  things,  this  man  could  vie  with  God.  The  men  left  the  hospital  together,  and  Nikolay  Viktorovich  saw  two  German  sentries  standing  beside  the  doors;  it  was  no  longer  possible  for  anyone  to  come  or  go  freely.  The  Gestapo  bureaucrat  offered  to  drive  Nikolay  Viktorovich  home.  Sit-  ting  in  silence  on  the  hard  seats  of  the  staff  car,  they  looked  out  at  the  friendly  streets  and  comfortable  houses  of  the  world-famous  spa  town.  Before  saying  goodbye  to  Niko-  lay  Viktorovich,  the  German  quickly  went  through  everything  once  again:  &quot;A  car  will  come  for  you  in  the  morn-  ing.  The  hospital  staff  will  have  to  leave  thebuilding  for  a  brief  period  while  you  attend  to  the  medical  side  of  proceedings.  Once  the  covered  trucks  have  left  the  hospital,  it  must  be  ex-  plained  to  the  staff  that  the  chron-  ically  ill  and  the  seriously  wounded  have  been  transferred,  on  instructions  from  higher  authorities,  to  a  spe-  cial  hospital  outside  of  town.  It  is,  of  course,  crucial  that  you,  Nikolay  Vik-  torovich,  should  exercise  discretion;  itis  probably  of  more  importance  to  you  than  to  anyone  that  this  operation  should  not  attract  publicity.&quot;  Nikolay  Viktorovich  recounted  all  this  to  Elena  Petrovna  and  said,  &quot;For-  give  me!&quot;  After  a  minute  or  two  of  silence,  she  said,  &quot;And  I'd  got  everything  ready  for  the  theatre.  I'd  taken  your  suit  out  and  ironed  my  dress.&quot;  He  didn't  answer.  She  went  on,  &quot;You're  right.  There's  nothing  else  you  can  do.&quot;  &quot;I've  just  had  a  thought,  you  know,&quot;  Nikolay  Viktorovich  said.  &quot;In  twenty  years,  I've  never  once  been  to  the  the-  atre  without  you.&quot;  &quot;I'm  going  with  you  this  time,  too.  This  is  another  theatre  we're  going  to  together.&quot;  &quot;You're  out  of  yourmind!&quot;  he  shouted.  &quot;Why?&quot;  &quot;You  can't  stay.  So  how  can  I?&quot;  He  began  kissing  her  hands.  She  put  her  arms  around  his  neck  and  kissed  him  on  the  lips;  she  kissed  his  gray  hair.  &quot;My  handsome  one,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;What  a  lot  of  orphans  we're  going  to  leave  behind.&quot;  &quot;My  poor  boys-but  there's  noth-  ing  I  can  do  for  them,  only  this.&quot;  &quot;It  wasn't  them  I  meant.
