 b"THE  surprising  thing  about  our  seven  years  under  the  limiter  system  was  not  that  every  one  of  us  in  the  family  suffered  minor  in-  juries-grazed  foreheads,  collisions,  bruised  noses-at  some  stage  or  other  but  that  it  caused  only  one  major  ac-  cident.  As  fate  would  have  it,  that  acci-  dent  had  to  befall  the  most  important  visitor  to  our  house  in  all  that  time-  Mr.  Thompson,  Inspector  of  Schools,  who  was  Father's  boss.  And  if  he  had  stood  his  ground  at  the  top  of  the  stairs  when  the  lights  failed  that  night,  and  waited  for  Father  to  guide  him  down,  he  would  never  have  fallen  and  frac-  tured  his  kneecap.  Indeed,  Father  was  on  his  way  up  to  get  him  when  Mr.  Thompson  bumped  into  the  grandfather  clock,  lost  his  balance,  crashed  thunder-  ously  down  the  stairs,  and  landed  in  a  heap  at  Father's  feet.  His  unfortunate  descent  put  him  out  of  action  for  months,  temporarily  ended  Father's  hopes  of  pro-  motion,  and  caused  us  to  switch  from  the  cheap,  restless  limiter  system  to  the  expensive  quiet  of  the  regular.  The  limiter  system  was  designed  by  the  Electricity  Board  of  our  town  in  [REDACTED]  Ireland  for  cautious  families  with  modest  incomes.  For  a  fixed  price,  a  constant  amount  of  current  was  channelled  to  one's  home.  This  limited  supply  could  be  used  all  day  every  day,  and  one  still  paid  the  same  honest  \xc2\xa317100  at  the  end  of  every  quarter.  But  one  could  not  cheat.  If  anun-  scrupulous  consumer  at-  tempted  to  get  more  voltage  than  he  had  contracted  for,  the  me-  ter  had  a  seizure,  all  the  lights  in  the  house  went  out,  and  the  pledge-breaker  was  left  in  darkness  until  the  Electricity  Board  de-  cided  that  he  had  learned  his  lesson,  and  consented  to  come  and  perform  a  com-  plicated  operation  on  the  fuse  box.  The  system  was  practical  and  eco-  nomical,  but  it  had  one  slight  drawback:  As  soon  as  one  began  tapping  one's  pre-  cious  supply  of  current,  the  meter,  which  in  our  house  was  placed  high  on  the  wall  inside  and  to  the  left  of  the  front  door,  began  to  gargle.  The  noise  began  with  the  first  light  switched  on,  and  as  one  switched  on  a  second  and  a  third  and  a  fourth,  its  volume  increased  with  each  extravagance  and  the  gargling  turned  to  a  grumbling  and  then  to  a  growling  and  then-if  one  was  squan-  dering  one's  full  quota  of  250  watts-to  the  horrible,  dry  death  rattle  of  some     NO  UPSTAIRS  great  woodland  monster,  filling  the  ground  floor  with  its  last  agonies  and  making  normal  conversation  in  the  hall-  way  impossible.  Since  we  could  never  be-  come  deaf  to  the  meter,  we  came  to  understand  its  every  inflection.  At  its  calmest,  it  was  the  controlled  warning  of  a  gruff  &quot;Careful,  there.  Only  two  hundred  watts  to  go.&quot;  At  its  tor-  tured  worst,  it  fairly  bellowed,  &quot;Enough!  You're  going  too  far!  Watch  out!  Watch  out!&quot;  And  if  at  a  peak  consump-  tion  period  one  were  to  disregard  its  thunderings  or  to  goad  it  deliberately  beyond  endurance,  it  almost  heaved  with  fury,  gave  a  sharp  yelp  of  anguish,  and  suddenly  went  into  a  coma,  dousing  all  the  lights.  It  used  to  take  the  Board  officials  hours  to  massage  its  greatheart  back  to  life.  It  was  no  easy  matter  spreading  250  watts  among  seven  lights.  (We  had  no  domestic  appliances  except  an  iron  and  a  radio.)  Mother  did  her  laundry  during  the  day,  so  that  the  rest  of  us,  all  readers  by  inclination,  could  have  lights  at  night.  After  weeks  of  trial  and  error,  Father,  who  was  a  teacher,  arrived  at  a  fairdis-  tribution:  living  room,  75  watts;  kitch-  en,  60;  scullery,  45;  hallway,  60;  two  bedrooms,  75  each;  bathroom,  45.  That  gave  a  total  of  435  watts-185  in  excess  of  our  allowance.  But  if  certain  rules  were  observed,  we  could  avoid  fusing  the  system  and  still  make  the  greatest  use  of  the  available  supply.  The  first  rule  was  Downstairs  No  Up-  stairs.  By  this  we  un-  derstood  that  if  all  the  lights  downstairs  were  on  (240  watts),  no  light  could  be  switched  on  upstairs,  and,sim-  ilarly,  if  all  the  lights  upstairs  were  on  (195  watts),  no  downstairs  light,  except  that  in  the  distant  scullery,  could  be  switched  on.  It  was  possible,  of  course,  to  go  upstairs  to  the  bathroom  or  to  one's  bedroom,  provided  one  first  switched  off  a  downstairs  light.  This  we  called  Compensating-an-  other  rule.  If  anyone  wished  to  go  to  bed  before  the  other  members  of  the  family,  he  would  call  from  the  head  of  the  stairs,  &quot;Compensate!,&quot;  at  which  someone  below  would  switch  off  a  downstairs  light  and  so  pacify  the  mon-  ster  in  the  hall.  Apart  from  an  occasional  nose  bumped  in  the  dark  or  a  rare  careless  mistake  that  extinguished  the  whole  house,  the  arrangement  worked  reason-  ably  well. We  came  to  know  all  the  possible  combinations  (living  room,  hall,  one  bedroom;  hall  and  two  bedrooms;  scullery,  hall,  bathroom,  and  one  bed-  room;  and  so  on),  so  that  our  move-  ments  through  the  house  required  no  j  mental  arithmetic,  and  as  time  went  on,  the  cries  of  &quot;Compensate!&quot;  and  &quot;Down-  stairs  No  Upstairs!&quot;  became  fewer.  Visitors,  however,  were  an  embarrass-  ment.  Our  welcome  to  them  had  to  be  delivered  in  overhearty  tones,  and  as  they  moved  about,  they  had  to  be  care-  fully  shepherded.  The  sensible  thing  would  havebeen  to  explain  the  limiter  to  guests  and  advise  them  of  its  pitfalls.  But  Father  preferred  to  pretend  that  the  growling  meter  was  behaving  abnormal-  Iy-&quot;Has  yours  been  going  on  like  this? People  who  roam  about  one's  upstairs  have  a  habit  of  try-  ing  all  the  lights  before  they  get  the  right  one.  So  Father  would  rise,  too,  and  say,  &quot;Certainly.
