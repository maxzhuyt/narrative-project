 b"I LOVED  my  mother,  but  never-theless  I  was  glad  when  the  time  came  to  go  to  her  mother's  house  each  summer.  It  was  a  little  house  in  the  mountains  and  it  commanded  a  fine  view  of  the  valley  and  the  great  lake  below.  From  the  front  door,  aided  by  a  pair  of  very  old  binoculars,  one  could  see  the  Lough  Derg  studded  with  var-  fous  islands.  On  a  summer's  day,  I  would  be  put  standing  on  a  kitchen  chair  while  someone  held  the  binocu-  lars,  and  sometimes  I  marvelled,  though  I  could  not  see  at  all,  as  the  lenses  had  not  been  focussed  properly.  The  sunshine  made  everything  better  and,  though  we  were  not  down  by  the  lake,  we  imagined  dipping  our  feet  in  it  or  seeing  people  in  boats  fishing  and  then  stopping  to  have  a  picnic.  We  imagined  lake  water  lapping.  I  felt  safe  in  that  house.  It  was  dif-  ferent  from  our  house,  not  so  imposing,  a  cottage  really,  with  no  indoor  water  and  no  water  closet,  We  went  to  the  well  for  buckets  of  water,  and  there  was  a  different  well  each  summer.  These  were  a  source  of  miracle  to  me,  these  deep  cold  wells,  sunk  into  the  ground  in  the  kitchen  garden  or  the  paddock  or  even  a  long  distance  away-wells  that  had  been  divined  since  I  was  last  there.  Nearby  there  was  always  a  tin  scoop  so  that  one  could  fill  the  bucket  to  the  very  brim.  Then,  of  course,  the  full  bucket  was  an  occasion  of  trepidation,  because  one  was  not  supposed  to  spill.  I  often  brought  the  bucket  to  the  very  thresh-  old  of  the  kitchen  and  then,  out  of  excitement  or  clumsiness,  water  would  get  splashed  onto  the  concrete  floor  and  there  would  be  admonishments,  but  they  were  not  like  the  admonish-  ments  in  our  own  house;  it  was  not  momentous.  My  grandfather  was  old  and  thin  and  hoary  when  I  first  saw  him.  He  was  the  color  ofa  clay  pipe.  After  market  day,  he  would  come  home  drunk  in  the  pony-and-trap,  and  as  soon  as  he  stepped  out  of  the  trap  he  would  stagger  and  fall,  intoa  drain  or  whatever.  Then  he  would  roar  for  help,  and  his  grandson-my  cousin  Joe,  who  was  in  his  twenties-would  pick  him  up,  or,  rather,  drag  him  along  the  ground  and  through  the  house,  up  the  stairs  to  his  feather  bed,  where  he  moaned  and  groaned.  The  bedroom  was  above  the  kitch-  en  and  in  the  night  we  would  be  below,  around  the  fire,  eating  warm  soda  bread  and  drinking  cocoa.  There  was  nothing  like  it.  The  fresh  bread  would                  only  be  an  hour  out  of  the  pot,  cut  in  thick  pieces  and  dolloped  with  butter  and  greengage  jam.  The  greengage  jamwas  an  Easter  present  from  the  postmistress,  who  gave  it  in  return  for  the  grazing  of  a  bullock.  She  gave  marmalade  at  Christmastime  and  a  barmbrack  at  Halloween. She  was  a  minute  wom-  an  with  a  minute  face,  and  her  thin  hair  was  pinned  up  tightly.  Her  little  face,  though  old,  was  like  a  bud,  and  when  she  was  young  she  had  been  beautiful.  There  was  a  photo  of  her  to  prove  it. Maybe  I  would  never  again  lie  in  bed  next  to  my  mother,  the  two  of  us  shivering  with  expectancy  and  with  terror.  Maybe  I  would  for-  sake  my  mother. I  stayed  up  as  late  as  they  did.  I  ate  soda  bread  and  jam  to  my  heart's  content;  I  rambled  around  the  fields  all  day,  admiring  sally  trees,  elder  bushes,  and  the  fluttering  flowers;  I  played  &quot;shop&quot;  to  my  heart's  content;  or  I  played  &quot;teaching&quot;  in  the  little  dark  plantation,  where  no  one  interfered  or  told  me  to  stop  doing  it.  The  plantation  was  where  I  played  secrets  and  yet  knew  the  grownups  were  within  shouting  distance  if  a  stranger  or  a  tinker  should  surprise  me  there.  It  was  pitch  dark  and  full  of  young  fir  trees.  The  ground  was  a  carpet  of  bronzed  fallen  fir  needles.  I  would  kneel  on  them  for  punishment,  after  the  playing. I  can  see  them  now  bright  reds,  like  lipsticks,  and  those  yel-  lows,  like  the  cloth  of  a  summer  dress,  and  those  pale  blues,  like  old  people's  eyes-with  the  bees  and  the  wasps  luxuriating  in  each  petal  and  each  little  bell  or  flute,  and  the  warmth  of  the  place,  and  the  drone  of  the  bees,  and  my  eyes  lighting  on  tea  towels  and  flannel  drawers  spreadout  on  the  hedge  to  dry.  The  sun  garden  they  called  it.  My  aunt  got  the  seeds  and  just  sprinkled  them  around  and  these  various  and  marvellous  blooms  sprang  up. I  would  have  to  give  it  to  the  mail-car  man  the  following  morning  and  ask  that  the  postman  deliver  it  by  hand.  She  said,  &quot;God  only  knows  what  commotion  there  will  be  all  day  and  into  the  night  looking  for  you.&quot;  The  ink  in  her  pen  gave  out,  and  I  held  the  near-empty  ink  bottle  sideways  while  she  refilled  it.  &quot;Go  back  to  your  place,&quot;  she  said,  and  I  went  back  to  the  far  end  of  the  table,  like  someone  glued  to  her  post.  I  thought  of  the  fields  around  my  grandmother's  house  and  the  various  smooth  stones  that  I  had  put  on  the  windowsill;  I  thought  of  the  sun  gar-  den,  of  the  night  my  grandfather  had  died,  and  of  my  vigil  in  the  cold  parlor.  I  thought  of  many  things.  Sitting  there,  I  wanted  both  to  be  in  our  house  and  to  be  back  in  my  grandmother's,  miss-  ing  my  mother.  It  was  as  if  I  could  taste  my  pain  better  away  from  her,  the  excruciating  pain  that  told  me  how  much  I  loved  her.  In  her  company,  all  was  in  hazard.  I  thought  how  much  I  needed  to  be  without  her  so  that  I  could  think  of  her,  dwell  on  her,  and  fashion  her  into  the  perfect  person  that  she  clearly  was  not.  I  resolved  that,  for  certain,  I  would  grow  up  and  one  day  go  away.  It  was  a  sweet  thought  and  it  was  packed  with  punishment. "
