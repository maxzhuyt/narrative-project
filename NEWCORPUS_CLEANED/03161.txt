Solemnity  befits  him,  for  he  is  the  third-weightiest  land  mammal,  yet  it  must  be  conceded  that  the  rhino  overdoes  things.  Only  at  the  water  hole  does  his  natural  joie  de  vivre  break  through.  There  he  forgets  himself  and  wallows  shamelessly,  rolling  like  a  stout  suitcase  in  the  sand,  frolicking  and  squealing  with  his  peers.  White  ox-  peckers  wait  glumly  forthe  end  of  these  festive  ablutions,  impatient  to  re-  sume  picnicking  on  that  broad  and  teeming  back.  Rhino  accepts  their  return  with  resignation.  He  is  too  self-effacing,  this  mammal.  He  dines  on  thornbushes  and  frowns  his  way  through  life,  expecting  the  worst  and  usually  getting  it.  It  was  not  always  thus.  The  found-  ing  rhino,  a  small,  nervous  perissodac-  tyl  named  Hyrachyus,  had  no  horn  or  very  much  of  anything  else  upstairs,  but  that  didn't  keep  him  from  trying  hard.  Hyrachyus  peeped  out  of  the  pri-  meval  ooze  some  sixty  million  years  ago,  not  in  Asia  or  Africa,  as  various  'Third  World  spokesmen  would  like  us  to  think,  but  just  outside  Fort  Bridger,  Wyoming,  during  the  hopeful  Eocene  epoch.  His  playmate  in  those  days  was  a  close  cousin  named  Hyracotherium,  who  later  went  on  to  win  fame  and  fortune  as  the  horse.  Hyrachyus's  descendants  might  well  have  become  merry-  andrews  like  Hyra-  cotherium's  if  it  had  not  been  forthe  danned  horn.  The  first  rhino  with  horns  was  probably  Di-  ceratheres,  a  sensitive  type  who,  while  in  his  teens,  woke  up  one  morning  in  the  Oligo-  cene  and  noticed  two  lumpish  protuberances  growing  side  by  side  on  his  nose. Baluchitheres  had  no  horn,  but  Brachypotheres,  Elasmotheres,  and  Teleoceras  fossiger  did,  and  so  did  Coelodonta  antiquitatis,  the  ice  age's  woolly  rhinoceros,  whose  bones  are  nearly  always  found,  embarrassingly,  in  the  same  tar  bed  with  those  of  the  mammoth.  Some  species  let  it  go  at  one  horn,  while  others  experimented  with  two,  in  various  combinations.  Gradually,  though,  as  they  carried  the  bright  flame  of  rhinoceros  consciousness  to  remote  corners  of  the  globe,  it  began  to  dawn  on  these  previously  insouciant  creatures  that  their  horn  was  not  quite  what  it  seemed  to  bee-that  it  was  not  a  horn  at  all,  in  fact,  but  merely  an  outgrowth  of  the  skin,  composed  of  rather  nasty  agglutinated  fibres.
