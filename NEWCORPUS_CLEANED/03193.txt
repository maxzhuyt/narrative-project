STEPHEN,  STEVE,  STEVIE  'M  remembering  a  certain  August  day  almost  three  years  ago.  It  was  the  day  following  Stevie's  thir-  teenth  birthday.  In  the  morning,  after  we'd  swum  for  a  while,  I  lay  on  my  side  at  the  edge  of  the  water  and  watched  himand  his  mother  playing  a  kind  of  improvised  water  polo  with  the  red  plastic  football  we'd  bought  on  the  way  down  to  the  beach.  Moyra  wanted  me  to  join  in,  but  I  was  content  to  lie  still  and  enjoy  the  patterns  they  made  in  the  blazing  sunlight.  When  she  threw  the  ball  in  my  direction,  I  waited  for  the  breaking  wavelets  to  lift  and  slide  it  up  to  me,  then  lobbed  it  back  between  the  two  of  them  so  that  they  would  race  for  it.  Stevie  got  there  first,  though  Moyra  could  have  beaten  him  by  yards.  She  made  it  look  as  if  she  hadn't  let  him  win.  She  reached  him  and  saw  that  he  was  holding  the  ball  in  the  air,  and  swam  back  very  fast  to  where  she  had  been,  using  the  stylish  and  economical  crawl  I've  al-  ways  envied.  &quot;Steve!&quot;  I  heard  her  shout,  and,  hearing  my  name,  I  looked  straight  up  into  the  sun,  expecting  the  ball  to  come  fying  over  from  Stevie.  I  rubbed  the  glaring  blindness  out  of  my  eyes,  and  then  I  realized  that  she'd  calledto  him,  not  me.  Several  times  lately  she'd  done  that,  confusing  me.
