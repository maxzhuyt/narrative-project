 b"WAsHINGTON  Square  Park  has  been  calm  through  the  October  days.  It  has  been  in  the  news  a  number  of  times  this  past  year,  and  the  papers  have  emphasized  its  role  as  a  cultural  center  (outdoor  art  shows  spring  and  fall,  orchestral  concerts  on  summer  Monday  nights,  folk  singing  on  clement  Sunday  afternoons  the  year  round),  a  battleground  (bohemia  vs.  Philistia),  and  a  traffic  problem  (bus  terminal?  bottleneck?  ).  The  park  is  all  of  these,  but  the  curse  of  journalism  is  to  be  wholly  accurate  and  entirely  misleading.  For,  day  in  and  day  out,  [REDACTED]  Square  remains  primarily  a  park-a  cafeteria  for  self-assured  pi-  geons,  a  rendezvous  for  dogs,  a  solarium  for  local  residents,  and  a  play  space  for  children.  On  bright  weekend  after-  noons,  true  enough,  the  square  blooms  with  tourists  from  Tenafly  wearing  outlandish  clothes  and  hoping  to  ob-  serve  art  or  trouble;  they  find  neither,  but  at  least  they  are  observed  by  other  tourists  from  Belle  Harbor,  who  take  them,  and  are  taken  by  them,  to  be  Villagers.  On  weekdays,  the  park  re-  verts  to  the  inconspicuous  natives  and  their  children.  Most  of  the  younger  childrenplay  in  the  park's  four  com-  pact  playgrounds-fenced,  paved,  and  equipped  with  sand  piles,  swings,  slides,  seesaws,  and  in  one  case  a  jungle  gym-  that  are  disposed,  fairly  sym-  metrically,  through  the  square.  The  playgrounds  differ  in  equipment,  and  their  clien-  teles  differ  in  age.  The  lines  are  firmly  drawn.  When  I  first  came  to  the  square,  some  years  ago,  I  met  a  friend,  whose  daughter  was  then  two  and  a  half  years  old,  in  the  northeast  playground,  which  includes  swings,  seesaws,  medium-high  slides,  and  a  sand  pile.  Almost  im-  mediately,  she  told  me  that  the  north-  west  playground,  which  has  a  sand  pile  and  nothing  else,  was  &quot;just  for  babies.&quot;  She  was  boasting  about  her  mature  child,  but  she  was  right  about  N.W.  I  have  never  seen  any  but  the  smallest  sizes  of  children  in  that  pile,  where  they  wriggle  indefatigably,  like  worms  in  a  mulch.  The  southwest  play-  ground  is  practically  identical  with  the  northeast.  The  southeast  has  no  sand  pile,  but  it  does  have  a  jungle  gym,  high  slides,  and  a  white-paint  outline  of  shuf-  fleboard  courts  on  its  pavement.  S.E.'s  swings,  unlike  those  of  N.E.  and  S.W.,        which  have  aluminum  bucket  seats  with  aluminum-bar  safety  belts,  have  simple,  flat  seats,  also  aluminum,  that  encour-  age  self-pumping.  S.E.  is  the  park's  pinnacle;  when  the  neighborhood  chil-  dren  outgrow  it,  they  are  ready  to  play  in  the  streets.  But  N.E.  is  the  liveliest  of  the  four.  I  don't  know  why  S.W.,  which  caters  to  the  same  age  group,  is  torpid  by  com-  parison;  whatever  the  reason,  N.E.  of-  fers  the  finest  spectacle-sentimental,  sophisticated,  or  half  and  half.  Senti-  mentality  says  children  are  cute,  but  sophistication  points  out  that  they  are  interesting  because  they  are  not  pretty.  They  are,  in  fact,  badly  proportioned,  taking  Greek  statuary  as  the  human  ideal;  after  infancy,  some  thin  out  and  resemble  attenuated  Etruscan  figures,  while  others  are  stubby,  like  pre-Colum-  bian  pieces.  And  they  move  awkward-  ly.  Their  imperfections  are  not  mere-  ly  physical;  children  are  ignorant.  On  the  other  hand,  they  are  learning.  The  babies  in  N.W.  are  learning  things  that  are  oftentoo  fine  for  the  specta-  tor  to  perceive-the  tickle  of  a  fallen  leaf  or  the  feel  of  sand.  And  the  chil-  dren  in  S.E.  have  already  learned  so  much-for  example,  the  rules  of  shuffleboard-that  they  have  grown  almost  as  predictable  as  adults.  In  N.E.,  self-education  is  visible.  The  playground  is  the  scene  of  constant  trial-and-  error  experimentation-the  collection  of  experiences,  as  recommended  by  Sir  Francis  Bacon.  Textbooks  invariably  remark  that  Bacon's  method  is  inadequate  to  the  require-  ments  of  science,  but  here  it  works  very  well,  particu-  larly  in  the  sand  pile,  where  no  a  priori  doctrine  limits  use.  Swings  can  only  be  swung  in.  Slides  offer  just  one  variant-climb  up  the  slide  and  walk  down  the  ladder.  But  nobody  knows  how  to  use  a  sand  pile,  and  everybody  tries  something  dif-  ferent.  HE  park's  sand  piles  are  generous  rectangles  sunk  a  foot  or  two  be-  low  the  level  of  the  pavement  and  fenced  with  black  iron  railings.  There's  one  entry-a  break  in  the  railing  on  one  side-and  a  single  concrete  step  down  to  sand.  But  one  doesn't  need  to  enter  the  sand  pile  to  use  it.  On  a  recent  Mon-  day  afternoon,  I  noticed  two  boys  on  tricycles  employing  N.E.'s  sand  pile  by" b"pedalling  around  it.  Their  purpose  was  to  meet  each  other  in  a  head-on  colli-  sion.  Afterward,  they  disentangled  their  machines,  passed  each  other,  and  raced  offto  collide  again.  And,  close  to  the  railing  but  outside  it,  a  boy  who  looked  about  four  and  who  seemed  to  know,  precociously,  that  neatness  always  counts,  collected  the  sand  the  insiders  had  thrown  out  and  was  making  a  series  of  precise,  hand-patted  sand  pies.  The  boy's  earnest  devotion  to  sand  bak-  ery  was  impressive,  but  his  dedication  was  surpassed  by  an  older  lad  (seven  was  my  guess)  on  the  inside,  who  by  his  cap  and  shirt  was  a  member  of  the  New  York  Yankees,  by  his  tight  Levis  and  gun  belt  a  lawman,  and  by  his  ac-  tions  a  worker.  He  was  excavating  a  ditch  all  around  the  perimeter  of  the  sand  pile.  He  worked  methodically,  and  he  might  have  completed  his  self-im-  posed  public-works  project  if  other  users  ofthe  sand  pile  had  not  keptkicking  in  fill.  One  of  the  saboteurs  in  particular  fascinated  me.  He  was  a  boy  past  three,  I  estimated,  who  was  making  a  slow  circuit  of  the  sand  pile,  but  he  had  no  interest  in  sand.  He  just  wanted  to  be  on  the  inside  looking  out.  He  squatted  in  the  newly  excavated  ditch,  gripped  the  railing  bars,  and  peered.  After  a  pause,  he  would  sidle  solemnly,  always  keeping  at  least  one  hand  clasping  a  bar,  and  then  squat  and  stare.  Perhaps  he  had  just  been  to  the  zoo,  for  he  was  giving  acreditable  monkey  imitation.  Or  perhaps  he  had  seen  some  old  prison  picture  on  television.  Or  perhaps  he  may  just  have  been  entranced  at  the  spectacle  of  a  world  striped  vertically.  He  said  nothing,  not  even  when  he  stopped  and  examined  the  piemaker's  activities  fora  long,  thoughtful  spell.  While  the  monkey  was  in  temporary  conjunction  with  the  piemaker,  two  little  girls  quite  near  them  were  sitting  on  the  sand  trying  out  being  elegant.  Their  mothers  had  put  them  in  dresses,  and  they  drew  their  legs  up  and  folded  them  sidewise  under  their  skirts.  In  this  pose,  they  conversed  gently.  I  should  not  have  been  surprised  if  they  had  been  talking  French;  they  looked  like  a  pleasant,  unambitious  Boudin  of  a  plage  somewhere  in  the  nineteenth  cen-  tury.  They  remained  composed,  lady-  like,  and  oblivious  of  their  surround-  ings.  Right  beside  the  nineteenth-century  girls,  an  up-to-date  boy  was  tipping  a  bucketful  of  sand  onto  the  head  of  a  shorts-wearing,  twentieth-century  girl.  He  might  have  been  hoping  to  make  her  cry,  but  she  was  beatific.  She  had  never  liked  anything  better,  and  when  he " b" emptied  the  bucket,  she  insisted  he  refill  it  and  pour  more  sand  over  her.  Did  they  prefigure  the  happy  mar-  riage  of  a  masochist  and  a  sadist?  I  doubt  it.  I  have  not  seen  much  cruelty  or  even  much  aggressiveness  in  the  sand  pile,  but  mainly,  as  I  have  said,  experi-  ment.  Observers  of  animal  life  nowa-  days  are  taught  not  to  inject  themselves  into  their  observations,  as  their  prede-  cessors  did  in  the  days  when  half  the  world's  naturalists  were  seeing  the  struggle  for  existence  and  the  other  half  were  seeing  mutual  aid  preserving  spe-  cies,  genera,  families,  orders,  classes,  and  phyla.  So  I  should  admit  it  is  pos-  sible,  because  I  myself  am  curious,  that  I  see  curiosity  and  inquiry  in  the  sand  pile.  Inevitably,  some  experiments  look  like  naughtiness.  At  any  rate,  four  little  boys  who  were  spending  the  afternoon  fighting  a  no-holds-barred,  catch-as-  catch-can  sand  war  looked  cubbish  to  me  rather  than  quarrelsome.  They  met  face  to  face;  they  also  stalked  each  other  from  behind  and  threw  sand  on  each  other's  heads  and  down  the  back  of  each  other's  shirts.  In  lieu  of  buckets,  they  splashed  sand  at  one  another  by  handfuls.  It  seemed  to  me  that  they  were  simply  exploring  the  limits  of  the  capabilities  of  sand  as  a  weapon.  The  other  children  in  the  sand  pile  seemed  to  agree  with  me;  at  any  rate,  as  the  boys  stumbled  around  in  their  free-for-  all,  they  drenched  other  sand-pile  oc-  cupants  with  sand,  but  their  victims  showed  no  resentment.  They  seemed  to  assume  that  accidents  would  happen.  Besides,  they  were  busy  with  their  own  affairs.  Possessiveness,  by  the  way,  seems  to  be  the  kind  of  naughtiness  that  most  worries  the  mothers  in  N.E.  It  brought  the  one  instance  of  major  maternal  intervention  I  observed  that  particular  afternoon.  A  boy  was  working  his  way  around  the  sand  pile  on  his  knees,  as  if  in  fulfillment  of  a  vow,  pushing  a  large  plastic  truck  along  the  con-  crete  rim  just  inside  the  railing.  He  bowled  past  the  Yankee-lawman-ditch-  digger  and  roared  under  the  imitation  monkey's  arm  to  keep  his  big  truck  roll-  ing  on  schedule.  'Then  he  happened  to  look  up  and  discover  that  his  other  vehi-  cles-he  had  parked  a  fleet  of  plastic  earth-moving  equipment  in  the  center  of  the  sand  pile,  making  a  tidy  motor  park-had  been  appropriated  by  some  of  the  children,  who  were  driving  off  in  every  direction.  In  a  flash,  he  aban-  doned  his  truck  and  flung  himself  at  his  thieving  colleagues.  Rage  impaired  his  coordination,  but  when  at  last  he  got  his  hands  on  one  of  his  toys,  he  re-  " b"placed  it  in  the  motor  park  exactly  where  he  had  had  it  before.  His  mother  didn't  appreciate  his  vision  of  order;  she  saw  only  possessiveness,  and  she  rose  from  her  bench,  stalked  to  the  railing,  and  shouted,  &quot;Michael,  share  your  trucks!&quot;  And  she  stayed  there  till  he  did. People  usually  re-  member  something-a  corner,  a  stair,  a  chair,  a  kind  of  candy,  abuilding,  or  a  person-fondly.  I  can't  help  wonder-  ing  what  the  children  in  N.E.  this  fall,  many  of  whom  are  at  an  age  where  memories  begin,  will  remember  with  affection.  There  are  magnificent  trees  in  the  park,  and  some  luxurious  grass  plots,  now  going  brown,  but  some  of  the  dreary  patches  of  bare  ground  have  been  bald  all  year.  The  bums  who  sleep  stretched  out  on  the  benches  are  picturesque,  but  there  are  too  many  of  them;  they  crowd  the  picture.  Some-  body  or  other  is  playing  a  musical  in-  strument-at  least  a  drum-outdoors  every  day,  usually  incompetently.  (Last  spring's  hullabaloo  about  the  Sunday-  afternoon  hoot-nannies  put  the  question  incorrectly;  we  were  asked  to  be  for  music  or  against  it,  when  the  real  issue  was  noise-bad  music-versus  silence.  Perhaps  open-air  guitarists  and  folk  singers  ought  to  be  tested  and  licensed,  like  automobile  drivers.  )  The  wheezing  Fifth  [REDACTED]  buses  that  turn  around  at  the  Arch  are  not  charming,  the  way  the  ancient  double-deckers  were.  Only  a  few  red  brick  mansions  are  left  from  Catherine  Sloper's  day.  The  massive  white  apartment  tower  to  the  north  of  the  Arch  weeps  rusty  streaks  from  win-  dow  air-conditioners  down  uncounted  stories-tears,  I  imagine,  for  its  own  ugliness.  Things,  in  short,  are  not  what  they  used  to  be.  Someday  the  children  in  the  sand  pile  will  find  that  out.  When?  In  twenty  years  or  so.  They  will  look  back  totheir  New  York,  the  square  in  the  nineteen-sixties,  weak  with  long-  ing  for  the  bums,  the  bare  earth,  the  terrible  guitar  playing,  the  ugly  build-  ing  with  a  facade  like  a  mouth  full  of  dental  braces.  Some  of  them  will  de-  cide  that  a  glory  has  gone,  and  that  they  cannot  raise  their  children  in  the  New  York  of  the  nineteen-eighties.  Heaven  knows  how  far  theywill  have  to  go-  all  the  way  to  Poughkeepsie,  perhaps-  to  escape  New  York.  And  even  then  they  won't  escape  it,  for  New  York  fol-  lows  the  fleeing  New  Yorker.  As  in  that  " b"unimaginable  concept  of  the  expanding  universe,  in  which  the  galaxies  fly  apart  and  yet  retain  their  identities,  New  York  extends  itself,  no  matter  into  what  juris-  diction.  My  guess  is  the  New  Yorker  carries  his  city  with  him.  How  many  delicatessens  were  there  in  Fairfield  County  in  1920?  How  many  are  there  now?  For  the  sake  of  their  children,  many  of  the  mothers  of  N.E.  have  joined  or-  ganizations  that  are  working  to  improve  the  city.  In  the  meantime,  every  bit  of  today's  squalor  is  forming  somebody's  &quot;&quot;*  happy  childhood  memory.
