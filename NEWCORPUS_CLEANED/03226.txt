Traditionally,  American  poets  have  listened  to  some  things-  stars,  oceans,  the  moon,  birch  trees,  the  thrush-and  ignored  the  rest.  But  some  poets,  such  as  the  celebrated  Arthur  Newell,  have  followed  Yin  Chang's  admonition,  &quot;Do  not/Lock  the  bird  in  the  cage!  /Follow  it  as  it  flies/On  quick  wings,&quot;  and  are  approaching  nature  with  an  open  mind.  In  the  Old  Poetry,  for  example,  a  tree  symbolized  life  and  a  bare  tree  meant  death.  But  is  this  what  the  tree  intended,  or  merely  what  the  poet  read  into  it?  Arthur  Newell  may  listen  to  a  tree,  an  ordinary  spruce,  for  a  month  and  then  suddenly  discover  that  it  is  his  mother  looking  out  at  the  frozen  snow  of  North  Dakota  as  if  her  old  heart  would  break.  Or  he  may  listen  to  a  mandrill  at  the  zoo  and  hear  him  say,  &quot;Forgive  me  for  leaving  Lincoln,  Ne-  braska./I  weep  for  your  misery./I  am  no  good  at  football.&quot;  I  visited  ArthurNewell  this  winter  on  his  farm  near  Winifred,  North  Da-  kota,  soon  after  &quot;Cold  Light  of  Day&quot;  (Anteater  Press,  $2.50)  was  published.  He  picked  me  up  at  the  bus  depot  and  we  drove  past  the  familiar  &quot;woods  pleading  for  love&quot;  and  the  Little  Plum  River  with  its  &quot;mad  fear  of  bridges.&quot;  &quot;This  is  beautiful,&quot;  I  said,  looking  out  at  the  wintry  land-  scape.  &quot;You  impose  your  own  con-  cept  of  beauty  on  it,&quot;  he  said  quietly,  &quot;because  you  have  no  wish  to  see  what  is  really  there  underneath  the  surface.&quot;  I  assured  him  that  I  did  wish  to  see  this  but  that  I  was  tired  from  my  long  trip.  &quot;At  Lake  Winifred/Under  the  clear  ice,  fish  sing/And  are  truly  happy,&quot;  he  said.  I  asked  him  to  show  me  the  lake  the  next  morning.  We  set  out  at  dawn  across  the  desolate  fields.  They  seemed  desolate  to  me,  at  any  rate.  Soon  we  came  to  the  lake,  which  was  covered  with  ice  and  snow.  As  we  sat  on  the  frozen  shore,  we  heard  music  that  seemed  to  come  up  out  of  the  ground.  It  was  the  deep  baritone  voices  of  walleyes  under  the  ice  singing  &quot;Her  Bright  Smile  Haunts  Me  Still,&quot;  &quot;Molly  Bawn,&quot;  &quot;O  Haste,  Crimson  Morn,&quot;  and  other  favorites.  A  passing  female  rabbit  heard  them,  too,  for  she  sat  down  near  us  and  said,  &quot;These  lovely  fish!/They  are  of  the  tribe  of  Sacajawea,  in  the  bus  depot  in  San  Diego.&quot;  &quot;Accountants  and  bank  presidents  skate  on  the  ice  on  envelopes/Trying  to  forget  the  French  Revolution,&quot;  re-  plied  Arthur.  &quot;But  in  the  cold  light/The  fish  study  the  colors  of  the  sun,&quot;  the  rabbit  said.  She  and  Arthur  agreed  to  collabo-  rate  on  a  poem  based  on  what  she  had  heard  from  bears.
