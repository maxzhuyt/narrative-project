Maybe  Daddy  will  even  be  here  to  see  it.&quot;  &quot;Sure,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;And  maybe  this  time  the  Trojans  will  win  the  war.&quot;  I  stand  offstage  with  Mr.  Dodd  and  wait  for  my  final  cue.  The  dry-ice  ma-  chine  has  been  turned  on  full  blast  and  an  incredible  amount  of  fake  smoke  is  making  its  way  toward  the  painted  backdrop  of  Troy.  Hector's  papier-  miaché  head  has  accidentally  slipped  from  Achilles'  hand  and  is  now  mak-  ing  a  hollow  sound  as  it  rolls  across  the  stage.  I  peek  around  the  thick  red  cur-  tain,  trying  to  see  into  the  audience.  The  auditorium  is  packed,  filled  with  parents  and  camcorders.  I  spot  my  mom  sitting  in  the  front  row,  alone.  I  try  to  scan  the  back  wall,  looking  for  a  sign  of  him,  a  familiar  shadow.  Nothing.  Soon  I  will  walk  out  on  the  ram-  parts,  put  my  hand  to  my  forehead,  and  give  my  last  speech.  &quot;Are  you  sure  you're  ready?&quot;  Mr.  Dodd  asks.  I  think  he's  more  nervous  than  I  am.  &quot;Re-  member,&quot;  he  tells  me,  &quot;this  is  [REDACTED]'s  big  moment.  Think  loss.&quot;I  nod,  think-  ing  nothing.  &quot;Break  a  leg,&quot;  he  says,  giving  me  a  little  push  toward  the  stage.  &quot;And  try  not  to  trip  over  thehead.&quot;  The  lights  are  much  brighter  than  I  had  expected,  making  me  squint.  I  walk  through  the  smoky  fog  toward  center  stage.  &quot;It  is  I,  the  hated  [REDACTED],  scourge  of  Troy.&quot;  With  the  light  on  me,  the  audience  ©  is  in  shadow,  like  a  big  pit,  dark  and  endless.  I  bow  before  the  altar,  feeling  my  tunic  rise.  &quot;Hear  my  supplication,&quot;  I  say,  pulling  down  a  bit  on  the  back  of  my  tunic.  &quot;Do  not  envy  me  such  beauty-  it  has  wrought  only  pain  and  despair.&quot;  I  can  hear  Mr.  Dodd,  offstage,  loudly  whispering  each  line  along  with  me.  &quot;For  this  destruction,  I  know  I  will  be  blamed.&quot;  I  begin  to  recite  [REDACTED]'s  wrongs-  beauty,  pride,  the  abdication  of  Sparta-  careful  to  enunciate  clearly.  &quot;Troy,  I  have  come  to  ask  you  to  forgive  me.&quot;  I'm  supposed  to  hit  my  fist  against  my  chest,  draw  a  hand  across  my  fore-  head,  and  cry  loudly.  Mr.  Dodd  has  shown  me  this  gesture,  practiced  it  with  me  in  rehearsal  a  dozen  times-the  last  line,  my  big  finish.  The  audience  is  very  quiet.  In  the  stillness  there  is  a  hole,  an  empty  pocket,  an  absence.  In-  stead  of  kneeling,  I  stand  up,  straighten  my  tunic,  look  toward  the  audience,  and  speak  the  line  softly:  &quot;And  to  say  goodbye.&quot;  There  is  a  prickly  feeling  up  the  back  of  my  neck.  And  then  applause.  The  noise  surrounds  me,  filling  me.  I  look  into  the  darkened  house  and,  for  a  second,  I  can  hear  the  beating  of  a  swan's  wings,  and,  then,  nothing  at  all.
