 b"THERE  is  a  popular  misconcep-  tion  in  Ireland  that  teachers  are  unloved  and  unloving  parents,  and  that  their  children  are  suppressed  and  unhappy.  At  least,  it  has  been  my  experience  that  when  I  tell  my  friends  that  my  father  was  a  teacher  in  a  small  country  school  near  the  town  of  Omagh,  in  County  Tyrone,  their  eyes  get  a  kind,  understanding  look.  We  know  what  it  must  have  been  like,  they  seem  tosay.  And  I  think  I  know  what  they  are  thinking:  a  tall,  stern  man  with  a  black  mustache,  looking  witheringly  at  suppressed,  unlovely  children-a  Mr.  Murdstone.  Years  ago,  it  annoyed  me  when  I  discovered  that  people  thought  that,  and  I  used  to  get  myself  involved  in  long,  hopeless  explanations  trying  to  point  out  that  yes,  Father  was  tall  and  stern  (though  he  had  no  mustache),  and  he  did  rear  us  exactly  as  he  thought  we  should  be  reared,  but  there  were  anomalies  in  his  makeup  that  left  him  larger  than  any  pigeonhole.  There  was  the  time,  for  example,  when  he  took  up  ballroom  dancing  and,with  Mother  as  a  bewildered  and  nervous  partner,  reached  the  semifinal  of  a  Grand  Dancing  Contest  in  the  Town  Hall.  And  then  there  was  the  summer  he  hired  a  horse  and  a  gypsy  caravan,  piled  us  into  the  back,  and  drove  us  round  the  Donegal  coast.  But  you  cannot  very  well  mention  inci-  dents  like  these,  because  taken  by  themselves  they,  too,  would  create  an  unbalanced  picture  of  him.  So  what  I  say  now  if  I  am  asked  about  my  childhood  is,  &quot;'Yes,  we  did  have  a  quiet  life.  But  we  kept  grey-  hounds.&quot;  I  mention  them  very  casually,  and  the  sympathetic  looks  vanish  at  once.  It  is  not  entirely  ac-  curate  to  say  that  we  kept  greyhounds.  Now-  adays,  keeping  grey-  hounds  suggests  bal-  anced  diets  and  long  hours  of  exercise  and  comfortable  kennelling,  and  the  slouching  beasts  we  had  enjoyed  none  of  these  things. I  do  not  remember  where  they  came  from,  nor  have  I  any  memory  of  what  happened  to  them  ultimately.  Mem-  ory  is  strange-and  kind-in  many  ways. Bravo!&quot;  All  eyes  turned  to  us.  &quot;Silence,  woman!&quot;  snapped  Father.  &quot;We're  the  laughing-  stock  of  the  place  as  itis  with  this-this-&quot;  He  gestured  helplessly  toward  the  pup.  But  the  damage  was  done.  The  wasted  man  was  nearby,  and,  sensing  an  opportunity  to  express  his  derision,  he  began  to  clap.  In  a  second,  everyone  on  our  side  of  the  field  was  clapping.  Then  the  whole  field  was  clapping  and  calling  &quot;Bravo!  Bravo!&quot;  in  falsetto  voices.  I  searched  the  faces  to  see  if  Lobster  and  our  other  ex-  perts  had  joined  in  deriding  us,  but  they  were  nowhere  insight.  Somehow  we  stumbled  out  of  the  field  and  away  from  those  mocking  voices.  Not  a  word  was  spoken  on  the  way  home.  Father  walked  at  a  brisk  pace,  and  Mother  did  her  best  to  keep  up  with  him.  I  fol-  lowed,  ten  yards  behind,  and  behind  me  ambled  the  fawn  pup.  But  later  that  night,  after  I  went  to  bed,  I  heard  Father  telling  Mother  that  the  bookie  who  had  accepted  our  wager  was  a  past  pupil  who  now  had  six  offices  in  County  Tyrone  and  was  soon  to  open  another  in  County  Derry.  &quot;He's  go-  ing  to  go  far,  that  fellow!&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Always  knew  he  would.&quot;  His  voice  was  strong  and  confident  through  the  ceiling.  He  went  on  to  explain  to  Mother,  in  his  usual  painstaking  way,  the  scheme  the  bookie  had  for  expan-  sion.  I  went  to  sleep  as  I  often  did,  listening  to  the  heavy  drone  of  his  voice  and  knowing  that  he  was  in  a  good  humor  again.  "
