You'll  be  in  time  for  work  after  all.&quot;  He  seemed  not  to  hear.  &quot;Have  you  had  breakfast?&quot;  I  asked.  He  nodded,  but  I  had  the  feeling  that  he  probably  had  not.  We  paid  our  bills,  and  went  to  the  station.  Even  the  sight  of  the  train,  when  it  came,  did  not  cheer  him,  I  got  into  a  compartment,  and  he  followed,  and  settled  in  a  corner  seat  and  pulled  down  the  blind.  Later,  we  had  lunch  in  the  restaurant   car,  but  except  for  that  interval  he  re-  mained  hunched  up  in  silence  in  his  cor-  ner  all  the  way  to  [REDACTED].  Even  [REDACTED]  itself,  with  lights  twinkling,  could  not  stir  him.  I  began  to  be  worried  about  him.  &quot;What  are  you  going  to  do?&quot;  I  asked  as  we  stood  on  the  platform  at  the  Gare  de  Lyon.  &quot;Fly?&quot;  &quot;No,&quot;  he  said  in  a  low  voice,  not  looking  at  me.  &quot;I  never  do.  Itold  you  that.  As  a  matter  of  fact,  I  can't.  I  get  airsick.  I'll  catch  the  early  train  for  [REDACTED]  from  the  Gare  du  Nord.  It  will  mean  changing  to  first  class,  but  I'll  be  in  [REDACTED]  by  two-five.  I'll  ring  the  office  and  tell  them  ..  .&quot;  His  words  trailed  off  as  he  wondered  what  he  would  tell  them.  &quot;I'll  think  of  some-  thing  in  the  train.  I'll  be  in  Birming-  ham  by  tomorrow  evening.&quot;  &quot;What  about  tonight?&quot;  I  said.  &quot;Why  don't  you  come  and  stay  at  my  hotel?  I  know  a  lot  of  people  here.  We'll  make  it  a  night  out  in  [REDACTED].&quot;  &quot;That's  very  kind  of  you-very  kind  indeed,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Under  any  other  circumstances,  I  would  accept  your  invitation  with  pleasure,  but&quot;--  his  voice  faltered  for  a  moment-  &quot;I'll  make  my  way  to  the  Gare  du  Nord  and  doss  down  there,  if  you  don't  mind.  I  want  to  be  sure  of  catching  my  train  in  the  morning.&quot;  &quot;Just  as  you  like,&quot;  I  said.  He  picked  up  his  bags,  and  tried  to  smile.  &quot;It's  been  nice  meeting  you,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;I'm  sorry  I  made  you  miss  your  train.  If  we  ever  meet  again,  which  is  unlikely,I  would  be  grateful  if  you  did  not  mention  this  episode.&quot;  He  gave  me  a  quick,  questioning  glance.  &quot;I  won't,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;It  wasn't  your  fault.&quot;  &quot;'Yes,  it  [REDACTED];  he  replied  forlorn-  ly.  &quot;If  I  hadn't  let  that  unofficial  porter  take  us  to  the  Mergellina,  we  wouldn'thave  been  put  on  the  wrong  train.&quot;  He  gave  me  the  hard  look  of  a  man  who  has,  at  great  cost,  been  proved  right,  and  then  took  up  his  bags  and  walked  sadly  down  the  platform.
