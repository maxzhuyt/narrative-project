For-and  this  is,  I  believe,  the  central  message  of  the  Encyclopedia's  authors-nothing  ever  " b" repeats  itself  in  human  history;  all  things  that,  at  first  glance,  seem  to  be  the  same  are  barely  similar;  every  man  is  a  single  star  unto  himself;  every-  thing  happens  always  and  never,  ev-  erything  occurs  endlessly  and  never  again. Were  it  not  for  the  compilers'  obses-  sion  with  the  uniqueness  of  each  hu-  man  being,  the  singularity  of  every  event,  what  would  be  the  point  of  giving  the  names  of  the  registrar  and  the  priest,  a  description  of  the  wedding  attire,  and  the  name  of  the  village-  Gledi\xc2\xa2i,  near  Kraljevo-together  with  all  those  details  that  connect  a  man  and  a  place? Yet  from  the  vantage  point  of  the  Encyclopedia's  compilers  Pogarevac  and  my  father's  seven-month  stay  in  the  barracks  were  unique:  never  again,  never,  will  a  certain  Dj. THAT  sea  he  gazed  at,  from  the  slopes  of  the  Velebit,  for  the  first  time,  in  his  twenty-fifth  year-April  28,  1935-lingered  in  him  as  a  revela-  tion,  a  dream  he  carried  within  him  for  forty  years  with  undimmed  inten-  sity,  like  some  secret  or  spectre  of  which  one  cannot  speak.  After  all  those  years,  he  himself  was  not  quite  sure  if  he  had  actually  seen  the  surface  of  the  open  sea  or  only  the  sky  meeting  the  horizon,  and  the  only  true  seafor  him  would  remain  that  aqua-  marine  on  maps  where  the  depths  are  marked  with  darker  and  the  shoals  with  lighter  tones  ofblue. In  that  refusal  of  his  there  was  astrange  fear,  as  if  he  were  afraid  of  being  disappointed,  as  if  a  close  encounter  with  thesea  might  destroy  that  distant  visionthat  had  illuminated  him  on  April  28,  1935,  when,  for  the  first  time  in  his  life,  he  had  seen,  from  afar,  sometime  in  the  early  dawn,  the  blue  of  the  Adriatic.  And  all  the  excuses  that  he  used  to  postpone  that  encounter  with  the  sea  were  somehow  unconvincing:  he  didn't  want  to  vacation  like  some  tour-  ist  on  abudget,  he  didn't  have  enough  money  (which  wasn't  far  from  the  truth),  Ke  couldn't  stand  strong  sun  (althoughhe  spent  his  whole  life  un-  der  strong  sun),  we  should  leave  him  in  peace,  he  was  perfectly  all  right  in  [REDACTED]  behind  drawn  shades.  Under  this  heading,  in  the  Encyclopedia  of  the  Dead,  his  association  with  the  sea  is  analyzed  in  detail,  from  that  first  lyric  sighting,  in  1935,  up  until  his  actual  face-to-face  meeting  with  it,  forty  years  later.  And  that-his  first  real  encounter  with  the  sea-took  place  in  1975,  when  finally,  at  the  family's  insistence,  he  agreed  to  go  to  Rovinj  with  my  motherand  stay  in  a  friend's  house  that  was  empty  that  summer. For  the  Encyclopedia  of  the  Dead,  history  is  the  totality  of  the  human  condition,  the  entiretyof  ephemeral  events. The  fear  that  overtook  my  father-  and  that  silence  I  myself  remember  so  well,  a  heavy  and  painful  silence-is  explained  by  the  book  as  an  infectious  fear:  one  morning  he  found  out  that  that  same  Petar  Jankovic,  his  kinsman  and  colleague  at  work,  went  at  six  every  morning  to  the  State  Security  building  for  a  talk  (on  the  heels  of  a  report  on  him  by  the  aforementioned  Dragovic)  and  arrived  at  work  late,  his  face  black  and  blue,  swollen  from  beat-  ings  and  lack  of  sleep;  it  went  on  like  this  every  single  morning,  at  daybreak,  for  about  six  months,  until  Petar  managed  to  recall  some  other  people  who  shared  his  delusions  about  the  Rus-  sians  and  listened  to  Radio  [REDACTED]. The  Encyclopedia  of  the  Dead  does  not  concern  itself  only  with  material  goods;  it  is  nota  double-entry  ledger  or  an  inventory  book,  nor  is  it  a  cat-  alogue,  like  the  Book  of  Kings  or  Genesis,  though  it  is  that  as  well.  It  also  speaks  of  the  state  of  a  man's  soul,  his  outlook  on  the  world,  on  God,  his  doubts  asto  theexistence  of  the  other  world,  his  moral  standards.  Yet  the  thing  that  is  most  astonishing  is  that  unique  link  of  the  external  and  the  internal,  that  insistence  on  concrete  facts  which  are  then  brought  into  a  coherent  connection  with  man,  with  what  we  call  his  soul. Discontented  with  his  life,  con-  sumed  by  the  melancholy  of  old  age  (which  nothing  can  assuage,  neither  devoted  children,  good  grandchildren,  nor  the  relative  serenity  of  everyday  life),  he  began  to  grumble  and  get  drunk  more  often. HE  authors  didnot  fail  to  notice  the  curious  fact  that  my  father  died  on  his  first  grandson's  twelfth  birthday. That  trail  of  the  living  and  the  dead,  that  prevail-  ing  myth  of  the  succession  of  genera-  tions,  that  feigned  solace  men  devise  that  they  may  more  easily  accept  the  thought  of  dying,  my  father  experi-  enced,  that  instant,  as  an  insult:  by  this  magical  act  of  giving  his  name  to  a  newborn  child,  however  much  his  own  flesh  and  blood,  they  &quot;broke  the  bread&quot;  over  him  and  pronounced  his  death  sentence.
