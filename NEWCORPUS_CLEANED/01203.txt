One  hundred  and  seven-not  including  the  profiles  I  cannot  see,  behind  the  armoire.  Each  time  I  get  a  different  number,  I  start  counting  her  profiles  all  over  again.  Also,  Charles  and  I  do  not  make  love  as  often;  when  we  do,  my  eyes  are  shut  and  I  make  believe  that  Charles  is  Didier.  Afterward,  I  blame  thered  and  pink  wines  we  drank  the  night  before;  I  blame  the  hundred  and  seven  profiles  of  the  woman  in  the  wall-  paper  watching-no,  spying-on  us;  I  blame  myself.  Outside,  the  air  seems  dense  and  hard  to  breathe;  in  the  house,  there  are  many  more  mosquitoes  and  flies.  The  younger  boy  collects  them  in  a  jelly  jar  that  he  leaves  on  the  table  in  the  kitchen.  I  want  to  tell  him  not  to,  but  I  pick  on  him  enough  as  it  is.  Also,  he  complains  about  how  his  arm  in  the  cast  itches  and  he  does  not  sleep  at  night.  Since  he  cannot  swim,  he  spends  most  of  the  morning  in  the  driveway,  under  the  pine  trees  where  itis  cooler,  picking  pifion  nuts  with  the  little  French  girls.  Our  boys  tower  over  the  little  French  girls,  but  at  least  they  are  speak-  ing  together.  The  two  big  bombonnes  of  wine  are  nearly  empty  and  neither  Charles  nor  I  can  believe  that  we  have  drunk  so  much  wine.  &quot;It  does  not  seem  possible,&quot;  Charles  says,  shaking  his  head.  Al-  ready,  I  am  anxious  at  the  prospect  of  leaving  La  Mayonette  and  I  wonder  when  Francine  and  I  will  see  each  other  again.  During  our  last  meal  together,  after  the  four  of  us-Francine,  Didier,  Charles,  and  I-have  finished  off  all  the  wine  in  the  two  large  plastic  bonbonnes,  Didier  brings  out  an  old  bottle  of  marc  and  we  drink  that.  It  is  then-a  little  drunk-  that  I  tell  Francine  that  I  went  to  bed  with  our  tour  guide  in  [REDACTED].  &quot;I  only  did  it  once,&quot;  I  tell  her,  and  Francine  laughs  and  says  she  had  guessed  it.  She,  too,  sounds  a  little  drunk-all  four  of  us,  probably,  have  drunk  too  much  of  the  mare,  which  is  much  stronger  than  the  wine.  And  anyway,  Francine  continues,  she  slept  with  him  as  well.  When  I  ask  Francine  how  many  times-once,  twice,  three  times?-she  only  shakes  her  head  so  that  the  long  braid  down  her  back  swings  from  side  to  side.  Then  I  say  to  Didier,  who  is  sitting  next  to  me,  that  now  itis  his  turn  to  tell  us  something  he  is  ashamed  of  or  that  he  regrets.  Wink-  ing  at  me,  Didier  says,  Nothing,  noth-  ing  at  all-rien  du  tout.  In  hisentire  life,  Didier  says  again,  there  is  nothing  that  he  feels  ashamed  of  or  that  he  would  not  do  over  again  if  he  had  the  chance,  and  Francine  breaks  in  and  says  something  sharp  like  &quot;Th  te  fous  du  monde,  quoi!&quot;  She  sounds  angry.  To  change  the  mood,  Charles  says  that  one  of  the  things  he  feels  most  ashamed  about  happened  a  long  time  ago  when  he  was  a  young  boy,  and  it  has  nothing  to  do  with  sex.  Quite  the  contrary,  Charles  says,  it  was  while  he  was  at  boarding  school  and  his  mother  used  to  write  him  a  letter  once  a  week  and  Charles  never  read  those  letters.  In  fact,  Charles  tells  us,  he  never  even  bothered  to  open  those  let-  ters,  so  that  one  time  when  he  went  home  for  the  holidays  and  his  mother  unpacked  for  him  she  found  all  her  let-  ters  there  at  the  bottom  of  his  suitcase,  a  whole  packetof  them,  unopened. Naturally,  this  time  I  hope  it  is  aboy.  But  if  itis  an-  other  girl,&quot;  she  says,  &quot;I  will  name  her  af-  ter  you.&quot;  Charles  has  packed  the  car  and  our  boys'  bicycles  again  are  tied  precariously  upside  down  on  the  roof  rack,  I  shake  my  head  in  disapproval.  &quot;This  time,  for  sure,  the  bicycles  are  going  to  fall  off,&quot;  I  tell  Charles.  Annoyed,  he  does  not  answer  me.  &quot;We  have  so  much  more  stuff  than  we  came  with,&quot;  I  complain.  The  older  boy  appears,  carrying  a  large  stack  of  French  comic  books-a  parting  gift,  I  suppose,  from  the  little  girls.  &quot;Can't  you  get  rid  of  some  of  that?&quot;  I  ask  him.  Exasperated,  I  go  back  inside  La  Mayonette  for  one  last  look  and  to  see  what  we  have  forgotten.  I  find  a  hair-  brush  in  the  boys'  bathroom,  a  few  cen-  times  on  a  bureau,  one  of  Charles's  ties  hanging  from  the  doorknob  ofour  bedroom.  I  would  like  to  do  something  before  we  leave;I  am  not  sure  what.  I  stand  ir-  resolute  in  the  middle  of  our  bedroom.  I  hear  Charles  calling-they  are  waiting  for  me.  Just  before  I  walk  out  the  door,  I  touch  the  wallpaper  profile  nearest  me  and  I  kiss  the  woman  on  the  lips. "
