He  felt  depressed,  and  sorry  for  her,  and  also  disgusted  with  himself.  &quot;You  silly  god-  dam  fool,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Anything  that  hap-  pens  to  you,  you've  deserved  it.&quot;  INALLY,  around  noon,  the  fog  began  to  lift.  At  first,  it  became  brighter,  and  then  Dayton  noticed  that  he  could  see  more  and  more  of  the  sur-  rounding  water,  until  at  last  only  the  horizon  was  blurred  and  the  sun  was  shining  hotly  overhead.  Then  a  small,  squat  fishing  boat  emerged  from  the  haze  in  the  distance,  and  Dayton  beat  the  bell  and  waved  and  shouted,  and  with  a  blast  on  its  horn  the  boat  turned  and  headed  toward  him.  Two  men  in  dungarees  and  hip  boots  helped  Dayton  step  from  the  buoy  onto  the  boat,  and  when  his  feet  touched  the  deck,  his  legs  buckled  like  rubber  hoses,  and  the  men  carried  him  to  the  top  of  a  hatch  and  sat  him  down. His  strength  came  back  in  a  warm  rush,  and  he  looked  up  at  the  men,  who  were  watching  him  in  si-  lence.  &quot;Thanks,&quot;  Dayton  said,  and  smiled.  &quot;Guess  you're  the  one  they  were  lookin'  for,&quot;  said  the  taller  of  the  men,  who  wore  a  swordfisherman's  hat  with  a  long  black  visor.  &quot;Could  be,&quot;  said  Dayton.  &quot;I  hope  so,  at  any  rate.&quot;  &quot;Alarm  went  out  last  night,&quot;  the  man  said.  &quot;Allover  the  radio. Dayton  drained  his  mug  and  looked  back  over  the  stern,  where  the  buoy  was  becoming  a  slim  speck  in  the  distance.  By  this  time,  it  looked  like  any  other  buoy;  in  fact,  Day-  ton  couldn't  be  sure  that  the  buoy  he  saw  was  the  one  on  which  he  had  spent  the  night.  In  the  wheelhouse  he  heard  a  man  talking  on  the  radio,  and  the  crackling,  spluttering  replies  from  the  shore  station,  and  he  realized  that  his  wife  must  be  all  right,  because  nobody  else  would  have  known  that  he  was  missing.  She  had  probably  awakened  in  time  to  take  the  boat  into  Vineyard  Haven  and  give  the  alarm.  The  buoy  was  gone  now,  and  ahead  of  the  boat  he  could  hear  the  ringing  of  another  one,  but  for  some  reason  it  sounded  dif-  ferent.  The  tall  man,  who  had  disappeared,  came  back  with  a  pair  of  salt-stained  dungarees  and  held  them  out  to  Day-  ton.  &quot;Guess  you  may  want  these,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Don't  want  to  go  ashore  nekked  like  that.&quot;  It  was  only  then  that  Dayton  remem-  bered  that  he  had  torn  off  most  of  his  clothes  in  the  water,  and  he  smiled  and  stepped  into  the  dungarees,  which  were  about  two  sizes  toobig  for  him.  Thanks,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;I'll  give  them  back  after  I  get  ashore.&quot;  &quot;Don't  make  no  odds,&quot;  the  man  re-  plied.  &quot;They're  kind  of  out  at  the  tail, " b"anyway.  You  want  some  more  coffee?&quot;  Dayton  nodded,  and  when  the  man  brought  him  the  coffee,  he  sipped  it  slowly  and  watched  the  outline  of  Martha's  Vineyard  loom  larger  on  the  horizon.  He  visualized  the  scene  that  would  take  place  when  he  went  ashore;  his  wife  would  be  there,  and  probably  the  Martins,  and  there  would  be  a  great  to-do  and  tears  and  embraces,  and  he  would  go  back  to  the  Martins'  and  sleep  for  the  rest  of  the  day,  and  then  tomorrow  they  could  go  on  with  the  trip.  Tonight,  they'd  have  cocktails  be-  fore  dinner,  and  everybody  would  want  to  hear  about  his  experience,  and  for  a  while  he'd  be  a  minor-league  hero,  and  nothing  that  he  could  do  would  be  wrong.  He  thought  about  his  falling  off  the  buoy  during  the  night,  and  what  a  good  story  that  would  make,  and  he  could  hear  Alice  Martin  laugh  when  he  told  about  trying  to  make  the  bell  ring  in  rhythm  to  keep  himself  awake.  She'd  love  the  whole  story,  he  thought,  and  he  began  mentally  to  sift  out  the  details  that  would  make  the  best  telling.  Then  he  paused,  and  thought  again.  Well,  why  not?  heasked  himself.  You're  in  the  driver's  seat  now  -for  a  while,  at  any  rate-so  why  not  make  the  most  of  it?  You've  just  got  to  be  careful,  that's  all.  Just  be  very  careful.  He  put  his  coffee  down,  and  lay  back  on  the  hatch  and  fell  into  adeep,  con-  tented  sleep.
