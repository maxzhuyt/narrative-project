And  yet,  for  all  his  triumphant,  playful  gesturing  and  smiling,  Rose  noticed  there  was  an  air  of  real  majesty  about  this  little  man,  which  suddenly  silenced  the  cook's  laughter.  &quot;'And  this  is  my  husband,  my  Ko-  bus!&quot;  Rose  said  to  herself,  and  a  great  feeling  ofpride  settled  disturbingly  in-  side  her  throat.  She  and  the  cook  watched  him  in  silence  for  some  time.  &quot;Of  course,  you  know  why  he  is  carrying  on  like  that,&quot;  said  the  cook  at  last,  sounding  as  if  she  were  anxious  to  understand  both  herself  and  the  majestic  air  of  the  gardener.  &quot;You  know,  Rose,  why  he  is  fancying  himself  so.&quot;  Rose  turned  and  looked  at  her  through  the  window,  and  the  cook  pursed  her  lips  and  lifted  her  eyes  sig-  nificantly.  Then  Rose  walked  heavily  off  the  stoep  onto  the  trim  grass  and  looked  up  at  the  upstairs  windows,  softly  curved  under  one  of  the  gables.  Out  of  one  of  them  there  fluttered  a  bright-yellow  duster,  as  gay  as  the  flirtatious  notes  of  young  laughter  that  came  spilling  through  the  air  after  it.  She  caught  a  glimpse  of  Karima's  face,  and  then,  without  changing  the  position  of  her  feet,  she  pivoted  round,  wiping  small  beads  of  perspiration  off  her  up  per  lip.  Kobus  rose  in  his  machine,  looked  high  up  at  the  window,  and  removed  his  cap.  He  bowed  with  it  magnificently,  but  with  that  solemnity  with  which  an  old  king  might  bow  as  he  offered  up  his  heart to his new queen.  IT  was  still  dark  when  Rose  woke  up  the  next  morning,  feeling  depressed  and  as  much  sickened  by  her  own  weak-  ness  as  by  the  nauseous  vapor  of  stale  wine  that  filled  the  room.  The  good  Lord  only  knew,  she  thought,  what  a  fine  thing  it  would  be  if  she  could  punish  this  little  man  who  lay  beside  her,  stink  ing  so  miserably  of  cheap  wine.  Moving  very  gently,  she  leaned  across  his  shrunken  form,  slipped  the  fingers  of  her  left  hand  under  his  head,  and  lifted  it  in  her  palm,  as  carefully  as  a  mother  might  hold  the  wobbling  head  of  a  new  baby.  Then  she  was  able  to  remove  her  right  arm,  on  which  he  had  been  lying,  drunk  and  content.  She  used  both  hands  to  settle  the  little  head  back  on  the  pil-  low. She  lit  the  candle,  and  for  a  while  she  lay  in  the  flickering  light,  supporting  her  heavy  chin  on  one  hand,  her  elbow  pushing  down  the  side  of  the  bed  while  she  glowered  at  all  the  inanities  and  cruelties  of  life.  Here  she  was,  too  old  ever  to  have  a  child  of  her  own,  when  the  good  Lord  Himself  knew  how  she  had  always  longed  for  a  child,  just  like  every  other  woman. Rose  turned,  heaving  her  body,  and  lay  looking  at  Kobus.
