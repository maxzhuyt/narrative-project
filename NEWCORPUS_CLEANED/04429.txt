I  remember  imagining  the  confusion  that  might  result  if  the  people  of  my  little  South  Dakota  town  had  had  to  de-  cide  where,  in  the  prairie  that  sur-  rounded  us,  we  were  to  gather  for  the  occasion.  When  the  final  rush  of  blinding  flame  seared  the  earth,  instead  of  being  lined  up  in  dignified  ranks,  sing-  ing  hymns  of  praise,  we  would  be  found  scurrying  about  on  the  plains  like  frightened  ants.  Eventually,  of  course,  too  many  last  days  were  predicted,  and  I  lost  interest  in  them.  Then,  too,  our  newspaper  was  filled  with  such  other  fascinating  phenomena  of  the  period  as  flagpole-sit-  ting  contests,  dance  marathons,  and  the     exploits  of  John  Dillinger.  Nearly  a  whole  winter  passed,  while  I  was  in  the  fifth  grade,  without  my  giving  any  thought  at  all  to  the  possibility  of  the  world's  ending. The  time  announced  was  6  a.m.-too  late  for  a  July  sunrise. Its  very  amateur-  ishness  made  it  seem  strangely  authentic.  If  the  end  of  the  world  was  coming,  and  these  people  knew  about  it,  would  they  care  what  happened  to  unbelievers  and  scoffers?  Wasn't  it  their  job  simply  to  make  the  facts  known?  And  wouldn't  their  excitement  make  them  forget  the  more  customary  form  of  announce-  ment? The  dark,  bare  trees  and  the  gray  wooden  buildings,  with  their  sharp  cor-  ners  and  black  roofs,  were  starkly  de-  fined. I,  of  course,  did  not  forget  the  date.  As  a  matter  of  fact,  I  wrote  it  down  on  a  piece  of  adhesive  tape,  which  I  stuck  to  the  bottom  of  my  alarm  clock. This  was  the  depth  of  the  depression.  Summer  began  that  year  without  spring,  and  drought  and  dust  storms  scourged  the  land.  Swarms  of  grasshoppers,  riding  the  constant,  hot  Western  wind,  descended  upon  us  like  a  plague.  Every  housewife  whose  gar-  den  had  been  stripped  to  the  earth,  ev-  ery  man  caught  in  the  twin  coils  of  debt  and  fear  spoke  in  the  accents  of  one  forsaken  by  God.  Even  when  people  declared  that  they  could  bear  no  more-  not  another  hour  of  the  heat,  notan-  other  day  of  the  dust,  not  another  month  of  the  depression-the  sun  would  rise  again  in  the  morning  in  a  pale   sky,  the  dust  would  blow  again  out  of  the  west,.  No  house  could  be  shut  tight  enough  to  keep  out  the  fine  silt,  and  the  grasshoppers,  having  completely  de-  voured  all  the  available  grain,  were  re-  portedly  eating  fence  posts.  In  spite  of  the  drought,  the  town's  water  supply,  from  artesian  wells,  was  plentiful,  and  the  municipal  water  com-  pany  gave  free  water  to  all  residents  to  use  on  lawns  and  trees.  Every  morning,  my  father  started  the  lawn  sprinkler,  and  I  moved  it  at  half-hour  intervals  until  the  yard  had  been  covered.  In  the  evening,  he  let  the  water  run  at  the  base  of  the  trees.  My  mother  would  sit  in  a  lawn  chair  in  the  back  yard,  fanning  herself  and  slapping  mosquitoes  with  the  evening  paper.  We  stayed  outdoors  late,  because  the  wind  usually  died  when  the  sun  went  down,  and  the  house  became  airless  and  hot.
