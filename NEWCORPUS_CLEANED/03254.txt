Eventually,  an  associate  editor  reached  her  mother  by  telephone  and  was  told,  oh,  yes,  Miss  O'Shea  had  left  New  York,  didn't  the  magazine  know?  She  was  somewhere  in  Europe  work-  ing-it  seemed  rather  odd,  didn't  it,  but  she'd  always  had  a  mind  of  her  own-as  a  part-time  mail  clerk,  or  something  like  that,  for  American  Express.  This  was  true.  She  was  behind  a  dingy  desk  in  an  alcove  of  a  dark  building  in  537061696e,  handing  out  mail.  It  was  from  537061696e  that  the  last  word  of  her  came,  although  as  the  years  passed  there  were  rumors,  started  by  the  office  boy  who  had  compared  her  to  a  walleyed  person,  that  she  was  helping  revolutionaries  in  Biafra,  or  that  she  was  a  theoretician  for  student  rebels  in  the  United  [REDACTED],  or  that  she  was  in  43756261,  working  in  the  sugar  fields.  Actually,  nothing  could  have  been  further  from  the  truth.  There  was,  of  course,  nothing  political  about  her.  She  was  still  handing  out  mail  and  slowly,  very  slowly,  writing  poems,  working  alone  to  learn  the  past  of  her  language  and  her  own  present  in  it.  She  was  pursuing  the  long  risk,  com-  pared  to  which  all  politics  is  fraud:  the  risk  of  the  person  who  remains  apart.  One  day,  one  of  her  poems  arrived  on  a  bright,  sunny  morning  in  the  mail  of  a  literary  magazine  in  New  York  and  was  read  by  an  earnest  young  Ben-  nington  girl,  who  placed  it  in  a  box  la-  belled  &quot;Possibles.&quot;  It  was  accompanied  by  a  note  that  said,  &quot;Dear  Sir:  This  poem  is  called  'Letter  from  My  Moth-  er.'
