Branches  inclined  toward  the  ground  bore  more  fruit  than  those  that  soared  skyward  (though  the  fruit  hanging  higher  up  was  less  likely  to  rot). Odd  that  a  young  man  of  twenty  should  spend  whole  afternoons  in  a  foreign  country  leaning  against  a  secluded  barn,  immersed  in  a  dictionary-no,  in  a  single  page;  no,  a  single  word;  that  he  should  look  up  from  that  word,  shake  his  head,  laugh,  drum  his  heels  on  the  ground,  clap  his  hands  (scaring  off  the  grasshoppers  and  butterflies),  jump  now  and  then  to  his  feet  and  take  a  turn. Only  the  litanies-even  more  than  the  hymns-made  me  prick  up  my  ears  to  the  language.  I  would  join  with  all  my  heart  in  entreating  the  Saviour  to  have  mercy  on  us  and  the  saints  to  pray  for  us.  In  the  dark  nave,  filled  with  the  barely  recognizable  silhou-  ettes  of  the  villagers  turned  with  their  voices  toward  the  altar,  the  Slovene  syllables-those  of  the  priest  chang-  ing,  those  of  the  congregation  un-  changing-resounded  with  infinite  fervor.  It  was  as  though  we  were  all  lying  prostrate,  addressing  our  suppli-  cation  to  a  closed  Heaven.  Those  for-  eign  sequences  could  never  be  long  enough  for  me;  I  wanted  them  to  go  on  and  on,  and  when  the  litany  came  to  an  end  I  experienced  not  a  dying  away  but  a  breaking  off.  But  for  me  as  a  growing  child  it  was  only  those  litanies  and  the  thought  of  my  missing  brother,  my  hero,  that  de-  terred  me  from  regarding  the  region's  second  language-for  many  its  first-  as  a  personal  assault  on  me;  and  even  now,  toward  the  end  of  the  century,  the  local  German-speaking  majority  feel  the  same  way,  often  in  spite  of  themselves.  It  was  reading  my  brother's  old  Slovene-German  dictionary  that  first  helped  me  over  this  prejudice.  It  was  published  in  1895,  the  year  of  my  fa-  ther's  birth.  Aiming  at  completeness,  it  was  a  collection  of  words  and  phrases  from  every  part  of  Slovenia.  Just  as  the  sun  inching  over  the  dark-  ened  landscape  painting  above  the  desk  where  I  now  sit  writing  helps  me  to  pick  out  the  minutest  objects  and  figures  and  the  spaces  between  them-  the  bent  arm  of  the  girl  sitting  by  the  water,  a  bowed  tree  on  the  horizon,  a  boy  at  the  end  of  the  path  looking  toward  the  girl-so,  as  I  read  the  entries,  the  words  helped  me  to  see  the  little  details  that  had  almost  always  been  lacking  when  I  tried  to  visualize  a  childhood.  Word  by  word-my  brother  had  ticked  many  of  them,  so  I  was  able  to  skip  quite  a  lot-a  people  took  shape  before  my  eyes.  Its  members  in  their  world  were  an  exact  replica  of  our  villagers  at  home,  but  they  did  not,  as  in  the  usual  stories  and  anecdotes,  shrivel  into  types,  caricatures,  and clich\xc3\xa9s;  I  saw  only  the  glowing  forms  of  people  and  things.  The  words  be-  fore  me  sprang  from  a  rural  people,  whose  metaphors  had  their  source  in  country  life:  &quot;He  uses  his  tongue  the  way  a  cow  uses  her  tail;&quot;  &quot;You're  as  slow  as  fog  on  a  windless  day;&quot;  &quot;Your  house  is  as  cold  as  a  burned-out  barn.&quot;  But  cities  held  no  terrors  for  them,  stood  waiting  to  be  conquered.  The  countryfolk  would  &quot;rattle&quot;  to  town  in  the  wagon  or  &quot;glide&quot;  there  in  the  sleigh.  The  vocabulary  of  profanity  was  rich  and  varied;  &quot;he  swore  his  last&quot;  was  a  way  of  saying  &quot;he  died.&quot;  These  people  had  any  number  of  terms  for  dying,  but  even  more  for  the  fe-  male  sex  organ.  From  one  valley  to  the  next  the  names  changed  for  vari-  eties  of  apple  and  pear,  and  they  were  as  numerous  as  the  stars  in  the  sky  (which  were  named  after  farm  imple-  ments,  called  &quot;reapers&quot;  or  &quot;mowers,&quot;  or  simply,  like  the  Pleiades,  the  &quot;Densely  Sowed  Ones&quot;).  The  Slovenes  had  never  set  up  a  government  of  their  own,  so  they  had  to  resort  to  literal  translations  from  the  German  or  Latin  of  their  over-  lords  for  everything  connected  with  politics,  public  life,  or,  for  that  matter,  conceptual  thought-the  terms  seemed  as  stilted  as  saying  &quot;far-writer&quot;  for  telegraph;  on  the  other  hand,  the  lan-  guage  had  familiar  names,  nicknames  as  it  were,  for  all  commonplace  ob-  jects,  and  not  just  the  useful  ones.  Everything  indoors  seemed  to  have  been  named  by  women,  and  everything  outdoors  by  men.  A  kind  of  bread  baked  under  hot  ashes  was  literally  called  &quot;underash,&quot;  and  a  variety  of  pear  &quot;the  little  woman.&quot;  It  is  typical  of  this  language  that  the  addition  of  a  mere  syllable,  and  not  of  another  word,  can  transform  terms  for  large  areas  into  diminutives,  which  serve  as  names  for  the  things  and  creatures  in  these  areas.  The  area  thus  becomes  a  refuge  and  hiding  place  for  the  crea-  tures  that  bear  its  name.  A  wood,  for  example,  harbored  &quot;woodsies,&quot;  a  term  that  might  designate  a  human  inhabi-  tant  of  the  wood,  or  a  wood  rush,  or  a  particular  species  of  forest  flower,  a  wild  cherry  tree,  a  wild  apple  tree,  a  wood  nymph,  and-the  forest's  heart,  as  it  were-the  coal  titmouse. I  HAD  had  a  teacher  in  high  school  who  wrote  fairy  tales. My  teacher  found  fault  with  the  stories  I  was  writing  at  the  time;  he  said  that  I  had  a  weakness  for  the  macabre,  that  I  was  positively  addicted  to  the  gloomy  and  gruesome,  whereas  the  law  of  writing  was  to  create-letter  after  letter,  sylla-  ble  after  syllable-the  brightest  of  brightnesses;  even  one's  last  breath,  he  said,  must  be  transformed  into  the  breath  of  life.  Now,  though,  immersed  in  the  dictionary's  &quot;rain  of  blood,&quot;  &quot;rat  turds,&quot;  &quot;spittle  of  disgust,&quot;  &quot;the  earthworm's  fecal  sausage,&quot;  &quot;shoes  moldering  in  a  corner,&quot;  a  beast  named  &quot;&quot;understone&quot;&quot;  (a  viper),  a  place  called  &quot;land  of  moles&quot;  (the  grave),  I  felt  free  of  my  addiction  to  the  gruesome  or  even  to  the  tragic,  and  found  in  the  contemplation  of  names  a  pattern  in  the  world,  a  schema,  that  transformed  country  people  and  a  village  house  into  world  people  and  a  city  house.  Every  word-circle  a  world-circle!  The  crux  of  the  matter  was  that  every  circle  emanated  from  a  single  foreign  word.  When  people  felt  unable  to  communi-  cate  an  experience,  didn't  they  always  wail,  &quot;Oh,  if  there  were  only  a  word  for  it!&quot;  And  when  they  recognized  an  expression  as  true,  weren't  they  much  less  likely  to  say,  &quot;Yes,  that's  it!&quot;  than  &quot;That's  the  word!&quot;  But  wasn't  I  taking  the  side  of  a  foreign  language  against  my  own!? To  eliminate  someone  like  my  brother-who,  unlike  the  great  mass  of  those  who  speak  and  write,  had  the  gift  of  bringing  words  and,  through  them,  things  to  life,  who  never  ceased  to  exercise  that  gift  and  to  point  out  examples  as  he  was  doing  now  to  me-was  to  kill  language  it-  self,  the  living  tradition,  the  tradition  of  peace;  it  was  the  most  unforgivable  of  crimes,  the  most  barbarous  of  world  wars. But  I  knew  I  could  bring  the  picture  writing  back-that,  unlike  grief,  it  couldbe  willed.  I  shut  my  eyes.  Only  then  did  I  notice  that  they  were  wet.  But  I  was  not  weeping  for  myself  or  my  family;  no,  the  source  of  those  tears  was things  and  their  words.  For  without  the  refuge  of  words,  the  earth-the  black,  red,  greening  earth-would  be  nothing  but  one  great  desert,  and  I  will  no  longer  acknowledge  any  drama,  any  history  other  than  the  drama  of  the  things  and  words  of  this  beloved  world.  I  shall  find  a  word  for  the  dark  interior  of  a  white  chestnut  blossom,  the  yellow  of  clay  under  the  wet  snow,  the  bit  of  blossom  that  clings  to  the  apple,  and  the  sound  of  a  river  fish  leaping  out  of  the  water. I  had  convinced  myself  that  I  was  unable  to  sing,  and  then  one  day  the  teacher  called  on  me  to  sing.  With  my  heart  in  my  mouth  I  stood  up  and  took  a  deep  breath.
