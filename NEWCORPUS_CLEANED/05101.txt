Then  the  hurry-up  boat  came  foaming  through  the  water,  the  hoggee  whaling  his  tandem  with  an  ox  quirt  borrowed  for  the  occasion.  The  boat  had  been  pried  off  the  mudbank  and  was  making  twelve  miles  an  hour.  Grandfather  ran  down  to  meet  the  su-  perintendent,  who  made  a  flying  leap  from  the  prow  and  stared  in  dismay  at  the  cataract  roaring  through  the  breach.  &quot;Good  Lord  of  mercies!&quot;  he  shouted  in  Grandfather's  ear.  &quot;Every  keel  be-  tween  here  and  Montezuma  Marsh  will  be  mudlarked  by  sunup!  If  we  only  had  timber!&quot;  &quot;'Timber's  coming!&quot;  Grandfather  yelled  back,  and  hoped  that  he  was  right.  As  it  happened,  Erie  Water  delivered  at  that  immediate  moment  two  fine  tree  boles-a  hemlock  and  a  sycamore.  Recovering  quickly  from  a  moment  of  complete  amazement,  the  superin-  tendent  yelled,  &quot;Poles!&quot;  His  crew  swarmed  over  the  gunwales  of  the  boat  and  ran  to  him.  Grand-  father's  worries  were  lifted  from  his  overburdened  shaulders  as  the  experts  caught  up  poles  and  prodded  the  logs  into  place.  He  watched  another  great  trunk  come  speeding  down,  then  two  more.  The  woodsmen  were  doing  a  job.  Grandfather  got  a  pole  for  himself  and  fell  to  work.
