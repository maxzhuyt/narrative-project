Mostly,  I  said  noth-  ing  and  worried  about  whether  all  the  people  who  were  staying  in  my  house  were  comfortable.  Later,  after  every-  one  left  and  I  was  alone,  I  thought  about  Margaret's  death  a  great  deal.  My  mind  had  composed  a  sort  of  pho-  tograph  album-still  pictures  of  the  brief  months  of  her  final  illness  and  the  funeral.  Also,  my  dreams  turned  bizarre  and  troubling,  though  Margaret  never  appeared  in  them  as  a  character.  If  Matt  were  to  die  before  me,  I  don't  think  I  could  bear  it.  I  feel  such  a  curiosity  about  his  life.  It's  this  sus-  picion,  this  intuition  of  mine,  that  his  life  is  moving  toward  a  kind  of  fulfill-  ment  that  has  been  denied  me  and  my  other  sons.  We  have  known  content-  ment,  even  happiness,  I  suppose.  Matt  has  never  been  happy,  yet  I  sense-  and  the  dock  is  the  sole  basis  of  my  belief-he  may  prove  to  be  the  most  fortunate  ofus  all.  I  could  not  bear  to  see  Matt  die.  It  would  mean  that,  for  all  his  trouble,  he  had  not  known  hard-  ship  sufficient  to  polish  away  the  thick  husk  and  release  the  damp,  green  shoot.  HAVE  been  thinking,  this  fall,  of  insulating  the  cottage  and  staying  up  here  all  winter.  There  is  no  need  for  me  to  return  to  the  city.  Paul  runs  the  business  as  well  when  I'mup  here  as  when  I'm  in  [REDACTED],  and  it  would  be  little  trouble  to  winterize  the  cottage,  though  of  course  I'm  a  bit  late  in  considering  it.  The  tedious  part  would  be  taking  down  the  panelling.  The  sheets  of  pine  we  have  through-  out  the  cottage  are  much  nicer  than  anything  that's  currently  available,  Panel  by  panel,  I  would  need  to  pull  out  the  nails  and  strip  the  walls  down  to  the  framing.  Then  it  would  be  a  simple  matter  of  putting  in  the  insula-  tion,  in  both  the  walls  and  the  attic  crawl  space.  I  have  amused  myself  with  the  thought  that  Matthew  might  come  up  weekends  and  during  the  school  va-  cations  and  help.  In  fact,  I  have  proposed  as  much  in  a  letter.  I  say  &quot;amused  myself&quot;  because  it's  not  Matt's  kind  of  work.  Nothing  new  is  being  created,  he  lacks  the  necessary  patience,  and  the  work  is  not  physical  enough  to  fatigue  him  into  re-  laxation.  He  mightendup  throwing  things.  With  the  house  properly  insulated,  I  think  I  could  make  it  through  the  winter.  When  it  got  extremely  cold,  I  could  live  in  the  living  room  and  kitch-  en  only.  There  is  a  large  fireplace,  and  I  have  a  couple  of  kerosene  stoves,  too.  Of  course,  if  things  became  completely  desperate  I  could  get  into  the  station  wagon  and  drive  to  a  motel-or,  for  that  matter,  [REDACTED].  Certainly  there  is  no  lack  of  worth-  while  work  I  could  do  up  here.  I  have  several  designs  I  want  to  do-designs  for  structures  I  know  will  never  be  built.  That  is  their  value  to  me,  that  they  will  remain  forever  lines  on  paper.  All  my  life,  my  buildings  have  solved  problems.  I  flatter  myself  in  thinking  that  they  have  done  so  with  efficiency.  However,  there  has  been  nothing  fan-  ciful  in  my  work.  Now,  approaching  sixty,  I  would  like  to  draw  some  places  that  would  not  suffer  construction.  I  would  like  to  play  a  bit.  There  is  reading  and  writing,  also.  But  the  truth  is,  I  know  I  won't  get  to  either.  I  will  do  the  drawings-at  least  a  couple  of  them-but  that  is  all.  I  have  known  of  others  who  have  re-  tired  in  just  the  way  I  am  planning,  andno  doubt  I  will  follow  their  model.  I  will  select  some  clearly  arbitrary  task  to  devotemyself  to  and  then  squander  vast  amounts  of  time  on  it.  I  will  write  long  letters  to  Matthew,  letters  that  will  go  unanswered.  I  will  make  elabo-  rate  plans  for  periodic  meetings  be-  tween  the  two  of  us-plans  whose  cleverness  will  be  evident  in  Mat-  thew's  inability  to  escape  them.  When  the  snow  comes,  I  will  give  myself  to  clearing  and  maintaining  a  faultless  path  between  the  back  door  and  the  dock.  In  addition,  I  will  buy  a  great  metal  pike  and  keep  the  water  around  the  dock  free  of  ice.  T  has  been  my  habit,  this  summer,  to  spend  the  late  afternoons  and  early  evenings  down  at  the  dock.  I  sit  in  a  lawn  chair,  have  a  cocktail,  eat  an  early  supper,  and  observe  the  lake  in  the  last  hours  of  daylight.  'The  sun  is  behind  me  at  that  time,  and  the  east,  ern  shore  of  the  lake,  opposite  the  dock,  is  incised  by  the  slanting  rays.  How  much  more  clearly  detailed  the  far  shore  looks  in  the  fading  light  than  in  the  foggy  morning!  My  thoughts  dwell  on  those  singu-  lar  birches  across  the  lake.  In  winter,  when  the  snows  are  covering  the  dry  husks  of  the  fallen  leaves,  is  there  something  in  the  young  trees'  heart-  wood  that  remembers  their  summer  green?  And  is  the  function  of  this  memory,  in  part,  to  generate  a  new  spring?  And  what  of  the  old,  fallen  trees-the  trees  decayed  by  microbes  and  insects  in  the  hot  months?
