Now  we  are  in  a  place  where  there  are  street  lights  on  the  corners,  and  they  are  more  brave  for  this  light,  saying:  &quot;We  have  slaughtered  the  Tsotsis  And  turned  them  to  jackals,  They  howl  in  the  night  To  show  how  we  beat  them,  We  who  came  here  by  lorry  From  a  far  hilly  country,  We  slaughtered  them,  beat  them  And  turned  them  to  jackals  ...&quot;  And  again  I  am  saying  to  them,  &quot;Perhaps  it  would  be  much  better  if  you  are  keeping  more  quiet  about  this.&quot;  So  the  first  one  is  saying  to  me,  &quot;What  is  it  that  is  making  you  so  frightened,  my  friend?  We  will  stay  together  and  look  after  you.&quot;  But  I  tell  them,  &quot;In  this  place  you  cannot  always  stay  together,  unless  you  arebe-  coming  Tsotsis.  Only  the  bad  people  who  are  not  working,  with  no  wife  and  children,  can  stay  together.  You  will  learn  that.&quot;  But  they  are  laughing  again  and  saying,  &quot;Our  peo-  ple  are  far  away.  Our  fathers  and  mothers  and  wives  and  children,  we  have  left  them  behind  to  make  money  here.&quot;  I  am  thinking  it  is  no  use  to  speak  to  these  people.  You  cannot  tell  a  man  those  things  he  must  learn  himself.  And  these  red  people  are  very  ig-  norant,  and  they  cannot  even  read  a  notice  to  tell  them  where  the  white  people  must  go  and  the  black  people  must  go.  And  I  am  thinking,  too,  how  the  other  people  of  the  township  will  laugh  to  see  me  with  the  Pondo  people  always  walking  after  me  like  chick-  ens.  So  I  am  taking  them  to  that  place  where  they  can  sleep,  and  I  am  giving  them  some  money.  They  are  still  talking  loud  about  what  they  were  doing  to  those  Tsotsis.  They  are  shouting  to  me,  &quot;Good  night,  friend!  We  will  meet  in  the  morning.&quot;  UT  I  am  gone  home  to  my  wife,  very  fast  to  my  wife  and  children,  and  I  am  telling  her,  &quot;Tomorrow  first  thing  I  take  out  my  Building  Society  savings  and  we  go  back  to  the  Tran-  ske.&quot;  My  wife  is  crying,  saying  to  me,  &quot;What  about  our  children  going  to  school,  our  furniture,  where  shall  we  get  food?&quot;  But  I  am  saying,  &quot;Better  we  starve  a  little  than  we  die  altogether.&quot;  For  you  see,  sir,  there  are  some  things  a  man  is  doing  because  he  must  do  them-sometimes  for  himself,  and  sometimes  for  his  wife  and  children.  That  is  all  a  man  is  living  for.  But  those  Tsotsis  are  riding  every-  where  in  the  land,  and  perhaps  one  day  we  are  meeting  again."
