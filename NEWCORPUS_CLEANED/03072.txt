From  their  chairs,  these  members  must  be  able  to  observe  the  drives,  see  the  ball  sail  into  the  sky,  curve  into  what  was  once  a  meadow,  bound  along  the  low  ground.  This  morning,  it  does  not  appear  to  be  a  golf  course,  because  the  snow  has  fallen  all  night  long,  and  the  golf-course  features-sand  traps,  hazards,  bunkers,  greens-have  all  been  buried.  It  is  white,  not  green  or  brown,  and  it  is  no  longer  empty,  as  it  was  in  the  cold  months  when  the  carth  was  indistinguishable  from  rock  to  the  feet.  Now  the  hill  is  speckled  with  children  pelting  down  the  slope  on  sleds,  shrieking  alarm  and  delight,  and  dogs  yelp.  At  the  top,  adults  stand  on  shifting  feet,  gazing  out  acrossthe  rooftops  and  church  spires  of  the  villages,  over  the  highway  and  the  railroad  tracks,  over  the  tawny  marsh  to  the  gray-blue  river  and  the  paler  hills  beyond. It  is  a  long  way.  Perhaps  it  hurts-  the  tumble  off  the  sled-or  frightens:  the  sudden  lunge,  the  wind  knocked  out,  the  cold  snow  down  the  neck,  the  icy  feet,  the  long  climb  back,  the  tears  that  freeze  on  the  cheek.  But  they  stand  at  thetop  of  the  hill  near  where  their  cars  are  parked,  or  sit  in  their  cars  with  the  heaters  on,  and  they  look  out  over  the  abyss,  past  the  bright  vanishing  riders,  across  the  village  and  the  river  and  the  hills,  and  they  think  perhaps  of  spring-a  spring  gone  by,  mostlikely-and  a  green  land  with  well-clipped  grass  and  numbered  flags  that  indicate  one's  des-  tination.
