 THE  FAIR  THAT  VYAS  HE  newspapers  these  days  are  full  of  breathless  reports  about  the  final,  scrambling  preparations  for  the  opening  of  our  World's  Fair  this  month,  and  I  have  been  waiting  patient-  ly  for  my  first  anticipatory  tingle  of  ex-  pectation  and  interest.  So  far,  however,  all  those  press  releases  and  feature  stories  about  the  doings  at  Flushing  Meadow  have  had  a  curious  reverse  effect  on  me.  A  mental  Time  Capsule,  long  buried  in  the  loose  sand  of  my  unconscious,  has  suddenly  popped  open,  and  I  keep  being  reminded  about  the  lest  New  York  World's  Fair.  I  was  a  press  agent  em-  ployed  by  the  celebrated  1939-40  ex-  position,  and  now,  after  twenty-five  years,  I  keep  getting  the  unsettling  feel-  ing  that  I  am  back  in  yesterday's  World  of  Tomorrow.  I  remember  reporting  for  work  one  crisp  spring  morning  just  a  few  weeks  before  the  official  opening  day.  The  fair-  grounds  were  a  muddy  bedlam  of  hasty  hammering,  sawing,  and  riveting,  and  I  had  the  impression  that  an  ill-organ-  ized  army  of  irregulars  was  preparing  last-minute  defenses  of  a  city  against  an  approaching  band  of  Visigoths.  Riggers  suspended  in  bosun's  chairs  were  finish-  ing  up  the  Trylon  and  Perisphere,  the  seven-hundred-foot  spire  and  two-hun-  dred-foot  globe  that  comprised  the  Fair  symbol.  Other  workmen  were  rushing  to  complete  two  hundred-odd  exhibition  structures  and  pavilions-a  wild  archi-  tectural  paella  that  included  the  Gen-  eral  Motors  Futurama;  a  two-hundred-  and-fifty-foot  Parachute  Jump,  with  captive  parachutes;  Salvador  Dali's  &quot;Dream  of  Venus&quot;  building,  which  would  feature  some  delightfully  under-  dressed  female  swimmers;  an  electrified  Farm  of  Tomorrow;  a  sample  incubator  hospital  for  premature  babies;  the  Rus-  sian  Pavilion,  with  its  seventy-nine-foot  stainless-steel  statue  of  a  Russian  worker  holding  aloft  a  huge  red  star  (he  became  known  to  Fair  employees  as  Ivan  Macy  );  a  replica  of  a  Shinto  temple;  and  a  factory  for  manufacturing  Fiberglas,  which  made  its  first  public  appearance  at  the  Fair,  as  did  nylon,  fluorescent  lighting,  rubber-base  paint,  and  Henri  Soulé,  of  the  French  Pavilion.  I  was  picking  my  way  gingerly  through  this  unkempt  landscape  one  morning  when  a  sudden  shout  made  me  jump  aside,  and  none  too  soon,  either.  'There  was  a  clat-  ter  ofhoofs,  and  a  troop  of  ten  coppery-  visaged  horsemen  swept  by  me;  they  wore  blue-and-orange  uniforms  and  were  carrying  lances  topped  with  flut-  tering  pennons.  I  blinked  my  eyes,  but  the  horsemen  were  real  enough,  as  was      the  long  black  limousine  following  them.  The  limousine  was  carrying  a  single  passenger,  a  portly,  mustached  gentle-  man  wearing  a  blue  shirt,  a  black  hom-  burg,  and  ablue  carnation  in  his  but-  tonhole,  whom  I  lateridentified  as  Grover  A.  Whalen,  the  president  ofthe  Fair  and  the  Kubla  Khan  who  had  de-  creed  these  various  pleasure  domes.  The  horsemen  were  a  band  of  Pawnee  In-  dians  who  had  just  been  hired  as  Mr.  Whalen's  honor  guard.  Just  what  part  Indians  and  horses  were  meant  to  play  in  the  World  of  Tomorrow  remained  a  mystery  to  me,  although  the  Fair's  publicity  releases  valiantly  explained  that  they  symbolized  &quot;America's  link  to  the  heritage  of  the  past.&quot;  Personally,  I  always  believedthat  Mr.  Whalen,  who  was,  like  all  great  showmen,  a  boy  at  heart,  just  liked  having  Indians  and  horses  around.  At  any  rate,  the  Indian  guard  became  a  familiar  sight  that  spring  as  it  escorted  Mr.  Whalen  on  his  ceremonial  rounds  of  ribbon-snipping  and  button-pushing,  and  I  always  got  a  smile  when  I  saw  them  cantering  down  Constitution  Mall,  the  Fair's  main  drag,  followed  by  the  excited  ex-  clamations  and  questions  of  children  and  the  puzzled  shrugs  ofparents.  My  role  at  the  Fair  was  a  modest  one,  but  I  might  have  cut  a  much  bigger  swath  had  I  been  able  to  carry  out  the  plans  that  a  friend  of  mine  and  I  had  for  a  concession  in  the  amusement  area.  He  was  one  of  my  press  colleagues,  a  lively  chap  improbably  named  Wambly  Bald,  and  it  was  actually  his  idea.  He  had  been  hearing  about  the  fantastic  sums  that  Billy  Rose's  Aquacade  had  been  earning  in  the  first  weeks  of  the  Fair  (Mr.  Rose  wound  up  with  two  million  dollars  in  his  pocket,  which  made  him  one  of  the  few  winners  in  our  midst),  and  he  approached  me  with  the  intention  of  getting  us  on  the  gravy  train.  Wambly's  proposal  was  to  put  up  a  &quot;Screamatorium,&quot;  a  soundproof  brick  building  where  people,  upon  paying  a  small  admission  charge,  could  sit  around  and  scream.  &quot;I'm  telling  you,  Zachary,  this  idea  is  a  gold  mine,&quot;  Wambly  told  me.  &quot;'Think  of  all  the  millions  of  visitors  here-hot,  tired,  waiting  in  line  for  hours  to  see  the  Futurama,  and  all.  Hell,  they're  developing  all  éinds  of  anti-social  pressures  and  frustrations!  They  need  a  quick  release,  a  sudden  pick-me-up,  so  that  they  can  really  en-  joy  themselves.  Our  Screamatorium  would  be  the  ideal  place  for  them.  It's  psychologically  sound,  and,  besides,  it  won't  cost  much  to  operate.&quot;  I  agreed  with  Wambly  that  he  really  had  something,  and  I  made  the  suggestion  that  inasmuch  as  we  were  nov-  ices  in  the  entertainment  business,  it  might  be  to  ouradvantage  to  associate  ourselves  with  a  good  front  man.  Wambly  beamed.  &quot;Think  no  more  of  it,  my  boy.  I  have  already  written  to  Sig.&quot;  I  was  puzzled.  &quot;Sig?  Who's  he?&quot;  &quot;Why,  Sigmund  Freud,  of  course,&quot;  Wambly  replied.  &quot;The  father  of  psy-  choanalysis.  I  promised  him  a  cut  of  the  take  for  the  useof  his  name.  Think  of  it-'Sigmund  Freud's  Screamatori-  um'!&quot;  I  shrugged  philosophically.  If  Dali  was  willing  to  make  a  fast  buck,  why  not  Freud?  Wambly  and  I  sat  down  at  once  and  drew  up  our  plans  for  the  Screamatori-  um.  As  a  refinement,  we  decided  toin-  stall  decibel  meters  that  would  single  out  a  few  truly  manic  screamers  and  en-  title  them  to  free  screaming.  In  due  course,  we  made  our  presentation  to  the  Fair's  Board  of  Design,  a  magisterial  body  that  had  to  pass  on  the  ethics  and  aesthetics  of  all  exhibits  and  concessions.  We  got  our  answer  a  couple  of  days  later.  It  was  a  polite  but  firm  turndown,  on  the  ground  that  the  Screamatorium  was  &quot;not  consistent  with  the  forward-  looking  theme  of  the  Fair,  which  is  to  celebrate  the  interdependence  of  man  in  the  World  of  Tomorrow.&quot;  Evidently  they  weren't  the  only  ones  to  disap-  prove  of  our  vision,  for  we  never  did  hear  from  Sig.  HE  Fair,  though,  must  have  rec-  ognized  my  latent  ability  as  a  showman,  because  in  its  second  year  I  was  assigned  to  help  publicize  &quot;Ameri-  can  Jubilee,&quot;  a  musical  extravaganza  that  Arthur  Schwartz,  the  composer,  and  Oscar  Hammerstein  II,  the  libret-  tist,  had  been  commissioned  to  write  and  produce.  My  first  task  in  this  post  was  to  come  up  with  a  new  name  for  this  show;  the  Fair's  executives  felt  that  &quot;American  Jubilee&quot;  lacked  come-on.  One  dav,  while  the  show  was  in  re-  hearsal,  I  met  with  the  Messrs.  Schwartz  and  Hammerstein  to  discuss  the  prob-  lem.  Mr.  Schwartz,  a  handsome,  debo-  nair  fellow  with  a  triple-A  A.S.C.A.P.  rating,  was  nattily  turned  out  in  a  beau-  tiful  double-breasted  blue  suit  and  hand-  made  shoes.  Mr.  Hammerstein  arrived  in  a  pair  of  old  dungarees  and  carpet  slippers,  wearing  the  dogged  look  of  a  man  whose  last  nine  offerings  onBroad-  way  had  flopped-as  in  fact  they  had.  After  wrestling  with  titles  most  of  the  afternoon,  we  decided  that  &quot;Yankee  Jubilee&quot;  had  the  patriotic  echoes  needed  to  lure  long  queues  of  money-bearing        tourists.  I  reported  back  proudly  to  my  immediate  superior,  an  irascible  and  pro-  fane  Irishman  named  Casey,  who  glared  at  me  with  naked  scorn  when  I  told  him  the  show's  new  title.  &quot;You  geniuses  are  going  to  wreck  this  show  before  it's  even  opened!&quot;  he  shouted.  &quot;Don't  you  know  there  are  a  hell  of  a  lot  of  people  from  the  [REDACTED]  visiting  the  Fair?  Do  you  think  any  self-respecting  bastard  from  below  the  Mason-Dixon  Line  is  going  to  plunk  down  his  hard-earned  dough  to  see  a  show  that  sounds  like  it  was  glorifying  General  Grant  or  some  other  damn  Yankee?  The  hell  with  it-we'll  use  the  old  title.&quot;  As  things  turned  out,  very  few  Americans,  from  either  the  North  or  the  [REDACTED],  came  to  see  the  show,  and  my  first  venture  in  the  world  of  the  theatre  turned  out  to  be  Oscar  Hammerstein's  tenth  consecutive  turkey.  (Happily  for  Mr.  Hammerstein,  his  eleventh  effort  was  &quot;Oklahoma!&quot;  )  The  Fair  never  did  make  a  go  of  it  financially,  despite  a  colossal  attend-  ance-some  forty-five  million  visitors  paid  to  see  it.  In  theatrical  parlance,  the  nut  was  just  too  big.  In  fact,  it  was  later  discovered  that  an  attendance  of  ninety  million  would  have  been  needed  to  make  the  Fair  break  even.  At  the  final  reckoning,  World's  Fair  bond-  holders  got  back  only  thirty-two  cents  on  the  dollar,  and  I  thought  that  that  was  a  good  deal  more  than  they  had  a  right  to  expect.  It  was  apparent  on  the  very  first  day,  back  in  that  long-ago  April  of  1939,  that  the  Fair  was  going  to  run  into  heavy  financial  weather.  It  had  been  arranged  that  the  Fair's  at-  tendance  figures  would  be  tabulated  and  proudly  displayed  every  hour  on  a  forty-  foot  cash  register,  which  stood  in  the  middle  of  the  grounds  and  served  as  part  of  the  exhibit  of  the  National  Cash  Register  Company.  Officials  confidently  expected  a  million  visitors  on  the  Fair's  opening  day,  but  by  nightfall  the  two-  and-a-half  foot  numerals  on  the  cash  register  showed  fewer  than  two  hun-  dred  thousand.  &quot;I  think  they  better  ring  up  the  'No  Sale'  sign&quot;  was  my  friend  Wambly's  remark.  The  consternation  that  set  in  at  once  among  the  Fair's  management  was  al-  most  palpable,  and  from  that  moment  on  the  executives  were  under  constant  pressure  from  bondholders  anxious  to  save  their  investment.  As  the  summer  wore  on,  the  Fair  had  to  tighten  its  belt,  and  hundreds  of  employees  were  fired.  Among  the  first  to  go  were  Mr.  Whalen's  Pawnees.  I  was  not  present   when  they  got  their  dismissal  notices,  but  a  friend  of  mine  who  was  there  told  me  that  they  emerged  from  the  Admin-  istration  Building  with  their  faces  as  im-  passive  as  ever,  leaped  onto  the  backs  of  theirhorses,  and  then  turned  their  lances  upside  down  and  trotted  back  to  the  Fair's  stables  for  the  last  time,  dragging  their  pennons  behind  them  in  the  dust,  like  warriors  defeated  in  battle.  I  managed  to  stay  on  the  Fair's  pay-  roll  until  the  official  closing,  in  the  fall  of  1940,  when  I  joined  a  mail-order  company  that  sold  salami  to  Jewish  boys  who  had  been  drafted  into  the  Army.  I  made  a  bundle.
