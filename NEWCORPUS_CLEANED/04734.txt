The  Thais,  he  said,  have  been  flying  kites  for  at  least  seven  hundred  years. Sometimes  there  are  elimination  contests,  first  between  teams  within  a  village,  then  between  villages,  until  the  provincial  champions  assemble  in  [REDACTED].  Kites  are  of  two  kinds:  the  chula,  or  male,  kite  and  the  pakpao,  or  female,  kite.  The  chule  is  star-shaped,  seven  or  eight  feet  high  and  perhaps  five  feet  wide,  fast,  powerful,  but  not  as  agile  or  as  graceful  as  the  smaller,  diamond-  shaped  pakpao,  which  measures  about  five  feet  by  three.  The  chule  has  as  its  weapon  several  six-inch  hooks,  fastened  to  the  string  just  below  the  kite  itself;                     " b"in  the  air  the  chule  makes  a  running  pass  at  the  string  of  the  pakpao,  to  hook  it  and  bring  it  down.  Defensively,  the  pakpao  is  more  maneuverable;  it  can  climb  faster  and  turn  more  tightly,  and  as  a  weapon  it  has,  just  under  the  kite,  two  loops  of  twine,  four  or  five  feet  in  circumference,  which  the  pakpao  kite-  men  try  to  cast,  lariatlike,  over  the  points  of  the  star-shaped  chule.  Both  kites  are  made  simply,  of  rice  paper  and  bamboo.  A  kite  team  usually  has  at  least  seven  members.  'The  chief  kiteman,  or  cap-  tain  of  the  team,  holds  the  kitestring  at  a  point  nearest  the  kite  and  directs  or  executes  the  maneuvering.  Some-  times,  with  his  teammates  ranged  be-  hind  him,  he  may  stand  in  one  spot  and  let  the  line  play  out  through  his  fingers,  as  when  the  kite  is  being  carried  up-  ward  by  the  wind.  At  other  times,  he  may  keep  a  tight  hold  on  the  line  and,  by  a  series  of  intricate  wiggles,  make  the  kite  climb,  turn,  slide,  or  dip.  Or,  again  holding  fast  to  the  string,  he  may  run  with  it  in  any  direction.  At  such  times,  the  other  members  of  the  team,  some  of  them  usually  boys,  will  pick  up  the  slack  of  the  line  and  run  after  him,  carrying  the  slack  over  their  shoulders.  At  still  other  times,  the  captain  may  remain  stationary  while  the  rest  of  the  team  runs  with  the  string;  at  these  times  the  captain,  letting  the  string  slip  through  his  fingers,  acts  as  a  pivot.  His  teammates  may  run  to  therear  or  to  the  right  or  left  of  him,  depending  upon  the  exact  maneuver  he  wishes  the  kite  to  make.  Maneuvering  the  kite  is  a  complex  operation,  and  a  champion  kiteman  exhibits  great  skill,  particularly  when  his  kite  is  closing  in  on,  or  being  attacked  by,  another  kite.
