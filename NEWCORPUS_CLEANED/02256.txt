 b" IN  1944,  someone  in  [REDACTED]   ordered  Albert  Gering's  father-a  doctor  and  native-born  American  citizen-to  leave  his  home  in  a  pros-  perous  farming  town  in  central  [REDACTED]  and  to  move  with  his  wife  and  two  children  to  the  Trinity  Alps,  a  moun-  tainous,  remote  part  of  Northern  Cali-  fornia.  Dr.  Gering  was  a  Mennonite,  and  had  informed  his  local  draft  board  that  he  could  not  serve  in  the  Medical  Corps  or  any  other  branch  of  the  military.  He  was  willing,  how-  ever,  to  do  any  appropriate  civilian  work,  and  he  was  eventually  assigned  to  be  a  doctor  at  a  Civilian  Public  Service  unit-in  this  case,  ironically,  a  work  camp  for  uncooperative  con-  scientious  objectors.  Dr.  Gering  took  his  wife  and  son  and  infant  daughter  along  with  him.  His  son,  Albert,  was  only  seven  years  old,  and  thus  did  not  understand  that  he  was  to  become  one  of  the  few  neutral  observers  at  what  amounted  to  an  American  concentra-  tion  camp.  All  he  understood  was  that  he  was  turning  into  a  worrywart,  as  his  mother  put  it.  The  camp  consisted  of  four  standard  barracks,  constructed  by  the  Civilian  Conservation  Corps  in  the  nineteen-  thirties,  and  placed  ina  row  in  a  soggy  meadow  a  few  miles  outside  the  small  town  of  Miners-  ville.  The  internees  worked,  or  were  sup-  posed  to  work,  on  proj-  ects  assigned  by  the  United  States  Forest  Service.  There  had  been  about  a  hundred  and  fifty  C.P.S.  camps  set  up  during  the  Second  World  War  to  house  conscientious  objectors,  but  the  Minersville  camp  was  reserved  for  the  most  intransigent  objectors-those  who  refused  on  principle  to  cooperate  in  any  way  with  Selective  Service.  At  the  other  camps  the  residents-  Mennonites  or  Quakers,  for  the  most  part-worked  willingly  and  did  not  see  them-  selves  as  forcibly  in-  carcerated.  But  at  Mi-  nersville  many  of  the  men  were  too  principled  even  to  lift  a  shovel  or Out  of  erder  wield  an  axe  as  long  as  the  govern-  ment  laid  claim  to  their  labor.  There  were  guards  at  the  camp  who  wore  sidearms  and  even  brandished  them  occasionally,  when  it  seemed  necessary  to  enforce  some  semblance  of  order  and  discipline  over  the  bored  and  dis-  putatious  men  in  their  charge.  Al-  though  the  inmates  had  not  been  offi-  cially  sentenced  by  any  judge  or  jury,  they  were  for  all  practical  purposes  prisoners  of  the  federal  government.  The  guards,  who  never  lasted  long,  were  unemployed  loggers  and  sawmill  operators  from  the  town.  Minersville  had  once  enjoyed  a  modest  boom  in  gold  mining,  but  by  the  time  Albert  and  his  family  arrived  the  mining  machinery  had  been  hauled  away  or  left  to  rust.  Huge  denuded  mounds  of  rock  and  gravel  almost  filled  the  nar-  row  river  that  trickled  through  the  middle  of  town.  A  few  old  men  still  hung  around  the  main  street,  mostly  loitering  in  front  of  a  decrepit  wooden  hotel  that  had  an  iron  staircase  spiral-  ling  up  from  the  sidewalk  to  a  second-  story  balcony,  but  the  gold  was  gone.  The  new  local  industry  was  logging,  and  young  men  drove  lumber  trucks  at  breakneck  speed  right  through  the   center  of  town,  raising  clouds  of  dust  around  the  benches  where  the  old-  timers  waited.  Later,  well  after  the  war  was  over,  a  water-reclamation  project  completed  the  destruction  of  the  town,  and  today  Minersville,  the  C.P.S.  camp  out  in  the  woods,  and  the  entire  mountain  valley  lie  at  the  bot-  tom  of  Clair  Engle  Lake,  a  reservoir  that  forms  part  of  [REDACTED]'s  exten-  sive  water-distribution  system.  The  spoon  in  this  story  is  probably  down  there,  too.  The  guards  at  the  camp  went  back  to  their  homes  in  Minersville  at  night,  but,  oddly,  none  of  the  inmates  ever  tried  to  escape.  Albert's  father  also  came  home  every  evening,  to  a  run-  down  log  cabin  built  in  a  clearing  along  the  road,  halfway  between  camp  and  town.  The  Gerings  rented  the  cabin  for  fifteen  dollars  a  month  from  a  hard-bitten  old  man  named  Lit-  tleberry,  who  lived  in  a  shack  on  the  other  side  of  the  clearing.  Littleberry  eked  out  a  living  by  sharpening  saws  or  working  on  cars  or  butchering.  Al-  bert  sometimes  watched  him  string  up  a  pig  or  heifer,  but  the  old  man  was  poor  company  and  never  had  much  to  say.  Occasionally,  the  family  made  a  weekend  trip  to  town,  but  Albert  felt  uneasy  there;  he  noticed  that  one  of  the  elderly  patriotic  loungers  by  the  hotel  would  spit  on  the  ground  contemptuously  when  his  father  passed. It  was  a  fancy-looking  X,  with  the  same  de-  sign,  the  boy  noticed,  as  the  gold  trademark  on  the  Lucky  Lager  beer-  bottle  label.
