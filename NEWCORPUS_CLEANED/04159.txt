 b"IT  has  struck  me  more  than once  in    my  life  that  the  most  eloquently  pathetic  and  moving  incidents  are  often  of  the  most  trivial  nature-triv-  ial,  that  is,  in  a  comparative  sense-  and  I  recall  three  that  remain  bright  and  alive  in  my  mind  because  each  of  them  slipped  the  veil  off  a  truth  and  re-  vealed,  in  its  own  oblique  way,  and  only  for  a  moment,  the  face  of  pathos.  The  first  happened  when  I  was  eight  or  nine  years  old,  quitea  long  time  ago.  It  was  in  the  small  town  of  Ham-  burg,  in  western  New  York  State,  and  was  not,  properly  speaking,  an  incident  at  all-just  a  word  or  two  spoken,  a  frightened,  diffident  look  on  a  man's  face,  an  attitude  struck  unconsciously,  and  it  was  over.  Small  boys  are  not  sentimental,  and  rarely  are  they  sensitive,  and  I  have  since  wondered  about  myself  at  that  age;  where  did  I  get  the  understanding  with  which  to  seize  in  a  moment  a  fully  real-  ized  concept  ofa  whole  life  (or  lives)  and  see,  in  a  sudden  burst,  the  whole  meaning  of  impoverishment-the  kind  of  impoverishment  that  begins  before  the  child  is  born  and  continues,  remorse-  lessly,  with  toothaches  that  cannot  be  at-  tended  to,  with  miseries  in  the  back  and  uneasy  pains  in  the  groin  that  are  never  explained,  and  with  the  grinding  bore-  dom  and  all  the  hopeless  rest  of  it,  until  the  grown  man  comes  to  a  stop  in  pot-  ter's  field?  There  was  nothing  excep-  tional  about  me  when  I  was  a  child.  I  was  a  little  more  introspective  and  re-  fective  than  most  of  my  friends,  I  guess,  but  not  markedly  so,  and  I  can  remem-  ber,  too  clearly,  occasions  when  I  ran  with  the  pack  when  it  was  harrying  some  poor  kid  who  was  funny-looking,  or  who  had  some  hilarious  handicap  like  a  stutter,  or  who  had  come  to  school  in  what,  to  the  broker's  eye  of  childhood,  were  obviously  his  father's  old  pants  cut  down  to  size. While  I  was  waiting  for  the  black-  haired  daughter  of  the  Greek  proprietor  to  finish  getting  me  whatever  it  was  I  wanted-and  she  always  took  her  time  about  it  with  the  nickel  trade-a  man  came  into  the  store. These  people  were  poor  as  dogs;  their  fields,  tilled  in  an  indolent  and  ignorant  way-  and  God  knows  what  could  be  raised  in  them-were  little  better  than  plots  of  dust,  and  not  a  blade  of  grass  grew  within  ten  yards  of  their  broken  porch-  es. The  families  were  often  on  the  county  for  the  winter  months;  when  they  were,  their  names-all  Anglo-  Saxon  names,  like  Godwin  and  Johnson  and  Hawkins-were  duly  printed  in  the  local  weekly.
