She  smiled  at  him  even  when  he  didn't  say  anything.  Now  she  had  moved  on  to  a  section  praising  countries  where  sex  education  was  highly  developed.  &quot;In  the  United  States,&quot;  she  read,  &quot;ten-year-olds  know  how  to  use  condoms!&quot;  Sergei's  eyes  lit  up  with  excitement.  &quot;I  wonder  what  they  put  them  on,&quot;  he  said. On  Monday,  I  would  have  to  walk  into  my  classroom  and  announce  to  the  tenth-  grade  girls  that  I  was  there  to  teach  them  about  sex.  Would  they  laugh,  or  would  there  be  a  terrifying  silence?  Would  they  ask  probing  questions?  And  when  I  tried  to  answer  them  would  they  exchange  knowing  smirks,  sly  winks? I  wanted  to  talk  to  my  mother,  who  was  in  our  home  town,  thousands  of  miles  from  [REDACTED].  But  I  knew  better  than  to  call  her. The  girls  who,  like  me,  had  come  to  the  uni-  versity  from  small  towns  all  lived  on  campus.  They  had  formed  a  tight  circle,  held  together  by  their  own  specific  inter-  ests.  They  talked  about  how  to  use  for-  bidden  electric  teakettles,  how  to  dry  clothes  on  rusty  radiators,  how  to  sneak  boyfriends  into  their  rooms  at  night.  They  exchanged  advice  for  dealing  with  hangovers,  flooded  toilets,  and  mean  Muscovites.  I  couldn't  be  a  part  of  this  set.  Simply  by  living  with  my  aunt,  I  wasn't  a  part  of  it.  What's  more,  pre-  cisely  because  I  lived  with  my  aunt,  they  considered  me  one  of  the  Muscovites. Both  men  had  stubby  noses  and  small,  gloomy  eyes.  They  could  have  been  brothers.  Every  morning,  Aunt  Galya  climbed  onto  my  bed,  spat  on  the  glass,  and  wiped  the  pictures  with  a  dishrag. She  appeared  in  my  doorway  almost  every  night,  hold-  ing  her  faded  silk  robe  across  her  large  breasts.  The  robe  had  once  been  a  ki-  mono,  but  she  had  cut  holes  on  one  side  and  sewed  buttons  on  the  other,  so  that  she  could  now  button  it  up  when  she  wanted  to,  but  she  never  did.  &quot;Come,  have  a  cup  of  tea  with  me,&quot;  she'd  say,  but  she  never  served  me  tea.  I  wasn't  even  sure  she  had  a  teakettle.  Aunt  Galya  wasn't  big  on  cooking.  She  usually  ate  her  meals  at  the  factory  where  she  worked.  On  weekends,  she  had  a  bologna  sand-  wich  for  breakfast,  a  bologna  sandwich  for  lunch,  and  chocolates  for  dinner.  Every  time  I  came  out  of  my  room  for  &quot;a  cup  of  tea,&quot;  she'd  produce  a  glass  jar  full  of  turbid  greenish  liquid.  This  was  moonshine,  which  she  made  herself,  a  fresh  batch  every  Saturday  morning,  and  kept  in  glass  jars  with  old  faded  labels:  &quot;Strawberry  Jam,&quot;  &quot;Dill  Pickles,&quot;  &quot;Pick  led  Mushrooms.&quot;  She  wasn't  as  strong  as  she  used  to  be-she  was  only  fifty-  seven,  but  a  stomach  ulcer  had  made  her  complexion  sallow-so  she  never  drank  more  than  half  a  litre  in  the  course  of  an  evening.  Aunt  Galya  was  a  distant  relative  of  my  mother.  In  fact,  they'd  met  only  a  few  times.  It  had  been  very  generous  of  her  to  take  me  in.  My  mother  would  have  gone  crazy  with  worry  if  I'd  had  to  share  a  dorm  room  with  three  other  girls  three  &quot;drunken  sluts,&quot;  in  her  words.  And  it  would  have  been  base  ingratitude  for  me  to  refuse  to  sit  with  Aunt  Galya  and  listen  to  her  stories,  so  most  nights  I  listened.  The  stories  were  either  about  Aunt  Galya's  dead  husbands  or  about  her  countless  admirers.  I'd  learned  all  their  names,  their  addictions,  their  habits,  and  their  physical  peculiarities.  I'd  learned,  for  example,  that  Uncle  Boris  had  ahairy  back,  that  Uncle  Ivan  had  small  balls,  and  Uncle  Ivan's  best  friend,  Grigory,  had  even  smaller  ones.  Aunt  Galya's  footsteps  stopped.  She  must  have  sat  down.  Long,  drunken  yawns  were  beginning  to  replace  her  humming.  This  meant  that  she  would  soonbe  falling  asleep  and  I  wouldn't  have  to  come  out  and  listen  to  her.  On  the  other  hand,  even  listening  to  her  stories  would  have  been  better  than  sitting  there  alone,  mourning  my  ruined  life.  y  Monday  morning,  I  felt  better.  As  I  walked  past  the  huge  gray  buildings  of  [REDACTED]  on  my  way  to  school,  the  crisp  air  stung  my  cheeks.  The  trees  were  stripped  of  leaves  but  not  yet  covered  with  snow.  This  made  the  streets  appear  wider.  When  I'd  first  moved  to  [REDACTED],  the  streets  had  seemed  strange  and  hostile,  but  I  quickly  got  to  like  them.  They  were  like  big  rivers:  wide,  endless,  and  flowing.  Ev-  erything-cars,  people,  leaves-was  in  constant  motion,  and  as  I  walked  fast,  my  scarf  flapping  in  the  wind,  I  felt  swept  up  in  the  current.  I  caught  a  glimpse  of  my  reflection  in  a  supermar-  ket  window.  Even  in  its  murky,  poorly  washed  glass  I  could  see  that  I  looked  pretty.  The  handle  of  my  bag  cut  into  my  palm,  weighted  down  by  the  book  that  I  had  checked  out  of  the  library  over  the  weekend.  The  nine  hundred  and  eighty  pages  of  &quot;The  Nature  of  Sexuality&quot;  had  been  written  by  several  authors,  each  one  with  a  Ph.D.  next  to  his  name.  It  was  the  only  book  in  the  library  with  the  word  &quot;sex&quot;  in  its  title.  I  shifted  the  bag  in  my  hands.  It  also  contained  a  bright  folded  poster  that  I  had  made  the  night  before,  by  copying  a  diagram  from  the  book.  I  recalled  that  I  had  been  frightened  before  teaching  my  first  math  class,  too,  but  that  had  turned  out  all  right.  The  students  had  sat  quietly,  leaning  over  their  notebooks,  and  my  voice  had  fal-  tered  only  two  or  three  times.  Maybe  my  sex  class  would  be  all  right,  too.  I  had  my  book  and  my  poster,  and  it  wasn't  as  if  I  had  no  experience  whatsoever.  I  wasn't  a  virgin.  Or,  at  least,  I  didn't  think  |  I  was.  I  wasn't  entirely  sure.     I  arrived  at  school  a  few  minutes  ear-  lier  than  usual,  so  I  had  time  to  pin  my  poster  up  on  the  board.  I  had  made  it  by  gluing  together  eight  sheets  of  regular  paper.  At  the  center  was  a  colorful  draw-  ing  of  the  female  reproductive  organs,  with  their  Latin  names  shooting  out-  ward  like  fireworks.  I'd  got  the  colors  right,  and  I  was  especially  happy  with  the  intense  purple  of  the  uterus,  a  color  I  had  created  by  applying  blue  paint  on  top  of  red.  I  wasn't  as  happy  with  the  shapes  and  sizes,  though.  It  had  proved  difficult  to  make  the  poster  eight  times  bigger  than  the  picture  in  the  book  and  still  maintain  the  right  proportions.  I  could  see  now  that  one  ovary  was  larger  than  the  other.  But  at  least  the  poster  was  eye-catching.  Even  intimidating.  And  that  was  a  good  thing.  I  usually  started  my  math  classes  by  writing  some  difficult  equations  on  the  blackboard  and  demanding  that  they  be  solved  in  ten  minutes.  I  called  it  &quot;warm-  ing  up,&quot;  but  my  real  goal  was  to  intimi-  date  the  students.  When  I  stumbled  into  the  classroom  every  day  in  my  mother's  pumps,  with  parched  lips  and  cold  trickles  of  sweat  running  down  my  sides,  they  were  already  sitting  there,  all  thirty-nine  ofthem.  Everything  about  them  was  terrifying:  their  pimpled  fore-  heads  and  red  fingers,  their  blue  uni-  forms,  darkened  under  the  arms,  the  boys'  cracking  voices  and  enormous  feet,  the  girls'  awkward  makeup. I  gave  them  tests  every  cou-  ple  of  days  and  assigned  excruciating  homework.  I  was  very  strict  about  grad-  ing  exams.  Not  a  single  mistake  went  unnoticed.  Needless  to  say,  I  never  smiled  during  my  lessons.  Behind  my  back,  my  students  called  me  the  Math  Hound  or  simply  the  Hound.  I  didn't  mind  the  name.  It  meant  that  I  had  them  under  control.  Once  I  had  pinned  up  the  poster,  I  pulled  down  the  projector's  screen  to  cover  it  until  the  appropriate  time.  I  was  ready.  The  girls  straggled  into  the  room,  which  looked  strange  with  empty  desks  where  the  boys  usually  sat. The  girls  moved  their  chairs  carefully  to  avoid  making  scrap-  ing  noises,  talked  in  whispers,  and  ex-  changed  shy  looks.  &quot;Good  morning!&quot;  I  said,  and  my  voice  rang  out  clearly.  Al-  though  I  always  said  those  words  at  the  beginning  of  math  class,  I  wasn't  pre-  pared  for  the  girls'  reaction  today.  In-  stead  of  groaning,  then  sighing,  then  peering  at  their  homework,  they  looked  up  at  me  expectantly.  I  had  never  seen  them  look  this  way  before.  Then  some-  thing  occurred  to  me,  something  that  would  have  made  me  laugh  if  I  hadn't  been  so  nervous:  They  saw  me  as  their  teacher.  They  thought  I  possessed  cer-  tain  knowledge  of  sex  the  way  I  pos-  sessed  certain  knowledge  of  math.  But,  unlike  math,  sex  was  something  they  really  wanted  to  learn  about.  A  girl  in  the  first  row  was  tapping  her  foot  on  the  floor.  Another  girl,  by  the  window,  rubbed  her  forehead  with  the  cap  of  her    pen.  My  best  student,  the  pretty  Lara  Orlova,  twisted  her  ash-blond  curls  around  her  finger.  Suddenly,  these  girls  didn't  seem  so  scary  to  me.  They  were  just  girls  as  I'd  once  been,  whose  moth-  ers,  like  mine,  had  probably  told  them  only,  &quot;You'll  figure  it  out  when  the  time  comes.&quot;  I  unveiled  my  poster  and  spoke  the  sentence  that  I'd  been  working  on  for  the  last  two  days:  &quot;We'll  begin  our  lesson  by  studying  the  organs  responsi-  ble  for  sexual  functions  in  the  female  body.&quot;  Aunt  Galya  had  come  home  early  that  day  and  was  cooking  when  I  ar-  rived.  As  soon  as  I  opened  the  front  door,  I  felt  waves  of  heat  coming  from  the  kitchen,  and  the  sweetish  smell  of  burned  milk  pervaded  the  hall.  Some-  thing  was  gurgling  under  the  lid  of  a  big  aluminum  pot.  I  had  never  seen  this  pot  before.  Aunt  Galya  must  have  bor-  rowed  it  from  a  neighbor,  along  with  the  green  apron  that  was  tied  over  her  kimono.  She  stood  besidethe  stove  with  a  dishrag  in  one  hand  and  a  wooden  spoon  in  the  other.  Every  few  seconds  she  raised  the  lid,  let  out  clouds  of  white  steam,  and  stirred  whatever  was  inside.  Her  face  was  flushed.  &quot;I'm  making  kasha!&quot;  she  announced.  &quot;It's  good  for  the  stomach.  I  had  another  doctor's  ap-  pointment  today,  and  guess  what  he  said. Isat  down  at  my  desk,  then  immediately  stood  up  and  walked  to  Tanya  Mysh-  kina's  desk,  in  the  first  row.  I  couldn't  sit  there,  either.  I  walked  to  the  back  of  the  room  and  sat  in  Vera  Bunina's  chair  for  a  minute,  then  on  the  edge  of  her  desk.  It  occurred  to  me  that  I  was  behaving  just  like  Goldilocks,  trying  out  my  students'  chairs.  What  if  I  told  the  girls  about  my  date  with  Sergei?  What  if  I  told  them  about  the  look  in  his  eyes?  What  if  I  told  them  how  his  hand  had  got  stuck  in  the  knot  of  my  scarf  when  he  tried  to  un-  button  my  coat  in  the  crowded  movie  theatre?  And  how,  later,  he  had  kissed  me  all  the  way  through  &quot;The  Godfa-  ther:  Part  III,&quot;  pausing  only  when  he  saw,  in  his  peripheral  vision,  that  one  of  the  characters  was  about  to  be  killed?  What  if  I  told  them  that  the  best  part  of  the  date  wasn't  even  being  on  a  date  but  walking  to  meet  him  at  the  Pushkin  sub-  way  station,  because  men  had  turned  to  look  at  me  as  I  passed?  I  wondered  if  the  girls  would  understand  it  all.  Then  I  saw  something  that  made  the  laughing  springs  in  my  stomach  go  still.  The  brown  shoebox  lay  under  my  desk.  I  had  forgotten  to  pick  up  the  questions  on  Friday.  I  slowly  slid  off  Vera's  desk  and  went  over  tothe  box.  Inside  there  was  a  sheet  of  notepaper  folded  in  four.  My  first  thought  was  to  throw  it  away,  to  get  rid  of  it,  and  pretend  that  the  box  had  been  empty.  But  what  if,  by  some  miracle,  I  knew  the  answer?  I  unfolded  the  note.  About  half  the  page  was  cov-  ered  with  firm,  rounded  letters.  It  looked  like  Lara  Orlova's  handwriting,  but  it  wasn't  as  neat  as  her  usual  notes-she  must  have  been  nervous  when  she  wrote  it.  I  smoothed  the  paper  in  my  hands  and  read  the  question  quickly.  I  read  it  a  second  time,  this  time  out loud,  right  after  the  bell  had  rung  and  the  girls  had  taken  their  places  at  their  desks.  &quot;  I've  been  dating  Boy  X  for  some  months  now.  I  like  him  very  much.  We  have  a  steady  relationship.  When  he  touches  me  in  certain  places,  it  feels  very  good.  But  lately  I've  gone  out  with  Boy  Y  a  few  times.  He  doesn't  read  books,  he  hasa  dumb  laugh  and  pimples.&quot;  I  paused  and  looked  out  at  the  class.  Some  of  the  girls  glancedaround,  trying  to  guess  the  author  of  the  note.  Others  seemed  genuinely  interested  in  the  prob-  lem  itself.  With  their  mouths  open  and  their  brows  pulled  together,  they  strained  to  digest  the  question  and  possibly  apply  it  to  their  own  experience.  Tanya,  in  the  first  row,  was  chewing  the  tip  of  her  pen  so  hard  it  seemed  as  if  she  might  bite  off  a  piece  and  swallow  it.  I  tried  not  to  em-  barrass  Lara  by  looking  at  her,  but  out  of  thecorner  of  my  eye  I  saw  that  she  was  gazing  out  the  window  at  the  school-  yard,  twisting  and  twisting  her  curls  around  her  finger.  I  cleared  my  throat  and  read  the  note  to  the  end.  &quot;  I  don't  like  Boy  Y  at  all.  Why,  then,  when  he  touches  me  in  certain  places,  do  I  feel  ex-  actly  the  same  as  I  do  with  Boy  X&quot;  &quot;  Now  they  were  all  looking  at  me.  They     truly  believed  that  I  could  answer  the  question.  The  springs  of  laughter  were  coming  back,building  up  somewhere  in  my  chest  and  fighting  to  get  higher  and  higher.  And  then  I  said  something  that  I'd  been  wanting  to  say  for  a  very  long  time:  &quot;I  don't  know!&quot;  I  enjoyed  saying  these  words  so  much  that  it  made  me  light-headed.  I  felt  like  hopping  around  the  classroom  singing,  &quot;I  don't  know!  I  don't  know!  I  don't  know!&quot;  The  laughter  was  getting  stronger  and  stronger  inside  me.  It  seemed  about  to  break  through  my  skin,  leaving  my  body,  filling  the  room.  Then  I  heard  the  first  giggles.  I  wasn't  sure  if  it  was  me  or  one  of  the  girls.  Soon  everybody  was  laugh-  ing,  even  Lara  Orlova.  The  separate  sounds  became  one  loud,  steady  rumble  in  the  room.  I  didn't  hear  Maria  Petro-  vna  open  the  classroom  door.  I  couldn't  hear  what  she  was  saying.  I  saw  only  the  tips  of  her  black  shoes  and  her  pink  face  squeezed  between  the  door  and  the  doorframe,  her  eyelashes  blinking  and  her  lips  moving.  If  she  was  saying  some-  thing  about  inappropriate  behavior  and  disrespectto  our  young  teacher,  none  of  uscould  hear  it.
