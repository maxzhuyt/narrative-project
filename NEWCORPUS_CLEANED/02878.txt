 b"THE  following  are  excerpts  from  the  soon-to-be-published  memoirs  of  Flo  Guinness.  Certainly  the  most  colorful  of  all  speakeasy  owners  during  Prohibition,  Big  Flo,  as  her  friends  called  her  (many  enemies  called  her  that,  too,  mostly  for  con-  venience),  emerges  in  these  taped  in-  terviews  as  a  woman  with  a  lusty  appe-  tite  for  living,  as  well  asa  disappointed  artist  who  had  to  give  up  her  lifetime  ambition  to  become  a  classical  violinist,  when  she  realized  it  would  mean  studying  the  violin.  Here,  for  the  first  time,  Big  Flo  speaks  for  herself.  RIGINALLY  I  danced  at  the  Jewel  Club  in  [REDACTED],  for  Ned  Small.  Ned  was  a  shrewd  businessman  who  made  all  his  money  by  what  we  would  now  call  &quot;stealing.&quot;  Of  course,  in  those  days  it  was  quite  different.  Yes,  sir,  Ned  had  great  charm-the  kind  you  don't  see  today.  He  was  famous  for  breaking  both  your  legs  if  you  disagreed  with  him.  And  he  could   do  it,  too,  boys.  He  broke  more  legs!  I'd  say  fourteen  or  sixteen  legs  a  week  was  his  average.  But  Ned  was  sweet  on  me,  maybe  'cause  I  always  told  him  straight  to  his  face  what  I  thought  of  him.  &quot;Ned,&quot;  I  told  him  over  dinner  once,  &quot;you're  a  mealymouth  grifter  with  the  morals  of  an  alley  cat.&quot;  He  laughed,  but  later  that  night  I  saw  him  looking  up  &quot;mealymouth&quot;  in  a  diction-  ary.  Anyhow,  like  I  said,  I  danced  at  Ned  Small's  Jewel  Club.  I  was  his  best  dancer,  boys-a  dancer-actress.  The  other  girls  just  hoofed,  but  I  danced  a  little  story.  Like  Venus  emerging  from  her  bath,  only  on  [REDACTED]  and  Forty-  second  Street,  and  she  goes  night-club-  bing  and  dances  till  dawn  and  then  has  a  massive  coronary  and  loses  con-  trol  of  the  facial  muscles  on  her  left  side.  Sad  stuff,  boys.  That's  why  I  got  respect.  One  day,  Ned  Small  calls  me  into  his  office  and  says,  &quot;Flo.&quot;  (He  always  called  me  Flo,  except  when  he  got  real  mad  at  me.  Then  he'd  call  me  Albert Schneiderman-I  never  knew  why.  Let's  say  the  heart  has  strange  ways.)  So  Ned  says,  &quot;Flo,  I  want  you  to  mar-  ry  me.&quot;  Well,  you  could've  knocked  me  over  with  a  feather.  I  started  cry-  ing  like  a  baby.  &quot;I  mean  it,  Flo,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;I  love  you  very  deeply.  It's  not  easy  for  me  to  say  these  things,  but  I  want  vou  to  be  the  mother  of  my  children.  And  if  you  don't  I'll  break  both  your  legs.&quot;  Two  days  later,  to  the  minute,  Ned  Small  and  I  tied  the  knot.  Three  days  later,  Ned  was  machine-gunned  to  death  by  the  mob  for  spilling  raisins  on  Al  Capone's  hat.  After  that,  of  course,  I  was  rich.  First  thing  I  did  was  buy  my  mother  and  father  that  farm  they'd  always  talked  about.  They  claimed  they  had  never  talked  about  a  farm  and  actually  wanted  a  car  and  some  furs,  but  they  gave  it  a  try.  Liked  the  rural  life,  too,  although  Dad  got  struck  by  lightning  in  the  north  forty  and  for  six  years  afterward  when  asked  his  name  could  only  say  the  word  &quot;Kleenex.&quot;  As  for  me,  three  months  later  I  was  broke.  Bad  investments.  I  backed  a  whaling  expedition  in  [REDACTED],  on  the  advice  of  friends.  I  DANCED  for  Big  Ed  Wheeler,  who  made  bootleg  hooch  that  was  so  strong  it  could  only  be  sipped  through  a  gas  mask.  Ed  paid  me  three  hundred  dollars  a  week  to  do  ten  shows,  which  in  those  days  was  big  money.  Hell,  with  tips  I  made  more  than  President  Hoo-  ver.  And  he  had  to  do  twelve  shows.  I  went  on  at  nine  and  eleven,  and  Hoo-  ver  went  on  at  ten  and  two.  Hoover  was  a  good  President,  but  he  was  al-  ways  sitting  in  his  dressing  room  hum-  ming.  It  drove  me  crazy.  Then  one  day  the  owner  of  the  Apex  Club  saw  my  act  and  offered  me  five  hundred  dollars  a  week  to  dance  there.  I  put  it  squarely  to  Big  Ed:  &quot;Ed,  I  got  an  offer  of  five  hundred  bucks  from  Bill  Hal-  lorhan's  Apex  Club.&quot;  &quot;Flo,&quot;  he  said,  &quot;if  you  can  get  five  hundred  a  week,  I  won't  stand  in  your  way.&quot;  We  shook  hands  and  I  went  to  tell  Bill  Hallorhan  the  good  news,  but  several  of  Big  Ed's  friends  had  gotten  there  first  and  when  I  saw  Bill  Hallor-  han  his  physical  condition  had  under-  gone  a  change  and  he  was  now  only  a  high-pitched  voice  that  came  from  in-  side  a  cigar  box.  He  said  he  had  decid-  ed  to  get  out  of  show  business,  leave  [REDACTED],  and  settle  somewhere  closer  to  the  equator.  I  went  on  dancing  for  Big  Ed  Wheeler  till  the  Capone  mob  bought  him  out.  I  say  &quot;bought  him  out,&quot;  boys,  but  the  truth  of  it  was  Scar-  face  Al  offered  him  a  tidy  sum  but  " b"Wheeler  said  no.  Later  that  day,  he  was  having  lunch  at  the  Rib  and  Chop  House  when  his  head  burst  into  flames.  No  one  knew  why.  BOUGHT  the  Three  Deuces  with  money  Id  saved,  and  in  no  time  it  was  the  hot  spot  in  town.  They  all  came-Babe  Ruth,  Jack  Dempsey,  Jolson,  Man  o'  War.  Man  o'  War  was  there  every  night.  My  God,  how  that  horse  could  drink!  I  remember  once  Babe  Ruth  had  this  crush  on  a  showgirl  named  Kelly  Swain.  He  was  so  crazy  about  her  he  couldn't  keep  his  mind  on  baseball  and  twice  greased  his  body,  think-  ing  he  was  a  famous  channel  swimmer.  &quot;Flo,&quot;  he  saidto  me,  &quot;I'm  nuts  about  this  red-  head,  Kelly  Swain.  But  she  hates  sports.  I  lied  and  told  her  I  give  a  course  on  Witt-  genstein,  but  I  think  she  sus-  pects  something.&quot;  &quot;Can  you  live  without  her,  Babe?&quot;  I  asked  him.  &quot;No,  Flo.  And  it's  affecting  my  concentration.  Yesterday,  I  got  four  hits  and  stole  two  bases,  but  this  is  Jan-  uary  and  there  are  no  games  scheduled.  I  did  it  in  my  hotel  room.  Can  you  help  me?&quot;  I  promised  him  I'd  speak  to  Kelly,  and  the  next  day  I  stopped  by  the  Golden  Abattoir,  where  she  was  danc-  ing.  I  said,  &quot;Kelly,  the  Bambino  is  nuts  about  you.  He  knows  you  like  culture  and  he  says  if  you  date  him  he'll  give  up  baseball  and  join  the  Martha  Gra-  ham  troupe.&quot;  Kelly  looked  me  squarely  in  the  eye  and  said,  &quot;Tell  that  palooka  I  didn't  come  all  the  way  from  Chippewa  Falls  to  wind  up  with  some  overstuffed  right  fielder.  I  got  big  plans.&quot;  Two  years  later,  she  married  Lord  Osgood  Well-  ington  Tuttle  and  became  Lady  Tut-  tle.  Her  husband  gave  upan  ambas-  sadorship  to  play  shortstop  for  the  Tigers.  Jumpin'  Joe  Tuttle.  He  holds  the  record  for  most  times  beaned  in  the  first  inning.     AMBLING?  Boys,  I  was  present  when  Nick  the  Greek  got  his  name.  There  was  a  small-time  gambler  named  Jake  the  Greek,  andNick  called  me  and  said,  &quot;Flo,  I'd  like  to  be  The  Greek,&quot;  And  I  said,  &quot;I'm  sorry,  Nick,  you're  not  Greek.  And  under  New  York  State  gambling  laws  it's  for-  bidden.&quot;  And  he  said,  &quot;I  know,  Flo,  but  my  parents  always  wanted  me  to  be  called  The  Greek.  You  think  you  could  arrange  a  lunch  meeting  with  Jake?&quot;  I  said,  &quot;Sure,  but  if  he  knows  what  it's  for  he  won't  show,&quot;  And  Nick  said,  &quot;Try,  Flo.  It  would  mean  a  lot  to  me.&quot;  So  the  two  met  at  the  Grill  Room  of  Monty's  Steak  House,  which  did  not  allow  women  but  I  could  go  there  be-  cause  Monty  was  a  great  friend  of  mine  and  didn't  regard  me  as  either  male  or  female  but,  in  his  own  words,  &quot;undefined  protoplasm.&quot;  We  ordered  the  speciality  ofthe  house,  ribs,  which  Monty  had  a  way  of  preparing  so  they  tasted  like  human  fingers.  Finally,  Nick  said,  &quot;Jake,  I'd  like  to  be  called  The  Greek.&quot;  And  Jake  turned  pale  and  said,  &quot;Look,  Nick,  if  that's  what  you  got  me  here  for-&quot;  Well,  boys,  it  got  ugly.  The  two  squared  off.  Then  Nick  said,&quot;T'll  tell  you  what  I'll  do.  Pll  cut  you.  High  card  gets  to  be  called  The  Greek.&quot;  &quot;Butwhat  if  I  win?!&quot;  Jake  said.  &quot;I'm  already  called  The  Greek.&quot;  &quot;If  you  win,  Jake,  you  can  go  through  the  phone  book  and  pick  any  name  you  like.  My  compliments.&quot;  &quot;No  kidding?&quot;  &quot;Flo's  the  witness.&quot;  Well,  you  could  feel  the  tension  in  that  room.  A  deck  of  cards  was  brought  out  and  they  cut.  Nick  cut  a  queen,  and  Jake's  hand  was  shaking.  Then  Jake  cut  an  ace!  Everybody  let  out  a  cheer,  and  Jake  went  through  the  phone  book  and  selected  the  name  Grover  Lembeck.  Everybody  was  hap-  py,  and  from  that  day  on  women  were  allowed  into  Monty's,  provided  they  could  read  hieroglyphics.  REMEMBER  once  there  was  a  big  musical  review  at  the  Winter  Gar-  den,  &quot;Star-Spangled  Vermin.&quot;  Jolson  was  the  lead,  but  he  quit  because  they  wanted  him  to  sing  a  song  called  &quot;Kasha  for  Two,&quot;  and  he  hated  it.  It  had  the  line  in  it  &quot;Love  is  all,  like  a  horse  in  a  stall.&quot;  Anyway,  eventually  it  was  sung  by  a  young  unknown  named  Felix  Brompton,  who  was  later  arrested  in  a  hotel  room  with  a  one-inch  card-  board  cutout  of  Helen  Morgan.  It  was  in  all  the  papers.  Well,  Jolson  come  into  the  Three  Deuces  one  night  with  Eddie  Cantor,  and  he  says  to  me,  &quot;Flo,  I  hear  George  Raft  did  his  tap  dance  here  last  week.&quot;  And  I  said,  &quot;No,  Al.  George  has  never  been  here.&quot;  And  he  said,  &quot;If  you  let  him  do  his  tap  dance,  I'd  like  to  sing.&quot;  And  I  said,  &quot;Al,  he  was  never  here.&quot;  And  Al  said,  &quot;Did  he  have  any  accompaniment  on  piano?&quot;  And  I  said,  &quot;Al,  if  you  sing  one  note  I'll  personally  throw  you  out.&quot;  And  with  that  Jolie  got  down  on  one  knee  and  started  on  &quot;Toot-Toot  Tootsie.&quot;  While  he  was  singing,  I  sold  the  place,  and  by  the  timehe  was  finished  it  was  the  Wing  Ho  Hand  Laundry.  Jolson  never  got  over  that  or  forgave  me  for  it.  On  the  way  out,  he  tripped  over  a  pile  of  shirts. "
