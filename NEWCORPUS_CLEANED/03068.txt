The  cottage  stood  on  the  crest  of  a  high  ridge  over-  looking  Botany  Bay,  some  eight  miles  distant,  and  was  built  foursquare,  cast-  west,  so  that  they  could  look  from  the  veranda  straight  between  the  head-  lands,  Cape  Banks  and  Cape  Solander,  to  the  [REDACTED].  They  could  see  the  obelisk  standing  where  Captain  Cook  first  landed  with  his  botanists-Banks  and  Solander-and  they  could  see  on  stormy  days  the  little  launch  they  called  the  Peanut  tossing  between  the  heads  as  it  went  toward  Kurnell.  The  cottage  was  built  of  rough-  hewn  sandstone  blocks  cut  out  in  the  quarry  down  the  hill  and  hauled  up  in  the  old  days.  The  trees  round  the  house-Norfolk  Island,  Monterey,  and  other  pines,  pittosporum,  camphor  laurel-were  seventy  and  more  years  old,  and  the  pines  had  seeded  in  the  old  neglected  orchard,  where  the  seedlings  grew  higher  every  year,  faster  than  the  children.  The  knoll  itself  was  ironstone,  capped  and  penetrated  by  heavy,  thick,  and  almost  pure  clay,  gamboge  yellow,  stained  red  where  the  ironstone  stuck  out  its  nodules.  It  was  almost  country  still-few  houses,  large  pastures,  unpaved  streets  of  sand  or  clay,  foul  and  grassy  gutters.  The  short  street  Lydham  Avenue,  which  went  over  the  hump  before  the  house,  westward,  was  a  hazard,  almost  impassable  in  wet  weather.  Cart  wheels,  horseshoes,  boots,  umbrella  fer-  rules  were  sucked  in  by  the  clay.  In  the  hot  sun  the  clay  soon  turned  to  dough  and  then  to  pottery.  A  messenger  boy,  the  young  postman,  the  women  and  children  of  Lydham  Hill  had  to  cross  the  clay  to  get  to  the  tram  or  the  shops  and  might  lose  a  handkerchief,  a  parasol,  a  shoe,  a  parcel;  poking  at  it  gin-  gerly,  afraid  to  fall  in  the  muck,  they  would  abandon  it  and  struggle  to  the  clay  bank  and  look  back  just  as  if  the  thing  had  been  carried  out  to  sea.  The  postman's  prints,  first  of  a  sand  shoe  that  he  lost  as  he  crossed  to  Lydham  and  then  of  his  naked  foot,  and  a  copy  of  the  Sr.  George  Call  that  he  dropped  while  trying  to  get  his  shoe  back  remained  week  after  week.  The  footprints  and  tracksdid  not  dis-  appear  even  at  the  next  big  rain,     but  only  formed  little  foot-shaped  pud-  dles  and  long  canals.  The  council  oc-  casionally  sent  men  with  a  cart  and  shovels  to  scrape  offthe  surface,  and  they  gleaned  the  lost  articles  and  went  away  in  the  cart,  leaving  behind  a  similar  clay  surface  but  with  the  banks  higher  now,  until  the  people  in  Lyd-  ham  Hill  had  to  cut  steps  in  them-  yellow  clay  steps. As  he  still  poked  stupidly  about  looking  for  milk,  he  saw  a  gold  coin,  a  sovereign,  under  the  tussock.  He  knew  it,  for  his  father  brought  home  his  pay  on  Saturdays,  spread  the  coms  on  the  table-gold,  sovereigns  and  half  sovereigns,  silver-and  let  the  children  finger  them.  He  was  proud  of  it,  what  he  earned.  Matthew  picked  up  the  gold  coin  and  hurried  home  up  the  yellow  clay,  past  the  saffron  tea  rose,  a  tree  with  yellow  bellflowers-the  Chinaman's  Finger  Tree,  so  called  because  they  put  the  flowers  on  their  fingers  and  rushed  at  each  other,  shrieking,  &quot;I'm  a  China-  man!&quot;  He  had  his  statement  ready.  &quot;I  fell  over  the  grass  and  spilled  the  milk.&quot;  His  mother  was  in  the  kitchen  in  her  grav  silk  dressing  gown  with  the  silver  and  gold  dragons  on  it,  at  the  bottom  gold  water  waves  and  a  gold  tower.
