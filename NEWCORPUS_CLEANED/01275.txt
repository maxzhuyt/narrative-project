She  can't  tell  if  Paul  is  joking  or  not.  &quot;Tell  me,&quot;  he  says,  emptying  the  can  against  the  house,  spraying  a  swath  over  the  grass,  a  line  that  leads  straight  to  the  grill.  &quot;You  have  to  tell  me.&quot;  &quot;I  don't  know,&quot;  Elaine  says.  The  briquettes  are  almost  ready;  the  coals  are  dead-white,  red-edged,  glow-  ing.  Elaine  goes  to  Paul  and  puts  her  hand  around  his  waist.  She  balances  her-  self,  then  raises  her  leg,  her  foot,  her  toe,  and  taps  her  toe  against  the  leg  of  the  grill.  &quot;Harder,&quot;  Paul  says.  Elaine  pulls  her  leg  back  and  kicks  the  grill,  The  coals  fly  up  and  out,  the  grill  tips  over.  Everything  sputters  and  smokes  for  a  minute,  and  then  slowly  the  fire  builds  up  from  the  ground,  the  grass,  and  moves  toward  the  house.  They  stand  watching  as  fire  creeps  up  the  back  wall  of  the  house.  Wordless,  each  wonders  if  it  is  a  game-a  dare  to  see  who  will  run  for  the  garden  hose.  As  the  fire  builds  their  nervousness  and  excitement  grow.  Elaine  begins  to  laugh  and  then  stops  herself,  pinching  her  cheeks  together  with  her  hands.  In  the  early-evening  light,  the  blue  flame  is  nearly  invisible.  Fire  seeps  into  a  crack  in  the  wall.  A  line  of  white  smoke  rises.  Elaine  watches,  wishing  she  could     83  hurry  it,  wishing  she  could  be  sure.  Paul  leans  on  Elaine  and  puts  his  shoes  and  socks  back  on.  &quot;Get  the  kids  and  get  in  the  car,&quot;  he  says.  Elaine  walks  away,  turning  back  to  see  Paul  blowing  on  the  fire,  fanning  it  with  his  hands,  encouraging  the  flame.  &quot;We're  going  out  for  dinner,&quot;  Elaine  tells  Sammy  and  Daniel.  &quot;Roger,&quot;  they  say  into  their  walkie-  talkies.  They  get  in  the  car  and  wait  for  Paul.  A  few  minutes  later,  red-faced,  breath-  less,  he  joins  them.  They  drive  to  a  nearby  restaurant.  The  waitress  fills  their  water  glasses.  Paul  and  Elaine  smile  at  each  other.  &quot;Tll  have  the  steak,&quot;  Paul  tells  the  waitress.  &quot;And  the  baked  potato  with  sour  [REDACTED];  &quot;The  fat,&quot;  Elaine  starts  to  say.  &quot;I  just  want  to  live,&quot;  Paul  says.  &quot;Live  and  not  worry.  Is  that  possible?&quot;  &quot;Tll  have  the  same,&quot;  Elaine  says,  clos-  ing  her  menu.  During  dinner  they  hear  sirens.  They  linger  over  coffee.  The  children  eat  ice-  cream  sundaes.  When  the  check  is  paid,  they  get  back  in  the  car  and  head  toward  home.  The  street  is  blocked  off;  there  are  fire  engines  and  police  cars.  In  the  dis-  tance,  they  can  see  their  house  in  flames,  with  fire  coming  out  of  the  roof.  In  the  back  seat,  the  children  are  oddly  silent.  &quot;Is  that  our  house?&quot;  Daniel  asks.  &quot;Yes,&quot;  Paul  and  Elaine  say.  They  watch  for  a  few  minutes,  and  then,  worried  that  someone  will  recog-  nize  them,  they  drive  away.  They  check  into  a  motel.  The  chil-  dren,  stunned  by  the  spectacle,  stoned  on  hot  fudge,  go  quickly  and  quietly  to  sleep.  &quot;We're  awful,&quot;  Elaine  says,  crawling  into  bed  next  to  Paul.  &quot;Really  bad.&quot;  &quot;We  have  to  try  harder,&quot;  Paul  says.  &quot;We  have  to  be  nicer  to  each  other,&quot;  Elaine  says.  Paul  plays  with  the  blankets,  pulling  them  up  over  their  heads.  &quot;We  should  build  a  fortress  to  protect  ourselves  from  the  world,&quot;  he  says.  &quot;To  protect  us  from  each  other,&quot;  Elaine  adds.  &quot;From  ourselves,&quot;  Paul  says,  letting  the  blankets  fall  down  over  them.
