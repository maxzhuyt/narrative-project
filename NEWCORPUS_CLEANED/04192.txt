 b"THE  tawny  Tiber  meets  the  Tyr-  rhenian  Sea  at  Ostia,  clouding  the  surf  and  darkening  the  sand.  It's  better  to  go  and  bathe  down  the  coast  a  few  miles,  at  Castel  Fusano  and  beyond  it.  The  sand  lightens  there,  and  a  half  mile  of  free  beach  stretches  ahead,  be-  fore  one  comes  to  the  fenced-off  gov-  ernment  reserve  of  Castel  Porziano.  Soon  the  free  beach,  too,will  probably  be  taken  over  by  private  concerns,  and  long  rows  of  tiny  cabins  will  rise.  But  today  there  are  still  scrub  pines  and  bushes  be-  tween  the  road  and  the  shore.  Lizards  leave  little  tracks  on  the  sandy  soil.  The  railroad  doesn't  come  here,  and  buses  run  only  once  in  two  hours,.  Most  people  arrive  either  in  cars  or  on  scoot-  ers,  which  they  leave  by  the  road-  side  in  thecare  of  a  self-appointed  at-  tendant.  They  have  only  to  make  their  way  through  the  bushes  and  walk  for  a  few  minutes  to  reach  the  open  sea.  The  shore  is  quite  crowded,  and,  sitting  on  the  sand,  one  may  pick  up  conversa-  tions.  Often,  it's  a  foreign  language  one  hears.  One  day  last  summer,  three  Ameri-  can  girls  were  on  the  beach.  Two  of  them  were  sunbathing,  ly-  ing  prone,  cheek  to  cheek  with  the  sand,  their  eyes  closed,  paying  heed  to  nothing  except  the  sun,  which  was  changing  their  color  to  a  deep  red.  The  third  one,a  thin  and  nerv-  ous-looking  blonde  with  a  cascade  of  hairthat  prac-  tically  concealed  her  face,  was  sitting  up,  doing  her  best  to  expose  as  little  of  herself  as  she  could  to  the  blazing  mid-July  sun.  She  looked  for  shade.  But  this  was  the  free  beach,  and  there  weren't  any  cabins  or  umbrellas  to  cast  a  square  or  a  circle  of  shadow;  there  were  only  human  shadows,  foreshortened,  and  sand,  and  half-naked  people  stretched  out  with  nothing  between  them  and  the  sun  except  a  thin  layer  of  oil.  She  looked  longingly  at  the  sea.  She  could  go  and  cool  herself  in  it  again,  but  she  had  been  in  the  water  till  her  fingertips  were  white  and  puckered;  she  couldn't  go  back  in.  Nor  could  she  leave  the  beach  yet-her  friends  were  enjoying  it.  Their  only  ambition  seemed  to  be  to  acquire  a  tan.  She  might  al-  ready  have  left  them  had  there  been  a  bus  back  or  had  they  not  had  a  car.  She  tilted  her  head  and  looked  at  them  as  if  trying  to  make  out  who  was  who.     They  both  looked  alike,  forshe  had  only  their  bare  backs  to  tell  them  by-  backs,  backs  of  thighs,  backs  of  knees,  calves,  pink  soles  of  feet.  A  young  man  a  few  feet  away  looked  at  her  as  though  he  were  trying  to  draw  from  the  visual  connection  more  than  it  could  ever  concede,  and  in  a  semicircle  near  them  were  a  half-dozen  Roman  boys,  aged  eighteen  or  nineteen.  They  were  also  lying  down,  but  with  heads  lifted,  chins  propped,  and  eyes  gaping  at  the  three  American  girls.  Every  once  in  a  while,  two  of  them  would  rise  and  walk  close  by  the  girls,  brushing  past  them,  making  a  hazardous  pass,  naively  assuming  they  could  make  a  conquest  that  way.  One  of  them  pushed  the  other  so  that  he  fell  at  the  feet  of  the  blond  girl,  touched  her  legs.
