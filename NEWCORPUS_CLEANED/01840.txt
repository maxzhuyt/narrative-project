Only  once  a  year,  when  the  lilies  arose  from  the  ground,  like  so  much  incense,  was  there  a  transformation:  traffic  stopped.  The  gentle  trespassers,  black  and  white,  had  been  persuaded  that  the  spidery  blooms  carried  belladonna,  whichwould  make  their  hearts  stand  still.  This  year  it  seemed  to  Anna  that  the  lilies  were  an  omen  of  an  even  greater  change  than  thatof  cooler  weather-perhaps  a  change  of  fortune  itself.  Laurance  Ingles,  the  adolescent  son  of  old  friends,  was  to  come  to  her  for  lessons  in  piano  and  English.  The  Ingleses  and  the  Merediths  had  been  close  friends  since  Missis-  sippi  Territory  days.  They  were  just  about  the  only  Catholic  families  in  Protestant  King  County.  But  whereas  the  Merediths  had  been  loyal  to  the  Confederacy  and  lost  everything  the  Ingleses  played  cards  with  New  Or-  leans  friends  who  persuaded  them  to  shift  their  funds  to  [REDACTED]  banks  before  it  was  too  late.  When  the  cur-  rency  of  the  South  was  repudiated,  the  Ingleses'  wealth  survived,  and  with  it  the  tradition  of  generosity-in  fact,  they'd  put  Anna  through  Wellesley.  Anna  reminded  herself  of  this.  There  was  no  telling  how  much  it  might  mean  that  the  two  families  were  again  in  touch  after  this  long  while.
