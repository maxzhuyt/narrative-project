He  would  have  to  ask,  when  he  went  in  to  King's  Town,  if  there  weren't  some  sick  folks  I  could  tend  or  a  nice  little  job  of  housework  I  could  get.  Well  here  he  came,  sooner  even  than  I  thought  he  would,  and  already  had  big  news.  The  Barrands'  cook-that's  out  at  Barrand's  Landing  about  three  miles  the  other  side  of  King's  Town  and  right  on  the  river-was  having  trouble  with  her  nerves.  Miss  Bonnie  Barrand,  who  had  been  just  about  com-  pletely  raised  by  this  same  lady,  had  tried  out  everything  she  knew  of,  up  to  and  including  Peacock's  bromide,  to  straighten  her  out,  and  all  the  family  too  (they  have  a  lot  of  brothers  and  uncles),  but  nothing  would  do,  the  cook  said,  but  to  get  her  some  help  and  I  could  go  right  then.  All  I  took  time  to  do  was  set  my  ferns  in,  be-  cause  the  way  the  weather  was  acting  it  was  getting  closer  and  closer  to  a  frost.  When  we  got  out  there  to  Barrand's  Landing  ev-  erything  we'd  heard  was  true.  That  early  in  the  morning  the  cook  had  al-  ready  caught  a  nervous  headache  andhad  to  put  on  this  hairnet  and  stick  two  toothpicks  out  of  each  side  of  the  front  totry  to  drain  it  off.  &quot;Be  ready  for  any-  thing  here&quot;  was  the  first  I  heard  this  lady  say.  &quot;Any-  thing,  Jesus.&quot;  She  had  to  have  help.  Before  I  could  find  out  her  name-the  name  of  the  cook  I  was  there  to  help-  she  told  me  it  was  school-  bus  time  and  not  all  the  children's  clothes  were  found,  much  less  pressed.  She  would  hunt,  she  said,  while  I  took  the  board.  Her  iron  was  already  good  and  hot.  I  had  just  started  in  when  I  heard  a  sweet  voice  calling  &quot;Lutie,  Lutie.&quot;  So  that  must  be  the  cook's  name.  Miss  Lutie-if  that  was  her-came  flying  back  in  with  more  clothes  she'd  found  and  then  jogged  around  as  fast  as  she  could  to  get  a  cup  and  saucer  and  pour  some  coffee  for  whoever  it  was  calling.  And  right  when  she  left  out  with  thecoffee  here  came  four  or  five  fine  looking  children  rushing  at  my  board.  &quot;It's  bus  time,&quot;  the  biggest  of  them  said,  about  to  cry  because  they  didn't  have  anything  too  much  on  yet  and  were  worried  they  wouldn't  be  ready.  I  tried  to  match  them  to  their  different  sizes  the  bestI  could  and  Miss  Lutie  came  back  and  we  had  them  looking  fairly  good  by  the  time  it  honked,  but  there  were  some  raggedy  placesI  would  have  to  mend  when  they  got  home  this  evening.
