Paul  chipped  onto  the  green  and  hastily  putted  twice.  On  the  fifth  tee,  beneath  the  plum  tree,  looking  across  toward  the  two  pink  flags,  Paul  felt  ex-  alted  and  certain.  The  kid,  well  on  his  way,  passed  in  front  of  the  far  flag,  and  Paul  aimed  for  the  double  image.  'The  ball,  socked  flatly,  floated  for  a  great  distance  in  a  leftward  sweep  and  never  rose,  it  seemed,  three  feet  off  the  ground.  That  he  had  hooked  did  not  diminish  his  conviction  that  he  was  des-  tined  to  give  the  kid  a  deserved  trounc-  ing.  'The  ball  bounced  once  in  the  open  and,  as  if  a  glass  arm  from  Heaven  had  reached  down  and  grabbed  it,  vanished.  His  eyes  marked  the  exact  spot  in  the  air  where  it  had  disappeared.  He  walked  there.  The  scarcely  slant-  ing  land  where  his  ball  should  have  been  was  unmarked  by  a  bush  or  tree  or  ditch;  any  hint  of  white  would  have  glared.  Hardly  aware  he  had  made  a  decision,  Paul  shifted  the  bag  of  clubs  on  his  shoulder  and  walked  toward  the  road.  It  was  almost  suppertime;  the  little  girl  would  be  in  the  tub.  He  had  never  seen  the  fifth  green,  and  he  pictured  it  as  paradisical-broad-leaved  trees,  the  cry  of  water. The  abandoned  road-building  machines  stood  among  piles  of  dirt  like  extinctbeasts  paleontol-  ogists  had  uncovered.
