Where  else  in  the  hungry  world  such  a  pros-  pect?  Barrels  and  baskets  of  wine,  six-  packs  of  beer  and  bran  muffins,  still  warm  sourdough  bread  that  he  would  break  and  gnaw  on  as  he  shopped.  Was  this  a  cunning  regression?  As  a  child  he  had  craved  raw  sugar  cookies.  But  his  euphoria  sagged  at  the  meat  counter,  as  he  studied  the  gray  matter  being  sold  as  meat-loaf  mix;  it  de-  clined  further  at  the  dairy  counter,  where  two  cartons  of  yogurt  had  been  sampled,  and  the  low-fat  cottage  cheese  was  two  days  older  than  dated.  By  the  time  he  entered  the  checkout  lane,  hemmed  in  by  scandal  sheets  and  romantic  novels,  the  cashier's  cheer-  fully  inane  &quot;Have  a  good  day!&quot;  would  send  him  off  forgetting  his  change  in  the  machine.  The  girl  who  pursued  him  (alwayswith  pennies!)  had  been  coached  to  say  &quot;Thank  you,  sir!&quot;  A  special  on  avocados  this  week  re-  quired  that  Bundy  make  a  careful  se-  lection.  Out  in  front,  as  usual,  dogs  were  barking.  On  the  airwaves,  from  the  rear  and  side,  the  &quot;Wang  Wang  Blues.&quot;  Why  wang  wang,  he  won-  dered.  Besides  wang  wang,  how  did  it  go?  The  music  was  interrupted  by  an  announcement  on  the  public-address  system.  Would  the  owner  of  the  white  dog  leashed  to  the  bike  rack  please  come  to  the  front?  Was  Bundy's  dog  white?  The  point  was  debatable.  Nev-  ertheless,  he  left  his  cart  by  the  avocados  and  followed  the  vegetable  display  to  the  front.  People  were  huddled  to  the  right  of  the  door.  A  clerk  beckoned  to  Bundy  through  the  window.  Still  leashed  to  the  bike  rack,  the  dog  lay  out  on  his  side,  as  if  sleeping.  In  the  parking  lot  several  dogs  were  yelping.  &quot;I'm  afraid  he's  a  goner,&quot;  said  the  clerk.  &quot;These  other  dogs  rushed  him.  Scared  him  to  death.  He  just  keeled  over  before  theygot  to  him.&quot;  The  dog  had  pulled  the  leash  taut,  but  there  was  no  sign  that  anything  had  touched  him.  A  small  woman  with  a  shopping  cart  thumped  into  Bundy.  &quot;Is  it  Tiger?&quot;  she  said.  &quot;I  hope  it's  not  Tiger.&quot;  She  stopped  tosee  that  it  was  not  Tiger.  &quot;Whose  dog  was  it?&quot;  she  asked,  peering  around  her.  The  clerk  indicated  Bundy.  &quot;Poor  thing,&quot;  she  said.  &quot;What  was  his  name?&quot;  Just  recently,  watching  the  Royal  Wedding,  Bundy  had  noticed  that  his  emotions  were  nearer  the  surface:  on  two  occasions  his  eyes  had  filmed  over.  He  didn't  like  the  woman's  speaking  of  the  dog  in  the  past  tense.  Did  she  think  he  had  lost  his  name  with  his  life?  &quot;What  was  the  poor  thing's  name?&quot;  she  repeated.  Was  the  tremor  in  Bundy's  limbs  noticeable?  &quot;Victor,&quot;  Bundy  lied,  since  he  could  not  bring  himself  to  admit  the  dog's  name  was  Victrola.  It  had  always  been  a  sore  point,  the  dog  being  tooold  to  be  given  a  new  one.  Miss  Tyler  had  felt  that  as  a  puppy  he  looked  like  the  picture  of  the  dog  at  the  horn  of  the  gramophone.  The  resemblance  was  feeble,  at  best.  How  could  a  person  give  a  dog  such  a  name?  &quot;Let  him  sit,&quot;  a  voice  said.  A  space  was  cleared  on  a  bench  for  Bundy  to  sit,  but  at  the  sound  of  the  word  he  could  not  bend  his  knees.  He  remained  standing,  gazing  through  the  bright  glare  at  the  beacon  revolving  on  the  police  car.
