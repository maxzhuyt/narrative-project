Before  she  could  take  further  action,  the  screen  was  darkened  by  a  ragged  flurry  of  wings  as  the  skulldrake  dropped  to  the  ledge  to  take  up  his  sitting-on  duties.  To  Evelyn  Fanshaw,  sinking  into  a  trance  of  mortified  astonishment  and  disappointment,  it  seemed  that  the  next  three  weeks  in  the  life  cycle  of  these  unpleasing  birds  would  be  difficult  to  endure,  but  she  was  too  much  in  the  center  of  her  row  to  take  flight.  If  her  island  had  not  been  Skullda-and  by  now  it  was  quite  evident  to  her  that  it  had  not-what  could  it  have  been?  A  dream  of  sun  and  cider?  Of  fishermen's  and  sailors'  stories  drifting  up  in  phrases  through  the  floor  boards  of  her  bedroom  at  the  little  pub  where  she  had  stayed          with  her  father?  And  yet  it  had  been  of  such  substance  as  to  outlast  every  other  detail  ofthat  summer.  In  storms  of  hail,  rain,  and  sleet,  the  male  bird  sat  on.  Drake  replaced  drake  until,  in  a  gleam  of  watery  sunshine,  a  repulsive  object  with  a  troll's  head  and  weary  eyelids,  tightlyclosed,  struggled  clear  of  the  broken  shell.  United  at  last,  with  all  misunderstandings  cleared  up,  the  parent  birds  ranged  far  and  wide  to  feed  their  offspring.  Sitting  side  by  side  on  the  edge  ofthe  shelf,  they  torea  young  rabbit  to  pieces.  Drawing  out  long  pieces  of  intestine  in  the  manner  of  spinning  Fates,  they  snipped  it  up  with  their  serrated  beaks  and  poked  it  into  the  ready  maw  of  the  young  bird.  It  might,  thoughtEvelyn,  have  been  the  last  chapter  in  a  parish-magazine  serial.  But  not  all  domestic  life  ran  smooth-  ly  on  the  island.  Pursuing  the  fortunes  of  another  pair,  Mr.  Chance's  camera  recorded  tragedy.  A  drake,  his  return  to  the  nest  delayed  by  some  mischance,  arrived  in  time  to  witness  the  terrible  final  act,  as  his  wife,  standing  on  one  webbed  foot  and  addressing  the  egg,  drew  back  the  other  foot  and  kicked  the  egg  far  out  to  sea  with  all  the  aplomb  of  a  professional  footballer.  The  lec-  turer  reassured  his  horrified  audience,  in  his  kind,  flat  voice-the  incident  had  occurred  early  in  the  season;  the  duck  would  lay  again,  and  the  pair  would  have  plenty  of  time  to  resume  the  old  tussle.  Skullda,  he  said,  illustrated  neat-  ly  the  beneficent  balance  of  nature  when  left  to  itself.  A  colony  of  rabbits  existed  on  the  short,  wind-swept  turf  of  Skull-  da.  The  population  was  kept  to  a  work-  able  minimum  by  the  skullduck,  which  preyed  on  them.  Without  the  birds,  the  rabbits  would  have  nibbled  the  island  bald  within  six  weeks  and  perished  of  starvation.  Without  the  rabbits,  the  skullducks  would  have  become  extinct  in  a  week,  as  there  was  nothing  else  for  them  to  feed  on  except  snails,  and  they  were  bad  at  both  flying  and  swim-  ming,  and  could  never  hope  to  reach  the  mainland.  They  did  not  care  for  fish  or  plankton,  and  only  ventured  on  the  water  in  a  dead  calm  for  the  fun  of  the  thing.  On  this  note  of  hope  and  cheer,  the  lecture  ended,  and  the  audience  rose  to  the  national  anthem,  for  the  most  part  feeling  instructed  and  uplifted. He  had  some-  times  contemplated  marriage,  but  had  never  been  able  to  find  time  for  the  elaborate  courtship  ritual  that  his  calling  had  led  him  to  suppose  indispensable.  After  a  few  miles,  he  left  the  Lon-  don  road  and  turned  into  a  network  of  lanes  writhing  across  flat,  watery  country  and  hopping,  by  means  of  humpbacked  bridges,  over  small,  slug-  gish  streams.  This  was  a  district  of  elms  and  willows,  and  idle  mills  crumbling  gently  by  melancholy  backwaters.  He  had  taken  a  sudden  fancy  to  have  a  word  with  Miss  Fanshaw,  and,  without  con-  sulting  Mrs.  Leech,  he  had  apprised  himself  of  her  address  by  consulting  the  telephone  directory.  His  birdnester's  faculty  led  him  without  much  difficulty  to  a  shabby  flint-and-brick  house  on  the  edge  of  an  elm-muffled  village.  Open  gates  sagged  on  their  hinges,  and  the  rutted  drive  must  have  seen  its  last  dressing  of  gravel  many  years  before.  The  front  door  was  open.  Mr.  Chance  pulled  at  the  rusty  bell  and  heard  an  echoing  note  from  within  that  told  him  the  house  was  empty.  The  hollow  voice  of  a  grandfather  clock  in  the  hall  repeated  this  assurance,  and  in  a  moment  an  old,  half-blind  clumber  spaniel  waddled  out  to  inspect  him.  No  doubt  Miss  Fanshaw  was  at  church  and  would  presently  return.  He  sat  down  on  a  bench  in  the  deep  porch  and  looked  about  him.  Peering  into  the  hall,  he  noted  the  ghostly  pattern  of  the  wallpaper,  faded  from  claret  to  apricot  where  the  sunlight  of  half  a  century  had  fallen.  He  counted  sixteen  walking  sticks  bristling  out  ofa  drainpipe  paint-  ed  with  storks  and  irises.  A  number  of  immense  tweed  overcoats  and  water-  proofs  hung  like  bats  from  the  antlers      of  a  warrantable  stag,  crowned  by  a  flotsam  of  shapeless  caps  and  a  straw  boater  draped  with  a  bee  veil.  It  seemed  that  at  any  moment  Dr.  Jevons  Fan-  shaw  might  emerge  from  his  house  in  the  wake  of  his  dog,  but  nobody  came,  and  the  clumber,  with  a  profound  sigh,  laid  his  noble  head  on  the  visitor's  right  instep.  Mr.  Chance  began  to  feel  that  he  had  spent  his  boyhood  in  this  place  and  had  returned  to  lay  his  bones  here.  Happy,  happy  Dr.  Fanshaw-darting  out  on  his  expeditions  from  this  porch,  returning  to  it  again  and  again  until  the  last  of  his  archeological  wild  oats  were  sown  and  he  could  safely  leave  the  har-  vest  to  his  daughter's  care.  Miss  Fanshaw  came  walking  up  the  drive.  She  was  hatless  and  carried  a  Book  of  Common  Prayer  in  a  palely  gloved  hand. Come  in.&quot;  Miss  Fanshaw  dropped  prayer  book  and  gloves  onto  a  marble-topped  table,  and  led  the  way  into  a  small  room  where  the  smoldering  ashes  of  a  log  fire  were  adding  to  the  dust  of  the  day  before.  On  a  desk  in  the  window  Mr.  Chance  observed  an  office  typewriter  under  a  shroud,  and  a  most  orderly  arrange-  ment  of  files  and  books  of  reference.
