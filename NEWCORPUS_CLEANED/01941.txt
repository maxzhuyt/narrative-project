The  apartment  was  dark,  but  the  building  was  quaint,  and  I  could  al-  most  afford  it.  I  left  a  small  deposit  with  the  landlady.  The  woman  was  going  to  call  my  references,  and  I  was  to  leave  my  security  deposit  and  first  " b"38  month's  rent  with  her  that  afternoon.  I  had  the  rest  of  the  morning  to  fill.  For  some  reason,  I  decided  to  go  back  downtown  to  the  area  where  I  saw  the  apartment  with  my  mother  the  previ-  ous  summer.  I  still  felt  that  that  was  really  my  apartment.  Maybe  by  chance  the  tenants  had  moved  out  and  there  was  once  again  a  &quot;For  Rent  sign  in  the  window.  For  less  money  than  I  would  be  paying  for  the  uptown  place,  I  could  have  had  a  whole  floor-  through-sunny,  with  a  yard.  As  I  got  near  the  address  I  felt  the  strong  feeling-the  sense  of  place,  whatever  -coming  back  even  more  intensely  than  on  the  first  visit.  Standing  in  the  front  entrance  of  the  building  was  Ray.  &quot;Hi,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;Ray,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;What  are  you  doing  here?&quot;  &quot;My  father  bought  the  first  floor,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;I  don't  believe  this,&quot;  I  said.  &quot;I  tried  to  rent  this  place  last  year.  I  came  back  to  look.&quot;  &quot;The  building  went  co-op  a  little  whileago,&quot;  Ray  said.  &quot;We're  in  the  middle  of  renovating.&quot;  He  seemed  pleased  with  himself;  I  wondered  if  he  thought  I  had  come  looking  for  him.  &quot;Do  you  want  to  come  in?&quot;  he  said.  The  first  floor  had  been  completely  stripped.  Now  the  rococo  molding  was  gone,  there  was  wall-to-wall  carpeting  and  track  lighting.  The  bathroom  fixtures  had  been  replaced  with  mod-  ern  ones.  It  was  probably  neater  and  cleaner,  but  the  crazy  charm  of  the  place  was  gone.  Now  that  it  had  new  windows  and  plain  doors,  it  was  just  like  a  million  other  apartments.  I  looked  out  a  window  to  the  back  yard;  it  had  been  paved  over  with  bricks,  and  small  trees  were  stuck  into  redwood  planters.  It  was  too  cold  to  go  outside,  and  Ray  said  I  should  sit  down  for  a  minute.  He  pointed  to  a  gray  sofa.  &quot;I'm  in  the  middle  of  work-  ing  on  a  project,&quot;  he  said.  &quot;For  my  parents.  They  bought  a  house  upstate  and  hired  me  to  renovate.&quot;  I  said  I  was  about  to  go  and  put  a  deposit  on  an  apartment  uptown.  \xc2\xab&quot;We're  just  subletting  a  place  right  now,&quot;  Ray  said.  &quot;As  soon  as  this  place  is  finished,  in  a  couple  of  weeks,  then  we  can  move  in.&quot;  &quot;Who's  we?&quot;  I  said. &quot;I  got  married  last  month,&quot;  Ray  said.  He  looked  at  me  with  a  wry  expression.  I  thought  he  was  waiting  for  me  to  burst  into  tears.  &quot;My  father  got  us  this  place  as  a  wedding  pres-  ent.&quot;  &quot;Oh,  how  great!&quot;  I  said.  &quot;Congrat-  ulations!  Who  is  she?&quot;  My  voice  sounded  artificial,  not  because  I  was  upset  over  this  news  but  because  I  just  didn't  care.  &quot;Someone  I  know?&quot;  I  said.  &quot;&quot;No,  no,&quot;  Ray  said.  &quot;She's  a  secre-  tary  at  my  firm;  she  just  started  work  there  a  few  months  ago.&quot;  &quot;You  didn't  know  her  before?&quot;  &quot;No,&quot;  Ray  said.  &quot;It  was  just  one  of  those  things  that  happen  fast.&quot;  &quot;Great,&quot;  I  said. Out  on  the  street,  I  went  to  a  nearby  phone  booth  and  called  my  mother,  who  was  hav-  ing  lunch  with  friends  in  the  city.  We  met  on  Thirty-fourth  Street  to  go  shopping  for  shoes.  My  mother  bought  me  two  pairs:  gray  pumps,  with  a  me-  dium  heel,  and  a  pair  of  purple  san-  dals,  which  resembled,  at  least  as  far  as  I  was  concerned,  those  worn  by  French  prostitutes.
