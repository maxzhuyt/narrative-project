Prairie Rose

The hard winter was over. Cassie Dunham stood on the green-brown prairie rise surveying the land. She breathed deeply, filling her lungs with warm air mingled with a snap of lingering cold. Winter continued its meager fight to keep hold while spring asserted its grip on the earth.

The breeze played catch with Cassie’s skirt, lifting and swirling it around her ankles. The dress hung loosely on Cassie. She, along with the rest of her family, lost weight during a hard winter that deprived them of good nutrition. Her pale skin drank in the spring air, breathing deeply of the restorative breeze.

Spring meant renewal. Soon, the cows would give milk again, and fresh vegetables would pop up in the garden. Cassie knew she should help with the gardening. Ma was busy planting, and she needed to care for the chickens. But the prairie called her.

Slowly, Cassie walked down the rise. A new shanty sat across the property line. A woman knelt in the newly turned soil, planting seed. Cassie had never seen this woman before. New settlers were arriving every day, and new shanties were popping up all over the place.

The town was growing too. Across the horizon, Cassie saw the smoke billing up from the train. New buildings appeared daily. The one street expanded into two then three with cross streets and houses filling the lots. Cassie longed for the open prairie. She wanted to move further west, leaving civilization behind.

“How do you do?” a male voice stated, startling Cassie.

A man in a buggy drawn by two brown horses stop beside her. At first Cassie didn’t recognize him. His face seemed familiar then his name dawned on her.

“Arthur Butler!” Cassie exclaimed, remembering her schoolmate from back in Illinois. How long it seemed since the Dunham’s moved away from their old farm.

“Do I know you?” Arthur asked, his brow furrowing.

“I’m Cassie, Cassie Dunham,” she exclaimed, smiling broadly. “Don’t you remember? We went to school together in Illinois.”

Recognition appeared on Arthur’s face. He smiled broadly and leaped from his buggy. Cassie smiled at him when he stood beside her.

“You pinned my braids to your desk,” she exclaimed.

“Guilty as charged.” Arthur laughed. “How’s your sister? Um…”

“Josey,” Cassie supplied. “She’s well. She’s at home with Ma. That’s where I should be, but I couldn’t resist the prairie this morning.”

“I was sure smitten with her back in Illinois.” Arthur sighed in memory.

Cassie felt deflated. Josey always had admirers. She was prettier than Cassie, more delicate. Josey liked to sit at home, reading, studying. She kept her hair neatly plaited and her dresses clean, wrinkle-free. Cassie wanted the fresh spring air and sunshine. Her dress wore grass stains, and her hair always escaped her braids. Arthur would prefer Josey.

“Would you like to see the family?” Cassie asked tentatively. “It’s been a long time.” The Dunham’s and Butler’s had been close friends in Illinois.

“Yes,” Arthur answered over-eagerly. “Get in the buggy and I’ll drive you. It’s the shanty beyond the rise, right?”

Arthur helped Cassie into the buggy, and they drove the short distance.

“Look who I found,” Cassie exclaimed, rushing into the shanty. “Arthur Butler. What a surprise.”

“Pleased to see you again, Art,” Ma Dunham exclaimed, rising from her rocker. “How’s your family. Are they settlers here too?”

“They’re still back home on the farm.” Art smiled. “I’m homesteading. Got a quarter section other side of town. Fancy meeting you here. Hello, Josey.”

“Nice to see you, Arthur.” Josey barely looked up from her knitting.

“Yes, well.” Arthur cleared his throat. “I hope we’ll see more of each other, in future.” He tried to sound hopeful, but Josey continued to freeze him out. He said a quick goodbye and backed out of the shanty.

Cassie continued to think of Arthur while she milked the cows. The small farm thrived once spring settled on the prairie. There were milk and eggs. With flour bought in town, Cassie baked bread and made cookies and cake. It was nice to have fresh food to eat again although meat was still scarce. Pa occasionally got a rabbit, but he grumbled because the settlers were scaring away the game.

Spring, Cassie decided, was her favorite time of year. The earth renewed itself, and so did the small animals and the farm stock. Little yellow chicks delighted her, and she became fond of the calves and heifers.

All over the prairie, settlers worked their farms. They planted crops and cared for their stock. No one socialized in spring. It was a busy time. Arthur, Cassie knew, worked in his fields. He didn’t visit, but she knew he was there, close.

“Did you like Arthur?” Cassie asked Josey. Cassie was kneading bread while Josey embroidered. She noticed Josey rarely worked in the fields, she never milked cows and never cleaned or baked.

“I can’t say I noticed,” Josey muttered, dismissing the subject.

“He was smitten with you,” Cassie ventured. “I remember he was always staring at you. Didn’t you notice?”

“Not particularly.” Josey set her embroidery hoop aside and picked up a book.

“Oh.” Cassie thought a moment. “I think he’s nice.”

Ma appeared with a basket filled with spring onions and new potatoes. She handed it to Cassie.

“I wish we had lettuce for salad,” Cassie suggested. “And tomatoes.”

“You can cream the potatoes and onions,” Josey stated, looking up from her novel.

“You can cream them,” Cassie shot back.

“Girls!” Ma cut in.

“She can do something for a change,” Cassie challenged. “It’s not me all the time.”

“Hm.” Josey stood and brushed off her skirt as though dust clung to it. She walked to her bedstead and plunked down. A stony look crossed her face.

“Miss Perfect,” Cassie muttered, placing the basket on the table.

“I won’t have animosity between my girls,” Ma stated. “You two get along.” It sounded like an order.

Cassie did not respond. She marched outside and up to the rise, her private area. It wasn’t fair that Josey did nothing while Cassie slaved on the farm. Cassie loved the farm. She loved the animals, the garden, the fresh air and the spring. It was a part of her soul. But she didn’t want to do everything. They should share the work.

Josey got away with it because Josey had scarlet fever. That was years ago when they were little girls. Josey was older, and, although still delicate, stronger. It was a crutch that Josey clung to. Ma and Pa allowed it.

Cassie started to run. She ran across the prairie, tears flying off her cheeks. She wanted to be wild and free, like the prairie in spring. Her hair unraveled from her loose braids and clung to her moist cheeks. Her skirt twisted between her shins; she stumbled and fell.

“Are you okay?” Arthur asked, kneeling beside her.

“Yes,” Cassie’s voice was muffled. She pulled herself up and sat amongst the green grass.

“You fell. Are you hurt?”

“No, not really.” Cassie looked up into his golden eyes. They looked like hawks’ eyes.

Arthur sat beside her, hugging his knees. They remained in companionable silence.

“The east has developed so much, there’s no room to move anymore,” he ventured. “I suppose it will happen out here in the west too, someday. I like the wide-open spaces. I had to get away.”

“We lived in Missouri for a while until Pa couldn’t stand it,” Cassie stated. “He would keep going west, and I would too. Ma wants to stay here. Josey would go back to Illinois.”

They talked about the east and farming, about the shanties and the feel of grass beneath bare feet. Cassie sighed in contentment. After a while, they decided to walk. The prairie seemed flat to look at but it rose and fell in small rises. For a moment, Arthur disappeared. Cassie searched for him and finally discovered him in a curved depression in the ground. He held a flower out to her.

“A prairie rose.” Cassie placed it under her nose and drew in the sweet scent. She twirled its stem as they walked on.

“You’re beautiful,” Arthur stated.

Cassie turned quickly to look at him. He followed her a short distance behind. She was not beautiful; she knew it. Her undone hair swung across her broad back, her face was freckled from the sun. She wasn’t tall or delicate like Josey. She stated the fact, turning away from him.

“A man wants a woman to work beside him—a woman who knows how to farm and who loves animals,” Arthur started. “He doesn’t want a delicate flower. His flower must withstand all the prairie throws at her. Like this rose.” He plucked the stem from between her fingers. “It thrives here because it’s strong. Because of its strength, it’s beautiful. Be my prairie rose, Cassie?”

“Yes,” Cassie responded. She didn’t think, she answered because she knew. Arthur handed her the rose again. She smelled it and smiled at him. Then they kissed. The day had grown to a close while they lingered in the prairie depression. Golden sunlight dipped into the horizon, bathing them in its last rays.

The awakening of spring brought new life to the prairie. It budded with freshness and hope. The years would bring joy and hardship to Cassie and Arthur, but they would always celebrate spring with its eternal hope of renewal.
